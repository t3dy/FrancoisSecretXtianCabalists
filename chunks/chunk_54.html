Secret concludes this extended treatment by demonstrating that Christian kabbalah became not merely a scholarly pursuit but a widespread cultural fashion transcending sectarian boundaries. The dissemination occurred through reference literature, pedagogical works, and the vast enterprise of Renaissance symbolism. **Encyclopedic Canonization** Petrus Crinitus's *De l'honnête discipline* (frequently reprinted) cited Pico's *Apologia* extensively and devoted an entire chapter to Ezra's learning and memory in organizing Hebrew philosophy. Ludovicus Cœlius Rhodiginus's *Antiquae lectiones* (1517), equally widespread, summarized Pico's kabbalah and magic chapters. Jacques de Middendorp's *De clarissimis magistratibus* (1567, reprinted 1602) proliferated this transmission further. Celio Calcagnini's *Opera* addressed a nephew on kabbalah's etymology, divine and angelic names, and systematically redirected him to Capnion (Reuchlin). Paul Jove's *Eulogia* lauded Pico for discovering sacred script secrets in single words, praised Reuchlin for extracting Jewish mysteries confirming Christian faith, and condemned Agrippa—"found only in the hands of the impious." Conrad Gesner's *Bibliotheca universalis* (1545+), continued by Lycosthenes and Simler, functioned as the first kabbalistic bibliography, cataloging Agrippa, Chéradame, Galatin, Giorgi, Giustiniani, Heredia, Reuchlin, Ricius, and Postel. This encyclopedia became foundational to subsequent kabbalistic scholarship. **Pedagogical Transmission** Henry Mauroy, a Reims Franciscan, compiled an *Apologie pour les Juifs convertis* (1553) drawing on Gesner to illustrate converted Jewish learning. Robert Constantin's *Nomenclator* (1605) devoted a chapter to kabbalah, distinguishing philosophical mysticism from exegetical mysticism while warning students against dangerous modern curiosities. Crucially, he identified Rabbenu Haccados as responsible for superstitious (not legitimate) kabbalah, citing his refutation of Agrippa and directing readers to Rabbi Abraham ben Levi, Pico, Reuchlin, Lulle, and Hostraten's *Destruction of the Kabbalah*. Michel Neander, a Basel corrector for Oporin, published *Interrogationes linguæ sanctæ* (1556; enlarged 1567), substantially expanding Gesner. Despite virulent antisemitism (cataloging Jewish "impiety, vanity, blindness, superstition, perfidy, cruelty, blasphemy"—crucifixions, desecrations), Neander acknowledged "good kabbalah" preserved in Ezra's 70 books, distinguishing it from Egyptian-corrupted modern kabbalah. His personal copy at the Bibliothèque Nationale (annotated by Gilbert Gaulmin) meticulously marked all kabbalistic references, including Postel's Zohar translation. **Kabbalah and Renaissance Symbolism** The section situates Christian kabbalah within broader Renaissance symbolic enterprises—Alciato's emblems, Piero Valeriano's hieroglyphics—as expressions of Pico's vision of "poetic theology" (*poetica theologia*) seeking "new symbolics irresistibly bearing linguistic properties beyond prior hopes." Kabbalah merged with emblematics, providing hermeneutic methods for extracting hidden meanings from text, image, and natura naturans (nature itself). **Concluding Synthesis** The work concludes by emphasizing the eschatological dimension—the "Elias Artista" motif pervading Christian kabbalah. Postel's claim to have witnessed divine script in the heavens; Paracelse's successive incarnations of wisdom; Glauber's *Elias Artista*—all express the conviction that essential truth remains hidden until a transformative revelation. Secret notes that André Chastel identified obscurity itself as philosophically valorized in Renaissance thought, elevating mystery as an epistemological principle. Thus Christian kabbalah transcended technical exegesis to become a metaphysical claim about the veiled nature of ultimate reality and the mystical illumination reserved for the elect—a fashion that united scholars, physicians, alchemists, theologians, and visionaries in a shared pursuit of hidden wisdom.
