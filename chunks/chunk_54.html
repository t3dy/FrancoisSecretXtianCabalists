Secret concludes its extended treatment of Christian kabbalah by demonstrating that kabbalah became not merely a specialized scholarly pursuit or theological controversy but a widespread cultural fashion transcending sectarian and confessional boundaries. The dissemination of kabbalistic ideas occurred through multiple channels of intellectual transmission: reference literature, pedagogical works designed for students, and the vast enterprise of Renaissance symbolism itself. These mechanisms transformed esoteric Jewish mysticism into common intellectual property available to educated Europeans regardless of religious affiliation or institutional position.

Encyclopedic works functioned as primary vehicles for canonizing kabbalah within Renaissance learning. Petrus Crinitus's De l'honnête discipline (frequently reprinted and widely accessible) cited Pico's Apologia extensively and devoted an entire pedagogical chapter to Ezra's learning and memory in organizing Hebrew philosophy—positioning kabbalah within classical learning traditions. Ludovicus Cœlius Rhodiginus's Antiquae lectiones (1517), equally widespread and frequently consulted, summarized Pico's kabbalah and magic chapters for general audiences. Jacques de Middendorp's De clarissimis magistratibus (1567, reprinted 1602) proliferated this transmission further through subsequent generations. Celio Calcagnini's Opera addressed a nephew on kabbalah's etymology, divine and angelic names, and systematically redirected readers to Capnion (Reuchlin) as authoritative source. Paul Jove's Eulogia lauded Pico for discovering sacred script secrets hidden in single words, praised Reuchlin for extracting Jewish mysteries confirming Christian faith, while condemning Agrippa—"found only in the hands of the impious." These encyclopedic treatments established kabbalah as legitimate knowledge within Renaissance education while policing its boundaries and Christian interpretation.

Conrad Gesner's Bibliotheca universalis (1545+), continued by Lycosthenes and Simler, functioned as the first comprehensive kabbalistic bibliography, cataloging Agrippa, Chéradame, Galatin, Giorgi, Giustiniani, Heredia, Reuchlin, Ricius, and Postel as authoritative sources. This encyclopedia became foundational to subsequent kabbalistic scholarship, creating a canon of reliable texts and establishing canonical figures. The bibliography's existence meant that kabbalah became institutionalized as a field of study with recognized authorities and primary sources—a crucial step in its transition from exotic novelty to established discipline within Renaissance intellectual culture.

Pedagogical transmission through teaching works designed specifically for student audiences extended kabbalah's reach into educational institutions. Henry Mauroy, a Reims Franciscan, compiled an Apologie pour les Juifs convertis (1553) drawing on Gesner to illustrate how converted Jewish learning could be integrated into Christian theology. Robert Constantin's Nomenclator (1605) devoted an entire chapter to kabbalah, carefully distinguishing philosophical mysticism from exegetical mysticism while warning students against "dangerous modern curiosities" that pursued kabbalah without Christian doctrinal safeguards. Crucially, Constantin identified Rabbenu Haccados as responsible for spurious and superstitious kabbalah rather than legitimate kabbalah, citing his refutation of Agrippa and deliberately directing students toward Rabbi Abraham ben Levi, Pico, Reuchlin, Lulle, and Hostraten's Destruction of the Kabbalah—establishing a canon of safe authorities while warning against dangerous alternatives.

Michel Neander, a Basel corrector for the printer Oporin, published Interrogationes linguæ sanctæ (1556; enlarged 1567), substantially expanding Gesner's earlier work with new material on Hebrew mysticism. Despite expressing virulent antisemitism throughout—cataloging Jewish "impiety, vanity, blindness, superstition, perfidy, cruelty, blasphemy" including accusations of ritual crucifixions and desecrations—Neander simultaneously acknowledged the existence of "good kabbalah" preserved in Ezra's seventy books, carefully distinguishing it from Egyptian-corrupted modern kabbalah practiced by contemporary Jews. His personal copy at the Bibliothèque Nationale, annotated by the Protestant scholar Gilbert Gaulmin, meticulously marked all kabbalistic references with manuscript annotations, including Postel's Zohar translation. This paradoxical combination—virulent antisemitism coupled with serious engagement with kabbalistic texts—reveals how Christian scholars could affirm kabbalah's spiritual value while condemning Jews themselves as spiritually corrupted.

The broader context situates Christian kabbalah within the vast enterprise of Renaissance symbolic interpretation. Alciato's emblems and Piero Valeriano's hieroglyphics represented parallel attempts at extracting and systematizing hidden meanings from image, text, and natural forms. All emerged from Pico's foundational vision of "poetic theology" (poetica theologia)—seeking "new symbolics irresistibly bearing linguistic properties beyond prior hopes." Kabbalah merged seamlessly with emblematics, providing hermeneutic methods for extracting hidden meanings from text, image, and natura naturans (nature itself as divine text). The entire symbolic enterprise reflected Renaissance confidence that reality itself contained multiple layers of meaning accessible to trained interpretation.

The work concludes by emphasizing the eschatological dimension pervading Christian kabbalah: the "Elias Artista" motif—the transformative revelation reserved for future ages. Postel's claim to have witnessed divine script written in the heavens; Paracelse's doctrine of successive incarnations of wisdom through history; Glauber's Elias Artista celebrating the coming age of alchemical enlightenment—all express the conviction that essential truth remains hidden until a transformative revelation occurs. Secret notes that André Chastel identified obscurity itself as philosophically valorized in Renaissance thought, elevating mystery as an epistemological principle rather than a deficiency. Thus Christian kabbalah transcended technical exegesis to become a metaphysical claim about the veiled nature of ultimate reality and the mystical illumination reserved for the elect—a fashion that united scholars, physicians, alchemists, theologians, and visionaries in a shared pursuit of hidden wisdom and the conviction that surface reality concealed deeper truths awaiting decipherment by those with eyes to see.
