Chunk 23

Pages 71-75

Opening the *Scechinah* with Ezekiel's gate (XLVI:2—closed except on Sabbath), Gilles positions Kabbalah as the "domestic, not foreign" key to revealed Scripture. He castigates exegetes who prize eloquence over sacred letters, preferring pagan authorities (Pliny, Dioscorides, Theophrastus, Aristotle) to the divine mysteries embedded in Hebrew. The dialectic of Kabbalah—revealing the seventy-two aspects of Torah—supersedes Aristotelian logic. Crucially, Gilles defends the theological legitimacy of Scriptural abbreviation: God's word, if fully written, would require volumes; hence the Divine Scribe compressed revelation "after the manner of ancient notation" (like MTC for Marcus Tullius Cicero). The Matthean dictum ("not one iota shall pass"—V:17) and John's promise of continued revelation through the Spirit justify an exegetical infinity: the Shekinah (divine indwelling) transmits continuous intelligence across centuries, not merely through Gospels. The *Scechinah*, personified as the tenth Sephirah (Malkhuth, the Kingdom), narrates a cosmic ascent for Charles V through the angelic palaces of Uriel, Raphael, Gabriel, and Michael, revealing the divine names arranged in three ternaries plus the Shekinah herself. The invocation of eagle-wings, the Parnassus (mystically read as Gan Heden, the Garden), and the theophanic descent draws on both Psalms and Greek mythology—a characteristic Renaissance fusion. The text emphasizes correspondence between high and low: God inscribed signs in matter as Egyptians imitated in hieroglyphics, guaranteeing symbolic continuity between celestial and terrestrial realms. The Shekinah embodies this mediatorial function: she is the Bride, the Church, Zion, Mother, Dove, Eagle, and the luminous Parnassa nourishing humanity. She dwells not in abstract theology but in the lives of men, making mystical knowledge accessible and redemptive. This chunk includes engraved portraits: Portrait IV (Pietro Galatino, from De Angelis, 1713) and allegorical title-pages (Novum Testamentum Syriacum, Vienna 1555) featuring the sephirotic vision of Saint John, demonstrating how Christian Kabbaliste visualized mystical theology through emblem and engraving.
