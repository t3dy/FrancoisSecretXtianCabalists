Pages 38-40

The *De arte cabalistica* presents Kabbalah as an alchemical science transmuting external perceptions into internal images, thence into opinion, reason, intelligence, spirit, and finally light—a deification symbolized by Tiferet (the microcosmic Great Adam) at the center of the sephirotic tree. Simon ben Eliezer opens by citing the *Shaare Orah* (Gates of Light): the Law begins "Tohu" (chaos) and "Bohu" (void) to signify that the earth—understood as divine Idea—produces living beings according to their species through the creative power Adonai. All creatures tend upward; the *Sefer Yetzirah* (Book of Creation) teaches that the ten Sephiroth must be understood in wisdom and intelligence, established in purity, and the Creator restored to his throne. Kabbalistic ascent requires moral and intellectual ascesis, not mere mathematics or metaphysics, but maturity and divine grace—exemplified by Rabbi Eleazar's refusal to receive mystical instruction until aged sufficiently. Kabbalah is "receptio" (reception), the auricular tradition of the Law received by Moses on Sinai. It is the universal doctrine of human redemption (*Iesu'ah* in Hebrew, Salvation in Latin), beginning with Raziel the angel's revelation to Adam after the Fall, promising a just man from his seed who would bear mercy in the four letters YHWH. The transmission passes through patriarchs (each taught by guardian angels—Zadkiel for Abraham, Raphael for Isaac, Peliel for Jacob, Metatron for Moses) and prophets into the Talmudic era, thence to the medieval Kabbalists, finally entering humanist circles through Pico. Simon elaborates a distinction between *Ma'ase Bereshit* (Work of Creation—natural wisdom, like silver) and *Ma'ase Merkava* (Work of the Chariot—divine wisdom, like fine gold), corresponding to Maimonides' allegory of the gold apple in a silver net: the outer silver beauty conceals inner golden truth. The Fifty Gates emerge from multiplying the ten Sephiroth by five categorical orders of being (elements, elemental compounds, souls, celestial bodies, supercelestial incorporeal beings), each subdivided by ten types of consideration (most general genera through individual accidents)—yielding fifty gates through which all cosmic knowledge passes. This multiplication and geometric demonstration (drawing five diameters through a circle's center) proves the quinaire's constancy: subtracting the excess above five from the maximum and adding the deficit below five to the minimum always yields five. Through these gates, Sabbatic restoration occurs: the world achieves perfect rest when the masculine (Iod) joins the feminine (Vau), fulfilled in Christ as true God and Man.
