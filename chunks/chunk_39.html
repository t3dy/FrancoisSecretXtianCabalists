Spain presents a unique historical paradox: the Iberian peninsula, birthplace of the apologetic impulse that would disseminate Christian kabbalah across Renaissance Europe, became after 1492—the year of the Spanish Inquisition's consolidation and the expulsion of the Jews—increasingly hostile to kabbalistic speculation. Yet the earliest Spanish ecclesiastical responses to kabbalah reveal considerable intellectual complexity. Pedro García, Bishop of Osma, mounted an attack on Pic's theses as superstitious magic, contending that Pic's claim of historical support from Josephus was fraudulent and that the Hebrew authorities (Jerome, Eusebius, Augustine) whom Pic invoked were exegetes rather than kabbalists. García's critique lacked deep engagement with actual kabbalistic texts and methods, instead setting an archly defensive tone that would echo through subsequent Spanish polemics. Alfonso de Zamora (c. 1474–1544), a Complutensian Polyglot collaborator and accomplished Hebraist, adopted a radically different strategy. His *Letter to the Jews of Rome* (1526), appended to his Hebrew grammar, attacked the "imaginative science" of kabbalah while simultaneously deploying Christian kabbalism as a polemical weapon against Jewish exegesis. Zamora demonstrated arithmetically that the Christological name *Ysu* (Jesus) contained within it the sacred *Yahweh* and the name *Mary*; he deployed gematria techniques learned from Heredia or adapted from Galatin to reveal Trinitarian implications hidden in Torah's orthography—the unusually large Dalet in *Ehad* (one/unity) and the shrunken Aleph in *Waikra* (and He called)—all pointing to Christian truth. He incorporated Zohar passages on the forty-two-letter name and divine emanation, reversing the polemic: Hebrew mysticism itself, properly understood, vouches for Christianity. F. Luis de Carvajal (early sixteenth century) followed Zamora's precedent. In *De restituta theologia* (1545), dedicated to Charles V, Carvajal ostentatiously disavowed "superstitious cabalism" while eagerly rehearsing Reuchlin's arguments concerning the divine name and reporting the kabbalistic tradition of the angel Raziel's revelation to Eve of a future Savior. His conclusion crystallizes this rhetorical maneuver: "This cabalistic tradition must be admitted because it is true and suits our faith"—accepting kabbalah's premises when they support Christian doctrine while maintaining nominal skepticism toward its Jewish practitioners. Pedro Ciruelo (fl. 1538) inaugurated a systematic refutation of Christian kabbalah in his *Paradoxæ Quæstiones* (Salamanca), attacking Pic, Reuchlin, and Cardinal Gilles of Viterbo for claiming divine origin and transmission through Esdras. Ciruelo's critique operates on three distinct registers: first, concerning kabbalah's coherence, he notes that Hebrew authorities themselves disagree fundamentally on basic categories—fifty gates, thirty-two paths, seventy-two divine names, three categories—proving the whole enterprise uncertain and non-revelatory; second, on its historical status, he brands kabbalah a clever Jewish invention born when Jews sought to rival the University of Paris and Lombard's *Sentences*, deriving ultimately from Porphyry, Aristotle, or Raymond Lull rather than from Hebrew antiquity; and third, concerning utility, he argues that kabbalistic rules convert no Jews, establish no Christian dogma, and constitute mere "Gerigonça"—the secret jargon of criminals and vagabonds. This three-fold denunciation—incoherent, invented, useless—established an argumentative template subsequent Spanish skeptics would expand. Miguel de Medina (1517–1580), a Dominican who participated in the Council of Trent, refined Ciruelo's framework while carefully distinguishing Talmud from kabbalah. Citing Thomas Waldensis's medieval refutation of Wycliffe, Medina acknowledges that legitimate biblical allegory exists and was cultivated by Philo, Origen, and Augustine, but he condemns the extremism of later Jews who obsess over points and vowel marks as if they encoded cosmic secrets. His most cutting formulation exposes the logical absurdity: "The ease of juggling with points is such that from the same words you can draw the Trinity as well as the Antichrist! What immense farrago of ineptitudes invented by vagabonds to astonish the stupid masses!" Medina questions even whether Hebrew constituted the primitive language, thus undermining the entire presupposition on which Christian kabbalism rested. Jacopo Mazzoni (1508–1590) systematized Medina's scattered objections into formal philosophical propositions defended at Rome (1576), mimicking Pic's own methodological approach. Benedictus Pererius (Pereyra) (1535–1610), a Jesuit from Valencia, composed *Adversus fallaces et superstitiosas artes* (Lyon, 1592), appended to his *Commentary on Daniel*, which associated kabbalistic divine names with magical operations and attack the superstition that Hebrew words possessed inherent supernatural power—a belief Pererius recognized that mages and exorcists cynically exploited. Franciscus Vallesius, Alcalá's physician and an Aristotelian natural philosopher, attacked "mages, kabbalists and Platonists" in *De sacra philosophia* (1587) for imagining that words possessed miraculous efficacy and that Adam's primordial language retained power in Hebrew ecclesiastical hymns like Alleluia and Sabaoth. Franciscus de Torreblanca's *Demonologia* (1623) compendiously marshaled Ciruelo, del Rio, and Pererius, attacking both Jewish and Christian kabbalists alongside magicians like Agrippa, Paracelsus, and Johann Weyer. A related strand of criticism condemned the folk practice *Ensalmo* (healing incantations by charming words) as magical kabbalah, linking popular superstition to elite heterodoxy. Yet the pattern was not uniform. Joannes Baptista Villalpandus (1552–1608), a Jesuit, discussed the mystical animals of Merkabah mysticism and Hebrew commentaries on Ezekiel's throne-vision, citing Reuchlin and the Tetragrammatic exegesis. Ludovicus Ystella, Blasius Viegas, and Sebastianus Barradius each selectively deployed kabbalistic sources—on the Trinity, the Tetragrammaton's numerical properties, the closed Mem—while nominally distancing themselves from superstition. Even Ludovicus ab Alcazar, despite affecting skeptical amusement at Galatin's interpretations, cited Valverdius on how the word *Argaman* (purple) encoded angel names visible to Ezekiel. Jean des Anges exegeted Jesus as four Hebrew letters (*Iod, He, Vau, Chet*) mirroring divine names and effects. Don Juan de Sancta Maria, a converted Turk entertained at the Ghent castle court, challenged Pic's antiquity claims by asking whether the terms "Rabalistes" or "Talmudistes" appear in Arab historical records before Pic, concluding the whole edifice rested on fraud. Michel Servet, operating mostly outside Spain, cited the *Fascicule* on the Messiah enthroned before creation but demurred from deeper engagement, leaving kabbalists to their secrets. Spain's intellectual trajectory moved from receptive engagement (Zamora, Carvajal) through systematic defense against kabbalah (Ciruelo, Medina, del Rio) toward pragmatic selective reengagement (various Jesuits), reflecting both institutional Inquisitorial pressure and genuine theological anxiety about kabbalah's compatibility with Christian doctrine and ecclesiastical authority.
