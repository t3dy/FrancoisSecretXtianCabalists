## Early Spanish Engagement: Zamora and Carvajal Spain, cradle of the apologetic current that spread Christian kabbalah across Europe, paradoxically turned hostile after 1492 and the Inquisition's consolidation. Yet the earliest Spanish responses show complexity. Pedro García, Bishop of Osma, attacked Pic's theses (1489) as superstition and magic, challenging Pic's claim that Josephus never mentioned kabbalah and arguing that the Hebrew authorities cited by Jerome, Eusebius, and Augustine were exegetes, not kabbalists. García lacked deep kabbalistic knowledge but set a defensive tone. Alfonso de Zamora (c. 1474–1544), a Complutensian Polyglot collaborator, took a different tack. His *Letter to the Jews of Rome* (1526, appended to Hebrew grammar) attacked "imaginative science" of kabbalah while simultaneously deploying Christian kabbalism: he demonstrated arithmetically that the name *Ysu* (Jesus) contained *Yahweh* and *Mary*; he cited Gimat Egoz and gematria techniques borrowed from Heredia or Galatin. Zamora sought to turn kabbalistic methods against Jewish claims, showing that the Trinitarian implications in the Torah's orthography (larger Dalet in *Ehad*, smaller Aleph in *Waikra*) and numerical patterns pointed to Christian truth. He also incorporated Zohar passages on the forty-two-letter name and divine emanation. F. Luis de Carvajal (early sixteenth century) followed a similar pattern. In *De restituta theologia* (1545), addressed to Charles V, he disavowed "superstitious cabalism" but eagerly reported Reuchlin on the divine name and the angel Raziel's promise to Eve of a Savior, declaring: "This cabalistic tradition must be admitted because it is true and suits our faith." ## Systematic Condemnation: Ciruelo and Medina Pedro Ciruelo (fl. 1538) initiated systematic refutation in his *Paradoxæ Quæstiones* (Salamanca). He attacked Pic, Reuchlin, and Cardinal Gilles of Viterbo for claiming divine origin and Esdrine transmission. Ciruelo dissects kabbalism into questions: (1) its sense and doctrinal coherence—he notes Hebrew authorities disagree on fifty gates, thirty-two paths, seventy-two divine names, and three categories (*Ginat*)—proving it uncertain and non-revealed; (2) its status as clever Jewish invention, excluded from Scripture and Catholic tradition, derived from Porphyry, Aristotle, or Raymond Lull, born when Jews sought to rival the University of Paris and the Sentences; and (3) its utility—kabbalistic rules neither convert Jews nor establish Christian dogma and constitute "Gerigonça," the secret jargon of criminals. Miguel de Medina (1517–1580), a Council of Trent participant, carefully distinguished Talmud from kabbalah, citing Thomas Waldensis's refutation of Wicleff. Medina grants that great secrets lie under biblical allegory (as Philo, Origen, and Augustine taught) but condemns the extremism of later Jews who obsess over points and letters. He ridicules the ease of deriving either the Trinity or Antichrist from Hebrew wordplay: "The ease of juggling with points is such that from the same words you can draw the Trinity as well as the Antichrist! What immense farrago of ineptitudes invented by vagabonds to astonish the stupid masses!" He questions whether Hebrew was even the primitive language. ## Critique Extended and Codified Jacopo Mazzoni (1508–1590) compiled Medina's scattered arguments into formal propositions defended at Rome (1576) on Pic's model. Benedictus Pererius (Pereyra) (1535–1610), a Jesuit from Valencia, wrote a treatise against "fallacious and superstitious arts" (magic, divination, astrology) in his *Commentaries on Daniel* (1587, separately 1592), associating kabbalistic names with magical operations. He noted that Hebrew words possessed power, a common superstition. Franciscus Vallesius, Alcalá's physician and an Aristotelian, attacked mages, kabbalists, and Platonists who believed words held miraculous power and that Adam's language preserved efficacy in Hebrew ecclesiastical hymns. Later, Franciscus de Torreblanca's *Demonologia* (1623) compiled Ciruelo, del Rio, and Pererius, attacking both Jewish and Christian kabbalists alongside magicians like Agrippa, Paracelsus, and Johann Weyer. A related strand condemned the folk practice *Ensalmo* (healing incantations) as magical kabbalah. ## Nuanced Dissent and Selective Use Not all Spanish ecclesiastics dismissed kabbalah wholesale. Joannes Baptista Villalpandus (1552–1608), a Jesuit, discussed the mystical animals and Hebrew commentaries on the Merkabah (Ezekiel's Chariot) and cited Rabbi Haccados and Tetragrammatic exegesis. Ludovicus Ystella cited Reuchlin and Galatin on the Trinity. Blasius Viegas reproduced Giorgi on the Tetragrammaton's numerical properties. Sebastianus Barradius used themes like the closed Mem and Elijah's prophecy. Even Ludovicus ab Alcazar, despite smiling at Galatin's interpretations, couldn't resist citing Valverdius on the name *Argaman* (purple) concealing angel names from Ezekiel. Jean des Anges exegeted Jesus as composed of four Hebrew letters mirroring divine names and effects. Don Juan de Sancta Maria, a convert Turk entertained by the Gante castle court, questioned whether "Rabalistes" and "Talmudistes" appear in history before Pic, finding no mention in Arab annals, and concluding that Pic's claims of antiquity are unbelievable. Michel Servet, living mostly outside Spain, cited the *Fascicule* on the Messiah enthroned before creation but left kabbalists their secrets, declining deeper engagement. The overall trajectory shows Spain's transition from receptive (Zamora, Carvajal) to defensive (Ciruelo, Medina, del Rio) to cautiously reengaging (various Jesuits), reflecting both Inquisitorial pressure and genuine theological debate over the legitimacy of extracting Christian doctrine from kabbalist methodology.
