Chunk 62

Pages 193-195

This chunk comprises scholarly apparatus—nearly entirely footnotes (notes 52-88) referencing and explaining Christian Kabbalah literature and figures from the late 16th through early 17th centuries. The notes detail works and personalities discussed in the preceding chapter text. Among key figures documented: Pierre Leloyer (1550-1634), an Angevin whom Bayle mocked for claiming Hebrew had scrambled his brain, published *Quatre livres des spectres* (1586, 1605, 1608) on spiritual apparitions and visions. Leloyer adopted the cabala enthusiastically, developing his *Albam*, a cipher system for letter-substitution (the 24th permutation alphabet), which he applied to decoding hidden meanings in ancient texts. The footnotes record his correspondence with Mersenne, demonstrating how these scholars circulated their cabalistic discoveries and methods. Scipion de Grammont and Antoine Auzi represent the playful, erudite margins of cabalistic engagement—Auzi (hiding under the pseudonym "Master Victor Greve") published the *Mastigophore* (1609), and Grammont composed works on the Kabbale's numeric and combinatorial dimensions, citing Vigenère and other predecessors. The apparatus notes that the *Bureau d'adresse* (a Parisian intellectual forum) devoted multiple sessions (1633-34) to cabalistic theory and practice, with speakers debating how to read the Talmud and distinguishing authentic from spurious cabalistic teachings. The footnotes also track manuscript holdings, publication history, and scholarly attribution. They reference Gaffarel's theft of Aquin's cabala diagram (with Mersenne and Peiresc noting the scandal), multiple editions and reconstructions of cabalistic tree diagrams, and Gaulmin's role as a textual editor and corrector of kabbalistic works. The notes establish the bibliographic foundation for understanding who read, owned, and transmitted Hebrew and cabalistic texts across Catholic, Protestant, and Jewish scholarly networks in this period.
