This section chronicles Augustin Giustiniani (1470-1536), a Genoese Dominican and humanist who embodied the convergence of biblical erudition, Kabbalistic learning, and patriotic service. Having entered the Dominicans at fourteen and later relocated to study at Valence, Giustiniani returned to the order at Pavie, where he devoted himself to languages and scriptural questions. He explicitly rejected visionary illuminism, necromancy, and superstition—positioning himself as a critical, orthodox scholar rather than a mystical sectary. Giustiniani's defining achievement was the 1516 polyglot Psalter, printed at Turin in eight columns: Hebrew, Latin juxta-linear, Vulgate, Greek, Arabic with juxta-linear translation, Chaldean Targum in Hebrew letters, plus extensive scholia drawn from Hebrew commentaries. Six years before Alcalá's Complutensian Polyglot, this work was revolutionary. Secret quotes Bayle's account of Giustiniani's ambitious but ultimately disappointing distribution strategy: he printed 2,050 copies, gifted them to princes Christian and infidel, and prepared 50 on vellum, hoping to fund a complete Bible. Yet the work sold poorly—a cautionary tale about Renaissance patronage and the market for polyglot scholarship. The fragment dedicates the Psalter to Leo X and recounts Giustiniani's French sojourn (until 1522), where he became one of the first Hebrew masters alongside Aléandre. He published Kimchi's grammar, translated the *Guide for the Perplexed*, and produced 107 *Quaestiones* on Genesis attributed to Philo. Giustiniani's library, dispersed after his drowning en route to Corsica, contained over 1,000 volumes—a treasure trove of Latin, Greek, Hebrew, Arabic, and Chaldean manuscripts treating both common and secret sciences. Critically, the scholia preserve the first Latin transcriptions and translations of Zoharic texts—seven passages, mostly from the *Sifra di Tzniutha* (Book of the Hidden), later incorporated into Knorr von Rosenroth's *Kabbala denudata*. One passage, showing Christ's incarnation encoded in Zoharic symbolism, later served Denis Saurat to illustrate themes of divine sexuality and Renaissance pantheism.
