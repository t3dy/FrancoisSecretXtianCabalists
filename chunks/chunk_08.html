Pico's 900 theses included two distinct series of Kabbalistic Conclusions: 47 attributed to "secret doctrine of Hebrew Kabbalists" and 71–72 according to Pico's own interpretation, both harmonized with Christian revelation. Rather than presenting them systematically, Pico organized them thematically, a choice that invited later exposition by Reuchlin, Archangelus de Burgonovo, Paul Rici, and François Georges de Venise. These attempts at comprehensive glossing—most thoroughly by Scholem's analysis—reveal how Pico synthesized Hebrew mystical sources, especially Mithridates' translations. Key themes include the sacred power of Hebrew letters and their forms: Conclusion 33 states that every letter in the Law manifests secrets of the ten sephiroth through shape, conjunction, separation, curvature, and diacritical marks. Pico enumerated the Sephiroth (the ten divine emanations)—Kether (Crown), Hochmah (Wisdom), Binah (Understanding), Hesed (Mercy), Geburah (Severity), Tiferet (Beauty), Netzah (Eternity), Hod (Majesty), Yesod (Foundation)—showing their correspondence to celestial spheres and human soul-powers. He explained the Thirty-Two Paths (combining ten Sephiroth and twenty-two Hebrew letters) and the Fifty Gates of Understanding, linking these to Sabbatical cosmology: in Conclusion 9 he audaciously calculated the end of the world at "514 years and 24 days" from his time using secret Kabbalistic method. The Divine Names receive extensive treatment: the Tetragrammaton (four-lettered YHWH), the 72-lettered name derived through gematria, and Adonai. Pico asserts that the Name of Jesus, in its Kabbalistic interpretation, encompasses all these powers, synthesizing Jewish mystical nomenclature with Christian trinitarian theology. The Conclusions on these subjects proved foundational: later Christian Kabbalists (Galatin, Agrippa, Postel) returned constantly to this nexus of sephirotic emanation, Hebrew gematria, and Christological symbolism, making Pico's youth theses the blueprint for generations of humanist mysticism.
