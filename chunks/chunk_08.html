The most immediate and tangible product of Pico's Hebrew learning materialized in his famous 900 theses presented for public disputation at the end of 1486, organized into two distinct series on Kabbalah. The first series comprised 47 conclusions derived from "the secret doctrine of the wise Hebrew Kabbalists, whose memory shall always be respected"; the second series offered 71 conclusions (actually 72 in some counts) drawn "from the author's own opinion, derived from the principles of the wise Hebrews, which most confirm the Christian religion." These were supplemented by Kabbalistic conclusions appearing in other series, most notably the famous ninth conclusion among the conclusions on magic according to the author's own opinion, which became a primary target of papal censorship. This organizational strategy—distinguishing between conclusions faithfully reporting kabbalistic doctrine from those representing Pico's own synthesis—reveals an important methodological awareness: Pico presented himself as both transmitter of Jewish tradition and creative interpreter capable of extracting Christian meaning from materials that, at their source, possessed no such meaning. The theses were not systematic in their arrangement; a public disputation would presumably have clarified their logical relationships, but lacking that disputation, the theses remain aphoristic and enigmatic, inviting subsequent interpretation and commentary.

The intellectual task of clarifying Pico's kabbalistic conclusions fell to his successors, most systematically to Archangelus of Burgonovo, a Franciscan scholar who glossed each conclusion in both series methodically. However, Archangelus' glosses often illuminate less Pico's original sources than the speculative developments of Pico's immediate successors—Reuchlin, Paul Rici, and François Georges of Venice (Archangelus' own master). Subsequent centuries of commentators attempted the same work: Paul Skalichius aspired to become another Pico and produced his own commentary; Joseph de Voisin, the Jesuit Menocchio, and Athanasius Kircher all attempted explanations; the modern scholar M. Scholem's work remains "without doubt the best" attempt, collaborating with Hebrew specialists U. Cassutto, though even Scholem's efforts have not produced comprehensive manuscript-based study of the surviving materials. Crucially, Scholem demonstrated how Pico's 15th conclusion in the first series concerning Zoroaster should be explained not through the Bahir text that Pico explicitly cited but through passages in commentaries translated by Mithridates—a crucial methodological insight showing that Pico's explicit citations sometimes masked different textual sources, that his scholarly apparatus obscured rather than revealed his actual learning.

Within this complex thematic landscape, one fundamental intellectual theme stands preeminent: the doctrine that Hebrew itself, the language in which Torah was revealed, contains secrets accessible only through technical analysis of the letters, their forms, conjunctions, separations, twists, directions, character marks, fullness or defect, diminution or enlargement, and crowns, closures, and openings. Conclusion 33 of the first series states this principle with crystalline clarity: "There is not a single letter in the entire Law whose forms, conjunctions, separations, twists, directions, characters full or defective, smallnesses, greatnesses, crowns, closures, openings and order do not manifest the secrets of the ten numerations." This doctrine of literal mysticism—that the very shapes and combinations of Hebrew letters encode divine mysteries—represents one of the most distinctive features of medieval Kabbalism, and Pico embraced it with full recognition of its radical implications. Examples multiply: the addition of the letter He to Abraham's name effects his generative power; the mystery of the closed Mem reveals why Christ sent the Paraclete after himself; the letter Shin in the midst of the name of Jesus signifies divine perfection. Each of these conclusions represents both a genuine assertion extracted from Jewish mystical sources and a Christianizing reinterpretation that the sources themselves would not necessarily support.

The doctrine of the ten sephiroth, the fundamental organizational structure of medieval Kabbalism, permeates Pico's conclusions, though he never provided a complete enumeration and explanation of all ten. The sephiroth present themselves variously as a tree, as an edifice, as garments, and as interconnected through channels of circulation and emanation. The conclusions systematically work through crucial distinctions: En Soph (the Infinite, or Boundless), the supreme reality beyond predication or determination, must not be counted among the other sephiroth because it represents "the abstract unity and communicating unity of these numerations, not a unity coordinated with them." The fall of Adam represented "the amputation of the Kingdom, the trunk of the other plants"; the sin of Sodom "was accomplished by the amputation of the final plant." This botanical metaphor—the sephiroth as interconnected plants in a cosmic garden—would long fascinate Renaissance thinkers, notably Leibniz, who recorded in his notes: "Ancient tradition: the sin of Adam was the severance of Malcouth from the other plants. Malcouth or the Kingdom, the final of the Sephiroth." The ten sephiroth, enumerated as Keter (Crown), Chokmah (Wisdom), Binah (Intelligence), Chesed (Mercy), Gevurah or Din (Power or Judgment—the principle of divine severity), Rahamim or Tiferet (Compassion or Beauty—the mediating principle between severity and mercy), Netzach (Eternity), Hod (Majesty), Yesod (Foundation of all active forces in God), and Malkhuth (Kingdom)—each carried elaborate theological implications and mystical correspondences that Pico selectively deployed.

Pico's methodology for engaging the sephiroth demonstrates sophisticated philosophical ambition. In one conclusion of the second series, he identifies Chokmah (Wisdom among the Kabbalists) with the Pallas of Orpheus, the divine intelligence of Zoroaster, the Son of God of Hermes Trismegistus, the Wisdom of Pythagoras, and the intelligible sphere of Parmenides—a syncretic move that positions Hebrew mysticism as expressing the same philosophical truths articulated across multiple ancient religious and philosophical traditions. Tiferet appears as the heart of the cosmic edifice: "It is more correct to say that Paradise is the entire edifice rather than just the tenth, and in its center is placed the great Adam who is Tiferet." Chesed and Din (Mercy and Judgment) reveal themselves as complementary divine attributes eternally balanced in God's action: "Although the ineffable name belongs to the property of Clemency, one must not deny that it contains the property of Judgment." The relationships among the sephiroth manifest themselves hierarchically: "It is said that a river flowed out of Eden which divided into four arms, to signify that from the second numeration proceeds the third, which divides into the fourth, fifth, sixth, and tenth." In a systematic series of conclusions, Pico established correspondences between the sephiroth and the celestial spheres: Jupiter corresponds to the fourth sephiroth, Mars to the fifth, the Sun to the sixth, Saturn to the seventh, Venus to the eighth, Mercury to the ninth, the Moon to the tenth, with the Firmament corresponding to the third, the First Mover to the second, and the Empyrean heaven to the first—a mapping that transforms sephirothic theory into coherent cosmological doctrine.

The sephiroth function fundamentally as the mechanism of divine revelation and cosmic creation. Conclusion 35 of the first series articulates this principle: "No spiritual thing descending below does so without an envelope. From the conclusion preceding one can understand why it is said among the Kabbalists that God clothed himself in ten garments when he created the world." The act of creation involves progressive veiling and emanation, each level obscuring yet manifesting the divine through ever-thicker envelopes of materiality. This process grows complex when one considers the multiple interpretive layers: to say "Bereshit" (in the beginning) means simultaneously to say "He created in Wisdom," as the Chaldean Targum (interpretive paraphrase) Onkelos renders it, revealing that "from the principle things [he] intelligized thirty-two ways of Wisdom." This multiplicity of interpretive layers—linguistic, exegetical, numerical—reflects the hermeneutical density that Pico discovered in Jewish sources and which he deployed to argue for depths of meaning that literal reading alone could never yield. He even proposed in a separate conclusion that the thirty-two paths (derived from the ten sephiroth plus the twenty-two letters of the Hebrew alphabet) should be understood through sapient ideas rather than the traditional formulation of thirty-two paths, "although both explanations are valid in Kabbalah."

The doctrine of the fifty gates represents the complement to emanation: it symbolizes the return to God, the reintegration of the emanated with its source. Conclusion 13 of the first series states: "Whoever knows in Kabbalah the mystery of the fifty gates of Intelligence will know the mystery of the great jubilee." This mystery receives elaboration in the second series' conclusions 68 and 69, where Pico promises that knowledge of formal arithmetic—the nature of the first spheric number—will reveal secrets "which I have not yet read in any Kabbalist, and which is the foundation of the secret of the great jubilee." These fifty gates, connected to the jubilee year when all debts are forgiven and all slaves freed in biblical law, symbolize cosmic restoration and the end of temporal exile—a profoundly resonant concept for Jewish thinkers in the medieval and Renaissance periods. The mystery crystallizes in the doctrine of Sabbath: through the three letters in the word "Sabbath" (Shin, Beth, Tav), one interprets kabbalisticalistically that "the world will keep Sabbath when the Son of God becomes man, and the coming Sabbath when men have been regenerated into sons of God." Most daringly, Pico proposed in conclusion 9 of the second series a numerical calculation of the end times: "If there is any human conjecture about the final times, we can discover through the most secret method of Kabbalah that the consummation of the age will occur 514 years and 24 days hence." This calculation, made in 1486, would echo through the Renaissance, variously interpreted and reinterpreted by subsequent thinkers who seized upon Pico's apparent kabbalistic methodology for calculating apocalyptic dates.

The implications of this apocalyptic calculation preoccupied Renaissance thinkers. In his Disputationes against the Astrologers (composed after the Heptaplus in 1489, published by his nephew in 1496 with Savonarola's endorsement), Pico ridiculed Arab, Jewish, and Christian astrologers who predicted the end of Christianity, the coming of the Messiah, or the arrival of Antichrist—a critical move that seemed to contradict his own prophetic calculation in the Conclusions. Yet in the Heptaplus itself, calculating the ages of the world according to the famous "Prophecy of Elijah" (which fixes human history at 6,000 years), Pico deliberately avoided prophecying about the future. Mellin de Saint-Gelais, writing his Warning on Astrological Judgments to a studious Young Woman in 1546 at Lyon, observed with some irony that Pico, having powerfully attacked all judiciaries astrology in his Disputationes, yet in his Heptaplus on the first chapter of Genesis relied so heavily on the qualities of planets that "the reader can take him for the author of contrary opinion against himself"—a shrewd observation that Pico's reasoning sometimes worked against his stated positions. The fate of the specific calculation became curious: Gohory, in a treatise on Paracelsus employing Trithemius, merely reported the thesis within the context of Elijah's prophecy, but in 1570 Theodorus Gramineus, after citing it, added that "Picus also used these conjectures, fixing [the end of the world] in the year 1586." Paschalini Regiselmi, an Italian commentator on Joachim of Fiore's prophecies (edited by Paul Skalichius), in 1589 reported that "It was in 1584 when in the church of SS. Giovanni and Paolo in Venice we publicly discussed this subject using Hebrew calculations, which only the ignorant opposed." The poet J. E. du Monin, who died tragically assassinated in 1586, in an Annotation on the Decease of the World, noted the "divine Pico, prince of Mirandola among his 900 problems, stated since the year 1586 that Kabbalah could demonstrate the consummation of the age, namely after 514 years and 25 days." Finally, Gregorius Michael, a commentator on Gaffarel's Unusual Curiosities in 1676, redating the conclusion to 1486, calculated that "the pantocratic day would fall in the year of Christ 1986"—a striking example of how Pico's methodology generated cascading reinterpretations across centuries, each reader extracting from his calculations a different apocalyptic date.

Beyond the sephirotic framework, Pico proposed elements of a coherent cosmology and psychology. Conclusion 67 of the second series demonstrates the philosophical sophistication: "From the statement of the Kabbalists that the heavens are composed of water and fire (Samain, the heavens, composed of Esh, fire and Maim, water) manifests both the theological truth of the Sephiroth and the philosophical truth that the elements in heaven exist only in relation to active virtue." This remarkable synthesis argues that Hebrew mysticism expresses the same truth about heavenly composition that Greek natural philosophy articulates—a move that legitimizes both traditions simultaneously. Conclusion 66 of the second series extends this mapping to psychology: "I thus make the powers of our soul correspond to the ten sephiroth: its unity to the first, the intellect to the second, reason to the third, the superior concupiscible part to the fourth, the superior irascible part to the fifth, free will to the sixth; the whole insofar as it turns toward the superior to the seventh, and insofar as it regards the inferior to the eighth, their mixture which occurs rather through indifference or by alternating adhesion than by simultaneous contiguity to the ninth, and the power by which it inhabits the first dwelling to the tenth." This mapping of psychological faculties to sephiroth would profoundly influence subsequent Renaissance psychology and philosophy, creating a technical vocabulary for discussing the hierarchy of mental powers. A significant number of conclusions addressed divine names: Conclusion 56 of the first series declares that "Whoever can explain the quaternary within the denary will have the means, if skilled in Kabbalah, to deduce from the ineffable name the name of 72 letters. By the preceding conclusion, whoever understands formal arithmetic will comprehend that to operate by the Shem ha-Mephorash is the property of rational nature." Conclusion 29 of the first series identifies the four-lettered name composed of Mem, Tsade, Pe, Tsade (Maspaz) as the divine name appropriate to the Kingdom of David. Conclusion 2 attributes the agent intellect—the highest intellectual principle in medieval philosophy—to Metatron in Kabbalah, that commanding angel and intermediary between human and divine realms.

These numerous conclusions on divine names converged powerfully on the name of Jesus itself. Conclusion 6 of the second series states with audacious Christological claims: "The three four-lettered names of God found in the mysteries of the Kabbalists should be attributed by an admirable proportion to the three persons of the Trinity: the name Ehie to the Father, the name YHWH to the Son, and Adonai to the Holy Spirit. No Hebrew Kabbalist can deny that the Name of Jesus, if we interpret it according to the principles and manner of Kabbalah, signifies nothing other than God Son of God and Wisdom of the Father through the third person of the divinity which is fire of most ardent love and is united to human nature in the unity of its substance." Conclusion 14 adds: "By the letter Shin, which is in the middle of the Name of Jesus, we are signified kabbalisticalistically that the world rests perfectly as in its perfection when the Iod is conjoined with the Vau, which was done in Christ, who was true God and man." These conclusions represent perhaps the most brazen assertion of Christian Kabbalah's central thesis: that the very language and numerical structure of Hebrew mysticism, properly decoded, yields the central mysteries of Christian trinitarian theology and Christology. Such conclusions introduced a bewildering new vocabulary into humanist discourse: the names of angels—Cherubim, Seraphim, Hasmalim, Hagot, Aralim, Tarsisim, Ophanim, Thephsraim, Isim; the assembly of Israel; Chineseth Israël; Binsica (the death by the kiss); Thesua—terms rendered increasingly obscure by typographical errors (such as Merchiana for Mercava, the Chariot of Ezekiel's throne vision) and the difficulty of Hebrew printing in the Renaissance without adequate fonts. These enigmatic utterances positioned Pico as a guide to sacred mysteries, inviting the most learned humanists of Europe to attempt their decipherment and interpretation.
