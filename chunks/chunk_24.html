Following Gilles of Viterbo's death in 1532, his extraordinary library and manuscripts dispersed among scholarly heirs, most notably Johann Albrecht Widmanstetter (1506-1557), one of the sixteenth century's most versatile humanists and Orientalists. Widmanstetter had studied Hebrew directly with the legendary Johannes Reuchlin, the foundational figure of Christian Kabbalah, and through Reuchlin had inherited not merely linguistic competence but a sophisticated understanding of how Hebrew mysticism could be integrated into Christian theology. His encounter with Teseo Ambrogio at the 1529 coronation of Charles V in Bologna gave him further entrée into the networks of Christian Hebraists. Through his inheritance of or access to portions of Gilles's library, Widmanstetter developed a formidable collection of Oriental manuscripts—Hebrew texts, Arabic commentaries, Syriac documents—assembled with scholarly rigor and substantial expense. Yet his stance toward Kabbalah remained fundamentally ambivalent: fascinated by its intellectual possibilities, theologically cautious about its potential to introduce heterodoxy, intellectually skeptical of its claims while maintaining institutional respect for it as part of Jewish tradition.

Widmanstetter recorded a particularly telling encounter with a converted Jew named Dattylus in Turin—allegedly the same scholar whom Pico della Mirandola had converted through Kabbalistic arguments generations earlier. (If true, this would testify to Dattylus's longevity or suggest confused genealogy, but the story circulates in Widmanstetter's notes with apparent conviction.) Dattylus expounded a doctrine of vital seeds permeating the elements of earth, ascending through herbs and animals, progressively refining into the human soul-faculties, and ultimately achieving divine participation through mystical knowledge. The doctrine articulated something like a theurgic vision where human spiritual practice could harmonize with cosmic principles and effect real transformation in consciousness. Widmanstetter recorded this with evident unease, noting it as a cautionary example of the "monstrous opinions" that could be drawn from Jewish Kabbalah—opinions potentially weaponized (like a Trojan horse within Christian thought) against Christendom's theological foundations. The notation reveals Widmanstetter's cognitive dissonance: he respected Dattylus as a scholar and was intellectually intrigued by kabbalistic doctrine, yet he remained suspicious that the system, if accepted wholesale, might seduce Christians away from orthodox theology toward pantheism, theurgy, or heterodox pneumatology.

Yet Widmanstetter's annotations and scholarly work reveal considerable nuance beyond simple suspicion. His annotations to an Epitome of the Quran (1543) credit the Prophet Mahomet with corrupting admirable Kabbalistic mysteries—specifically, the mysteries of the seven lower Sephiroth (the supernal world reflected in manifestation). Yet Widmanstetter simultaneously acknowledges the symbolic integrity of Kabbalah's "golden and silver keys," his poetic terminology for the balanced principles of Severity (Rigour) and Mercy that govern the Sephirotic system. The phrase reveals Widmanstetter reading kabbalistic symbolism as genuinely profound, recognizing in the paired principles of divine judgment and compassion a framework that Christian theology could validate. His contribution to the 1555 Syriac New Testament edition (produced collaboratively with Moses Mardenus and the prolific Orientalist Guillaume Postel) included a striking innovation: a diagram mapping the five "stigmata" of Christ's crucifixion (the five wounds) onto the Sephiroth. The Trinity is represented in Christ's crowned head (the supernal triad of Keter-Chokmah-Binah); Mercy (Hesed) in the right hand; Rigour (Gevurah) in the left hand; the subaltern symbols (perhaps referring to Beauty-Tipheret, Victory-Netzach, and Glory-Hod) in the feet; and divine Beauty itself in the opened side (the heart, traditional locus of divine indwelling). This diagram simultaneously achieves multiple interpretive goals: it demonstrates Christ's crucifixion as the enactment of Sephirotic principles, translates Christian soteriology into kabbalistic language, and claims that the Kabbalists themselves—whether consciously or unconsciously—had intuited the Christological mysteries embedded in their system.

Girolamo Seripando (1493-1563), who succeeded Gilles as General of the Augustinian hermits, inherited not Gilles's towering erudition in Oriental languages but demonstrated keen, sometimes passionate interest in Kabbalah through extensive personal memoranda and sermon compositions spanning the 1530s through 1560s. Seripando drew on Pico della Mirandola and Reuchlin as intellectual ancestors, studied Galatin's work, drew from Giustiniani's exemplary scholarship, and most importantly, had access to unpublished treatises by Gilles—notably the Historia viginti saeculorum (History of Twenty Centuries) and a translation of Nachmanides' Torah commentary. These manuscript sources made Seripando's private theological work radically different from what would have been accessible to ordinary clergy. He reproduced extensive Zoharic passages on the Trinity, on Elijah's prophecy (a favorite theme of Renaissance kabbalists seeking biblical warrant for their system), and excerpts from Gale Razeia (secrets of the revelation), though he perpetuated Reuchlin's erroneous enumeration of the Sephiroth—a textual error that would prove remarkably durable and influential.

Seripando embraced with particular enthusiasm the Christological theme of Jesus' holy name rendering the Tetragrammon (YHVH) pronounceable for the Christian believer. The name YESHUA (Jesus) contains within itself the four letters of the divine name plus the letter shin (fire, spirit, transformation), suggesting that Jesus' incarnation had made what was previously unutterable and transcendent into speakable, incarnate, and accessible. Seripando also adopted the legend circulating among Christian Hebraists that Jeremiah had authored the Sefer Yetzirah (Book of Formation), the foundational medieval kabbalistic text attributed in the tradition itself to Abraham but plausibly of much later origin. This attribution gave the Sefer Yetzirah ancient prophetic authority within Christian intellectual frameworks—if Jeremiah, the great Jewish prophet, had composed it, then Christian scholars could study it as part of the continuous prophetic tradition rather than as Jewish innovation. The legendary attribution appealed to Kircher (the great Jesuit polymath of the next generation) as befitting Christ the God-Man—the figure in whom all wisdom converges and through whom all ages of prophecy find their interpretation.

The chapter's concluding emphasis underscores that Gilles' kabbalistic vision survived the Counter-Reformation and the Reformation's divisions, transmitted through careful but committed interpreters like Widmanstetter and Seripando who navigated ecclesiastical suspicion while defending kabbalistic learning's legitimacy. These figures maintained a delicate intellectual posture: they acknowledged the dangers of uncritical enthusiasms for Jewish mysticism, insisted on rigorous textual scholarship, refused claims to magical efficacy or theurgy, yet simultaneously maintained that Kabbalah, properly understood, illuminated Christian theology and clarified scriptural meaning in ways scholastic logic could not achieve. This defensive yet learned posture—combining critical suspicion with reverent engagement—would characterize Christian Kabbalah well into the seventeenth century, ensuring that despite mounting ecclesiastical caution, the synthesis Gilles had articulated retained intellectual prestige among the most learned members of Catholic Christendom. The tradition proved resilient not because it achieved universal acceptance but because it had cultivated defenders of sufficient erudition and institutional standing to preserve access to kabbalistic texts and methods within Christian intellectual culture.
