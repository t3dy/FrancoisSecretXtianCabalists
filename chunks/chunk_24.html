Chunk 24

Pages 76-78

Following Gilles of Viterbo's death, his Kabbalistic legacy dispersed among scholarly heirs, notably Johann Albrecht Widmanstetter (1506-1557), humanist and Orientalist. Widmanstetter, who had studied Hebrew with Reuchlin and encountered Teseo Ambrogio at the 1529 coronation of Charles V in Bologna, inherited access to Gilles' library (1532) and developed a formidable collection of Oriental manuscripts. Yet his stance toward Kabbalah remained ambivalent: fascinated but theologically cautious. Widmanstetter recorded a notable encounter with a converted Jew named Dattylus in Turin—allegedly the same scholar Pico della Mirandola had converted through Kabbalistic arguments. Dattylus expounded a doctrine of vital seeds permeating earth and elements, ascending through herbs, animals, and human soul-faculties toward divine participation. Widmanstetter noted this as a cautionary example of "monstrous opinions" drawn from Jewish Kabbalah, potentially weaponized (like a Trojan horse) against Christendom. Yet Widmanstetter's annotations to an *Epitome of the Quran* (1543) reveal nuance: he credits Mahomet with corrupting admirable Kabbalistic mysteries of the seven lower Sephiroth, while acknowledging the symbolic integrity of Kabbalah's "golden and silver keys" (rigour and mercy). He contributed extensively to the 1555 Syriac New Testament edition (with Moses Mardenus and Guillaume Postel), which includes a striking adaptation: a diagram mapping the five "stigmata" of Christ's crucifixion onto the Sephiroth—Trinity in the crowned head, Mercy in the right hand, Rigour in the left, subaltern symbols in the feet, and divine Beauty in the opened side. Girolamo Seripando (successor to Gilles as Augustinian superior), though less erudite in Oriental languages, demonstrated keen interest in Kabbalah through personal memoranda and sermon themes (1530-1560). He drew on Pico, Reuchlin, Galatin, Giustiniani, and unpublished Gilles treatises including the *Historia viginti saeculorum* and a Nachmanides commentary translation. Seripando reproduced Zoharic passages on the Trinity, Elijah's prophecy, and *Gale Razeia* excerpts, though he perpetuated Reuchlin's erroneous Sephiroth list. He endorsed the Christological theme of Jesus' name rendering the Tetragrammon pronounceable, and adopted the legend of Jeremiah and the *Sefer Yetzirah* (which Kircher later praised as befitting Christ the God-Man). This chunk concludes the narrative of Christian Kabbalah's golden age in Italy, showing how Gilles' vision survived through cautious but committed interpreters who defended Kabbalistic learning despite mounting ecclesiastical suspicion.
