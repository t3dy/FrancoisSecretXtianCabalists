Chunk 4

Pages 13-15

Secret details the extensive diffusion and reuse of Ramon Martí's **Pugio Fidei**, a text that shaped Christian Kabbalah more profoundly than almost any other medieval work. Though first printed only in 1651 by Joseph de Voisin (and thus mediated through later hands), it circulated widely in manuscript and was used by **Pascal** in his apologetics. The *Pugio* inspired a series of derivative and related works: **Porchetus de Salvaticis**'s *Victoria adversus Judaeos* (1303), which adapted Martí's methods; **Pierre Galatino** (c. 1460-1540), the Franciscan Hebraist and papal censor, incorporated vast sections of the *Pugio* into his own *De arcanis catholicae veritatis* (On the Secrets of Catholic Truth, 1518), sometimes acknowledged and sometimes not, leading scholars to accuse him of plagiarism; and **Alfonso de Zamora**'s *Sefer Hokmat Elohim* (Book of Divine Wisdom), which absorbed Pugio material into Spanish Kabbalistic-apologetic synthesis. João de Barros, the Portuguese historian, similarly drew on this tradition. Importantly, Secret notes that the *Pugio* did not draw on Kabbalah proper in its developed sense but rather on Talmudic and Midrashic material that overlapped with kabbalistic themes; the prologue frames the entire project as brilliant tactical reversal — turning the enemy's own weapon, the *Torah she-be'al-peh* (oral law) and its elaborations, against Jewish interlocutors by reading Christian doctrine (especially the Trinity and Incarnation) into its depths. He catalogs the recurrent themes that flowed from the *Pugio* through subsequent Christian Kabbalah. The first was the **"prophecy of Elijah"**: the doctrine that the world lasts exactly 6,000 years — two thousand of *Tohu* (void, prior to the Law), two thousand under the Torah and the covenants, two thousand for the Messianic age — a schema propagated by the medieval *Chronicles of Carion* and the Talmudic sources. This apocalyptic mathematics proved irresistible to Renaissance Christian thinkers confronting the novelty and apocalyptic urgency of their own age. **Martin Luther** invoked it in his *Supputatio annorum mundi* (Calculation of the Years of the World, 1541), using the scheme to argue that the End Times approached. **Guillaume Postel** adopted it enthusiastically as foundation for his visionary *Restitutio*. **Tommaso Campanella**, the visionary Italian Dominican and political philosopher, similarly weaponized it for apocalyptic theology. The second major theme was the **Sem ha-meforash legend** — the claim that Jesus, like the magicians of Egypt, had stolen the ineffable divine name (the 72-letter name or Sem Adonai) from the Holy of Holies and used it to work miracles, as reported by the medieval Jewish polemicist **Prophiat Duran**. Duran had claimed further that Jesus and the apostles were ignorant kabbalists who misread the *Sefirot* — specifically confusing *Tiferet* (beauty, the sixth and central Sefira corresponding to the Son) and *Malkhut* (kingdom, the material tenth Sefira corresponding to the body/world) — into the metaphysics of the Son, Logos, and Holy Spirit, and fundamentally misunderstood the doctrine of the three lights and the mystery of the divine "garment" (the *kelipah* or shell). Christian apologists seized on this narrative, inverting it: if Jesus had worked miracles through kabbalistic power, then Kabbalah itself testified to his divinity, and the Jewish misunderstanding of their own mystical tradition had blinded them to its Christian fulfillment. The third and most systematic theme was the **dialectic of rigor and mercy** (*Gevurah* and *Hesed*), rooted in *Bereshit Rabba* and elaborated through kabbalistic symbolism: YHWH (the four-letter name, the divine mercy and compassion) balanced against **Elohim** (the divine judgment, the limiting and differentiating power). This dialectic, appearing in the paired columns of the Sefiratic tree, became central to Christian Kabbalist cosmology and theodicy. The fourth theme involved the **12- and 42-letter divine names** — permutations and combinations of sacred letters believed to contain concentrated divine power — read Trinitarily as *Ab* (Father), *Ben* (Son), and *Ruach ha-qodesh* (Holy Spirit), the Holy Ghost. Secret follows this chain of transmission forward through the medieval authorities: **Arnald of Villanova** (c. 1235-1311), Martí's spiritual heir and alchemist-mystic, contributed to spreading Kabbalah-inflected theology. **Nicholas of Cusa** (1401-1464), the great dialectical theologian, demonstrated convergence of Christian philosophy with kabbalistic logic. And with the Renaissance flowering of Christian curiosity on Kabbalah proper came direct engagement with Jewish mystical texts. **Abraham Abulafia** (1240-1291), the ecstatic Kabbalist of Barcelona and Sicily, had described disciples drawn into Christian heresy through misreading the letter-combination methods he had systematized. These three methods — **Temurah** (letter-substitution and permutation), **Gematria** (numerical equivalence and calculus), and **Notarikon** (acrostic and abbreviation techniques) — were encoded in the title and program of *Ginnat Egoz* (Garden of Nuts), the medieval handbook that Renaissance Christians encountered. **Abner of Burgos** (also known as Alfonso of Valladolid, c. 1320-after 1380), a Spanish Jewish philosopher who converted to Christianity, gave the first clearly attested use of the term **"cabala"** in his *Mostrador de Justicia* (The Pointer to Justice), a polemical work deploying Jewish mysticism against Judaism. **Alfonso de Spina**'s *Fortalitium fidei* (Fortress of Faith, c. 1458) lists the *Mecubalim* — the ten sefirot, the angel Metatron, and the doctrine of transmigration (*metempsychosis*) — as key elements Christians must understand to engage Jewish kabbalah. The chunk closes on the theme of **metempsychosis/metensomatosis** (transmigration of souls), which struck Renaissance minds as philosophically profound and theologically problematic. **Marsilio Ficin** (1433-1499), the Platonic philosopher and priest, adapted Paul de Burgos's *Scrutinium fidei* (Scrutiny of Faith) to incorporate metempsychotic doctrine into Christian Platonism. **Ambrogio Fiandino**, **Elia Levita** (the great Hebrew grammarian and Kabbalist), and the Reformed theologian **Pierre Viret** — the last specifically targeting Guillaume **Postel**, who preached his doctrine of "palingenesis" (cosmic renewal and rebirth of souls) in 1553 and continued to scandalize orthodox Christian authorities with his syncretic visionary theology.
