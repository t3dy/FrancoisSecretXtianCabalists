Ramon Martí's Pugio Fidei (The Dagger of Faith), though not published in print until 1651 when Joseph de Voisin produced a Paris edition that was subsequently used by Blaise Pascal, exercised extraordinary influence throughout the medieval and Renaissance periods via manuscript circulation and direct copying. The text functioned as an apologetic arsenal targeting Jews, Muslims, and pagans, systematically dismantling non-Christian positions by deploying their own authorities and interpretive traditions against them. The Pugio's remarkable textual longevity is evident in the cascade of works it directly spawned or profoundly shaped: in 1303, a Genoese Carthusian named Porchetus de Salvaticis distilled the Pugio's substance into his own Victorie super Iudaeos (Victory over the Jews), which Augustin Giustiniani of Genoa edited and published in Paris in 1520, carefully attributing its intellectual debt to Martí. By contrast, Pierre Galatino, the Franciscan Hebraist and papal censor who lived c. 1460-1540, incorporated the Pugio's essential arguments into his celebrated De arcanis catholicae veritatis (On the Secrets of Catholic Truth), first published in 1518, often without acknowledgment, leading later scholars like Jean Gaffarel to accuse Galatino of wholesale plagiarism. Alfonso de Zamora, one of the collaborators on the celebrated Polyglot Bible of Alcalá, employed Pugio materials in his Sefer Hokmat Elohim (Book of Divine Wisdom) composed in 1532, creating a Spanish synthesis that married Talmudic-apologetic material with emergent kabbalistic symbolism. In 1540, the Portuguese historian João de Barros deployed the Pugio's arguments in his Dialogue evangelico sobre os artigos da fé contra o Talmud dos Iudeus (Evangelical Dialogue on the Articles of Faith Against the Talmud of the Jews), written specifically to combat the messianic movements and mass conversions among the cristianos nuevos (New Christians) and to stem their emigration toward Ottoman territories. Notably, this later generation of authors supplemented the Pugio with newer sources: Reuchlin's De arte cabalistica (On the Art of Kabbalah), Galatino's own De arcanis, and the polemical Flagellum of Adrianus Finus. When Giustiniani reissued Porchetus's work under the augmented title Victoria Porcheti cum titulo magico Cabbala (The Victory of Porchetus with the Magic Title Kabbalah), he signaled how Renaissance Christians were beginning to identify the mystical and numerical doctrines circulating in Talmudic material as "kabbala" proper. Manuscript copies of the Pugio were extraordinarily widespread: one copy resided in the Dominican convent of Naples, where it was known in the era of Pico's teacher Mithridates; the great Hebrew scholar François Vatable, one of the first professors at the College of the Three Languages (Collège Trilingue) in Paris and the master of Guillaume Postel, possessed a copy that he bequeathed at his death to his nephew Mathieu Bérault (or Beroalde, c. 1520-1576), who himself projected (following a plan Gaffarel would later adopt) a comparative study demonstrating Galatino's plagiarism. Philippe du Plessis-Mornay, the Huguenot diplomat and theologian, lent his copy to Jean Buxtorf, the great Hebraist of Basel, who conceived a plan (never realized) to publish a critical edition.

The Pugio itself was deliberately structured as an apologetic monument against the Gentiles, following the model of Thomas Aquinas's Summa Contra Gentiles, turning its weapons against Jews, Muslims, and pagans alike, yet crucially, it did not draw upon Kabbalah in its developed, ecstatic sense. Rather, it weaponized Talmudic and Midrashic traditions — the massive commentarial elaborations on Scripture and Law accumulated by generations of Jewish sages — exploiting their symbolic, numerical, and interpretive densities for Christian apologetic purposes. The Pugio's prologue brilliantly articulates this tactical reversal: it justifies plundering the Torah she-be'al-peh (the Oral Law, i.e., the Talmudic tradition) as the strategic capture of the enemy's own blade. The author famously declares that while the Talmud contains countless absurdities and impious doctrines unworthy of divine origin, certain passages shine with truth, resonating with the doctrines of the prophets and Church Fathers, remarkably and incredibly expressing Christian faith. The project becomes martial metaphor — as Judith severed the tyrant's head with his own sword, Christians would extract from the Talmud itself the weapons to slay Jewish objections and triumphantly vindicate Christian doctrine. This hermeneutical plundering would profoundly shape the hermeneutical strategies of Christian Kabbalah for the next two centuries.

Secret catalogs the dominant themes flowing from the Pugio through Renaissance apologetic and kabbalistic literature. First stands the "prophecy of Elijah" (Yoma 86b), the cosmological scheme stating that the world endures precisely six thousand years: two thousand years of Tohu (chaos, emptiness, the void state before the revelation of the Law); two thousand years le-Torah (under the Torah and covenants); two thousand years le-Messiah (in the Messianic age), with the corresponding claim that the cosmos will culminate after the 6,000-year period expires. This apocalyptic arithmetic, propagated throughout the medieval Chronicles of John Carion (Jean Carion, 1499-1537), which achieved exceptional frequency of printing and circulation, proved irresistible to Renaissance Christian thinkers urgently confronting the novelty and eschatological fervor of their epoch. Martin Luther seized upon it in his Supputatio annorum mundi (Calculation of the Years of the World), published at Wittenberg in 1541, calculating that in 1540 precisely 5,500 years had elapsed, compelling the conclusion that the sixth millennium could not be completed entire, just as the three days of Christ's death were not complete — therefore the End Times were imminent. Luther further identified apocalyptic "signs" distinguishing between the "French evil" and others' interpretations. Guillaume Postel, the visionary polyglot and mystical theologian, attached additional kabbalistic supputations to this framework, elaborating elaborate chronological reckonings. Tommaso Campanella, the Dominican visionary and political philosopher who was paradoxically both virulent Talmud-critic and apocalyptic enthusiast, repeatedly invoked this "admirable prophecy" throughout his theological writings, binding kabbalistic temporality to Christian eschatological fervor.

The second great theme was the Shem ha-meforash legend — the tradition, reported in the Pugio and earlier sources, that Jesus the Nazarene entered the Temple at Jerusalem during the reign of Queen Helena (whose historical chronology proves deeply confused), discovered inscribed upon a stone the Shem ha-meforash (the Name Exposed, the seventy-two-letter divine name), learned its permutations, and employed this kabbalistic power to perform miracles. The tradition held that the Temple sages, fearing Israelites might deploy this name to destroy the world, had stationed bronze dogs upon pillars whose terrible barking would frighten anyone exiting with the name learned, causing them to forget it. Jesus, the legend asserts, entered, learned the letters, inscribed them upon parchment, slit open his thigh, hid the parchment within, and miraculously felt no pain through the virtue of the divine name. Upon exiting the Temple, the bronze dogs barked terribly and the name fled his memory, but he returned home, reopened his thigh with a knife, retrieved the parchment, and relearned the seventy-two letters, using this mystical knowledge to accomplish his miracles. Prophiat Duran (Efodi), the Spanish Jewish philosopher and convert-poet who was forcibly baptized in 1391, would respond in his Kelimat ha-goyim (The Shame of the Gentiles), dedicated to Hasdai Crescas, by asserting that indeed Jesus and the disciples were kabbalists — but erroneous ones whose misunderstanding of the Sefirot, particularly their confusing of Tiferet (Beauty, the luminous center of the Sefiratic tree) and Malkhut (Kingdom, the lowest manifest Sefira) with the theological Trinity, and their fundamental miscomprehension of the doctrine of the three lights (primordial light, pure light, and radiating light) and the mystery of the divine garment (kelipah, the shell or veil), led them into Christian heresy. Christian apologists brilliantly inverted Duran's argument: if Jesus and the apostles were indeed kabbalists, then Kabbalah itself testified to his divinity and messianic power; Jewish blindness to their own mystical inheritance had blinded them to its Christian fulfillment. The very mystical science the Jews possessed and misunderstood became proof of Christian truth.

Third, the dialectic of rigor and mercy (gevurah and hesed), drawn from Bereshit Rabba on Genesis 3:23 and elaborated throughout Talmudic and kabbalistic discourse, permeated Christian mystical theology. Rabbi Joshua taught that God created humanity with both the property of judgment (gevurah, rigor, severity) and the property of mercy (hesed), expelling Adam from Eden with both properties balanced. This doctrine mapped onto the divine names: wherever the Tetragrammaton (YHWH, pronounced Adonai) appears alongside Elohim, the former signifies divine compassion and mercy while the latter signifies judgment and the limiting, differentiating power. This binary, which appears structurally in the Sefiratic tree's right and left pillars, became foundational to Christian Kabbalist theodicy and mystical cosmology. The fourth theme involved the divine names of twelve and forty-two letters — sacred letter-combinations believed to concentrate immense divine power. Raymond Martí cites Maimonides at length regarding these permutations, then provides elaborate Christian reinterpretations: the twelve-letter name consists of Ab (Father, two letters: aleph-bet), Ben (Son, two letters: bet-nun), and Ruach ha-Qodesh (Holy Ghost, the remaining letters), mirroring the twelve apostles to whom Jesus taught the four-letter Tetragrammaton. The forty-two-letter name expands this Trinity through Athanasian formulation: God the Father, God the Son, God the Holy Spirit, and yet not three gods but one God alone (Ab El, Ben El, Ruach ha-Qodesh El, akha [together] lo [not] shloshah Elohim [three gods] ki im [but rather] El [one] ehad [alone]). When Jewish exegetes objected that the vav (conjunction) should attach to the Son rather than the Spirit, Christian apologists responded by citing Hebrew grammatical precedent (Exodus 3:6, where vav appears only with the final enumeration) to justify their theological architecture.

The transmission of these themes through Christian intellectual history is traced through crucial medieval and early modern figures: Arnald of Villanova (c. 1235-1311), the famous physician, alchemist, and mystical theologian who was Raymond Martí's spiritual heir, authored an Allocutio super significationem nominis Tetragrammaton (Oration on the Signification of the Tetragrammaton in Hebrew and Latin and the Revelation of the Mystery of the Trinity) dedicated in 1292 to Pierre Puget, explicitly acknowledging his intellectual debt to his former master's work. Nicholas of Cusa (1401-1464), the great Renaissance dialectical theologian and natural philosopher, possessed a manuscript copy of Arnald's treatise and demonstrated sustained engagement with this apologetic-kabbalistic literature, citing the Sefer Raziel (Book of Raziel) and the Guide for the Perplexed and, through Nicolas of Lyra's critical apparatus, deploying kabbalistic arguments against Jews who believed the divine names El, Elohim, and YHWH to be mere properties (wisdom, goodness, power) rather than absolute designations.

Yet Renaissance Christian interest in Jewish mysticism could not help but eventually issue in direct engagement with Kabbalah proper. Abraham Abulafia (1240-1292), the ecstatic Kabbalist of Barcelona and Sicily, had recorded in his letters how certain of his disciples, through miscomprehension of the letter-combination techniques he had systematized, had lapsed into Christian heresy. These three supreme methods — Temurah (systematic letter-substitution, permutation, and recombination), Gematria (numerical equivalence of letters and words, yielding hidden harmonies), and Notarikon (acrostic reading, reading the first letters of words as secret abbreviations) — were celebrated and encoded in the title and hermeneutical program of the Ginat Egoz (Garden of Nuts), the medieval kabbalistic handbook by Joseph Gikatilla, whose very title acronym reveals its threefold method: G-N-T yielding Gematria-Notarikon-Temurah. Abner of Burgos (also known as Alfonso of Valladolid, c. 1320-after 1380), the Spanish Jewish philosopher-theologian who converted to Christianity around 1320, became the first attested user of the term "cabala" in Christian European texts, deploying it in his lengthy polemical treatise Mostrador de Justicia (The Pointer to Justice), composed as a dialogue between the author and the Rebel-Jew. Abner's theological writings, which survive in Spanish translation, report how in 1295 a miraculous event at Ávila struck him — a rain of crosses descended from heaven and attached themselves to the clothing of Jews — and subsequently two mystical visions led him to meditate upon the Sefer Yetzirah (Book of Creation) in the manner the kabbalists taught, focusing on the seal of God as truth, and to compose his Iggeret ha-Keriat ha-Adonai (Letters of the Battles of the Lord) in Hebrew, which he circulated cautiously among his disciples. His Mostrador de Justicia clearly demonstrates familiarity with kabbalistic writings and employs the term "cabala" to designate the mystical doctrine of the Mecubalim, those who receive or possess the Kabbalah. Alfonso de Spina (died 1469), another converted Jew, authored the Fortalitium fidei contra omnes Christianae religionis hostes (Fortress of Faith Against All Enemies of Christian Religion), composed around 1458 and published at Lyon in 1525, which catalogs the sects and heresies threatening Christian unity. In Book III, devoted to Jewish heresies, Spina lists the Mecubalim among the dangerous Jewish sects, describing them as those who believe in the ten divine personifications (the Sefirot), others who believe in two substances (master and disciple, identified as the angel Metatron), and others who accept transmigration of the soul from one human to another, from woman to woman, and from human to beast, serpent, or star.

This theme of metempsychosis/metensomatosis (the transmigration and reincarnation of souls) struck many Renaissance Christian minds with philosophical power and theological alarm. Marsilio Ficin (1433-1499), the great Platonic philosopher-priest who founded the Platonic Academy in Florence under Medici patronage and translated Plato and Plotinus, engaged repeatedly with metempsychotic doctrine, noting that Hebrew doctors and cabalistes (kabbalists) approved such punishment, believing human souls return many times in human form alone for purification of sins, with a maximum of three returns allowed, and furthermore that all souls were present when God gave the Law to Moses. Ficin significantly adapted this material from Paul of Burgos's Scrutinium Scripturarum (Scrutiny of Scripture), published at Mantua as early as 1474, a work that not only attacked metempsychosis as philosophically incoherent and scripturally unfounded but provided detailed exposition of Jewish mystical claims. Paul de Burgos (also known as Paul of Santa Maria, 1350-1435), who converted with his entire family and eventually became Patriarch of Aquileia while his son succeeded him as Bishop of Burgos, was famous as the author of the Additiones (Additions) to Nicholas of Lyra's biblical Postillae (glosses), an enormous work of exegetical synthesis. Paul explicitly rejected metempsychosis as incoherent and contrary to Scripture, yet his detailed refutation (citing the multiple sins of King David yet his salvation and sanctity) became a source for later Christian discussion of Jewish mysticism.

The metempsychotic theme echoed through the sixteenth century in distinctive ways, revealing both the philosophical seduction and the theological hazard of kabbalistic doctrines for Christian thinkers. Ambrogio Fiandino (also known as Ambrogio Flandino), an Augustinian hermit called the "Christian Plato," reproduced Ficin's entire account verbatim in his Quadragesimalium concionum liber (Book of Forty-Day Sermons), published at Venice in 1523, marshaling theological arguments while content merely to reference Thomas Aquinas for refutation of the doctrine, thus securing orthodoxy through appeal to scholastic authority while still engaging with the philosophical substance of transmigrationist theory. Elia Levita (Elijah Levita, 1469-1549), the great Hebrew grammarian, lexicographer, biblical commentator, and practicing Kabbalist whose works — particularly his Masoret ha-Masoret (The Essence of the Masoretic Tradition) and his Yiddish historical writings — dominated European Hebrew studies throughout the sixteenth century and beyond, provided yet another exposition of metempsychosis derived from different Jewish sources, enriching Christian understanding through his firsthand knowledge of Hebrew tradition. Pierre Viret (1511-1571), the Reformed theologian and polemicist of the Geneva circles, writing in his polemical works in 1564, attacked those professors and doctors of divinity who held metempsychotic views, claiming their souls had allegedly inhabited great ancient figures and who would accomplish great deeds because they possessed those same souls — a thinly veiled allusion to his bitter intellectual enemy Guillaume Postel, the mystical polyglot and radical visionary. The Guillaume Postel controversy illuminates the explosive potential of kabbalistic metempsychosis when absorbed into a mind of visionary intensity and rhetorical power: Postel, preaching in 1553 with an exaltation that orthodox Christian authorities found bizarre and heretical, proclaimed himself possessed by the spirit of John the Evangelist and claimed descent into the Terrestrial Paradise itself under the supernatural powers of Enoch, the antediluvian patriarch who walks throughout Jewish mystical traditions as the angel Metatron. His wild proclamations scandalized Paris — Couillard, seigneur du Pavillon-les-Lorriz, in his Contredits à Nostradamus (Refutations of Nostradamus) published in 1560, viciously attacked Postel for introducing "a holy John the Evangelist who walked the streets of Paris preaching," and Couillard claimed personally to have witnessed Postel preaching on street corners to crowds of ignorant people, claiming falsely to have descended from the Terrestrial Paradise under the virtues of Enoch. Postel himself, in successive editions of the Chronicles of John Carion that he edited and annotated, multiplied cryptic echoes of these teachings: in 1553, he notes that in Basel and Paris there began public discussion of sainte Palingénésie (holy Palingenesis — the cosmic renewal, resurrection of all things, and rebirth of souls), with one figure claiming to be filled with the spirit of John the Apostle and Evangelist, though Postel himself sardonically observes that "the thing seemed to everyone ridiculous and similar to the Pythagorean doctrine" — suggesting simultaneous fascination and detachment from his own visionary mythology. The unfortunate visionary protagonist of this drama allegedly ended up being burned at Toulouse, suggesting the extreme danger such claims posed within early modern Christian society. By 1602, the natural philosopher Scipion du Pleix (1569-1641), one of the first Renaissance figures to write substantial philosophy in French vernacular prose rather than Latin, and who gave considerable attention to kabbalistic doctrines throughout his systematic works, sardonically remarked in his Métaphysique ou science surnaturelle (Metaphysics or Supernatural Science) that metempsychosis seemed no less absurd than Plutarch (to whom he attributes the doctrine), the Cabbalists, and Origen all believed, and he amusingly cited Rabelais's comic vision from the Gargantua and Pantagruel that Alexander the Great in the other world hawked green sauce and performed other vile and sordid trades — a mordant reduction of philosophical pretension and aristocratic vanity that captured something of the skeptical wit increasingly directed at elaborate kabbalistic transmigration theories as the seventeenth century advanced and confessional polemic gave way to early modern skeptical criticism of mysticism.

The transmission of these themes — the Pugio fidei's arguments, the eschatological mathematics, the Shem ha-meforash legends, the dialectic of mercy and rigor, the divine names, and the metempsychotic doctrines — constitutes one of the great currents of medieval and Renaissance Christian intellectual history, flowing from medieval Spain through the Italian Renaissance and into Northern Europe, shaping the contours of Christian Kabbalah. Secret's account demonstrates how medieval Jewish intellectual traditions were not merely attacked and refuted by Christian polemicists but were systematically studied, incorporated, and reinterpreted to serve Christian theological purposes. The Pugio fidei, that medieval masterwork of apologetic violence and erudition, created a template: other works followed its pattern, elaborated its arguments, and extended its reach across centuries and linguistic boundaries. The network of converted Jews who mediated between communities — Abner of Burgos, Paul of Burgos, Alfonso de Spina, Pedro de la Caballería, and the many others Secret catalogs — created intellectual pathways through which Christian thinkers gained access to Jewish mystical traditions and learned to deploy them for Christian ends. The results were contradictory and unstable: sometimes these traditions supported orthodox Christian theology (as in the readings of the divine names as Trinity, or the Incarnation encoded in Creation), sometimes they generated heterodoxy (as in Postel's ecstatic visions and metempsychotic claims), and sometimes they simply enriched Christian mystical speculation without clear theological payoff. Yet across these variations, a fundamental project remained constant: the appropriation of Jewish intellectual authority — whether Talmudic, midrashic, or kabbalistic — to vindicate Christian truth and Jewish error, rendering the Jewish mystical tradition simultaneously a weapon of Christian apologetics and a symptom of Jewish spiritual blindness. The tensions and paradoxes embedded in this project would only intensify as Christian Kabbalah developed further in the hands of figures like Pico della Mirandola and the Italian kabbalists of the later Renaissance.
