Chunk 51

Pages 160-162

This section profiles remarkable Protestant personalities who embraced kabbalah not for theological systematization but for prophetic illumination. Their work reveals kabbalah as a tool for decoding hidden patterns in scripture and natural philosophy. Jacopo Brocardo, a Venetian prophet whom Bayle documented, represents kabbalah in service of apocalyptic vision. Exiled and persecuted, Brocardo claimed mystical insights through biblical encounter. In his 1581 *Interpretatio mystica et prophetica Geneseos*, he recounts his sudden conviction (1563) that God revealed prophetic truth through scripture—initially encountering the prophet Haggai with overwhelming emotional force. In 1565, deciphering another prophecy, he felt himself the predestined agent of divine history, an intuition that led to arrest by the Venetian Inquisition, where he experienced ecstatic liberation. Brocardo's hermeneutics are sephirothically encoded. He discovered in the opening syllables of Genesis (*Beresith Bara*) the Trinity expressed as letters: *Beresith* the Son, *Bara* the Father, and the Holy Spirit as "third state." Arranging the letters Aleph-Resch-Beth in symmetrical patterns yields six ages; adding Elohim produces seven. The ten *sephiroth* become historical-theological significations: the *Ensoph* (hidden God) reveals itself through emanations; the eight through ten sephirot chart the Church's states from Charlemagne's papal corruption to Christ's reign in the millennium. This sephirothic framework transforms kabbalah into a prophetic calendar. Raphael Goetz (Eglinus/Iconius, 1559-1622), a Zurich minister and alchemist, exemplifies the intersection of kabbalah and natural philosophy. In 1569, contemplating Daniel, he discovered a fish (xyphie or *cabela*) bearing hieroglyphic and arithmetico-geometric inscriptions. Deciphering them as acrostics (VICI = *Venit Iesus Christus Iudicare*), he produced a series of prophetic fish-diagrams—curious artifacts collected by the Dupuy brothers, symbolizing the grafting of numerical mysticism onto ecological observation. Philippe Nicolaï (1556-1608) represents a more restrained approach: citing Rabbi Haccados sparingly and employing gematria for *Romiith* (Rome = 666), he integrated kabbalistic method within theological confines. The section concludes with a crucial methodological observation: Catholic and Protestant reactions to kabbalah cannot be understood apart from their pseudoscientific and magical contexts—the broader intellectual ecosystem shaped by Agrippa and Paracelsus, who fundamentally transformed Renaissance magical tradition.
