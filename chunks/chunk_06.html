This chunk consists largely of the **endnotes to Chapter II** — a thick scholarly apparatus that anchors the narrative in primary sources and prior scholarship. Secret cites his own earlier studies published in learned journals (*Sefarad*, *Bibliothèque d'Humanisme et Renaissance*, *Rinascimento*, *Convivium*), demonstrates his deep engagement with **Scholem**'s *Zur Geschichte der Anfänge der Kabbala* (On the History of the Beginnings of Kabbalah) and *Major Trends*, draws on the fundamental text of the *Pugio fidei* (now available in modern critical editions and facsimiles), references major medieval sources like **Maimonides**' *Guide for the Perplexed* (*Moreh Nevukhim*), the *Fortalitium fidei* of Alfonso de Spina, and multiple editions and commentaries on Pico's *Heptaplus*. The endnotes establish Secret's method: layered access to sources (original texts, modern critical editions, scholarly intermediaries), careful attention to transmission history and textual genealogy, and a commitment to verifying claims against primary documentation. The plates reproduced here provide additional documentary evidence: the supposed portrait of **Mithridates** from a Vatican manuscript (likely later and legendary rather than contemporary); pages of his autograph **Passion sermon** in Latin, bearing witness to his learning and theological sophistication; and a **Talmud manuscript** translated for **Egidio da Viterbo**, one of the great Christian Hebraists of the early 16th century. The OCR transcriptions of the Latin and Hebrew manuscript pages are heavily garbled by early machine processing, but the original plates themselves constitute irreplaceable primary-source evidence of the textual world Christian Kabbalists inhabited. **Chapter III, "Pic de la Mirandole et le milieu italien de la kabbale chrétienne,"** then opens with Secret arguing for a historiographical correction regarding Pico's intellectual formation. He notes that the title of Pico's *Oratio de hominis dignitate* (Oration on the Dignity of Man) echoes the humanist **Giannozzo Manetti**, himself a serious Hebraist who had engaged Jewish mysticism, yet asserts that neither Manetti, nor Pietro Bruto, nor **Marsilio Ficin** (who knew fragments of the *Bahir*), nor **Ambrogio Traversari** actually prepared the flowering of Christian Kabbalah in Italy at its peak. That transformative role fell instead to **two converts** — Jewish scholars who embraced Christianity and became mediators of their former tradition to the Christian world. These were **Flavius Mithridates** (one of Pico's crucial masters) and **Paul de Heredia**, whom Galatino claimed also taught Pico, opening questions about lineage and intellectual debt that Secret must untangle. Secret reconstructs the biography of **Flavius Mithridates** from the *Chronicle of Volterra*, an invaluable source for the lives of converts and humanist scholars. This "**Guillaume de Sicile**" — a man of the household of the cardinal of Molfetta, learned in Hebrew, Greek, Arabic, and Latin — achieved fame through a celebrated two-hour **Passion sermon** delivered before **Sixtus IV** (the warrior pope who commissioned the Sistine Chapel) and the assembled cardinals, including the future Pope **Innocent VIII**, around 1481. The sermon was remarkable for its learned attempt to prove Christian mysteries (the Trinity, the Incarnation, the Passion) directly from Hebrew and Arabic sources, treating the languages themselves as repositories of revealed theology. Mithridates is identified as the convert **Juda Samuel ben Nissim Abul Farag** of Girgenti (Agrigento) in Sicily, who took the Christian name **Guglielmo Raimondo Moncada** upon baptism. His trajectory followed the common pattern of convert-apologists: he had been a Jewish scholar, recognized for his learning and eloquence, who embraced Christianity and sought to demonstrate that Hebrew wisdom vindicated Christian doctrine. After a mysterious and violent crime in Rome (probably a homicide) in 1483 — perhaps a crime of passion, perhaps a political entanglement — he fled Italy; by 1485 he had resurfaced in Germany, where he was praised by **Rudolf Agricola** (the German humanist and educational reformer), taught **Conrad Summenhardt** and most importantly **Johannes Reuchlin**, the man who would become the theoretician of Christian Kabbalah. Summenhardt reports that **Mithridates** had translated kabbalistic books — the very seventy hidden books that Pico invoked through the authority of IV Esdras — for **Pope Sixtus IV**, suggesting papal interest in Hebrew mysticism during the very years that Pico was forming his vision. Secret notes carefully that the Passion sermon itself never uses the term "kabbale" and appears instead to draw its exemplars of "ancient Talmudists" from the framework of the *Pugio fidei*, using traditional apologetic weapons rather than newer kabbalistic ones. Finally, in **1486** — a pivotal year — Moncada, now known as **Flavius Mithridates**, met **Pico della Mirandola** and undertook the crucial labor of translating kabbalistic manuscripts for him, providing Pico with direct textual access to the very sources (the *Zohar*, Gikatilla's *Sha'are Ora*, Abulafia's letter-mysticism, the Sefiratic symbolism) that would animate his synthetic vision.
