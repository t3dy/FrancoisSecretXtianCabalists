Chunk 61

Pages 190-192

This chunk traces the complex attitudes of Marin Mersenne (1588-1648) toward Christian Kabbalah in the early 17th century. Though Mersenne critiques the cabalistic method and condemns various hermetic and magical doctrines, he remains partially captivated by kabbalistic thought. His 1624 work *Impiété des déistes* distinguishes cabalistes from atheists, acknowledging that kabbalists properly honour divine perfections and Scripture, yet he doubts the methodological soundness of cabalistic principles, arguing they derive from insufficient sources rather than rigorous demonstration. Gaffarel (1601-1681), a younger, meridional scholar deeply drawn to Christian Kabbalah, responds with *Les secrets cachés de la divine cabale* (dedicated to Cardinal Richelieu), mounting a spirited defence. Gaffarel invokes Jerome and Pic de la Mirandole's vindication to counter those who dismiss the cabala as tainted with superstition. He attacks its critics—notably the Venetian physician Giorgi Ragusio, whose 1623 *Épîtres mathématiques* derided cabalistic interpretation of divine names as absurd. Gaffarel asserts instead that the cabala is a commentary on Scripture that elevates the spirit, not a magical practice, and defends Postel as a true explorer of its secrets in Zohar commentary. Mersenne responds (1625) with his *Jugement de Gaffarel*, accusing Gaffarel of ignorance in cabalistic knowledge (citing Elia Levita and Schindler on metempsychosis, and condemning Gaffarel's use of Reuchlin, whom the Church condemned). Yet despite this polemic, Gaffarel's career advances: Richelieu sends him to Italy to acquire Hebrew manuscripts. He returns with the famous Index of Pic's manuscripts and rare Syrian-Chaldaic texts, acquiring medical knowledge that colours his later editorial work on Campanella. Though Gaffarel publishes little—most of his ambitious projects remain projected or fragmentary—his proposed works reveal extraordinary range: a "Cabalistic Sieve," treatises on Hebraic music, grammatical aids, and a monumental compilation of kabbalistic and rabbinical learning. Secret cites Leon Allacci's 1633 inventory of Gaffarel's œuvre, noting its ambitious scope even if execution remained incomplete, reflecting the profound engagement of Renaissance scholarship with Jewish textual transmission and cabalistic exegesis.
