Chunk 61

Pages 190-192

This chunk traces the complex and intellectually rich debate surrounding Christian Kabbalah in early 17th-century Europe through the figures of Marin Mersenne and Jacques Gaffarel, two scholars whose exchange illuminates the period's ambivalent stance toward cabalistic learning. Though Mersenne (1588-1648) emerges as one of the era's most influential critics of cabalistic methodology, his critique is far from a simple dismissal. In his 1624 work *Impiété des déistes*, Mersenne carefully distinguishes kabbalists (*cabalistes*) from atheists, a crucial distinction that acknowledges the religious legitimacy of those engaged with the tradition. He grants that kabbalists, when operating properly within their own framework, do indeed honor divine perfections and maintain fidelity to Scripture. However, Mersenne remains profoundly skeptical of the methodological foundations upon which cabalistic exegesis rests. His core objection is that kabbalistic principles derive from sources he deems insufficient for rigorous philosophical demonstration—a challenge rooted in his commitment to rational method and epistemological rigor. This position places Mersenne in a peculiar intellectual position: sympathetic to kabbalistic piety, doubtful of kabbalistic methodology, and concerned about the doctrinal implications of cabalistic hermeneutics. Gaffarel's response represents the counterargument of a younger, deeply committed Christian kabbalist who embodies the French meridional scholarly tradition. Born in 1601 and far more enthusiastically invested in cabalistic studies than Mersenne, Gaffarel composed *Les secrets cachés de la divine cabale*, a work dedicated strategically to Cardinal Richelieu that mounts a spirited and learned defense against both skeptics and outright antagonists. Gaffarel's defense strategy is twofold: first, he invokes the authority of Jerome and Pic de la Mirandole, figures whose engagement with cabalistic learning grants historical legitimacy to his own pursuits, effectively arguing that Christian engagement with Kabbalah has deep roots in the Western intellectual tradition. Second, he directly confronts a specific critic: Giorgi Ragusio, a Venetian physician whose 1623 *Épîtres mathématiques* had derided cabalistic interpretation of divine names as absurd and methodologically unsound. Gaffarel's response to Ragusio's mathematical critique is fundamental to his defense: he reframes the cabala not as a magical practice or pseudo-mathematical system, but as a sophisticated commentary on Scripture designed to elevate the human spirit through deeper understanding of sacred text. In doing so, he positions cabalistic learning within the hermeneutical tradition rather than the magical arts, a distinction that would matter greatly to ecclesiastical authorities. Gaffarel's reading of Jewish sources also reflects the careful scholarship characteristic of Christian kabbalists engaged genuinely with the tradition. He defends figures like Guillaume Postel, positioning him not as a magical adventurer but as a serious scholar devoted to exploring the hidden secrets of the Zohar with genuine reverence for Jewish textual transmission. This defense of Postel is significant because it stakes the legitimacy of Christian Kabbalah on the quality of engagement with Jewish sources themselves, not on allegorical or purely Christian reinterpretation. In this way, Gaffarel's work represents a moment when Christian intellectuals were still defending their cabalistic interests with reference to Jewish learning rather than subordinating Jewish sources to Christian doctrinal frameworks—a generosity that would not always characterize later encounters. Mersenne's response, published in 1625 as *Jugement de Gaffarel*, escalates the polemic while simultaneously revealing deep knowledge of cabalistic sources. Mersenne accuses Gaffarel of fundamental ignorance in cabalistic learning, supporting this charge by citing Elia Levita and Heinrich Schindler on complex cabalistic doctrines like metempsychosis—demonstrating that Mersenne himself commanded significant knowledge of the tradition he critiqued. His condemnation of Gaffarel's recourse to Reuchlin carries particular weight, given that Reuchlin had faced ecclesiastical condemnation, making his advocacy a potentially dangerous intellectual association. Yet despite—or perhaps because of—this sharp polemic, Gaffarel's career advanced remarkably. Richelieu's patronage sent him to Italy on an important mission: to acquire Hebrew manuscripts for the royal collection. The results of this venture proved intellectually significant: Gaffarel returned with an important Index of Pic de la Mirandole's manuscripts and a collection of rare Syrian-Chaldaic texts that positioned him as a genuine manuscript scholar with access to material resources unavailable to most of his contemporaries. This Italian sojourn also exposed Gaffarel to medical learning, particularly through contact with scholars like Campanella, whose medical and mystical interests influenced Gaffarel's later editorial work. Gaffarel's intellectual legacy, though externally modest in publications, reveals the extraordinary ambitions of the Christian kabbalist project at its height. Most of his most ambitious works never reached completion or print: a "Cabalistic Sieve" (*Cribrum cabalicum*) intended to winnow authentic from spurious cabalistic doctrine; treatises on Hebraic music and its mystical dimensions; grammatical aids designed to facilitate deeper Hebrew study; and a monumental compilation attempting to synthesize kabbalistic learning with rabbinical exegesis. As Secret notes, citing Leon Allacci's 1633 inventory of Gaffarel's œuvre, the scope of these projects demonstrates the intellectual reach of mid-17th-century Christian scholars toward comprehensive cabalistic learning. The fragmentary and projected nature of Gaffarel's major works reflects not lack of ambition or learning, but rather the practical difficulties of producing Hebrew-language scholarship, acquiring rare manuscripts, managing ecclesiastical suspicion, and coordinating the complex textual apparatus that such synoptic works would require. The Mersenne-Gaffarel exchange thus represents a crucial moment in the institutional history of Christian Kabbalah: a moment when serious scholars, despite methodological disagreements, engaged with Jewish sources and traditions with a genuine intellectual commitment that would increasingly give way to antiquarian curiosity or doctrinal instrumentalization in later periods.
