Secret surveys a striking cohort of prominent Catholic intellectuals whose public intellectual postures explicitly rejected or expressed contempt for Kabbalah, yet whose writings and documented engagements reveal more ambivalent, complex, and often contradictory relationships with kabbalistic traditions. Erasmus, the preeminent humanist champion of the "trilinguis homo" (the three-language ideal of mastering Hebrew, Greek, and Latin), publicly detested Hebrew learning in general and dismissed Kabbalah with particular venom, characterizing it as so much Talmudic fable, mystical nonsense surrounding the Tetragram, and "nugæ" (trivial trifles unworthy of serious intellectual attention). His contemptuous dismissal appears consistently across his correspondence, in letters to Capito, Cardinal Wolsey, and Albert of Brandenburg. Yet this apparently settled judgment underwent significant revision: after encountering Paul Rici's vernacular introduction to Kabbalah designed for educated Christian readers, Erasmus grudgingly conceded in his own correspondence that reading Rici's work had rendered him "more equitable"—that is, more fair-minded and open—toward the subject, an admission that implicitly acknowledges the persuasive power of the tradition despite his intellectual prejudices against it.

Augustinus Steuchus (Steuco of Gubbio), an Augustinian canon and Vatican librarian of considerable influence, actively opposed Clement VII's planned Jewish-Christian biblical commission, articulating anxieties about potential Jewish deception and manipulation of the collaborative process. His Recognitio (1531) announced an ambitious planned attack on cabalists and their methodologies, signaling his intention to produce a comprehensive critique. Yet his later major work, the Cosmopoeia (1535), reveals a more nuanced intellectual stance: Steuco prudently distinguished between Jewish mystical deliriums and errors, on the one hand, and legitimate Platonic-influenced mysticism as practiced by wise Christian philosophers, on the other. He explicitly conceded that cabalists had extracted "interesting if uncertain" insights concerning the soul and immortality through their mystical methodologies, and crucially refused to conflate their practices with the ignorant superstition of the masses—a distinction that, while critical, nonetheless granted them intellectual respectability.

Jean Cochlaeus (1479–1552) essentially reproduced Steuco's formula of critical distinction: he condemned those who elevated Kabbalah to the status of an infallible oracle while simultaneously citing kabbalistic texts derived from Galatin (whom Cochlaeus knew personally during his time in Rome) and drawing on exegetical methods he had learned while studying Hebrew with Elia Levita. Angelo Canini characterized Kabbalah as "methodical folly," attacking practitioners who engaged in the arithmetical and geometrical dissection of divine names, yet mysteriously published Ludovicus Carret's mystical exegeses—a publication choice that Secret notes stands in unresolved contradiction to his explicit condemnation of kabbalistic methodology.

The Dominican and Jesuit critics demonstrate similar patterns of critique coupled with selective appropriation. Juan Maldonat, a prominent Jesuit exegete, mocked Archangelus of Burgonovo's elaborate angelic calculus (which enumerated 600 million distinct angels) and dismissed cabalist explanations of Jacob's wrestling match with the divine as fabulous nonsense unworthy of serious theological consideration. Ascanio Martinengo, himself a Dominican scholar trained by the Kabbalah-friendly master Hieronymus Vielmius, subsequently turned critic, rejecting Pico's and Hamerus's Bereshit exegeses as resting on false Hebrew philology and amounting to empty letter-play divorced from authentic linguistic meaning. Yet Vielmius himself, Martinengo's teacher and master, publicly praised Pico's cabalistic boldness and habitually invoked cabalists' explanations of Scripture in his own exegetical work, particularly regarding Mosaic exaltation within the Jubilee cycle and metaphorical readings of heavenly hearing and celestial audition. These contradictions—between public renunciation and private appropriation, between explicit critique and implicit reliance—permeate the entire tradition Secret documents.

The chunk contains two striking images (pages 149–150) that document the visual and material culture of Christian Kabbalah. The first is an engraved portrait labeled "XII. Blaise de Vigenère," a significant figure in French kabbalistic scholarship. The second is a complex diagram titled "XV. L'arbre de la cabale" (The Tree of Kabbalah), a sophisticated volvelle (a moveable wheel diagram) that displays the intricate correspondences between the ten sephiroth and various celestial bodies and planetary attributions, making visible the cosmological structure of kabbalistic theology. The engraving was originally published by Philippe d'Aquin in Paris in 1625 but was subsequently appropriated and republished by Jacques Gaffarel; the reproduction Secret reproduces dates from Aix-en-Provence 1735. These visual artifacts testify to Kabbalah's presence not merely as a textual tradition but as an institutional and artistic presence within Catholic Christian culture, with diagrams, engravings, and visual representations circulating widely among educated clergy and scholars.
