Chunk 13

Pages 41-43

This section presents Reuchlin's mystical framework built on three interconnected systems of divine knowledge. The fifty gates, drawn from Talmudic sources, represent ascending degrees of prophetic understanding, with Moses reaching the 49th gate and the 50th gate reserved exclusively for the Messiah as the ultimate illuminator. Central to Reuchlin's system are the thirty-two paths of wisdom, enumerated through Rabbi Jacob Cohen's commentary on the Sefer Yetzirah (Book of Creation). These thirty-two intelligences are arranged hierarchically and provide a structured map of divine emanations, from "miraculous intelligence" to "natural intelligence." Secret further notes George of Venice's parallel enumeration, which derives the thirty-two from numerical values in the Hebrew word "Leb" (heart), treating this number as cosmically significant (a number always divisible by two into equal parts). The text then details the ten sephiroth (divine emanations), which can be visualized as either a tree or an anthropomorphic figure. These are: Kether (Crown), Hochmah (Wisdom), Binah (Understanding), Hesed (Mercy), Geburah (Severity), Tiphereth (Beauty), Netzach (Victory), Hod (Splendor), Yesod (Foundation), and Malcuth (Kingdom), with Ein-Soph (the Infinite) above them all. Each sephira encompasses multiple divine names, attributes, and interpretive meanings—Malcuth alone is associated with the moon, the kingdom, the second Cherub, the Bride in the Song of Songs, and numerous scriptural symbols. Reuchlin draws heavily on Paul Ricius's *Porta lucis* (Gate of Light) and François George of Venice's commentaries. Guy Le Fèvre de la Boderie contributes poetic meditations on the sephiroth, rendering them in Renaissance French verse, mapping their theological and cosmological significance.
