Chunk 64

Pages 198-200

This is Secret's major concluding statement on Christian Kabbalah, running from page 198 to the start of the index. Secret opens by restating his methodological aim: a broad survey establishing the domain was poorly explored, requiring preliminary research before deeper analysis could be attempted. He then directly challenges the historiographical consensus inherited from J. L. Blau and F. C. Burkitt, both of whom dismissed Christian Kabbalah as a superstitious fashion swept away by Copernic, Kepler, and Galileo—rendering the Zohar mere curiosity for specialists. Secret argues this narrative is reductive. While admitting that Christian Kabbalah became a "mode" and did splinter into neo-Platonic, Pythagorean, and hermetic tangents alien to its core—especially in the hands of German mystical metaphysicians like Paracelsus—he contends that the core impulse was legitimate. Christian Kabbalah must be situated within the Renaissance inheritance of medieval exegetical and mystical traditions, facing the fundamental theological problem of the letter and spirit of Scripture. The advent of Hebrew studies, proliferation of polyglots, Reuchlin's controversy with Johannes Hochstraten, and the centrality of biblical translation and interpretation to the Reformation crisis all contextualize why kabbalists like Pic, Reuchlin, Gilles de Viterbe, and Postel engaged with cabalistic hermeneutics. Secret concludes that Christian kabbalists' sympathies aligned more closely with genuine scientific method—patient study, transmission of Jewish tradition, respect for rabbinical learning—than the contempt many hebraists later showed for "the ravings of the Rabbis." The Kabbalah was "a discovery as important as the New World," and while Christian kabbalists may have misappropriated cabalistic symbols, their openness to Jewish textual tradition represented a more honest scholarly approach than the anti-Semitic dismissiveness that followed.
