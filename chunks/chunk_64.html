Chunk 64

Pages 198-200

This chunk constitutes Secret's major concluding argument on Christian Kabbalah, a revisionist historiographical intervention running from page 198 through the beginning of the index materials. Secret opens by restating his foundational methodological commitment: the work itself has been conceived as a broad, preliminary survey establishing that the domain of Christian Kabbalah was profoundly under-explored in existing scholarship, requiring comprehensive documentary research before more sophisticated analytical work could be attempted. This framing is crucial because it positions Secret's monograph not as a definitive interpretation but as a comprehensive catalog and narrative that makes deeper future scholarship possible. With this groundwork established, Secret then launches a direct and sustained challenge to the historiographical consensus that had dominated cabalistic studies, particularly the dismissive accounts offered by J. L. Blau and F. C. Burkitt, both of whom had relegated Christian Kabbalah to the status of a passing superstitious fashion, a temporary enthusiasm destined to be swept away by the scientific revolution of Copernicus, Kepler, and Galileo. Under this historiographical model, the Zohar and its interpreters became mere curiosities for antiquarian specialists, marginal to the real intellectual progress of the modern era.

Secret's challenge to this reductive narrative is sustained and multifaceted. He acknowledges legitimate elements within the dismissive account: Christian Kabbalah did indeed become a "mode"—a fashionable intellectual pursuit—and did splinter into multiple subsidiary traditions that departed fundamentally from the authentic core of Jewish cabalistic thought. The German mystical metaphysicians, particularly figures like Paracelsus, incorporated cabalistic symbols and language into hermetic, neo-Platonic, and Pythagorean frameworks that were fundamentally alien to cabalistic esotericism. Moreover, Christian kabbalists frequently misappropriated, distorted, or subordinated Jewish cabalistic doctrines to Christian theological ends—evidence of which permeates the sources Secret has examined. Yet Secret argues that this acknowledgment of later deformations should not obscure the legitimacy of the original impulse and its earliest manifestations. Christian engagement with Kabbalah, he contends, must be situated within the broader context of the Renaissance recovery of medieval exegetical and mystical traditions—that vast intellectual current through which Renaissance scholars encountered, revalued, and partially reorganized the intellectual legacy of the Christian Middle Ages.

Central to Secret's argument is his understanding of the theological and hermeneutical crisis that made Christian Kabbalah meaningful and necessary. The fundamental problem driving Christian intellectual life in the 15th-16th centuries was the interpretation of Scripture itself: how should one understand the relationship between the letter of the text and its spiritual meaning? How could the traditions of Christian exegesis be preserved while remaining open to philological advances in Hebrew and Greek? The advent of Hebrew studies as a rigorous discipline, the proliferation of polyglot Bibles, and the profound theological and political crisis unleashed by the Reformation all made Hebrew language learning and scriptural interpretation matters of existential importance to Christian intellectuals. In this context, figures like Pico della Mirandola, Johannes Reuchlin, Gilles de Viterbe, and Guillaume Postel engaged with cabalistic hermeneutics not as dilettantes or occultists, but as serious scholars attempting to deepen Christian Scripture interpretation by learning from Jewish exegetical traditions. Reuchlin's controversy with Johannes Hochstraten, the Dominican inquisitor who sought to suppress Hebrew learning as dangerous to Catholicism, reveals the fundamental stakes: was Jewish textual learning a resource for Christian theology or a threat to Christian orthodoxy?

Secret's conclusion reframes the entire historiographical question by inverting the standard judgment. Rather than Christian kabbalists representing an embarrassing superstitious detour from genuine science, Secret argues that their scholarly methods and intellectual dispositions aligned far more closely with what would become genuine scientific method than the contemptuous dismissiveness many later hebraists displayed toward "the ravings of the Rabbis." The Christian kabbalists of the Renaissance and early 17th century pursued patient, detailed study of texts; they sought to understand and transmit the traditions of Jewish learning rather than subordinating those traditions to preconceived doctrinal demands; they displayed genuine respect for rabbinical learning and attempted to build collaborative relationships with Jewish scholars when possible. These practices represent a more honest and intellectually generous scholarly engagement than the anti-Semitic dismissiveness that increasingly characterized early modern scholarship's attitude toward Jewish textual tradition. Secret's famous formulation—that "the Kabbalah was a discovery as important as the New World"—inverts the temporality of the standard narrative: rather than viewing cabalistic learning as a superstition swept away by modernity, Secret positions it as a fundamental and transformative enlargement of European intellectual horizons, comparable in significance to the geographic discoveries that fundamentally expanded European geographical and economic understanding. While Christian kabbalists certainly misappropriated certain cabalistic symbols and concepts, their fundamental openness to Jewish textual tradition represented a scholarly and humane approach far superior to the dismissiveness and anti-Semitism that would subsequently characterize much of European intellectual culture's engagement with Hebrew learning and Jewish textuality. This concluding intervention represents Secret's definitive claim: that recovering the history of Christian Kabbalah requires not only documentary reconstruction but also a fundamental reassessment of how early modern Europe valued and devalued Jewish intellectual contributions to Western thought.
