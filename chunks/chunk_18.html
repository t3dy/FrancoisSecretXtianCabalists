Secret details Ricius's definitive hermeneutical method and magnum opus. Ricius distinguishes three interpretive modes of Scripture: the literal (Peshat), the allegorical/midrashic (Midrash), the tropological (Sechel, exemplified by Abraham ibn Ezra and Levi ben Gerson), and the anagogic (Cabale). Unlike Pico, who mapped these to four Christian senses, Ricius emphasizes that proper kabbalah requires integration of all methods: literal context and grammar, allegorical typology, and contemplative aspiration to transcendent realities. The kabbalist must understand not merely words but divine paradigms and eternal ideas within Scripture. Ricius's contemplative method encompasses imagination, reason, and intellect adhering to supernal realities—a practice yielding prophecy, healing, glossolalia, and wisdom. He cautions that differing kabbalists interpret letters, numbers, positions, and permutations variously; modern practitioners err by ignoring the underlying "virtue" or spiritual power of these operations. Taking the Tetragrammaton as example, Ricius shows how Yod (indivisible unity), He (divinity), Vau (six), and He compose a name signifying both the Godhead and the ten Sephiroth with their three supernal and seven lower aspects. *De cælesti agricultura* (1541) comprises four books. Book I dialogues between Friar Gometius and seekers, expounding faith through Aristotelian philosophy with invocations to the ten divine names. Book II proves New Testament fulfillment through rabbinic sources (Talmud, Maimonides, Joseph Albo, the Zohar via Recanati). Book III establishes scriptural concord via Constantinian authority. Book IV contains Ricius's most influential contribution: an apology against Hoogstraten (condemning kabbalah as blasphemy), 50 theorems on kabbalistic doctrine, and the Porta lucis translation on the ten divine names. The theorems, originally 66 but reduced to 50, establish how the ten Sephiroth correspond to divine names, angelic orders, patriarchs, and virtues—teaching Adam's prelapsarian knowledge and the Messiah's restoration of humanity to its celestial prototype. Ricius insists kabbalah confirms—not contradicts—Christian dogma: the Trinity, incarnation, redemption, resurrection, judgment, and sanctification all appear transparently in the kabbalistic texts when properly read. Secret concludes by noting Athanasius Kircher's later appropriation and sanitization of Ricius's contemplative praxis, stripping its Hebrew references and adding qualifications about Church authority. The full text preserved by Ricius includes exempla of Rabbi Ishmael and Rabbi Eliezer Kalir achieving divine illumination through sacred foods—episodes Kircher censored. These sources establish Ricius as the architect of Christian Kabbalah's intellectual maturity during the Italian Renaissance golden age.
