Chunk 31

Pages 98-100

This chunk spans the transition from Secret's survey of minor French Hebraists to the central figure of **Guillaume Postel**, establishing the decisive moment when French Christian Kabbalah moves from derivative scholasticism to prophetic mysticism. The opening (p. 98) continues an analysis of stock Christian-kabbalistic motifs as they were deployed by minor French grammarians throughout the early sixteenth century. These writers recycled a standard repertoire of cosmological schemes—*Olam hagilgulim* (world of orbs/spheres), *Olam haiesodoth* (world of elements), and *Olam hagaton* (the microcosm or "little man")—alongside Hebraic learning transmitted through St. Jerome's alphabetical teachings, metathetical practices (*Ziruph*), symbolic mysticism around the closed Mem, and air as the cosmic bonding principle. These motifs derived from Pico della Mirandola's foundational *Conclusiones* and were elaborated in Giorgi's *De Harmonia Mundi*, particularly in the numerical equivalences linking *Berith*, *Miriam*, and *Jesus*. **Chéradame** (listed as Charidème in Bourbon's *Nugæ*) typifies this minor tradition: he promised a never-published "mystic Myriad" (*Myriade mystique*) devoted to the divine name YSU, built entirely on numerological correlations—the Hebrew word for turtledove summing to 606, which equals the combined numerical values of *Iesu* and *Miriam*, creating a Christological-Marian symbolic concordance. **Joannes Vallensis**, serving as royal chaplain, dedicated his 1545 Hebrew grammar to the Dominican Gabriel de Guzman, decorating it with identical commonplaces drawn from the same limited kabbalistic repertoire. Secret's judgment is severe: France had not yet produced a Christian kabbalist approaching the stature of **Pico, Galatino, Giustiniani, Giorgi, Gilles de Viterbe, Reuchlin, Pellican, or Widmanstetter**—the German and Italian masters who had wrestled deeply with Hebrew texts and absorbed genuine Jewish esotericism. France possessed dedicated Hebraists like **Vatable**, who taught Hebrew methodology and text-criticism, but none who had transformed that linguistic mastery into a comprehensive mystical synthesis. Postel would become that transformation incarnate, finding in the Zohar what seemed to him the universal key unlocking all wisdom. A dense apparatus of footnotes underscores the archival scaffolding beneath this transition. The chunk's biographical core introduces **Postel** (born Barenton in the Avranches diocese of Normandy) as the man who would shatter the limitations of French Hebrew scholarship. His early life reads like a picaresque of learned hardship: orphaned by plague, apprenticed as a schoolmaster by age thirteen, robbed and nearly murdered on the road to Paris, he worked harvest labor in the wheat fields of Beauce to finance his education. Entering the Collège de Sainte-Barbe under the pedagogical direction of **Gouvea**, Postel achieved the degree of *maître ès arts* by 1530 and began—with remarkable self-directed intensity—to teach himself Hebrew from borrowed grammars and to master Arabic through private study. His linguistic talents attracted court attention: in 1536, he was selected to accompany **Jean de la Forest**'s diplomatic embassy to Constantinople, a voyage that brought him into contact with Hebrew and Arabic manuscripts and transformed him from provincial scholar into a man of international learning. Upon his return from the East, Postel's ambitions crystallized into institutional forms: he published a treatise on twelve languages, establishing himself as a linguist of remarkable breadth, and secured a teaching position at the trilingual college founded in Paris to promote Greek, Latin, and Hebrew. But his trajectory was disrupted by the political disgrace of his patron, Chancellor Poyet. This setback triggered a profound **religious crisis**. Rather than seeking another secular position, Postel withdrew from worldly advancement, refused royal patronage, and began writing prophetic exhortations to **François Ier** urging ecclesiastical reform. His magnum opus of this period, the *De orbis terræ concordia* (On the Concord of the World), proposed to establish universal peace through the recovery of original reason—a synthesis of Neoplatonic philosophy and mystical theology aimed at unifying Christendom under the banner of restored divine wisdom. Drawn into contact with the Jesuits and the prophetic fervor surrounding **Ignatius Loyola**, Postel absorbed apocalyptic expectations—notably **François de Paule**'s revelation to Louise de Savoie that a future universal monarch would arise to unite the world under Christian law. But Ignatius, finding Postel's increasingly visionary temperament incompatible with Jesuit discipline and rational obedience, **expelled him from the Society**, unable to redirect his mystical fervor into institutional channels. This rejection became the hinge of Postel's life. Traveling to **Venice**, the great entrepôt of Hebraic and Islamic learning in Renaissance Europe, he acquired an exceptionally precious manuscript of the Zohar (valued at 5,000 ducats, likely obtained through the circle of the Christian printer **Bomberg**). More consequentially, he encountered **Mère Jeanne**, a prophetic visionary described as the illiterate fifty-year-old "Venetian Virgin," who claimed knowledge of the apocalyptic restoration and who—in Postel's account—became his spiritual mother and the key that unlocked the Zohar's deepest mysteries, revealing how the Jewish mystical tradition encoded the Christian mysteries that had lain dormant since Simeon the Just received the infant Jesus in the Temple.
