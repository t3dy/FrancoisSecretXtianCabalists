Chunk 31

Pages 98-100

This chunk spans the transition from Secret's survey of minor French Hebraists to the central figure of **Guillaume Postel**. The opening (p.98) continues an analysis of stock Christian-kabbalistic motifs as deployed by French grammarians: the cosmological scheme of *Olam hagilgulim* (world of orbs/spheres), *Olam haiesodoth* (world of elements), and *Olam hagaton* (the microcosm or "little man"); the alphabet explained per **St. Jerome**; metathesis (*Ziruph*); the **closed Mem**; air as cosmic bond; *Bereshith* per **Pico della Mirandola**; and the numerical equivalence of *Berith*, *Miriam*, and *Jesus*, drawn from Giorgi's *De Harmonia*. **Chéradame** (a.k.a. Charidème in Bourbon's *Nugæ*) promised a never-published "mystic Myriad" on the name YSU, playing on numerology (the turtledove = 606 = Iesu + Miriam combined). **Joannes Vallensis**, royal chaplain, dedicated a 1545 Hebrew grammar to the Dominican Gabriel de Guzman, sprinkling it with the same commonplaces. Secret judges that France had not yet produced a Christian kabbalist rivaling **Pico, Galatino, Giustiniani, Giorgi, Gilles de Viterbe, Reuchlin, Pellican, or Widmanstetter**—only Hebraists like **Vatable**, whose "spiritual" pupil Postel would find in the Zohar a universal key. A dense set of footnotes follows. The chunk's heart is the biography of **Postel** (b. Barenton, Avranches diocese). Orphaned by plague, a schoolmaster at thirteen, robbed and nearly killed en route to Paris, he worked harvests in Beauce to fund his studies, entered the Collège de Sainte-Barbe under **Gouvea**, became *maître ès arts* by 1530, and taught himself Hebrew (from borrowed grammars) and Arabic. Chosen in 1536 to accompany **Jean de la Forest**'s embassy to Constantinople, he returned laden with Arabic manuscripts, published a treatise on twelve languages, and taught at the trilingual college. After his patron Chancellor Poyet's disgrace, Postel underwent a **religious crisis**, refused royal money, exhorted François Ier to reform, and wrote the *De orbis terræ concordia* aiming to establish universal concord by reason. Drawn toward Loyola, he absorbed prophecies (François de Paule's prediction to Louise de Savoie of universal monarchy), but Ignatius, unable to recall him to reason, **expelled him from the Society**. Postel then reached **Venice**, obtained a Zohar worth 5,000 ducats (likely via Bomberg's circle), and met **Mère Jeanne**, the illiterate fifty-year-old "Venetian Virgin" who prophesied the angelic Pope and whose inspiration unlocked the Zohar for him.
