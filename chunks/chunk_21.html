Egidio Antonini (1465-1532), known to history as Cardinal Gilles of Viterbo, stands as Renaissance Kabbalah's most erudite and institutionally powerful patron—a figure whose combination of ecclesiastical authority, humanist erudition, and genuine engagement with Hebrew mysticism was virtually unmatched among his peers. Born in Viterbo and entering the Augustinian hermits in his youth, Gilles exhibited precocious gifts in poetry, rhetoric, and theological speculation. His elevation through the Church hierarchy was meteoric: he became prior of his order, opened the Fifth Lateran Council in 1512 with a celebrated sermon on ecclesiastical reform that impressed Pope Leo X, served as papal ambassador and nuncio, and was created cardinal in 1517—at fifty-two, a remarkable honor testimony to both his spiritual authority and intellectual eminence. Yet Gilles's ecclesiastical prominence obscured his deepest intellectual passion: the systematic study of Hebrew, Aramaic, and Arabic, and the recovery of Jewish mystical and philosophical sources for Christian theology. Gilles's erudition spanned contradictory philosophical traditions, and he pursued them with genuine philosophical seriousness. He had mastered Aristotelian philosophy but developed a profound, almost prophetic critique of its limitations—arguing that Aristotle's system, for all its logical rigor, had missed the deeper mystical dimensions of reality that Platonic philosophy and Hebrew mysticism preserved. This critique was not merely rhetorical posturing; it reflected Gilles's conviction that Kabbalah offered access to truths that scholastic logic could not articulate. He cultivated relationships with the leading Hebraists of his generation: Giustiniani became his master and intellectual superior in Hebrew studies, teaching him the exegetical subtleties that only direct experience with Hebrew texts could convey. Galatin and Felice da Prato (a converted Hebrew master who worked under Daniel Bomberg in Venice) became his correspondents and collaborators. Through these relationships, Gilles established himself not as a theoretical enthusiast but as a serious practitioner of Hebrew learning, a cardinal who could credibly discuss the niceties of Hebrew grammar and kabbalistic symbolism with genuine expertise. Gilles's patronage of Hebrew learning extended through two institutional channels that proved enormously consequential for Renaissance Christian Kabbalah. First, through Daniel Bomberg's Venetian printing house (one of the era's most important centers for Hebrew publication), Gilles followed with keen interest the publication of the 1515-1517 Hebrew Bible, arguably the most important Hebrew printed text up to that time. He supported initiatives like Felice da Prato's translation and commentary on the *Sefer Temunah* (Book of Form), the *Imre Sefer* (Words of the Book), and various Hebrew grammatical works deemed "useful to Christian religion." Second, and more subtly, Gilles used his ecclesiastical authority to legitimize Hebrew studies within Catholic intellectual circles, signaling that the serious study of Jewish sources was compatible with—indeed, necessary for—true Christian theology. Abraham of Balmes' grammar, published with Gilles's encouragement and titled *Possession of Abraham*, exemplified this synthesis: it discussed mystical alphabets, enumerated the seventy traditional modes of Torah interpretation, and explored the role of letters in the *Sefer Yetzirah* (Book of Formation), while carefully framing these discussions as exploration of "secrets of nature and divinity" rather than magical practice or heretical speculation. The year 1512 marked a pivotal moment in Gilles's intellectual trajectory: he welcomed Elias Levita (1468-1549), the legendary grammarian and Masorah scholar who had fled Italy after the catastrophic 1509 sack of Padua by imperial forces. Levita's own account of his reception is moving in its particularity: Gilles embraced him, promised immediate patronage and protection for his family, and welcomed him into his household as both honored guest and intellectual collaborator. Levita, a Jew teaching Christians—a transgression in Jewish tradition—required theological justification, which Levita provided by arguing that his instruction was permissible under Noachide law (the ethical commandments binding all humanity). Levita remained in Gilles' household for years, copying manuscripts with meticulous care, and earned lavish praise in the preface to his 1518 *Bahur* (Young Man), Gilles's own Hebrew grammar printed under the cardinal's patronage. Levita's presence transformed Gilles's household into a workshop of Hebrew erudition; later, after the 1527 sack of Rome scattered the household, Levita taught other Augustinians, French ambassadors, and from 1527-1540 became the teacher of scholars at Bomberg's Venetian print shop—making him, over his remaining decades, perhaps the most influential Hebrew instructor in western Christendom. The chapter emphasizes Gilles's systematic, methodical, and extraordinarily expensive acquisition of kabbalistic texts through Hebrew and converted intermediaries. He spent thousands of ducats—a fortune by early sixteenth-century standards—assembling copies of the Zohar (in various recensions), the Recanati commentary (Moses Isserles's sophisticated mystical-philosophical annotations), the *Sefer ha-Temunah* (Book of Form), *Raziel* (the legendary grimoire), and *Ma'arechet ha-Elohut* (Order of Divinity). These were not theoretical purchases; Gilles read them with care, annotated them, and mined them for theological insights. The library he assembled became not merely a collection but an intellectual workshop—a space where Christian theology encountered Jewish mysticism directly through primary texts, not filtered through intermediate summaries or polemical commentaries. Though Gilles published nothing on kabbalistic subjects in his lifetime—a reticence likely reflecting ecclesiastical caution or perhaps a conviction that such matters belonged to manuscript circulation among the learned rather than printed dissemination—his two unpublished treatises became extraordinarily influential. The *Libellus de litteris hebraicis* (Treatise on the Hebrew Letters, 1517) and the *Scechinah* circulated in manuscript throughout the sixteenth and early seventeenth centuries, copied and recopied by scholars hungry for access to Gilles's synthesis of Kabbalah with Christian theology. These works became authoritative precisely because they represented Kabbalistic wisdom translated and filtered through the authority of a cardinal, sanctioned by Rome's intellectual elite, and articulated in elegant Neo-Latin that made mystical theology philosophically coherent. Subsequent Christian kabbaliste cited Gilles extensively (often without acknowledging their source), embedded his arguments in their own treatises, and treated his unpublished works as virtual scripture. This manuscript circulation created a form of intellectual authority distinct from printed authority—more restricted, more prestigious, more laden with the sense of access to hidden wisdom.
