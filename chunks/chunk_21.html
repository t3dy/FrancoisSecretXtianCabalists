This passage introduces Egidio Antonini (1465-1532), known as Gilles of Viterbo, a cardinal of towering intellectual authority who became Christianity's preeminent Kabbalistic scholar. Born in Viterbo, he entered the Augustinian hermits, where his gifts as humanist, poet, and preacher made him indispensable. He opened the Fifth Lateran Council with a celebrated sermon on ecclesiastical reform, served as papal ambassador and nuncio, and was created cardinal in 1517. His erudition spanned Aristotelian philosophy (which he critiqued), Platonism, and Oriental languages—Hebrew, Aramaic, and Arabic. Gilles cultivated relationships with leading Hebraists: Giustiniani (his master), Galatin, and Felice da Prato (a converted Hebrew master under Daniel Bomberg). Through Bomberg's Venetian press, Gilles followed the publication of the 1515-1517 Hebrew Bible and supported initiatives like Felice's translation of the *Sefer Temunah*, *Imre Sefer*, and Hebrew grammatical works deemed "useful to Christian religion." Abraham of Balmes' grammar—titled *Possession of Abraham*—exemplified this synthesis, discussing mystical alphabets, the seventy modes of Torah interpretation, and the role of letters in the *Sefer Yetzirah*, while carefully avoiding Kabbalah as "secrets of nature and divinity." In 1512, Gilles welcomed Elias Levita (1468-1549), the legendary grammarian and Masorah scholar who had fled Padua after the 1509 sack. Levita's account of his reception is moving: Gilles embraced him, promising patronage and protection for his family. Levita defended his teaching of Christians as permissible under Noachide law, and remained in Gilles' household until the 1527 sack of Rome, copying manuscripts and earning praise in the preface to his 1518 *Bahur* (grammar). Levita later taught other Augustinians, French ambassadors, and (from 1527-1540) scholars at Bomberg's Venetian shop. The chapter emphasizes Gilles' systematic acquisition of Kabbalistic texts through Hebrew and converted intermediaries, spending thousands of ducats assembling copies of the Zohar, Recanati commentary, *Sefer ha-Temunah*, *Raziel*, and *Ma'arechet ha-Elohut*. Though Gilles published nothing in his lifetime, his two unpublished treatises—the *Libellus de litteris hebraicis* (1517) and the *Scechinah*—became authoritative works, influencing subsequent Christian Kabbaliste through widespread manuscript circulation.
