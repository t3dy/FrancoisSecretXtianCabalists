Cardinal Frederick Borromée (1564-1631), cousin of Saint Charles Borromée, collected Hebrew commentaries at the Ambrosiana yet harbored contempt for kabbalistic fables. Two manuscripts on kabbalah remain unpublished. A fierce censorious convert bearing the Borromée name pursued rigorous suppression, detecting heresies and superstitions in Hebrew texts and following Alfonso di Castro's alphabetical method of denunciation. Tomasso Campanella (1568-1639), unlike Postel, rejected kabbalah decisively. Though an enemy of the Talmud (copying Sixte of Siena's critique), he admitted only Elijah's prophecy as valuable. His *Metaphysica* and *Magia et gratia* condemn kabbalah's letter-transposition methodology as neither divinely instituted nor natural. Rabbinic responses emerge. Simon Luzzatto, Venice's chief rabbi, published *Discourse on the Condition of the Hebrews in Venice* (1629), which provides a learned digression on kabbalah's etymology (reception, tradition) and divisions: practical kabbalah treats letter-combinations, numerology, and Hebrew character-geometry to extract divine names; theoretical kabbalah contemplates the corporeal world's dependence on spiritual and archetypal realms. Luzzatto distinguishes ten foundational principles parallel to Pythagorean and Platonic systems but hierarchically structured between Infinity and creation. Charles de Valliquierville, seeking the philosopher's stone and believing *Bereshith* contains all sciences and philosophies, studied Hebrew under Luzzatto in Venice (1646) for a month. León de Modena (1571-1648), Luzzatto's contemporary, denounced kabbalah in his *Lion Roaring* yet declined developing this critique in *Historia de Riti ebraici* (1635), which he entrusted to Jacques Gaffarel. The 1637 Paris edition and 1638 Venetian edition contain extensive questions to Modena about *Lilith*, secret fasts (*Malcud*), and R. Meir ben Gabbai's *Path of Faith*. Modena's caution reflects anxiety over Inquisitorial attention to kabbalistic discussions among Jews. In the Dutch sphere, Menasseh ben Israel (c. 1604-1657) published in Latin and Spanish, attracting erudite and illuminist audiences including Pierre Serrarius and Isaac La Peyrère. Abraham Frankenberg (1593-1652) reissued Postel's *Clavis absconditorum*, published mystical notes on the *Bechinas Olam* (examination of worlds), and produced a *Raphael* (sacred light) treatise synthesizing mages, alchemists, physicians, and theosophers (Boehme, Agrippa, Paracelsus, Campanella). Menasseh, knowing Pico, François Georges, Postel, and Duret while opposing numerological calculation, prophesied imminent Messianic times. His *Piedra gloriosa* (with Rembrandt illustrations) and history of the Ten Tribes—locating them in Tartary with Ortelius—became celebrated. The *Conciliator* (Amsterdam, 1633) supplies an extensive kabbalistic bibliography and cites the *Zohar*'s principle that Torah possesses multiple strata: body (history), spirit, and anagogic interpretation, with the ten fundamental principles organizing divine influx. Jean-Baptiste van Helmont (1577-1644) showed minimal interest in kabbalah, but his son François-Mercure (1619-c. 1698) became friend to Knorr of Rosenroth, Henry More, and Leibniz. When interrogated by ecclesiastical officials in 1627 concerning kabbalah after publishing on magnetic healing (1624), Jean-Baptiste claimed Reuchlin and Ricius taught him kabbalah was mere fable and useless mental torture, hence unnecessary for Christians.
