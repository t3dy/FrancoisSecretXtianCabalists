Cardinal Frederick Borromée (1564–1631), cousin of the sainted Charles Borromée and founder of the Ambrosiana library, cultivated an extensive collection of Hebrew commentaries yet harbored profound contempt for Kabbalistic mysticism and its purported revelations, viewing kabbalah as composed of mere fables devoid of theological substance. Two unpublished manuscripts on kabbalah, composed by Borromée himself, remain in manuscript form, their contents unknown to scholars. A separate fierce censorious convert bearing the Borromée name pursued relentless suppression of Hebrew textual materials, demonstrating obsessive skill in detecting alleged heresies and superstitions embedded within Hebrew texts and employing Alfonso di Castro's systematic alphabetical method of cataloging and denouncing dangerous doctrines. This internal ecclesiastical policing apparatus reveals the institutional stakes of Kabbalistic study within Counter-Reformation Catholicism, where controlling access to and interpretation of Hebrew texts became a mechanism for enforcing doctrinal conformity.

Tomasso Campanella (1568–1639), the visionary Dominican whose vast intellectual ambitions encompassed philosophy, theology, political theory, and magic, decisively rejected kabbalah in contrast to his contemporary Postel's enthusiasm. Although an explicit enemy of Talmudic Judaism, reproducing Sixte of Siena's comprehensive critique of the Talmud, Campanella admitted only Elijah's prophecy as possessing genuine spiritual value within Jewish sources. His philosophical masterwork Metaphysica and his treatise on magic and grace, Magia et gratia, both condemn kabbalah's fundamental methodology of letter-transposition with arguments grounded in natural philosophy: the procedures, he insists, are neither divinely instituted through revelation nor derived from natural law, rendering them worthless as both theology and magic. Campanella's rejection testifies to growing philosophical skepticism regarding kabbalah's foundational claims, even among thinkers sympathetic to hermetic and magical philosophy generally.

Jewish voices begin articulating kabbalah's authentic theurgic and mystical intentions in response to Christian appropriation and distortion. Simon Luzzatto, serving as Venice's chief rabbi, published his Discourse on the Condition of the Hebrews in Venice (1629), which includes a learned and significant digression on kabbalah addressing both etymology and internal divisions. Luzzatto clarifies that "kabbalah" literally signifies "reception" and "tradition," not mystical revelation in the supernatural sense. He distinguishes practical kabbalah, which treats letter-combinations, numerology, and Hebrew character-geometry as methods for extracting divine names and understanding creation's architecture, from theoretical kabbalah, which contemplates the corporeal world's essential dependence on spiritual and archetypal realms. Luzzatto articulates ten foundational principles organizing kabbalistic thought, deliberately paralleling Pythagorean and Platonic systems to ground kabbalah within western philosophical tradition, yet insisting on the distinctive hierarchical structure mediating between infinite divine being and created manifestation. His exposition attempts to reclaim kabbalah from Christian distortion while simultaneously establishing its philosophical legitimacy.

Charles de Valliquierville, a French alchemist and seeker of the philosopher's stone, became convinced that the Bereshith (Genesis) contained all sciences and philosophies in encrypted form, leading him to pursue Hebrew study under Luzzatto himself in Venice during 1646 for an intensive month-long program of instruction. León de Modena (1571–1648), Luzzatto's younger contemporary, had previously denounced kabbalah in his polemic Lion Roaring, yet strategically declined developing this critique when composing his authoritative Historia de Riti ebraici (History of Hebrew Rites, 1635), instead delegating extensive discussion to the Christian scholar Jacques Gaffarel. The 1637 Paris and 1638 Venetian editions of Modena's history contain numerous questions posed to the rabbi concerning kabbalistic topics: the figure of Lilith, secret fasts designated Malcud, and R. Meir ben Gabbai's mystical work Path of Faith. Modena's cautious rhetorical strategy reflects acute anxiety regarding Inquisitorial attention to any extensive discussion of kabbalistic doctrines among Jewish scholars, revealing the institutional pressures constraining Jewish intellectual expression.

In the Dutch sphere, Menasseh ben Israel (c. 1604–1657) emerged as the most philosophically ambitious and internationally connected Jewish intellectual of his generation, publishing prolifically in both Latin and Spanish to reach erudite Christian audiences alongside illuminist seekers. His works attracted the intellectual attention of figures including Pierre Serrarius and Isaac La Peyrère. His collaborator Abraham Frankenberg (1593–1652) reissued Postel's Clavis absconditorum (Key to Hidden Things), published mystical notes on the Bechinas Olam (Examination of Worlds), and produced a major synthetic work titled Raphael (Sacred Light) that amalgamated the methodologies and doctrines of mages, alchemists, physicians, and theosophers including Boehme, Agrippa, Paracelsus, and Campanella. Menasseh, while demonstrating deep knowledge of Pico, François Georges, Postel, and Duret, explicitly rejected numerological calculation practices advocated by less discriminating practitioners. Instead, he focused his prophetic attention on the imminent Messianic times, interpreting current events through eschatological theology. His illustrated work Piedra gloriosa (Glorious Stone), featuring engravings by Rembrandt, and his history of the Ten Tribes—locating them in Tartary based on Ortelius's cartography—achieved considerable contemporary celebrity. His Conciliator (Amsterdam, 1633) supplies an extensive bibliography of kabbalistic sources and cites the Zohar's sophisticated principle that Torah possesses multiple interpretive strata: the body (narratives and historical content), spirit (ethical teachings), and anagogic interpretation (mystical correspondences), with the ten fundamental sephiroth organizing the system of divine influx through creation.

Jean-Baptiste van Helmont (1577–1644) showed minimal personal interest in kabbalah, concentrating instead on iatrochemistry and magnetic healing. His son François-Mercure van Helmont (1619–c. 1698), by contrast, became an intimate friend to the Kabbalistic scholar Knorr of Rosenroth, the Cambridge Platonist Henry More, and the polymath Leibniz, facilitating Christian-Jewish intellectual exchange. When ecclesiastical interrogators challenged Jean-Baptiste in 1627 concerning kabbalah following his 1624 publication on magnetic healing, he defended himself by claiming that both Reuchlin and Ricius had taught him that kabbalah constituted mere philosophical fable and useless mental torture, hence entirely unnecessary for Christian theology or practice. This defensive maneuver suggests the continuing institutional vulnerability of anyone engaged with kabbalah, even senior intellectuals with established reputations.
