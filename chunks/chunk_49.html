Chunk 49

Pages 154-156

This section traces the fundamental tensions between Protestant theology and Christian Kabbalah. Luther's marginal annotations on Reuchlin's *De arte cabalistica* reveal his measured but critical stance: while he acknowledges "good kabbalah" (particularly when it aligns with Christological interpretation), he rejects the arithmetical methods (gematria) as "not the right kabbalah." Luther's broader context—his documented hostility toward Jews and reliance on previous anti-Jewish polemicists like Nicolas de Lyra and Raymond Martin—colors his dismissal of kabbalistic interpretation. Melanchton, Reuchlin's nephew, adopts a more nuanced position: acknowledging that ancient kabbalistic doctrines contain valid wisdom, he notes that later Jewish practitioners corrupted them with "fantastical theories." Calvin's condemnation is far sharper. His commentary demonstrates contempt for kabbalistic exegesis applied to biblical passages: arguing that explaining *Bereshit* (Genesis 1:1) as referencing Christ is "frivolous," he warns readers against "forced glosses." His attack on attempts to conflate the names Jesus and Jehova—claiming they share only two letters—expands into a broader assault on cabalists as mere tricksters, comparable to alchemists peddling "vain, putrid arguments." The section catalogs how "kabbaliste" became a standard term of religious abuse among Reformers. Conrad Huser (1575) lumps Jews into categories of deceivers: "Talmudists, cabalists, Mohammedans, soldiers in Satan's pay." Philippe de Marnix de Sainte-Aldegonde (1599-1605) sarcastically attacks the Church's wholesale adoption of Jewish doctrines and mystical practices, including transubstantiation proofs drawn from rabbinic "cabal-istry." Lutheran scholars like Salomon Gesner (1603) and David Chytraeus dismiss kabbalistic numerology as "tautologies and vain imaginings" mixed with old Pythagorean philosophy, though Chytraeus himself ironically employed gematria to calculate the Number of the Beast as the Pope. This contradiction highlights the inconsistency: Protestants rejected kabbalah philosophically while sometimes employing its methods when the result suited their theology.
