Chunk 49

Pages 154-156

This section traces the fundamental tensions between Protestant theology and Christian Kabbalah, revealing deep institutional skepticism toward Jewish interpretative traditions even as some Protestant humanists engaged seriously with kabbalistic texts. Luther's marginal annotations on Reuchlin's De arte cabalistica expose a measured but critically dismissive stance: while he acknowledges that "good kabbalah" exists—particularly when it aligns with Christological interpretation of scripture—he decisively rejects the arithmetical methods of gematria as fundamentally misguided, declaring them "not the right kabbalah." This distinction proves crucial to understanding Lutheran responses: not a blanket condemnation of kabbalah itself, but a theological argument about which interpretative methods qualify as legitimate. Luther's broader context—his well-documented hostility toward Jews and his reliance on previous anti-Jewish polemicists like Nicolas de Lyra and Raymond Martin—colors his dismissal of kabbalistic interpretation with the weight of medieval Christian polemic. His position thus represents not merely scholarly critique but the appropriation of kabbalah for Christian purposes while simultaneously reinforcing anti-Jewish sentiment.

Melanchton, Reuchlin's nephew, adopts a more nuanced and sympathetic position within this landscape of Protestant skepticism. While acknowledging that ancient kabbalistic doctrines contain genuine wisdom transmitted through the Jewish tradition, he carefully notes that later Jewish practitioners corrupted these original teachings with what he dismisses as "fantastical theories." This rhetorical move—distinguishing pristine ancient kabbalah from degraded later versions—becomes a characteristic Protestant maneuver, allowing engagement with kabbalistic content while maintaining doctrinal superiority and Jewish inferiority. Melanchton's approach opens intellectual space for studying kabbalah without endorsing Jewish interpretation, a position that would appeal to subsequent Protestant scholars.

Calvin's condemnation proves far sharper and more contemptuous than Luther's measured skepticism. His biblical commentaries demonstrate visceral distaste for kabbalistic exegesis when applied to scriptural passages. He ridicules attempts to explain Bereshit (Genesis 1:1) as referencing Christ as "frivolous" and warns readers against "forced glosses" that twist scripture to fit preconceived mystical schemas. His attack expands to encompass the entire kabbalistic project: when cabalists attempt to conflate the names Jesus and Jehovah—claiming they share only two letters and thus represent divine identity—Calvin pivots to wider assault, portraying cabalists as mere tricksters and charlatans comparable to alchemists peddling "vain, putrid arguments." His rhetorical strategy strips kabbalah of any philosophical legitimacy, reducing it to sophistic wordplay and deceit.

The section catalogs how "kabbaliste" became a standard term of religious abuse among Reformers, functioning as a marker of intellectual corruption and doctrinal deviance. Conrad Huser (1575) employs the term as one element in his catalogue of Jewish deception, lumping Jews into categorical types: "Talmudists, cabalists, Mohammedans, soldiers in Satan's pay." Philippe de Marnix de Sainte-Aldegonde (1599-1605) sarcastically attacks the Church's wholesale adoption of Jewish doctrines and mystical practices, including the use of rabbinic "cabal-istry" to defend transubstantiation—turning kabbalistic methods against Catholic theology itself. These polemical uses reveal kabbalah as a contested interpretative territory where confessional battles were fought through claims about Jewish authenticity and corruption.

Lutheran scholars like Salomon Gesner (1603) and David Chytraeus dismiss kabbalistic numerology wholesale as "tautologies and vain imaginings" admixed with old Pythagorean philosophy, echoing broader humanist skepticism toward supposed correspondences between text and cosmos. Yet Chytraeus himself, in a remarkable contradiction that Secret highlights, ironically employed gematria—the very numerical method he theoretically rejected—to calculate the Number of the Beast as 666, claiming this figure represents the Pope. This inconsistency reveals the fundamental contradiction running through Protestant engagement with kabbalah: systematic rejection of kabbalistic philosophy coupled with tactical application of kabbalistic methods when theological results suited Protestant interests. The contradiction suggests that Protestant objections to kabbalah were never purely methodological but thoroughly theological—kabbalah was rejected when pursued by Jews or in service of Catholic positions, but selectively deployed when it advanced Protestant interpretations of scripture and prophecy.
