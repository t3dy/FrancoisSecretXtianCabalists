After twenty-three years of gestation and intellectual development—during which his understanding of Kabbalah deepened through correspondence, manuscript acquisition, and reflection—Reuchlin published his magnum opus *De arte cabalistica* (On the Art of Kabbalah, 1517). The work represented both a comprehensive response to persistent requests from scholarly correspondents and a mature synthesis of Christian Kabbalistic theory at the height of the humanist movement. Among these correspondents was the Benedictine monk Nicolas Ellenbog (1481–1543), whose touching letters document the intellectual struggles and material hardships of early modern Hebraists. Ellenbog sought clarity on the seventy-two divine names and the biblical ladder of Jacob—fundamental mystical structures—and his epistles reveal both scholarly hunger for esoteric knowledge and the practical scarcity of Hebrew texts in monastic libraries. Ellenbog's correspondence tells of personal tragedy: he lost a Hebrew Bible to the Peasants' War (1524–1526), that violent upheaval that destroyed monastic centers and scattered collections, and he desperately sought replacement copies from Reuchlin's network to continue his mystical studies under the master's guidance. These letters humanize the scholarly enterprise: behind the systems and theologies stood individual monks, gripped by intellectual passion, struggling materially to preserve and transmit learning. The *De arte cabalistica* reprises and vastly expands the earlier *De Verbo mirifico*. Now Capnion (Reuchlin himself) clarifies with greater precision that YHWH is properly understood as the Messiah's essential name because it is comprehended within and flows from divine Mercy (Rahamim in Hebrew, Misericordia in Latin). Talmudic authorities, he notes, agree that the Messiah bears the name Hanina, which etymologically and mystically connects to Mercy and compassion. The four-lettered Tetragrammaton becomes pronounceable and intelligible through the addition of the consonant Shin, yielding YHSWH via the notaricon method (constructing words from the initials of a phrase—here, "Shem YHWH Niqra"—"The Name Tetragrammaton Called"). Gematric values confirm and multiply this mystical arithmetic: YHWH equals 180 (yod=10, he=5, vau=6, he=5 plus some reckonings); Maqom (the divine Place or Throne) equals 180; Shin combined with Berahamim (In Mercy) equals 300. These numerical identities are not coincidence but revelation, the substrate of creation expressing itself through the sacred numerology embedded in Hebrew letters. Reuchlin interprets the Fifty Gates of Understanding—a central Kabbalistic concept—through sophisticated sephirotic and numeric mysticism. The calculation proceeds from multiplication: the decad (the ten Sephiroth) multiplied by the quinary (five categorical divisions of being) yields fifty gates—the soul-gates through which Moses received the Torah on Sinai and through which illuminated humans pass in mystical ascent. This calculation involves squaring and cubing principles drawn from Pythagorean number philosophy: the quinaire (the number five and its properties) remains constant when raised to successive powers, symbolizing the eternal return to center in the sphere, the principle of cosmic balance and reintegration. This systematic multiplication creates a grid of mystical knowledge whereby each of the ten Sephiroth relates to five categorical orders of being, each further subdivided, yielding infinite complexity and yet perfect order. Critically important for the history of Kabbalah studies, Reuchlin appends the first substantial Kabbalistic bibliography—a canonical apparatus that shaped all subsequent Christian Kabbalistic scholarship. He lists works by Akiba, Simon ben Iohai (reputed author of the Zohar, the supreme mystical text), Abraham Alaphia, Menahem Recanati (medieval commentator on the Pentateuch), Aben Ezra (medieval exegete and philosopher), Saadia (early Gaon and systematizer), Joseph Gicatilla (author of the *Saaré Orah* or Gates of Light), and many others—a catalog that became canonical and was expanded by François Georges de Venise and Andreas Masius. Masius's own annotations in 1573 identify specific titles with greater precision (*Saaré Orah*, *Sefer Yetzirah*, *Maareket ha Elohim*, *Sepher Habahir*, *Kether Sem Tob*, etc.) and provide descriptive apparatus, offering humanist scholarship authoritative bibliographic scaffolding for accessing Hebrew mystical texts and distinguishing authentic medieval sources from later compilations or forgeries. This bibliography transformed Kabbalah from a collection of fragmentary sayings into a disciplined field of study with identifiable primary sources, textual genealogies, and exegetical traditions.
