After twenty-three years, Reuchlin published his magnum opus *De arte cabalistica* (1517), responding to persistent requests from correspondents like the Benedictine Nicolas Ellenbog (1481–1543), who sought clarity on the seventy-two divine names and the ladder of Jacob. Ellenbog's touching letters document the struggles of early modern Hebraists—he lost a Hebrew Bible to the Peasants' War and desperately sought copies to continue studies under Reuchlin's guidance. The correspondence reveals both scholarly hunger and the practical scarcity of Hebrew texts. The *De arte* reprises and vastly expands the *De Verbo mirifico*. Now Capnion (Reuchlin) clarifies that YHWH is the Messiah's proper name because it is comprehended in Mercy (Rahamim/Misericordia); Talmudists agree the Messiah bears the name Hanina (Mercy). The four-lettered Tetragrammaton becomes pronounceable through the consonant Shin, yielding YHSWH via notaricon (the initials of "Shem YHWH Niquera"—"The Name Tetragrammaton Called"). Gematric values (YHWH = 180, Maqom [place] = 180; Shin with Berahamim = 300) confirm this mystical arithmetic. Reuchlin interprets the Fifty Gates of Understanding through sephirotic and numeric mysticism: multiplying the decad (ten Sephiroth) by the quinary (five categorical divisions of being) yields fifty gates—the soul-gates through which Moses received the Law. This calculation involves squaring and cubing principles: the quinaire remains constant when raised to powers, symbolizing the eternal return to center in the sphere. Critically, Reuchlin appends the first substantial Kabbalistic bibliography, listing works by Akiba, Simon ben Iohai (Zohar), Abraham Alaphia, Menahem Recanati, Aben Ezra, Saadia, Joseph Gicatilla, and many others—a catalog that became canonical and was expanded by François Georges de Venise and Andreas Masius. Masius's own 1573 annotations identify specific titles (Saaré Orah, Sefer Yetzirah, Maareket ha Elohim, Sepher Habahir, Kether Sem Tob, etc.) with descriptive apparatus, providing humanist scholarship with authoritative bibliographic scaffolding for accessing Hebrew mystical texts.
