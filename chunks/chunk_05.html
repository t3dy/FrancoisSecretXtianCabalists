The chapter situates itself within the incandescent disputational culture of fifteenth-century Spain, particularly in the aftermath of the famous Disputation of Tortosa (1413-1414), where learned Jewish and Christian apologists engaged in direct theological combat in the presence of the anti-Pope Benedict XIII. This was the forge where anti-Jewish polemical argument reached sophistication and nuance, where Christian theologians internalized the complexity of Jewish textual and mystical traditions the better to refute them, and where conversions and counter-conversions of intellectuals created networks of learned men who moved between communities. Secret opens with a reference to Scipion du Pleix's comprehensive Métaphysique ou science surnaturelle (Metaphysics or Supernatural Science, published 1605), where du Pleix, among the first Renaissance philosophers to write in French vernacular and attentive to kabbalistic doctrines, remarked on metempsychosis. Yet the chapter's substance focuses on the Spanish converts and apologists who developed distinctly Christian approaches to Kabbalah: figures who, having learned Hebrew and Jewish mysticism as Jews, brought insider knowledge to their Christian polemical projects. Jerónimo de Santa Fe (born Joshua ha-Lorki, c. 1350-1431), the converted Spanish rabbi and theologian, participated directly in the Tortosa disputation and subsequently authored the Hebraeomastix or Hebræomastix (The Scourge of the Hebrews), a polemical work that deployed Hebrew learning, biblical exegesis, and knowledge of Jewish traditions against his former co-religionists. Paul of Burgos (Paulus de Santa Maria, 1350-1435) — mentioned already for his Scrutinium Scripturarum — appears again as a foundational figure whose Additiones to Nicholas of Lyra's Postillae incorporated mystical and kabbalistic materials into Christian exegetical tradition.

Moïse Botarel, a Kabbalist scholar of standing within the Spanish Jewish community, authored a commentary on the Sefer Yetzirah (Book of Creation) specifically intended for Christian study, providing a crucial textual bridge between Jewish mystical tradition and Christian appropriation. The tradition holds that around 1409 Botarel composed this commentary for a mysterious learned Christian known only as "Maestro Juan," suggesting a hidden network of Jewish-Christian intellectual exchange occurring beneath the surface of official polemics. Most strikingly, Pedro de la Caballería (fl. c. 1450), a convert-apologist and Dominican, authored the Tractatus zelus Christi (Zeal for Christ, first published at Venice in 1592 by Martin Alphonse Vivaldus of Toledo, c. 1544-1605, the latter adding glosses from more recent Christian Kabbalists). The Zelus Christi stands as the first Christian European text to cite and name the Zohar explicitly by its Hebrew title, Cefer Azohar (Book of Splendor or Radiance), and to attribute it to the legendary rabbi Simon bar Yohai, the Tannaitic authority of the 2nd century CE whose mystical teachings the Zohar purports to transmit in Aramaic. De la Caballería quotes the Zohar's exegesis of Isaiah 6:3, the Trisagion ("Holy, Holy, Holy"), reading its Aramaic language as expressing the Christian Trinity: Abba (Father), Bera (Son), and Ruha de-Cudsa (Holy Spirit, literally "Spirit of Holiness"). He further cites the authentic kabbalistic works of Joseph Gikatilla (1248-c.1305), specifically the Sha'are Tsedek (Gates of Justice) and the celebrated Sha'are Ora (Gates of Light), the foundational mystical-exegetical texts of Spanish Kabbalah, and references the teachings of Nahmanides (Moses ben Nahman, 1194-1270), the greatest Spanish Jewish philosopher and mystic, regarding the divine names and the virtues or emanations they express. In this way, de la Caballería anchors Christian theological reading directly in the most authentic Jewish mystical sources, not merely in Talmudic apologetics, but in the living tradition of Spanish mysticism. Martin Alphonse Vivaldus, when he eventually published the Zelus Christi in 1592, included extensive glosses drawn from more recent Christian Kabbalists like Gilles de Viterbe, demonstrating the text's enduring relevance.

Alfonso de la Torre, identified as a converted Christian and collegial fellow of San Bartolomé college in Salamanca as early as 1437, authored the Visión deleitable de la filosofía (The Pleasant Vision or Agreeable Vision of Philosophy), a learned allegorical work printed at Burgos in 1485 and subsequently translated into Italian by the Venetian patrician Domenico Delfini, whose heirs published it under Delfini's name in 1556, later retranslated into Spanish by the Spanish Jew Francisco de Caceres and published at Frankfurt in 1623. Chapter VI of the Visión deleitable, entitled "On Arithmetic and Its Inventors, Its Utility, Employment, and Very Singular Secrets," introduces Kabbalah through the discourse of arithmetic and numerical mystery. The personified Arithmetic speaks of obtaining the "count of letters" (cuenta de letras) through which "marvels are drawn that man is unworthy to explain," and celebrates the Gematria employed by the Mecubalim (the mystical masters who know the mysteries), residing in "the depths of the cabala, wherein dwells a great part of prophecy." Arithmetic becomes the gateway to understanding how mystical secrets hide beneath the literal sense of Scripture, in the creation of things and the order of the world, from the first book of the Pentateuch containing the count of six workdays and the Sabbath rest, to the counts of ages in antediluvian generations, the years of servitude in Egypt, the forty days of Moses's fasting for the Law, and the 613 precepts of the Law — a numerology that runs through all the biblical books and attains its apex in the counts of Passover and Jubilee cycles.

Jacobus Pérez de Valencia (Jacob Perez, d. 1490), an Augustinian hermit cited extensively by Gilles of Viterbo, the great Christian Hebraist of the next generation, served as bishop of Chrysopolis in 1458 and suffragant of the Bishop of Valencia in 1491, embodying the integration of mystical learning with ecclesiastical office. His Commentaria in Psalmos (Commentary on the Psalms), published in 1484 and reprinted in 1553, incorporates mystical discussion of the Semiforas or Shem (the four-letter divine name in its mystical permutations and pronunciations) and draws upon biblical legends transmitted through the medieval Hebrew commentaries of David Kimhi, most notably the story that when Solomon dug the Temple's foundations, water rose so catastrophically that Jerusalem risked submersion, but the wise man Ahithophel inscribed the Semiforas (the four-letter name) upon a potsherd and hurled it into the waters, adjuring them to descend to the depths of the earth sixteen thousand stadia (ancient units of measurement) — a legend Pérez employs to elaborate theological and mystical symbolism regarding the waters of chaos and divine word.

In the same period, a Censura Talmudinis (Critique or Condemnation of the Talmud) circulated, reportedly issued by the Dominican friar Ferdinand and addressed to Thomas of Torquemada, the infamous inquisitor since late 1483, composed by Anthony of Ávila and the prior of the Segovia convent. This Censura distinguished between the written Torah and the oral Torah, which the Jews called the Talmud or Cabale, thus equating Kabbalah with the entire rabbinic tradition — a conflation that reveals Christian uncertainty about mysticism's exact relationship to broader Talmudic-rabbinic authority. Petrus Niger (Peter Schwarz), the German Dominican who Latinized his surname as many humanists did, though he would also be called Petrus Nigri, learned the rudiments of Hebrew in Spain "with Jewish children, in secret from experts in the language," revealing the clandestine pedagogical networks through which Christian missionaries acquired Hebrew competence. This Dominican, who spent time at Montpellier, Salamanca, Tübingen, Ingolstadt, and ultimately Buda, where he directed schools at the request of Mathias Corvinus (the Hungarian king), died in 1481. He brought from Spain a Latin treatise Against the Perfidious Jews on the Conditions of the True Messiah, also called The Star of the Messiah, published in Latin at Cologne in 1475 and reissued in German translation as Der Stern Meshiach — the first incunabula (early printed book) to incorporate Hebrew type characters. Beyond the names of biblical books and the Hebrew alphabet with significations following Jerome's etymologies, the Stern Meshiach develops the Trinity directly from the Hebrew letters opening Genesis: Bereshit Bara ("In the beginning He created"). The first two words contain the three Hebrew letters Beth, Resh, and Aleph: Beth opens the word Ben (Son), Resh opens Ruach Qodesh (Holy Spirit), and Aleph opens Ab (Father) — thus the very first creative utterance encodes the Trinity.

Pietro Bruto, a Venetian (d. 1493), who had engaged various apologetic works, composed a Letter Against the Jews in 1477 and especially the Victoria contra Judæos (Triumph Against the Jews), published in 1489, imitating and elaborating upon Porchetus's model. Bruto specifically mentions "the arithmetic of the Jews" (arithmetica Iudæorum) in reference to the famous anomaly in Isaiah 9:6 where the word Lemarbe ("to increase/extend the kingdom") contains a Mem (final M) that appears in closed form (Mem sophit) when it should appear open, the Mem rendered anomalously imprisoned within the word. Jewish exegetes had exploited this textual anomaly to calculate the year of Messiah's arrival; Christians reinterpreted it to signify either the number of years that had elapsed until Jesus's first coming or the miraculous announcement that from the Virgin Mary would be born the Messiah. This manipulation of singular letters' shapes — the closed versus open Mem — demonstrates the kabbalistic principle that even typography carries mystical and prophetic significance. In these Spanish and Italian apologists, we witness the emergence of a distinctly Christian hermeneutics of Hebrew letters, where the material properties of the script itself — its shapes, numerical values, grammatical peculiarities — become theological testimony.

The chapter climaxes with Giovanni Pico della Mirandola's celebrated Heptaplus (Greek for "sevenfold") exegesis, first published in print in 1489 though written earlier, taking as its primary object the opening word of Genesis, Bereshit ("In the beginning"). Secret provides the exegesis at extraordinary length, presenting the elegant French translation by Nicolas Le Fèvre de la Boderie (the Boderie being the adopted pseudonym of the translator), one of the great Christian Kabbalists of later sixteenth-century France and author of major independent kabbalistic works. Pico's method is fundamentally and explicitly kabbalistic: by treating the five letters of Bereshit (Beth-Resh-Aleph-Shin-Yod-Tav) as a combinatorial and permutational system, regrouping them in various sequences and formations, he extracts a magnificent mystical formula consisting of twelve distinct words that allegedly encodes the entire cosmic mystery in its essential structure. The twelve extracted words are Ab (Father, from Aleph-Bet), Be-bar (in/by the Son, from Beth-Bet-Aleph-Resh), Reshith (beginning, from Resh-Shin-Yod-Tav), Shabbat (rest, Sabbath, from Shin-Bet-Tav), Bara (created, from Bet-Aleph-Resh), Rosh (head, chief, from Resh-Aleph-Shin), Esh (fire, from Aleph-Shin), Shet (foundation, from Shin-Tav), Rab (great, from Resh-Aleph-Bet), Ish (man, from Aleph-Shin), Berith (covenant, from Bet-Resh-Yod-Tav), Tov (good, from Tav-Aleph-Vav — with the Tav mutating to Tav as Hebrew grammar permits). When assembled in the order Pico prescribes, they form the mystical sentence: "The Father by/in the Son, beginning and rest/end or culmination, created the head, fire, and foundation of the great man through good covenant/alliance." This extraordinary extraction performs the kabbalistic technique of Temurah (systematic letter-permutation and substitution) applied to the highest theological purpose imaginable: the recovery of complete Christological and Trinitarian meaning from the very material substance of Hebrew revelation itself. The exegesis operates throughout within the complex cosmological framework of the four worlds (the Arba'ah Olamot in Hebrew mystical terminology) — Atziluth (Emanation, the pure divine realm), Briah (Creation, the realm of pure intelligences and archangels), Yetzirah (Formation, the realm of the celestial spheres and angelic hierarchies), and Assiyah (Action or Manifestation, the material realm of elements and the microcosmic human body) — the nested and interpenetrating levels of reality through which medieval and Renaissance Kabbalah imagined the progressive divine overflow and descent into material creation. Pico's exegesis thus performs the supreme integration of Sefiratic cosmology with Christological theology and Trinitarian metaphysics, achieving this integration through the direct manipulation and permutation of the Hebrew letters that constitute God's primordial utterance in revelation.

Lorenzo da Brindisi (Lawrence of Brindisi, born Giulio Cesare Rosignoli, 1559-1619), the Capuchin philosopher-theologian and mystic, who eventually became general of his Capuchin order and was canonized in 1881, whose voluminous theological and exegetical writings remain in ongoing scholarly publication, adopted Pico's foundational kabbalistic technique and developed it further with systematic elaboration in no fewer than three separate sermon-length treatments. Rather than Pico's foundational twelve words, Lorenzo deliberately expanded the permutational procedure to yield exactly thirty-three words, deliberately and explicitly corresponding to the thirty-three years traditionally assigned to Jesus Christ's earthly life (from birth to crucifixion), thus binding the numerical symbolism of the permutational extraction directly to Christological biography and making the mystical formula into a biographical epitome of Christ's incarnate existence. The thirty-three extracted words, listed serially by Lorenzo, range from Bar (Son) and Ascith (I shall place/establish), Rescith (principle/beginning), Berith (creation/covenant/bond), Rab (great), Isch (man), Boré (creator), through complex permutations yielding Tha (nuptial chamber/marriage bed, alluding to the Church as the bride of Christ), Taab (desire), Sceth (foundation), Berith (Law, justice), Irath (fear/reverence), Rabbi (master/teacher), Boscer (evangelizing/bringing good news), Scir (canticle/song of praise), Ba (coming), Iabo (will come), Rasc (poor/humble), Toar (form/shape/appearance), Bascar (flesh/incarnate substance), Bor (pure/clear), Oieb (enemy/opponent), Beusc (infection/iniquity/sin), Iithbare (shall be created/formed), Scar (prince/noble), Abir (mighty/powerful), Iascar (just/righteous), Rosc (head), Beth (house/dwelling), Ascre (blessedness/felicity), Ascer Scabat (rest/Sabbath/peace), Beab (in/with the Father), and finally As Ab Ben (fire of Father and Son). The complete thirty-three-word formula reads in Lorenzo's reconstruction and Secret's French translation: "The Son I shall place, principle of the creation of the great man, creator of the nuptial chamber of desire, foundation of the Law of fear, master bringing glad tidings, canticle of the future. He will come poor in form, pure in flesh, enemy of infection. He shall be created prince mighty and just, head of the house of blessed rest with the Father and the fire of Father and Son." In this extraordinary expansion, Pico's foundational combinatorial mysticism becomes explicitly and systematically biographical — the permutations of the sacred Hebrew letters map directly and serially onto the salvific history of Christ, encompassing his divine sonship, his incarnation in human form, his poverty and self-emptying, his conquest of sin and evil, his exaltation as prince and judge, and his eternal unity with the Father in the Holy Spirit. The method thus transforms mere lexical manipulation into a comprehensive theology of redemption.

Secret notes that Lorenzo da Brindisi, who demonstrated considerable personal facility with Hebrew learning and who engaged directly with Hebrew biblical texts and Hebraic scholarship, explicitly grounded his kabbalistic practice in a foundational theological principle: that God conceals himself in profound depths within Scripture precisely "so that we might seek with utmost diligence and fervor the hidden treasure of divine knowledge" (ut quaeramus cum maxima diligentia thesaurum occultum divinae scientiae). This intuition echoes the famous patristic testimony of Jerome (Eusebius Hieronymus, c. 342-420), the Church Father and biblical translator, who in his commentary on Isaiah testified that the Bible contains far richer treasures of meaning and wisdom than surface grammatical or literal reading could ever reveal, dwelling as it does on the significance of the divine names, the mysteries of fire and cloud and darkness and wind (invoking the theophanic appearances in Exodus and the visionary experiences of the prophets), mysteries that only the spiritually mature and trained in Hebrew learning and grammar could hope to penetrate and interpret. This vision of textual depths — the concept of Scripture as an infinitely deep well of meaning accessible only to the initiated few, those trained in Hebrew language, kabbalistic number-lore, and mystical hermeneutics — becomes the defining programmatic project of Renaissance Christian Kabbalah, from Pico through Galatino, from Gilles of Viterbo to the French Kabbalists of the later sixteenth century. The chapter establishes this entire Spanish-Italian trajectory as the foundation for Pico's synthesis, demonstrating how the convert-apologists, the ordinary preachers, and the mystically-inclined theologians of fifteenth-century Spain had prepared the intellectual ground upon which Pico would build his revolutionary and comprehensive kabbalistic vision.
