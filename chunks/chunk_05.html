Chunk 5

Pages 16-18

The chunk continues the **Spanish** chapter amid the bitter *Disputation of Tortosa* (1413-1414), where the learned Jewish and Christian apologists answered each other's arguments in the presence of the anti-Pope Benedict XIII. This was the hotbed where anti-Jewish polemic and anti-Christian apologetics honed their weapons — including Kabbalistic argument itself. Secret cites the early modern compilation by Scipion du Pleix on metempsychosis, then turns to specific figures. **Jerónimo de Santa Fe** (Joshua ha-Lorki, c. 1350-1431), the converted Spanish rabbi who participated in the Tortosa disputation, authored the *Hebraeomastix* (Scourge of the Hebrews), a polemical work deploying Hebrew learning against his former co-religionists. **Moïse Botarel**, a Kabbalist scholar who wrote a commentary on the *Sefer Yetzirah* specifically for Christian study, provided a textual bridge. Most crucially, **Pedro de la Caballería** (fl. c. 1450), a convert-apologist, authored the *Zelus Christi* (Zeal for Christ) — the first Christian text to name the **Zohar** explicitly by its title (*Cefer Azohar*, Book of Splendor), attributed to **Simeon bar Yohai**, the legendary 2nd-century Tanna. De la Caballería quotes the Zohar's Aramaic reading of Isaiah 6 — "Holy, Holy, Holy" — as expressing *Abba* (Father), *Bera* (Son), and *Ruha de-Cudsa* (Holy Spirit), the Christian Trinity encoded in the thrice-holy Isaiah vision. He cites **Gikatilla**'s *Sha'are Tsedek* (Gates of Justice) and *Sha'are Ora* (Gates of Light), the foundational mystical texts of Spanish Kabbalah, and references **Nahmanides** on the divine names and virtues, anchoring Christian theological reading in authentic Jewish mystical sources. He adds **Alfonso de la Torre**'s *Visión deleitable* (The Pleasant Vision, printed 1485), whose arithmetic chapter introduces both elementary arithmetic and the mysteries of Kabbalah, exploring the *Mecubalim* (the ten sefirot or mystical countings). **Jacobus Pérez de Valencia** (d. 1490), a Carmelite friar and mystic, authored the *Commentaria in Psalmos* (1484), which incorporates discussion of the *Semiforas* (the four-letter divine name in mystical permutation) and draws on the Solomon-Temple legend from **Kimhi**'s biblical commentaries, using architectural and liturgical symbolism to express theological depth. A Dominican *Censura* (Critique) of the Talmud circulated, attempting to separate acceptable mystical elements from heretical rabbinic claims. **Petrus Nigri** (Peter Schwarz), a German convert-apologist, authored the *Stern Meshiach* (Star of the Messiah, 1475) — significantly, the first incunable (early printed book) to display Hebrew type — and derived the Trinity directly from the letters of the first word of Genesis, *Bereshit*: the letter *Beth* (*B*) represents *Ben* (Son), *Resh* (*R*) represents *Ruach* (Spirit), and *Aleph* (*A*) represents *Ab* (Father), thus extracting Trinitarian doctrine from the very opening of Scripture. **Pietro Bruto**'s *Victoria* (Triumph, 1489) exploited the anomaly of the closed *Mem* (final *M*) appearing in the middle of Isaiah 9:6 ("His government will not end") to demonstrate the miraculous insertion of the Messiah's eternal kingship into the very letters of Scripture. The chapter then climaxes with **Pico**'s famous **Heptaplus** exegesis (first published 1489, though written earlier) of the first word of Genesis, *Bereshit* ("In the beginning"), presented here in **Nicolas Le Fèvre de la Boderie**'s elegant French translation. By permuting the letters of *Bereshit*, Pico extracts a magnificent twelve-word formula that reconstructs the cosmic mystery: *Ab* (Father), *Bebar* (by/in the Son), *Reshit* (beginning), *Shabbat* (rest), *Bara* (created), *Rosh* (head), *Esh* (fire), *Shet* (foundation), *Rab* (great), *Ish* (man), *Berith* (covenant), *Tov* (good) — yielding the mystical sentence "The Father by the Son, beginning and end, created the head, the fire and the foundation of the great man by good covenant." This extraction unfolds within the complex symbolism of the **four worlds** (*Atziluth*, *Briah*, *Yetzirah*, *Assiyah*) — the emanative levels of intelligences, heavens, elements, and the microcosm — allowing Pico to integrate Sefiratic cosmology with the very first utterance of revelation. The exegesis demonstrates the technique of *temurah* (permutation) applied to the highest purpose: recovering hidden Christological and Trinitarian meaning from the raw materials of Hebrew letters. Secret then gives **Lorenzo da Brindisi** (Lawrence of Brindisi, 1559-1619), the Capuchin theologian and mystic, whose expansion of Pico's device yields **thirty-three words** (corresponding to Jesus's thirty-three years of earthly life), further elaborating the technique and deepening its Christological symbolism. The section closes with Secret echoing **Jerome** on the hidden treasure of divine wisdom embedded in the Scriptures — a vision of textual depths that only the illuminated few, trained in Hebrew and Kabbalah, could access and decode.
