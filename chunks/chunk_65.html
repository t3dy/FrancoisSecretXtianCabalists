Chunk 65

Pages 201-204

This chunk presents the first major section of an extensive *Index des noms propres* (Index of Proper Names), spanning alphabetically from A through P and covering pages 201-203 of Secret's monograph. The index lists approximately 400 or more individual names with their corresponding page references, creating a comprehensive prosopographical guide to every personage mentioned throughout the work. The index functions simultaneously as a practical research tool for locating specific figures within the narrative and as an implicit cartography of the Christian Kabbalah movement's scope, institutional networks, and personnel distribution across nearly two centuries of European intellectual history. The names encompassed in this section span multiple overlapping categories that reveal the multidisciplinary and multiconfessional character of early modern cabalistic scholarship. Jewish medieval and early modern figures constitute an important foundational layer: *Bahya ben Asher*, the 13th-century Spanish cabalist and biblical commentator; *Abulafia*, the 13th-century Castilian ecstatic kabbalist; the *Abarbanel* family (including Isaac and Judah), prominent 15th-century Spanish Jewish philosophers and biblical exegetes; and *Abraham de Balmes*, a 16th-century Hebrew grammarian and cabalist. These figures represent the Jewish intellectual lineage from which Christian kabbalists drew their knowledge and inspiration. The Christian humanist and kabbalist scholars who form the work's narrative core appear with frequency reflecting their major roles: *Pico della Mirandola*, *Johannes Reuchlin*, *Gilles de Viterbe*, *Giorgio Venerio*, *Guillaume Postel*, and *Jacques Gaffarel*—figures whose multiple page references indicate their status as central subjects of Secret's narrative. Christian theologians and ecclesiastical figures of various orders and periods constitute another layer: *Jerome* (foundational patristic exegete), *Augustine* (patristic theology), *Bérulle* (17th-century French mystic and cardinal), *Coton* (Jesuit theologian), and numerous Dominican, Jesuit, Benedictine, and Franciscan figures whose appearances throughout the narrative reveal the institutional pathways through which cabalistic learning circulated within the Catholic Church. The index also includes major European intellectuals of the 16th-17th centuries whose work engaged with cabalistic themes or who represented the scientific and intellectual culture against which Christian Kabbalah developed: *Erasmus* (humanist philologist), *Luther* and *Calvin* (Protestant reformers), *Laurentius Valla* (philologist and critic of textual authenticity), *Jean Bodin* (political theorist), *Joseph Scaliger* (historical chronologer and Hebrew scholar), *Johannes Kepler* (astronomer), *Tommaso Campanella* (mystical philosopher), and *Paracelsus* (occult physician and mystic). The inclusion of such figures reflects Secret's attention to the broader intellectual context within which Christian Kabbalah developed and was eventually marginalized. The index further includes the printers and textual scholars who played crucial roles in the material transmission of cabalistic knowledge: *Daniel Bomberg* (the Venetian Hebrew printer), *Christopher Plantin* (the great Antwerp printer and sponsor of polyglot Bibles), and *Giovanni Benedetto Giustiniani* (another important printer of Hebrew texts). These printers emerge from the index as crucial figures whose commercial and scholarly decisions determined which texts were produced, circulated, and preserved. The geographic and confessional range encompassed in the index reveals the genuinely European scope of Christian Kabbalah scholarship. *Spanish conversos* (Jews converted to Christianity, often maintaining secret engagement with Jewish learning) appear throughout the record, as do Italian humanists from Venice and Florence, Northern European Reformers from the German-speaking territories, French royal officials and cardinals engaged in manuscript acquisition and patronage, German mystical metaphysicians pursuing their own cabalistic innovations, and Polish and Bohemian scholars representing the eastward extension of Renaissance humanism. The density of page references for certain figures—particularly Pico, Reuchlin, Postel, Gaffarel, and Giorgio Venerio—makes visible the argumentative structure of Secret's narrative: these figures emerge as pivotal historical actors whose networks of patronage, correspondence, and textual transmission shaped the institutional development of Christian Kabbalah. The index thus functions not merely as a bibliography of names but as a sociological and institutional map revealing how cabalistic learning traversed confessional boundaries (Catholic, Protestant, and Jewish scholarly networks), geographic distances, and institutional contexts (royal libraries, cardinal's studies, university faculties, printing houses, monastic scriptoria) across the landscape of early modern Europe. The comprehensiveness and care invested in this index reflects Secret's commitment to establishing the field's foundational documentary base, making visible the webs of scholarly relationship and textual exchange through which Christian Kabbalah constituted itself as a coherent intellectual movement despite its apparent dispersal across multiple languages, confessions, and national traditions.
