## Early English Reception: Colet, Fisher, and Humanist Circles English Christian kabbalah's development was "tardif" (tardy), though not entirely absent. John Colet (1466–1519), who spent two years in Italy and frequented Florence's intellectual circle, drew on Pic's *Heptaplus* for his explication of creation undertaken at a friend's request. In his work on Dionysius's *Hierarchies*, he inserted the entire passage from Pic's *Apologia* on the double law given to Moses and the seventy books of Ezra. Yet despite hosting Agrippa in 1510 and working through Saint Paul's epistles with him, Colet resisted deeper kabbalistic engagement. In a 1517 letter, Colet expressed unease with Reuchlin's *De arte cabalistica* (sent by Erasmus to John Fisher), confessing: "I dare not judge. I know my ignorance and blindness in such abstruse matters... many things seemed greater miracles in words than in reality, doubtless because I know not the mysteries hidden in Hebrew words, their characters and combinations. In my view, we shall obtain salvation only through ardent love and imitation of Jesus Christ." John Fisher (1459–1535), to whom Reuchlin's work was dedicated, proved more receptive. In his 1521 sermon condemning Luther's books, Fisher invoked kabbalah as non-written tradition transmitted by prophets, repeating the argument twice. Defending the sacrament of orders against Luther, he copied Galatin's citation of Rabbi Iohai on the bread and wine sacrifice never ceasing, drawing elaborate parallels between Hebrew texts and Christian sacramental theology. ## Mid-Sixteenth Century: Scattered Engagement Thomas More translated Pic's biography (written by his nephew), yet the earliest mention of kabbalah in English appears in a Frenchman's work: Giles du Guez, preceptor to Princess Mary and librarian under Henry VII and VIII, writing in 1532 in *L'éclaircissement de la langue francaise*, notes that kabbalists posit fifty gates of intelligence—all but one accessible to Moses, who reached forty-nine because direct knowledge of God is impossible; comprehension comes through His operations (posterior knowledge). Du Guez had read Reuchlin. Hebraists proliferated. Paul Fagius died shortly after arriving; J. Emmanuel Tremellius (1510–1586), an Italian convert to the Reformation, was lukewarm toward kabbalah, though his colleague Matthew Parker wrote *Cabala seu scrinia sacra* (1591). A. R. Chevallier (1523–1572), Vatable's student, taught J. Drusius and Henry Broughton (1549–1612), who wrote for Jewish conversion, including *Des années jubilaires contre l'attente du Messie*. Broughton extensively cited the Zohar and Menahem de Recanati on Christ prefigured in Melchisedech. ## Dee and the Magical Current Everard Digby, a Ramus opponent, published *Theoria analytica* (1579), demonstrating familiarity with Agrippa, Trithème, Postel, and Reuchlin's kabbalistic themes. He cited Pic on meaningless words possessing magical force and reported hearing learned men utter magical sounds (Ravarone, Hur, Asmobias, Mebrake, Geballa, Olune) without recognizable meaning in any language, yet wielding occult power. John Dee (1527–1618), "cabalist up to the ears" according to biographer Meric Casaubon, forged his own kabbalah. His *Monas hierogliphica* (1564), "mathematically, magically, cabbalisticalistically and anagogically explained," distinguished real (ontological) from vulgar (grammatical) kabbalah. Inspired likely by Postel, Dee derived all letters from *Iod* and *Chirek*. He attacked the Jewish cabaliste claiming notaricum and *Tzyruph* belong solely to Hebrew: "God's benevolence did not will that any should ignore kabbalah through language-ignorance." The mystical letters of Hebrews, Greeks, and Latins were given by God alone, produced from points, straight lines, and circles with admirable artifice. Wenceslaus Budowez, Baron of Budowa (1551–1621), a Czech writer, published *Circulus horologii lunaris et solaris* (Hanover, 1616), deeming kabbalah as dangerous as Enthusiast doctrines. Yet he praised Dee, whom he encountered in Prague and whose expulsion he witnessed (at the Nuncio's request) after Dee announced Christianity's imminent reformation, Constantinople's and Rome's fall. ## Late Sixteenth-Century Multiplications By 1583, Henry Howard, Earl of Northampton (c. 1539–1614), in *Defence contre le poison des Pseudo-prophéties*, distinguished valid kabbalah (method for expounding Scripture) from divination by letter-transposition, likely influenced by Reuchlin. Reginald Scott (c. 1538–1599) attacked kabbalah in *The Discovery of Witchcraft* (1584), opposing Bodin's *Démonomanie*, condemning it as magical and papistic superstition permitting words to create substances mimicking God. Henry Smith (c. 1550–1595), in a sermon circa 1590, cited Rabbi Hacadosh on the Messiah's birth—not from the *De verbo mirifico* as scholars claimed, but the *Gale Razeia* dispersed by Galatin. Samuel Purchas and Theseus Ambrosius reproduced kabbalistic alphabets; Thomas Nash recycled Pictorius's *Demonologia* (Basel, 1562), citing Rabbi Hama on protective amulets. John Rainolds attacked Galatin for raving kabbalist metempsychosis, noting Elias Levita stated kabbalists derived theory from Rabbins. ## Systematic Engagement: Ainsworth and Alabaster Henry Ainsworth (1571–1622), a Puritan exegete in Amsterdam, published *Annotations on the Pentateuch* (from 1616), citing the Zohar, Recanati, *Pirke Rabbi Eliezer*, and the *Fascicule* extensively. He cited them to illumine Moses's ordinances and demonstrate that Hebrew doctors approved the New Testament and condemned themselves. He remarked that Rabbi Menahem Recanati noted the thrice-mentioned divine name in Numbers VI, 24, each with different Hebrew accentuation implying the Trinity's mystery. William Alabaster (1567–1640), "Christopher Columbus of spiritual exegesis," served Essex as chaplain, converted to Catholicism in Spain, then reversed course after Roman imprisonment. His titles astound: *Commentary on the Apocalyptic Beast*, *Behold the Bridegroom Comes*, *Opening of the Trumpets or Fountain of Spiritualities*. He expounded classical kabbalistic methods but acknowledged their limitations; only his method revealed mystical and prophetic sense. In 1635, he claimed illumination in 1633 that the defined time approached when divine wisdom's treasures would open and Christ's messenger arrive. He superseded Georg Weigenmeier, a Tübingen Hebrew professor, who published *Gan Meir* (1592). Bonfrère condemned Alabaster; Father Kircher reproduced his exegeses repeatedly in the *Oedipus Ægyptiacus*, notably on Abraham's name decomposed into mystical meanings.
