English Christian kabbalah developed belatedly, as Secret suggests through the epithet "tardif" (tardy), yet the nation's early engagement reveals significant intellectual sophistication. John Colet (1466–1519), who spent two formative years in Italy and moved in Florence's humanist circles, drew on Pic's Heptaplus for an explicatio mundi (explication of creation) undertaken at a patron's request. In his commentary on Dionysius's Ecclesiastical Hierarchies, Colet incorporated the entire passage from Pic's Apologia concerning the double law delivered to Moses and the seventy books of Ezra—a kabbalistic argument central to Christian apologetics. Yet despite hosting Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa in 1510 and collaborating with him on Pauline epistles, Colet resisted deeper kabbalistic engagement. His 1517 letter to John Fisher, addressing the De arte cabalistica that Erasmus had sent to Fisher, exposes his fundamental discomfort: "I dare not judge. I know my ignorance and blindness in such abstruse matters... many things seemed greater miracles in words than in reality, doubtless because I know not the mysteries hidden in Hebrew words, their characters and combinations. In my view, we shall obtain salvation only through ardent love and imitation of Jesus Christ." Colet's honest confession—acknowledging his ignorance of Hebrew mysticism while ultimately privileging christological devotion over hermeneutical erudition—captures a persistent English intellectual stance: respectful distance from kabbalah's claims.

John Fisher (1459–1535), to whom Reuchlin dedicated his De arte cabalistica, adopted a more receptive posture. In a 1521 sermon condemning Luther's attacks on ecclesiastical tradition, Fisher invoked kabbalah explicitly as a non-written tradition transmitted by prophets—a claim he repeated twice for emphasis. In defending the sacrament of holy orders against Lutheran assault, Fisher deployed Galatin's citation of Rabbi Simon ben Yohai on the sacrificial offering of bread and wine continuing eternally in heaven, constructing elaborate parallels between Hebrew mystical texts and Christian sacramental theology to demonstrate continuity between Jewish and Christian worship.

Mid-sixteenth-century English scholarship multiplied kabbalistic references, though often obliquely. Thomas More translated the biography of Pic written by Pic's nephew, yet the first explicit English-language mention of kabbalah appears, intriguingly, in a Frenchman's work: Giles du Guez, preceptor to Princess Mary and librarian under Henry VII and VIII, wrote in 1532 in L'éclaircissement de la langue francaise that kabbalists posit fifty gates of understanding—all except one accessible to Moses, who achieved comprehension through forty-nine gates because direct unmediated knowledge of God remains impossible; mortals access divine wisdom only through understanding God's operations (posterior rather than direct knowledge). Du Guez's familiarity with Reuchlin's systematics marks the cosmopolitan reach of Christian kabbalistic discourse. Hebraist recruitment intensified: Paul Fagius arrived only to die shortly thereafter; J. Emmanuel Tremellius (1510–1586), an Italian Reformation convert, remained lukewarm toward kabbalah, yet his contemporary Matthew Parker composed Cabala seu scrinia sacra (1591), treating kabbalah as sacred treasury. A. R. Chevallier (1523–1572), Vatable's student, taught both J. Drusius and Henry Broughton (1549–1612), an exegete dedicated to Jewish conversion who wrote Des années jubilaires contre l'attente du Messie and extensively cited the Zohar and Menahem de Recanati's interpretations of Christ prefigured in Melchizedek.

The magical current within English kabbalah found expression in Everard Digby, a Ramist opponent, whose Theoria analytica (1579) demonstrates familiarity with Agrippa, Trithème, Postel, and Reuchlin's kabbalistic frameworks. Digby reports Pic's claim that seemingly meaningless words nonetheless possess magical force and recounts hearing learned practitioners utter magical incantations (Ravarone, Hur, Asmobias, Mebrake, Geballa, Olune) incomprehensible in any human language yet wielding occult efficacy. John Dee (1527–1618), whom his biographer Meric Casaubon characterized as "cabalist up to the ears," forged an original synthesis in his Monas hierogliphica (1564), subtitled "mathematically, magically, cabbalisticalistically and anagogically explained." Dee's work distinguishes real (ontological) from vulgar (grammatical) kabbalah, deriving all letters from Iod and Chirek (the yod and the vowel point i) in a systematic theology of alphabetic emanation. Inspired likely by Postel, Dee attacked the parochialism of Jewish kabbalists claiming notaricon and tzyruph (letter-permutation) as exclusively Hebrew phenomena: "God's benevolence did not will that any should ignore kabbalah through language-ignorance. The mystical letters of Hebrews, Greeks, and Latins were given by God alone, produced from points, straight lines, and circles with admirable artifice." This universalizing impulse—kabbalah as supralingustical system transcending Hebrew particularity—characterizes English kabbalists' theoretical ambition. Wenceslaus Budowez, Baron of Budowa (1551–1621), a Czech writer, published Circulus horologii lunaris et solaris (Hanover, 1616), condemning kabbalah as dangerous as Enthusiast doctrines; yet he praised Dee, whom he encountered in Prague and whose expulsion he witnessed (at the papal Nuncio's insistence) following Dee's proclamation of Christianity's imminent reformation and the fall of Constantinople and Rome.

By 1583, Henry Howard, Earl of Northampton (c. 1539–1614), distinguished in Defence contre le poison des Pseudo-prophéties between valid kabbalah (legitimate scriptural exegesis) and divination through letter-transposition, positions likely influenced by Reuchlin's systematic approach. Reginald Scott (c. 1538–1599), by contrast, mounted a fierce attack on kabbalah in The Discovery of Witchcraft (1584), opposing Bodin's framework and condemning kabbalah as magical and papistic superstition permitting words to create substances and effects as if humans could rival God's creative power. Henry Smith (c. 1550–1595), in a sermon around 1590, cited Rabbi Hacadosh on the Messiah's birth, drawing not from Reuchlin's De verbo mirifico (as some scholars incorrectly claimed) but from the Gale Razeia dispersed through Galatin's compilations. Samuel Purchas and Theseus Ambrosius reproduced kabbalistic alphabets in their encyclopedic collections; Thomas Nash recycled Pictorius's Demonologia (Basel, 1562), citing Rabbi Hama on protective amulets. John Rainolds attacked Galatin's enthusiastic promotion of kabbalist doctrine on metempsychosis, citing Elias Levita's own acknowledgment that kabbalists had derived their theories from rabbinical authorities.

Henry Ainsworth (1571–1622), a Puritan exegete working in Amsterdam, published his Annotations on the Pentateuch (from 1616 onward) as a systematic deployment of kabbalistic sources including the Zohar, Recanati, Pirke Rabbi Eliezer, and the Fascicule. Ainsworth marshaled these sources to illuminate the Mosaic ordinances and demonstrate that Hebrew doctors themselves approved the New Testament and condemned their own people—a polemical reversal typical of Christian kabbalah. He noted particularly that Rabbi Menahem Recanati had observed the thrice-occurrence of the divine name in Numbers VI:24–26, each instance bearing different Hebrew accentuation implying the Trinity's mystical presence in Torah itself. William Alabaster (1567–1640), whom scholars hailed as "Christopher Columbus of spiritual exegesis," served the Earl of Essex as chaplain, converted to Catholicism in Spain, underwent imprisonment in Rome, and subsequently reversed his conversion—a dramatic trajectory reflected in works of stunning titles: Commentary on the Apocalyptic Beast, Behold the Bridegroom Comes, Opening of the Trumpets or Fountain of Spiritualities. Alabaster expounded classical kabbalistic methods while claiming their limitations; only his unique method, he insisted, revealed mystical and prophetic truths. In 1635, he claimed illumination received in 1633 confirming that the appointed time approached when divine wisdom's treasures would open and Christ's messenger arrive. Alabaster's systematization superseded Georg Weigenmeier, a Tübingen Hebrew professor, who had published Gan Meir (1592). Though Bonfrère condemned Alabaster's exegetical audacity, Father Kircher repeatedly reproduced his interpretations in Oedipus Ægyptiacus, notably Alabaster's decomposition of Abraham's name into mystical meanings revealing cosmic principles.
