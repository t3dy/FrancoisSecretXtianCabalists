Joannes Buraeus (1568-1652), Sweden's royal secretary and antiquary, published eschatological placards including *The Sound of Elijah's Trumpet* (1616) and *A Table of Emerald Cherubinistic Chronology* (1639), culminating in *A Philosophy of the Hebrews* (1641) and *The Roaring of the Northern Lion* (1644). His predictions of apocalyptic termini (May 5, 1647; subsequently 1674) led him to distribute property to the poor; when the prophecies failed, he sought support from Queen Christina. Julius Sperber, later a Rosicrucian, published *Kabbalistic Prayers or Glorifications of the Divine Name* (Magdeburg, 1600), deriving from biblical and Davidic sources, amplifying traditions from Reuchlin, Giustiniani, and Guy Le Fèvre de la Boderie. Christophorus Helvicus (1581-1616), whose commentaries appear in the *Critici sacri* collection, spoke Hebrew natively and at twenty taught Greek and Hebrew. In 1613 the Landgrave tasked him with examining books confiscated from Frankfurt's Jews. This Lutheran employed Christian kabbalistes (Capnion, Galatin) while cautioning that Messianic kabbalah questions remain obscure in symbol, citing Midrash Canticles passages revealing Christ's propitiatory role through Kabbalistic word-play. André Rivet (1573-1651), a strict Calvinist from Daneau's school, teaching at Leiden from 1620, expressed dismay that learned, pious men like Reuchlin could "not only believe but recommend such fantasies damaging our cause against enemies." His *Theological Exercises* (1633) document exegesis of *bara* (created), *Elohim* (God), Messianic year-calculations from *Beresith*, and the angel Raziel legend, yet finds these "fantaisies" unworthy of credence. Johann Buxtorf (1564-1629), establishing scientific foundations for rabbinical studies among Christians, demonstrated interest in kabbalah from his *Synagoge judaica* (German 1603, Latin 1604). Citing Bechai's commentaries, he explains Genesis 21:8 through kabbalistic midrash: ignoring consonantal divisions (*He-gimel-mem-lamed*), the rabbis read *Beiom hagmal* (eighth day of circumcision), the numerical values of deleted letters yielding the Hebrew word for circumcision. His *Bibliotheca rabbinica* (1613) and *Lexicon chaldaicum* (completed by his son Jean, 1599-1664, published 1639)—a thirty-year effort—accumulate kabbalistic annotations. Buxtorf knew Zohar translations on Genesis and *Bereschith Rabba* excerpts that Postel sent to Oporin. Yet he reserved judgment on the Zohar's antiquity, noting its narratives "smell of novelty" and inviting capable scholars to document anachronisms. Louis Cappel (1586-1658) contested Buxtorf's vocalization-antiquity thesis in *The Secret of Punctuation Revealed* (1624), reviving Elias Levita's theory that the Massoretes of Tiberias invented vowel-points circa the 5th century. His *Critica sacra* (based on theological principles contrary to Protestant colleagues) proved dangerous; Catholic exegetes like Jean Morin (1591-1659), a converted Protestant and Oratorian glory, adopted his views. Morin taught that God ordained unpointed scriptural writing to enforce ecclesiastical authority over private judgment. Joseph Juste Scaliger (1540-1609), educated in Arabic by Postel (whom he said "ran the streets"), observed that the *Zohar*, composed after the Talmud, could not evidence antiquity of vowel-points. Scaliger discovered a *Pugio fidei* manuscript at Toulouse, initially attributing it to Raymond Sebond. Cappel dedicated vigorous polemics against Buxtorf, translating and commenting on *Zohar* Genesis passages, prompting prolonged disputes. Buxtorf's son, Jean (1599-1664), in *On the Antiquity of Hebrew Language* (1644), multiplied Christian and Jewish kabbalistic authorities to prove both the *Zohar*'s age and vowel-point antiquity. Yet in his commentary on the *Cuzari* (1660), Jean admitted: "The doctrine of kabbalah is an inscrutable abyss, an inextricable labyrinth without entrance or exit, bearing little fruit—so much so that when asked his opinion of kabbalah, someone replied it was the same as fable."
