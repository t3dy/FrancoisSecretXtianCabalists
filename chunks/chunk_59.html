Joannes Buraeus (1568–1652), serving as Sweden's royal secretary and antiquary, produced an extensive body of eschatological works including the alarming The Sound of Elijah's Trumpet (1616) and A Table of Emerald Cherubinistic Chronology (1639), culminating in his massive systematic treatises A Philosophy of the Hebrews (1641) and The Roaring of the Northern Lion (1644). Buraeus generated increasingly precise predictions of apocalyptic termini, initially specifying May 5, 1647, and subsequently revising to 1674. His prophetic conviction led him to distribute his property to the poor in preparation for imminent cosmic transformation; when the appointed dates passed without the anticipated cosmic rupture, he sought support from Queen Christina to maintain his reputation and financial security. His example illustrates the risks and institutional vulnerabilities of prophetic claims grounded in Hebrew chronology and kabbalistic numerology during an era when astronomical and chronological studies were increasingly subject to empirical verification.

Julius Sperber, who subsequently affiliated with Rosicrucian circles, published Kabbalistic Prayers or Glorifications of the Divine Name (Magdeburg, 1600), deriving these invocations from biblical and Davidic sources while amplifying traditions inherited from Reuchlin, Giustiniani, and Guy Le Fèvre de la Boderie. Christophorus Helvicus (1581–1616), whose commentaries appear throughout the prestigious Critici sacri collection and who spoke Hebrew as a native language from childhood, assumed teaching positions in Greek and Hebrew at age twenty with remarkable precocity. In 1613 the Landgrave commissioned Helvicus with the delicate task of examining books confiscated from Frankfurt's Jewish community. This Lutheran scholar demonstrates characteristic balance in his approach: he freely employs Christian kabbaliste authorities (Capnion and Galatin) in his exegetical work while maintaining cautious epistemic reserve regarding Messianic kabbalah, whose symbolic obscurities he illustrates through Midrash passages that reveal Christ's propitiatory role through kabbalistic wordplay. This balanced stance—engaging kabbalistic methods while questioning their theological conclusions—characterizes sophisticated Protestant engagement with kabbalah.

André Rivet (1573–1651), a strict Calvinist educated within the rigorous school of Daneau and teaching at Leiden from 1620 onward, articulated dismay that erudite and genuinely pious men including Reuchlin had succumbed to what he considered palpable fantasies regarding kabbalah, fantasies that moreover damaged Christianity's credibility against hostile enemies. His Theological Exercises (1633) systematically documents exegetical materials derived from kabbalistic sources: discussion of bara (created) and Elohim (God), Messianic calculations drawn from Bereshith, and legends concerning the angel Raziel. Yet Rivet's consistent judgment condemns these materials as unworthy of credence, mere "fantaisies" lacking legitimate theological substance. His skepticism represents the emerging Reformed Protestant position that kabbalistic claims, whatever their intellectual sophistication, fail to meet evidential standards for theological acceptance.

Johann Buxtorf (1564–1629) fundamentally transformed Christian engagement with Hebrew and rabbinic texts, establishing genuinely scientific foundations for rabbinical studies among Christian scholars through decades of careful philological work. He demonstrates sustained engagement with kabbalah beginning from his Synagoge judaica (German edition 1603, Latin 1604), where he cites Bechai's commentaries to explicate Genesis 21:8 through kabbalistic interpretive technique: the rabbis ignore consonantal divisions in He-gimel-mem-lamed and instead read Beiom hagmal (eighth day of circumcision), with the numerical values of deleted letters yielding the Hebrew word for circumcision itself. His monumental Bibliotheca rabbinica (1613) and the lexicographical masterwork Lexicon chaldaicum (completed by his son Jean, 1599–1664, published 1639)—representing thirty years of accumulated labor—contain extensive kabbalistic annotations integrated throughout their articles. Buxtorf accessed Zohar translations on Genesis and excerpts from Bereschith Rabba that Postel had transmitted to the printer Oporin. Yet Buxtorf exercised significant epistemic caution regarding the Zohar's antiquity, observing that its narratives "smell of novelty" and extending an invitation to capable scholars to document the anachronisms that he suspected riddled the text. This judicious combination of engagement with kabbalistic materials and critical evaluation represents genuine scholarly methodology.

Louis Cappel (1586–1658) challenged Buxtorf's foundational thesis regarding the antiquity of Hebrew vocalization in his polemical The Secret of Punctuation Revealed (1624), reviving the medieval theory articulated by Elias Levita that the Massoretes of Tiberias invented the vowel-point system circa the fifth century C.E., thereby undermining the assumption that Hebrew vocalization represented an ancient authoritative guide to scriptural meaning. His massive Critica sacra, grounded in theological principles diverging from those of his Protestant colleagues, proved theologically dangerous and attracted the influence of Catholic scholars including Jean Morin (1591–1659), a converted Protestant and celebrated Oratorian, who adopted Cappel's thesis. Morin articulated the theological consequence of Cappel's position: God ordained unpointed scriptural writing precisely to enforce ecclesiastical authority over private scriptural judgment. Joseph Juste Scaliger (1540–1609), educated in Arabic by Postel (whom he dismissively recalled "ran the streets"), observed that the Zohar, demonstrably composed after the Talmud, could not possibly evidence the antiquity of vowel-points. Scaliger discovered a Pugio fidei manuscript at Toulouse, initially attributing it to Raymond Sebond before recognizing the work's actual provenance. Cappel directed vigorous polemics against Buxtorf, translating and commenting on Zohar Genesis passages, stimulating prolonged scholarly disputes. Buxtorf's son, Jean (1599–1664), responded through On the Antiquity of Hebrew Language (1644), multiplying Christian and Jewish kabbalistic authorities to prove both the Zohar's antiquity and the vowel-points' primitive origin. Yet Jean's own later commentary on the Cuzari (1660) reveals profound intellectual uncertainty, containing the remarkable admission: "The doctrine of kabbalah is an inscrutable abyss, an inextricable labyrinth without entrance or exit, bearing little fruit—so much so that when asked his opinion of kabbalah, someone replied it was the same as fable." This confession from a scholar deeply engaged with kabbalistic sources testifies to kabbalah's fundamental obscurity and the difficulty of constructing coherent theological positions from its fragmented materials.
