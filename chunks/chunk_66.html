Chunk 66

Pages 205-205

This final chunk completes Secret's extensive *Index des noms propres* (Index of Proper Names), providing the concluding alphabetical entries spanning from S through Z and completing what has been a comprehensive prosopographical directory of all figures mentioned throughout the monograph. The index entries in this final section include important figures like *Stancari* (Reformation theologian), *Stapleton* (Catholic controversial writer), and concludes with figures like *Vigenère*, whose work on cabalistic hermeneutics and cipher systems proved foundational to later Christian kabbalist practice; *Vulliaud*, a 20th-century specialist on Kabbalah writing in the period shortly after Secret's own monograph; *Widmanstetter*, a Renaissance manuscript collector and Hebraist whose assembled collections became crucial repositories of rare Hebrew and cabalistic texts; and *Zwinger*, representing the Renaissance polymath tradition of universal learning that connected humanist philology, theological exegesis, and occult philosophy. The concluding entries thus span from major figures central to Secret's narrative to minor scholarly personalities whose appearance in one or two references nonetheless contributes to the documentary totality that the index represents. The final substantive lines of the text are followed by the publication colophon, which establishes the work's material and institutional provenance: "IMPRIMERIE NOUVELLE, ORLÉANS" (New Printing House, Orleans), along with standard publication information—print run details, the legal deposit number (4384) for the fourth trimester of 1963, and the statement "Imprimé en France" (Printed in France). This bibliographic information places the original publication of Secret's *Les kabbalistes chrétiens de la Renaissance* in November or December 1963, printed in Orleans through the facilities of the New Printing House. The work's publication by Éditions Dunod (a major French academic and technical publisher) underscores the work's intended scholarly audience and its position within French academic publishing, reflecting the particular strength of French scholarship on Renaissance intellectual history and occult learning in the postwar period. The index as a complete entity, spanning pages 200-205 and encompassing approximately 400-500 distinct individuals, documents the extraordinarily dense network of Renaissance and early modern scholars, clerics, mystics, Hebrew linguists, manuscript printers, and cabalists whom Secret has woven into his narrative of Christian engagement with Kabbalah. The index serves simultaneously as a practical research tool—allowing readers to locate specific personages and trace their role within Secret's argument—and as an implicit argument in itself about the scope, scale, and institutional embeddedness of Christian Kabbalah as a historical phenomenon. The alphabetical listing makes visible the geographic distribution of these figures: they span from Spain (conversos, Inquisitorial officials), Italy (humanists, printers, bishops), France (cardinals, royal officials, cathedral scholars), the Low Countries (printers, Louvain theologians), the German-speaking territories (Reformers, mystics, Hebraists), and extending into Poland and Bohemia. The index thus testifies to Secret's comprehensive research across manuscript collections, printed editions in multiple languages and locations, and an array of secondary scholarship spanning centuries and continents. For a work whose central claim is that Christian Kabbalah has been profoundly under-studied in modern scholarship, the comprehensiveness of the index itself constitutes part of the work's argumentative force—demonstrating through exhaustive documentation the sheer scale of the phenomenon that previous historiography had treated as marginal, superstitious, or merely fashionable. The colophon's identification of Orleans as the printing location and 1963 as the publication date situates Secret's monograph at a particular moment in postwar European intellectual life, when French scholarship was reasserting its leadership in the study of Renaissance and early modern occult and mystical traditions. The work thus becomes not only a scholarly monograph but also a material artifact reflecting the institutional and geographic contexts through which humanistic learning about the European intellectual past was being produced and disseminated in mid-20th-century France.
