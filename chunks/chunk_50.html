Secret demonstrates that Protestant engagement with Christian Kabbalah was far more heterogeneous than Calvin's wholesale condemnation might suggest. While systematic rejection coexisted with serious scholarly investigation, Basel emerged during this period as a rival to Venice as a center of kabbalistic interest among Protestant humanists and printers—the Froben, Amerbach, Oporin, Pellican, and Buxtorf families establishing Basel as a nexus of Hebrew learning and mystical scholarship. This shift reflects the growing Protestant investment in Hebrew texts and Jewish learning as sources of scriptural authenticity, even as doctrinal tensions remained unresolved.

Joannes Pistorius (1546-1608) epitomizes the ambivalent and ultimately paradoxical Protestant engagement with kabbalah. Though Blau classified him as Protestant initially, his trajectory reveals the confessional instability of the period: he eventually returned to Catholicism and was ordained a priest in 1591, suggesting that kabbalistic interests cut across conventional sectarian boundaries. His monumental De artis cabalisticæ (1587)—rightly called the "Bible of Christian Kabbalah" by scholars—republished Reuchlin and Paul Ricius (both on the Index of Trent), alongside Léon Hébreu's Dialoghi d'amore and Archangelus of Burgonovo's commentaries on Pico. Pistorius explicitly framed his project as correcting previous "inept" or corrupted presentations of kabbalah while carefully excluding writings that contained what he dismissed as "foolishness" or departures from Christian faith. His intended second volume, dedicated to presenting Jewish kabbalah on its own terms, never appeared—likely suppressed by Catholic censure, indicating institutional limitations on how far even sympathetic scholars could pursue kabbalistic inquiry.

Yet Basel's intellectual culture included other kabbalistic champions alongside critics like Andreas Libavius, who viciously attacked Pistorius as a charlatan uniting with Platonists and cabalists in defending worthless superstition. Jacques Curion (1557), influenced by Paracelsian thought, praised the kabbalistic "river of gold" in mystical terms, invoking the seventy books of Esdras and positioning kabbalah within visionary and prophetic literature. Sebastien Lepusculus examined Zohar passages discussing the Trinity, arguing defensively that Jewish Talmudists were inferior only to Christian cabalists in theological sophistication—a rhetorical move that preserved Christian superiority while granting kabbalah some philosophical standing. The Basel circle thus displayed considerable internal diversity, from outright rejection to cautious engagement to enthusiastic endorsement, reflecting broader tensions in how Protestant humanists negotiated Jewish learning.

The section catalogs a diverse intellectual network of kabbalistic practitioners in Basel and Switzerland. Martinus Borrhaus (Cellarius), a former Anabaptist seeking theological respectability, approved cabalists' claims about heavenly templates and divine blueprints governing material creation. Antonius Reuchlinus (Reuchlin's own nephew), drawing strategically on Galatin's Tetragrammatic exegesis, sought to integrate kabbalistic letter-mysticism into Christian theology. Particularly significant were Italian émigrés in Switzerland—religious refugees whose relocation brought Italian humanist approaches to kabbalah into Protestant contexts. Hieronymus Zanchius (1516-1590), a Latran canon who fled Italy for religious reasons, cited Reuchlin and Galatin cautiously in his theological works, accepting certain kabbalistic mysteries while deeming other interpretations "forced and puerile"—a compromise position that incorporated mystical depth without abandoning critical judgment.

The narrative culminates with two major figures whose synthesis proved especially influential: Pietro Martire Vermigli (1500-1562) and Joannes Guilhelmus Stuckius (1542-1607). Vermigli, a Hebraicist trained under a Jewish physician, developed a sophisticated understanding of kabbalah as legitimate oral transmission of wisdom—a definition grounded directly in the Torah's concept of father-to-son transmission of interpretative authority. His Genesis commentary interprets the addition of He (from the Tetragramme) to Abraham's name as signifying regenerated humans' mystical participation in divine nature—a thoroughly kabbalistic reading integrated into Christian anthropology. His exegesis of marital harmony using the letters Iod and He (representing man and woman in kabbalistic symbolism) demonstrates how Christian theology could adopt and domesticate kabbalistic symbolic methods while maintaining doctrinal orthodoxy.

Stuckius, who succeeded Bibliander at Zurich and thus inherited the center's Hebraist traditions, synthesized diverse kabbalistic and Christian sources into a more comprehensive framework. His Angeli (1595) cites Galatin and Rabbi Nehemias on the Trinity hidden in divine names and the principle of divine unity expressed through the formulas Ab (Father), Ben (Son), and Ruah (Spirit). Stuckius presents kabbalah not as Jewish superstition but as ancient wisdom fully compatible with Christian revelation—a positioning that allowed Protestant scholars to claim superior understanding of kabbalah's true meaning while simultaneously appropriating it for Christian theological purposes. This rhetorical move—the claim that Christian scholars understood authentic kabbalah better than Jews themselves—becomes increasingly common in later Protestant engagement with mystical Judaism.
