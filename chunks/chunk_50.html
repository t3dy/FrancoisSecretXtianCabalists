Secret explores the heterogeneous Protestant responses to Christian Kabbalah, revealing that blanket condemnation coexisted with serious scholarly investigation. The analysis shifts focus to Basel, which rivaled Venice as a center of kabbalistic interest among Protestant humanists and printers (Froben, Amerbach, Oporin, Pellican, Buxtorf). Joannes Pistorius (1546-1608) exemplifies the ambivalent Protestant position. Though Blau classified him as Protestant, he eventually returned to Catholicism and was ordained priest in 1591. His monumental *De artis cabalisticæ* (1587)—a "Bible of Christian Kabbalah"—republished Reuchlin and Paul Ricius (both on the Index of Trent), alongside Léon Hébreu's *Dialoghi d'amore* and Archangelus of Burgonovo's commentaries on Pico. His stated goal was correcting previous "inept" or corrupted presentations of kabbalah while excluding writings containing "foolishness" or departures from Christian faith. The second volume, intended for Jewish kabbalah, never appeared, likely due to Catholic censure. Andreas Libavius attacked Pistorius as a charlatan uniting with Platonists and cabalists in defending worthless superstition. Yet other Basel figures championed kabbalah: Jacques Curion (1557), a Paracelsian, praised the kabbalistic "river of gold," invoking the seventy books of Esdras. Sebastien Lepusculus cited Zohar passages about the Trinity, defending Jewish Talmudists as inferior only to Christian cabalists in theological sophistication. The section catalogs a diverse circle: Martinus Borrhaus (Cellarius), an ex-Anabaptist who approved cabalists' claims about heavenly templates; Antonius Reuchlinus (Reuchlin's nephew), who drew on Galatin's Tetragrammatic exegesis; and importantly, Italian émigrés in Switzerland. Hieronymus Zanchius (1516-1590), a Latran canon who fled Italy, cited Reuchlin and Galatin cautiously, accepting some kabbalistic mystery while deeming certain interpretations "forced and puerile." The narrative culminates with two major figures: Pietro Martire Vermigli (1500-1562) and Joannes Guilhelmus Stuckius (1542-1607). Vermigli, a Hebraicist trained under a Jewish physician, viewed kabbalah as legitimate oral transmission of wisdom from father to son—a definition rooted in the Torah itself. His commentary on Genesis interprets the addition of *He* (from the Tetragramme) to Abraham's name as indicating regenerated humans' participation in divine nature; his exegesis of marital harmony using the letters *Iod* and *He* (man and woman) exemplifies kabbalistic symbolism applied to Christian anthropology. Stuckius, who succeeded Bibliander at Zurich, synthesized diverse sources. His *Angeli* (1595) cites Galatin and Rabbi Nehemias on the Trinity hidden in divine names and the principle of divine unity (*Ab*, Father; *Ben*, Son; *Ruah*, Spirit), presenting kabbalah not as Jewish superstition but as ancient wisdom compatible with Christian revelation.
