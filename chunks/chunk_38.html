The Collège Trilingue of Paris, as the premier institution for Renaissance Hebrew studies north of the Alps, inevitably became the crucible wherein Christian kabbalah faced rigorous scrutiny. Jean Mercier (d. 1570), who succeeded François Vatable as the college's chief Hebraist, exemplifies the complex trajectory of French academic engagement with the kabbalah. In his earlier phase, Mercier displayed genuine enthusiasm for kabbalistic method and symbolism, publishing an edition of Horapollon's *Hieroglyphes* (1551) that connected Egyptian symbolic systems with Hebrew letters, and composing his *Libellus de abbreviaturis Hebræorum* (1561) as a careful technical documentation of kabbalistic procedures: notaricon (acrostic decomposition), athbash (letter transposition), and ginat (numerical equivalence). Mercier freely references Reuchlin and Chéradame, cites specific gematria equations (Zemach and Menachem both summing to 148), and acknowledges the sephirothic structure—all marks of serious engagement. Yet his hedging language—deferring fuller treatment to other works, avoiding definitive claims—signals underlying discomfort even in this early period. The publication of Mercier's *Commentary on Genesis* (posthumously in Geneva, 1598, under the editorship of Theodore of Beza) reveals a dramatic intellectual shift. The commentary bristles with warnings against "allegories strange and inept," insisting repeatedly on interpretative sobriety and fidelity to the literal sense. Mercier now mocks Hebrew exegetes who descry traces of metempsychosis (*Gilgul hanephasoth*, the transmigration of souls) encoded in the letters of Adam's name, and he cites the *Fascicule of Myrrhe*—a central kabbalistic text—only to dismiss its numerical equivalences as mere "reveries" unworthy of serious consideration. Theodore of Beza, who prepared Mercier's work for publication and who harbored no sympathy for Postel's Christian kabbalistic vision, sharpened this critical edge in the *Thesaurus*'s glossary, explicitly distinguishing between ancient Hebrew tradition (legitimate) and the "modern kabbalists" who corrupted it through "curious calculations and superstitious transmutations" patterned on Pythagorean occultism. This retrospective reframing—ancient good, modern corrupted—became a standard defensive move among skeptics seeking to preserve some residual respect for Hebrew antiquity while condemning contemporary kabbalists. The resistance to kabbalah varied considerably across regions and professional contexts. Petrus Martinus (also known as Petrus Martinez), a Navarrese Hebrew professor at La Rochelle who counted both Mercier and Génébrard among his teachers, exhibited contemptuous dismissal. His *Grammatica hebræa* (1580) treats the Mem clausum (the unusually shaped closed Mem of Isaiah 9:6) as a paleographic accident—a scribe's error—rather than a mystical sign encoding Messianic secrets, and he ridicules the entire notion that orthographic quirks might conceal hidden doctrinal truths. Yet beyond the academy's strict walls, engagement continued: Philippe Du Plessis-Mornay (1549–1623), an influential Protestant polemicist, incorporated Christian kabbalistic arguments into his *Traité de la vérité de la religion chrétienne* (1581), drawing on Pic, Reuchlin, and Galatin to defend the Trinity through Hebrew divine names and their numerological properties. His later *Advertissement aux Juifs* (1607) sustains this kabbalistic apologetics, though without the scholarly apparatus Mercier had marshaled, relying instead on Christian tradition and rhetorical force. Jean Bodin (1530–1596), the towering political theorist and polymathic intellectual, crystallizes the period's ambivalence toward kabbalah. Bodin acknowledged his indebtedness to Hebrew sources filtered through intermediaries like Cingarbres and Mercier, and his *Démonomanie des sorciers* launches a famous indictment of Pic, Reuchlin, and Agrippa for collapsing the boundaries between legitimate kabbalah and magical practice. Crucially, Bodin introduces a categorical distinction—*Bereshith kabbalah* (the legitimate kabbalah concerned with creation's order and natural philosophy) versus *Mercava kabbalah* (the mystical gnosis of the Chariot-throne, inherently perilous and prone to abuse). He accords the Zohar a measure of respect without ever quoting it directly, maintaining intellectual distance while acknowledging merit. Yet in the *Heptaplomères*, Bodin's Jewish interlocutor Salomon launches a devastating deconstruction of both Jewish and Christian kabbalistic claims. Salomon exposes Galatin's cherished Zohar passage on purgation as an interpolation; he demonstrates that the 12-letter and 42-letter divine name exegeses are modern inventions rather than ancient traditions; and he shows that Christian attempts to derive the Trinity from the equivalence of *Secel, Maskil, Muskal* (understanding, contemplation, knowledge) commit a category error by conflating accidental properties with substantial realities. Bodin's ultimate position—that while early Hebrew authorities like Philo, Leone Ebreo, and Origen pursued legitimate allegorical exegesis, later Jews descended into sophistry concerning syllables and vowel points, and Christians prudently should eschew the temptation to follow them—reflects the measured skepticism of the sophisticated humanist. The broader French intellectual landscape reveals considerable heterogeneity of approach. Petrus Costus (1554) deployed kabbalistic arguments to defend Christian Messianism; Barthélemy du Poix (1559) acknowledged the oral Torah (*torah scebealpe*) as the transmitted kabbalah; Guillaume de la Perrière (1555) recorded an intriguing anecdote of Avignon rabbis teaching kabbalah to Christians for payment, suggesting economic interest in transmission; and J. C. Scaliger (fl. 1484–1558) composed satires warning against "the deliria of kabbalists." This variegated panorama—ranging from Vigenère's systematic synthesis through Mercier's cooling skepticism to Bodin's philosophical critique—confirms that by the second half of the sixteenth century, Christian kabbalah had achieved the status of an established intellectual phenomenon in France, provoking both passionate advocacy and rigorous resistance from the educated elite.
