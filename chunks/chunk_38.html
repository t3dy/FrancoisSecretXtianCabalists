## The Parisian Hebraists: Mercier and Institutional Skepticism The Collège Trilingue of Paris produced numerous Hebraists confronting the kabbalistic question. Jean Mercier (d. 1570), successor to Vatable, initially embraced cabalist symbolism and published an edition of Horapollon's *Hieroglyphes* (1551). His *Libellus de abbreviaturis Hebræorum* (1561) carefully documents kabbalist procedures—notaricon, athbash, ginat—with references to Reuchlin and Chéradame. He cites gematria examples (Zemach/Menachem both equal 148) and the sephiroth, though hedges by deferring fuller treatment elsewhere. By his *Commentary on Genesis* (published posthumously in Geneva, 1598, edited by Theodore of Beza), Mercier has cooled considerably. He frequently interjects warnings against "allegories strange and inept," insisting on soberness. He mocks the Hebrew exegetes who find metempsychosis (*Gilgul hanephasoth*) in Adam's letters, and he cites the *Fascicule of Myrrhe* only to dismiss numerical equivalences as "reveries." Theodore of Beza, Mercier's editor and no friend of Postel, distinguishes in the *Thesaurus* glossary between ancient Hebrew tradition and the "modern kabbalists" who corrupted it with "curious calculations and superstitious transmutations" in the Pythagorean manner. ## Regional Variants and Individual Hesitations Petrus Martinus (Martinez), a Navarrese professor of Hebrew at La Rochelle and student of Mercier and Génébrard, shows pure disdain for kabbalah. His *Grammatica hebræa* (1580) dismisses the Mem clausum of Isaiah as a scribe's error, not a mystical sign, ridiculing the notion that such orthographic quirks conceal Messianic secrets. Yet Christian figures outside the strict academic guild embraced elements. Philippe Du Plessis-Mornay (1549–1623), a Protestant intellectual, borrowed Christian kabbalistic arguments for his *Traité de la vérité de la religion chrétienne* (1581), citing Pic, Reuchlin, and Galatin on divine names and the Trinity. His *Advertissement aux Juifs* (1607) continues this engagement, though he lacks Mercier's scholarly apparatus. ## Bodin: The Philosopher's Measured Critique Jean Bodin (1530–1596), the great political theorist, embodies ambivalence. He acknowledges learning from Hebrew sources via Cingarbres and Mercier. His *Démonomanie des sorciers* famously attacks Pic, Reuchlin, and Agrippa for conflating kabbalah with magic, distinguishing between *Bereshith kabbalah* (legitimate natural philosophy) and *Mercava kabbalah* (mystical vision of the Chariot—difficult and prone to abuse). He credits Zohar with merit but never quotes it directly. In the *Heptaplomères*, however, Bodin's Jewish interlocutor Salomon subjects both traditional and Christian kabbalah to withering critique. He shows that the Zohar passage on purgation circulated by Galatin is interpolated, that the 12- and 42-letter name exegesis is invented, and that attempts to prove the Trinity through *Secel, Maskil, Muskal* conflate accidents with substances. Bodin's overall stance is that while early Hebrew authorities (Philo, Leon Hebraeus, Origen) pursued legitimate allegory, later Jews pursued sophistry on syllables and points, and Christians should resist this temptation. ## Broader Diffusion Other voices pepper the French scene: Petrus Costus (1554) defends Christian Messianism with kabbalistic support; Barthélemy du Poix (1559) acknowledges the oral Torah (*torah scebealpe*) as transmitted kabbala; Guillaume de la Perrière (1555) records that Avignon rabbis taught kabbalah to Christians for payment; and J. C. Scaliger (fl. 1484–1558) penned satires warning against "the deliria of kabbalists." The overall picture shows that by the second half of the sixteenth century, Christian kabbalah had become a recognized intellectual phenomenon in France, generating both enthusiastic adoption and principled resistance.
