Chunk 19

Pages 59-61

This chunk documents the profound influence of Paul Ricius's *De anima caeli* (Treatise on the Soul of Heaven), a mystical-philosophical work that interprets the ten Sephiroth as a hierarchical system governing cosmic animation and divine action. The passage opens with an elaborate vernacular French poem by Ricius, mapping divine names (O Yah, YHWH, El, Elohim, etc.) to the nine celestial spheres and fundamental principles of creation. The poem demonstrates how Renaissance Christian kabbaliste adapted Hebrew mysticism to Aristotelian cosmology, treating the Sephiroth as instruments through which God moves the heavens and vivifies nature. Secret details how the *De anima caeli* sparked significant theological debate, particularly among Church authorities. Jean Eck lodged objections, prompting Claude d'Espence (1511-1571) to publish a systematic refutation in 1571, drawing on the Hebrew erudition of Gilbert Genebrard. D'Espence was troubled by the concept of a "world soul" (represented as a green line encircling the universe) and whether the Sephiroth could properly explain cosmic animation. Yet even critics like d'Espence acknowledged the work's power to shape theological imagination. The document traces how Ricius's reconciliation of Kabbalah with Aristotelian philosophy influenced subsequent figures, particularly Cesare d'Evoli (1532-1598), whose 1571 *De divinis attributis* (On Divine Attributes) frames the Sephiroth as mediums through which God directs the First Mobile and the planetary spheres. By 1703, Jonas Conrad Schramm was still reprinting Ricius's apologies, embedding his methodology in an *Introduction to the Dialectic of Cabalists According to the Method of the Peripatetics*. Secret emphasizes that Ricius's synthesis—treating Kabbalah as an esoteric philosophy compatible with scholasticism and Platonic ideals—established a durable intellectual framework. Though later thinkers contested particular doctrines, the fundamental apologetic strategy persisted: using Sephirotic symbolism to defend Christian theology while preserving the logical consistency prized by Renaissance humanists.
