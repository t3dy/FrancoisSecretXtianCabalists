**Engraved Portraits and Manuscript Documentation** This section is primarily visual and documentary in character, featuring two significant engraved plates that serve as tangible evidence of Postel's influence and the transmission of his teachings to subsequent generations. The first is a portrait of **Guy Le Fèvre de la Boderie** (identified as Plate X, sourced from Estampes Ed 12b, 418), an estampe or engraved portrait of one of Postel's most accomplished and celebrated disciples. The portrait is accompanied by a verse legend in French that reads: "Revit FEU VERGILE et sa fée / Revit en l'UN QUI GUIDE ORFEE" (Vergil and his Muse live again / Live again in the ONE who guides Orpheus). This lapidary verse cleverly establishes a genealogy of spiritual and linguistic authority: Le Fèvre is positioned as the heir not only to Vergil's classical poetic mastery and his muse-inspired eloquence, but also—through the figure of the "ONE who guides Orpheus"—as the successor to Postel's mystical wisdom and prophetic illumination. The engraved image thus becomes a visual testament to the continuity of tradition from classical antiquity through Renaissance Neoplatonism to Postelian esotericism. The second visual document is a **reproduction of an annotated manuscript page** from Bahya ben Asher's *Commentary on the Pentateuch* (catalogued as B.N. A. 602 in the Bibliothèque Nationale), densely marked with handwritten marginal glosses in Guy Le Fèvre's own hand. These annotations testify to Le Fèvre's patient, learned, and deeply engaged work with Hebrew exegetical tradition, demonstrating his mastery of kabbalistic terminology and his systematic integration of mystical interpretation into the scholarly study of biblical text. The manuscript evidence shows that Postelian Kabbalah was not merely a theoretical system or a collection of literary works, but a living practice of textual engagement, annotation, and transmission through manuscript culture—the ordinary mechanisms by which learning and tradition circulate among scholars in the pre-print and early-print Renaissance. These visual materials collectively serve as **documentary evidence of cultural transmission**: they demonstrate not through abstract argument but through concrete artifact—engraved image and annotated page—how Postel's doctrines were transmitted to his disciples, how they took root in educated minds, and how they were cultivated and elaborated through sustained manuscript study, annotation, and scholarly commentary. **Chronological and Eschatological Significance of 1543** The section moves to interpretive analysis with Secret's striking reframing of the year **1543** as a chronological pivot in sacred history. In that year, Postel published his major work *De orbis terræ concordia* (On the Concord of the World), the text that crystallized his vision of universal reconciliation. Secret cautions against the conventional scholarly gesture of comparing 1543 to the annus mirabilis of 1543 itself—when Copernicus published his *De revolutionibus orbium cælestium* (On the Revolutions of the Celestial Spheres) and Vesalius published his *De corporis humani fabrica* (On the Fabric of the Human Body), both works revolutionizing Renaissance thought by establishing heliocentric astronomy and anatomical precision. Rather than situating Postel's *Concordia* within this triumvirate of foundational modernity, Secret argues that 1543 should be understood through Postelian apocalyptic chronology itself: the year marks the publication and wide dissemination of the **Quran**—an eschatological event that, in Postel's reckoning, signified the imminent **end of Islamic dominion** and the approach of the **Messianic age of Concord** when Islam would be superseded by the recovered Christianity of universal reason. According to **Postelian prophetic calculation**, humanity has entered the threshold age in which **Christ's light will shine seventy times more brightly than it did at the moment of creation recorded in Genesis** (septante fois plus grande lumière que n'a eu au commencement du monde en l'création). This is not a mere historical marker or comparative fact, but the **threshold of universal restoration** where the **Messiah's promised illumination** becomes manifest not in future eschatology but in the **present moment**, calling forth the prophetic mission of those—like Postel himself—who have been illumined by the recovered Kabbalah to usher in the final age. **The School of Postel and Institutional Transmission** The final notation in this chunk indicates that despite the accusations of both **Reformed and Catholic critics** (who alleged that Postel had founded an organized sect of devoted "Postellians"), Postel did not establish a formal institutional structure or organization. Nevertheless, he gathered around himself a circle of **devoted disciples and admirers** who would carry his doctrines forward into the final decades of the sixteenth century and beyond. The subsequent section promises a systematic examination of this **"École de Guillaume Postel"** (School of Guillaume Postel)—understood not as a formal school in the institutional sense, but rather as a network of intellectuals, poets, scholars, and ecclesiastics bound together by shared commitment to Postelian mysticism and Christian Kabbalah. This framework will allow tracing how his mystical vision influenced second and third-generation French intellectuals—figures like the Le Fèvre brothers, Florimond de Raemond, and others—and how their contributions collectively shaped the trajectory of **Christian Kabbalah in France** through the sixteenth and into the early seventeenth centuries, embedding esoteric Hebraic wisdom within the mainstream institutions of royal pedagogy, ecclesiastical learning, and humanist letters. This chunk functions as a **visual and transitional hinge** in Secret's narrative, pivoting from detailed analysis of Postel's individual doctrines to evidence of their transmission and institutional embedding. By anchoring Postel's work in an **eschatological timeline** that frames 1543 as not merely a date in conventional history but as a **pivotal moment in sacred history**, Secret reorients the reader toward understanding Christian Kabbalah not as antiquarian erudition but as an active, prophetic intervention in the unfolding of divine time itself.
