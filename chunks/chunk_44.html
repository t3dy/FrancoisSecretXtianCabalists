Secret traces three converts' uses of Kabbalah to forge continuity between Jewish and Christian theology. Ludovicus Carret (published 1553 as *Epistola quæ inscribitur Liber visorum divinorum*) emerges as an improbable figure: a Tuscan manuscript acquired through Angelo Canini presents Carret as Theodorus sacerdos (Todros ha Cohen), a wealthy Italian Jewish physician whose conversion followed visionary experiences. The *Liber visorum* is a mystical-biographical narrative wherein dreams guide Todros toward recognizing Jesus as Messiah. After encountering an astrologer-oracle prophesying his conversion and reading the Gospels comparatively against Talmudic and kabbalistic sources, Todros experiences three linked visions. In the second and third, keys inscribed with biblical verses (Isaiah 22:22, 60:19, 62:2) contain the letters of Yeshua/YHWH, confirming Jesus's identity through gematria. Carret claims Kabbalah's doctrine of the three supernal lights (Zoharic motif) maps onto the Christian Trinity: Hai Gaon, Nahmanides, and other Jewish mystics affirmed three divine lights unified in En-Soph—terminology barely distinguishable from Father, Son, Spirit. Rafaele Aquilino (converted 1553, publications 1571–1581) similarly published apologetic treatises in Italian, notably extracting 13 blessings from Hebrew numerical equivalents (Aheba=13 letters=love; Agudda=union). He left manuscript works on the Hebrew medal (Star of David) and on Talmudic interpretation, drawing heavily from kabbalistic sources yet conceding that mysteries "end in Hebrew with *Vehamschil iabin*, 'let the wise understand.'" Fabiano Fioghi (converted 1559, published 1582–1628) became central to the Neophyte College founded by Gregory XIII, training other converts and becoming the reference point for later kabbalistic apologetics. His *Dialogo* acknowledges that Kabbalah contains errors yet insists God used even Jewish heresy to reveal truth (as with demons confessing Christ). Fioghi cites the interpolated Zohar passage on *Shema Yisrael* as revealing the Trinity: the divine names encode En-Soph (infinity, Father), the trunk (Son), and root (Holy Spirit). His Trinitarian exegesis recycles Galatin while modifying the Zohar quotation. The *Dialogo*'s structure—a catechizing priest instructing a Jew—became model for missionary literature. Giovan Paolo Eustachio (baptized 1568), Vitale Medici/Jehiel of Pesaro (1583), and others followed Fioghi's template, addressing kabbalistic proof-texts to convince Jews that their own mystics anticipated Christian doctrine.
