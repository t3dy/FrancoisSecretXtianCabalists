Secret traces three central figures among the convertite scholars who weaponized Kabbalah as a powerful apologetic tool, using mystical exegesis to construct elaborate arguments demonstrating that Christian doctrines—especially Trinitarian theology—were embedded within Jewish tradition long before the Messianic claims of Jesus. The first, Ludovicus Carret, emerges as a figure of considerable textual and biographical complexity. A Tuscan manuscript discovered through the archival work of Angelo Canini presents Carret under his original Hebrew name, Theodorus sacerdos (Todros ha Cohen), identifying him as a wealthy Italian Jewish physician whose religious conversion followed upon a series of visionary experiences that profoundly transformed his understanding of Christian claims. Carret's published work, the Liber visorum divinorum (Epistola, 1553), takes the form of a mystical-biographical narrative wherein dreams and visions systematically guide the protagonist—a figure modeled on the author's own spiritual history—toward the recognition of Jesus as the true Messiah. The narrative structure is deliberately persuasive: after encountering an astrologer-oracle who prophesies his conversion and after engaging in careful comparative reading of the Gospels against Talmudic and kabbalistic sources, the protagonist experiences three interconnected visions of escalating clarity. In the second and third visions, he encounters keys inscribed with biblical verses (Isaiah 22:22, 60:19, 62:2) whose letters, when subjected to gematrial analysis, reveal the name Yeshua and the four-letter divine name YHWH, thereby confirming Jesus's identity through the very mystical methods Jewish tradition had developed. Carret's apologetic strategy culminates in the audacious claim that Kabbalah's doctrine of the three supernal lights—a foundational Zoharic motif—corresponds exactly to the Christian Trinity. He marshals authority from Hai Gaon, Nahmanides, and other authoritative Jewish mystics who had affirmed the existence of three divine lights unified within En-Soph (the infinite Godhead), arguing that this terminology is barely distinguishable from the Christian formulation of Father, Son, and Holy Spirit.

Rafaele Aquilino, a convert of 1553 whose publications appeared between 1571 and 1581, pursued a similar apologetic direction while employing different methodological emphasis. He became known for extracting explicit kabbalistic meanings from Hebrew numerical equivalents in vernacular Italian treatises designed for a wider audience. His signature method involved demonstrating that Hebrew gematria yields Christian theological content—for instance, that Aheba (love) contains thirteen letters, the number thirteen encoding multiple layers of christological significance, while Agudda (union) similarly reveals divine union themes. Beyond these published polemics, Aquilino left behind a substantial body of manuscript work addressing the mystical meanings of the Hebrew medal (the Star of David) and offering kabbalistic approaches to Talmudic interpretation. Yet even as he drew extensively from kabbalistic sources, Aquilino maintained a rhetorical hedge, conceding that the deepest mysteries "end in Hebrew with Vehamschil iabin, 'let the wise understand'"—a formulation that simultaneously claimed profound hidden knowledge while acknowledging the limits of explicable doctrine.

Fabiano Fioghi, converted in 1559 with publications extending from 1582 through 1628, achieved perhaps the greatest institutional prominence of all the convertite apologists, becoming deeply embedded in the Neophyte College founded by Pope Gregory XIII. This institution, dedicated to training converts from Judaism and preparing them for missionary work among Jewish communities, positioned Fioghi as the central reference point for subsequent generations of convertite scholars and missionaries interested in kabbalistic apologetics. His dialogical treatise, the Dialogo, manifests a sophisticated theological strategy: it frankly acknowledges that Kabbalah contains genuine errors and superstitious elements, yet insists upon a more sophisticated theological principle—that God providentially used even Jewish heresy and error to communicate truth (by analogy with Jesus's use of demons to confess the truth of his divinity). Fioghi's most audacious move involved citing an interpolated passage from the Zohar concerning the recitation of Shema Yisrael (the foundational Jewish prayer affirming monotheism) as evidence that this prayer itself encodes Trinitarian doctrine: the divine names composing the prayer encode En-Soph (infinity, equated with God the Father), the trunk of the sephirotic tree (the Son), and the root (the Holy Spirit). His Trinitarian exegesis recycled and modified the foundational work of Galatin while simultaneously adjusting the Zoharic quotation to strengthen the christological inference. The Dialogo's structural innovation—presenting a catechizing priest systematically instructing a Jewish interlocutor toward Christian truth through kabbalistic reasoning—established a model that would dominate subsequent missionary literature. Giovan Paolo Eustachio (baptized 1568), Vitale Medici (also known as Jehiel of Pesaro, converted 1583), and numerous other convertite apologists subsequently adopted and refined Fioghi's template, addressing kabbalistic proof-texts directly to Jewish audiences to demonstrate that their own mystics and textual traditions had long anticipated Christian theological claims.
