Secret maps the Vatican's doctrinal trajectory, revealing a more ambivalent institutional stance than Counter-Reformation draconianism suggests. Leo X patronized Bomberg's Talmud, received dedications of Reuchlin's *De arte cabalistica* and other kabbalistic works, and only condemned the *Augenspiegel* due to Lutheran context—not Kabbalah per se. Clement VII sought a joint Jewish-Christian biblical commission and pressed Cardinal Giles of Viterbo to publish his kabbalistic works. The Cardinal Madruzzi defended Hebrew presses (Riva di Trento, 1543) and later opposed book condemnations (1562). Even Paul IV (notorious for severity) is contextualized: it was Pius IV (1559–1565) who licensed the Mantua Zohar edition. Clement VIII's Index entry explicitly condemned the "impious books of Talmud and Kabbalah" yet hedged with qualifications permitting certain texts if cleansed of blasphemy. Secret emphasizes that Index rules were fluid and regionally variant. The Portuguese Index (1581) specifically forbade "Commentary of the Zohar on *Bereshit*," yet Douai censors (1571) produced nuanced reviews. A Douai censor's evaluation of Reuchlin's *De Verbo mirifico* exemplifies this: paradoxes and Hebrew reveries should be tolerated as the Council of Trent had authorized the Talmud. The *De arte cabalistica* warranted retention with a prudential warning that Reuchlin speaks through various characters, not always in his own voice. His angelology and cosmology, if unorthodox, should be rejected as Jewish opinion rather than condemned as scandalous error. Antonio Possevino (d. 1611), Jesuit author of the *Apparatus sacer*, notes that Reuchlin, Giorgi, Rici, and Postel remain on the Index yet deems Archangelus of Burgonovo's Pic commentary (drawing on Reuchlin, Rici, and Giorgi) worth citing. Nicolas Serarius (1609) and Jacques Bonfrère enumerate three types of Kabbalah—ancient (pre-Christian Jews), modern indifferent, and magically superstitious—concluding that stripped of magical error, Kabbalah offers "ancient, pleasant, ingenious truths." Maximilien van der Sandt similarly defends Kabbalah's non-magical residue as defensible. Cornelius a Lapide, despite occasionally censuring Kabbalistic metempsychosis, liberally cites Galatin's Zohar interpolations on Trinity, even expanding them with sephirothic correspondences. The pattern: Catholic exegetes invoked Kabbalah to illuminate Scripture while maintaining that true Kabbalah (anagogy, Trinity) differs from Jewish superstition (necromancy, golem-making).
