Johann Reuchlin (1455–1522), the celebrated Pforzheim humanist and scholar of languages, acquired his taste for Kabbalistic wisdom during formative travels through Italy in the 1480s, where he encountered the intellectual world of Pico's Florence and studied Hebrew under Obadia Sforno in Rome—a teacher little inclined toward mystical philosophy but deeply versed in grammatical and exegetical precision. Reuchlin's Italian sojourns connected him to networks of Jewish and Christian Hebrew scholars; he also knew Bonet de Lates, the papal physician and astrological advisor, and he sent his brother Denis to Florence for advanced studies. Reuchlin's extraordinary career encompassed both linguistic authority—he authored the first Hebrew grammar written by a Christian (*De rudimentis hebraicis*, 1506), a foundational text that established Christian Hebraism as an academic discipline—and passionate political activism. He became famous equally for his linguistic expertise and his embattlement in the Pfefferkorn controversy (1510–1514), a bitter public dispute in which he defended Jewish texts and the legitimacy of Hebrew learning against Johannes Pfefferkorn, a baptized Jewish convert turned inquisitorial agent, and Johannes Hoogstraten, an inquisitor seeking to suppress rabbinic literature as dangerous to Christian faith. This controversy would make Reuchlin a celebrated defender of Jewish intellectual rights in the eyes of Protestant Reformers and humanists. His *De Verbo mirifico* (The Miraculous Word, 1494) recasts Pico's fragmentary Kabbalistic insights into a fully realized philosophical dialogue—a form chosen deliberately, as the dialogue permits multiple voices and perspectives to unfold Kabbalistic wisdom pedagogically. The dialogue features three interlocutors meeting at Pforzheim: Sidonius the Epicurean (representing worldly skepticism), Baruchias the Jew (representing authentic Jewish mystical doctrine), and Capnion the Christian (Reuchlin's own persona and guide to Christian Kabbalistic synthesis). Through their debate, the trio explores the power of words and letters in creation and redemption, with Baruchias expounding Hebrew's absolute superiority as the tongue through which God revealed cosmic secrets to humans—the language in which the angels themselves hear God's utterance directly without mediation. The Hebrew language, in Baruchias's exposition, contains divine names of extraordinary power: Jerome attests to ten divine names; the Platonist Denys (Dionysius) speaks of forty-five; other mystical authorities cite seventy-two. Baruchias explores specific names and their metaphysical significance: Ehieh (Exodus III, 14—"I Am"), the name of pure being and existence itself; Hu (Isaiah XIII, 8—the pronoun "He"), expressing divine transcendence and otherness; and Esh (fire), connecting God to the primordial element of dynamic creation. These are not mere words but vessels of creative power. Reuchlin's enumeration of the Sephiroth within the dialogue, though containing errors evident to later scholars (omitting Yesod from his initial list, conflating Geburah with Pahad), nonetheless attempts to ground these abstract mystical attributes in Scripture and reason. He finds Hochmah and Binah in Proverbs III, 13 ("Happy is the man who finds Wisdom ... more precious than rubies"), Netzah in 1 Kings XV, 29 (a verse about God's permanence), Hod in various psalmic passages celebrating understanding. The systematic correlation of mystical nomenclature with biblical proof-texts exemplifies humanist methodology applied to mysticism: no claim stands without textual warrant. The Tetragrammaton YHWH, developed through elaborate gematric calculation as a seventy-two-lettered name derived through the *Shem ha-meforash* liturgy (based on Psalm 113:3-5, three verses of 72 letters each), mirrors the Pythagorean Tetractys (the sacred triangle 1+2+3+4=10, encompassing unity, duality, trinity, and quaternary). The four letters encompass all philosophy and cosmology: four elements (fire, water, air, earth), four qualities (hot, cold, wet, dry), four geometric principles (point, line, plane, solid). Each letter of YHWH carries distinct philosophical meaning: Yod (the mathematical point representing unity and the decad encompassing ten), He (the union of God and nature, mediating transcendence and immanence), Vau (the copula or connective, numerically six, linking and balancing opposites), and final He (mediation and moiety—the half that when added to itself completes the decad). Capnion concludes the dialogue by gathering all divine powers into the Name of Jesus—written Kabbalisticalally as YHSWH with the divine fire-letter Shin added—fulfilling Isaiah IX, 5 ("His name shall be called Pele"—Admirable, Wonderful). The dialogue ends with esoteric silence, the proper response to mysteries beyond rational discourse: *Sile, cela, occulta, tege, tace, mussa* (Be silent, hide, conceal, veil, be mute, murmur)—a silence that honors the unspeakable nature of divine wisdom.
