Johann Reuchlin (1455–1522), the Pforzheim humanist, acquired his taste for Kabbalah during Italian travels, where he encountered Pico's Florence and studied Hebrew under Obadia Sforno in Rome (a teacher little inclined toward philosophy). He also knew Bonet de Lates, papal physician, and sent his brother Denis to Florence for studies. As the pioneering Christian Hebraist and author of the first Hebrew grammar by a Christian (1506), Reuchlin became famous equally for linguistic expertise and his embattlement in the Pfefferkorn controversy (1510–1514) defending Jewish texts against baptized inquisitor Hoogstraten. His *De Verbo mirifico* (The Miraculous Word, 1494) recast Pico's thought in dialogue form—three interlocutors (Sidonius the Epicurean, Baruchias the Jew, Capnion the Christian) meet at Pforzheim to debate the power of words and letters. Baruchias expounds Hebrew's superiority as the tongue through which God revealed secrets to humans and angels hear directly; it contains ten divine names according to Jerome, forty-five per Denys, or seventy-two per others. He explores names like Ehieh (Exodus III, 14), Hu (Isaiah XIII, 8), and Esh (fire)—three names representing divine essence. Reuchlin's enumeration of Sephiroth, though containing errors (omitting Yesod, conflating Geburah/Pahad), attempts to ground these mystical attributes in Scripture, finding Hochmah and Binah in Proverbs III, 13, Netzah in 1 Kings XV, 29, Hod in various psalms, and so forth. The Tetragrammaton YHWH, developed as a seventy-two-lettered name through the Psalm-verses litany, mirrors the Pythagorean Tetractys, encompassing all philosophy and cosmology (four elements, four qualities, four geometric principles). Each letter (Yod, He, Vau, He) carries meaning: Yod = mathematical point (unity and decad), He = union of God and nature, Vau = copula (six), and final He = mediation (moiety of the decad). Capnion concludes by gathering all divine powers into the Name of Jesus (YHSWH with added Schin), fulfilling Isaiah IX, 5 ("Pele"—Admirable). The dialogue ends with esoteric silence: *Sile, cela, occulta, tege, tace, mussa* (Be silent, hide, conceal, veil, be mute, murmur).
