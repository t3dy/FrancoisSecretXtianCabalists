Jean Reuchlin (1455-1522), the renowned Hebraist and humanist of Pforzheim, acquired his passion for Kabbalah during his studies in Italy, where the humanist renaissance provided unprecedented access to Hebrew manuscripts and the intellectual milieu of Florentine Neoplatonism. His name itself became the vehicle of his intellectual transformation: the Greek humanist Hermolaus Barbarus suggested that Reuchlin Hebraize his own name, which means "smoke," into Capnion—a symbolic act of linguistic and cultural assimilation that marked his commitment to the sacred language he would spend his career mastering. His Latin studies at Poitiers and Paris had been rigorous, but it was Italy that opened the world of Hebrew learning: in Rome he purchased a Hebrew Bible still preserved in manuscript form and took instruction from Obadia Sforno (d. 1537), author of the influential mystical commentary Or amim (Light of the Nations), a scholar whose approach combined practical philology with deeper mystical interpretation of the sacred text. Reuchlin's Italian connections extended into the highest circles of papal patronage and humanist erudition—he encountered Bonet de Lates, physician to the Pope itself, establishing networks that would sustain his scholarly ambitions for decades. Even his family became integrated into this Italian intellectual world: his brother Denys was sent to Florence to pursue advanced studies, suggesting that the entire Reuchlin household oriented itself toward this new humanist synthesis. Yet Reuchlin's reputation in Protestant and Catholic Europe rested not merely on his Hebrew erudition but on his decisive interventions in the great intellectual disputes of his age: he authored the first Hebrew grammar composed by a Christian scholar (published 1506), establishing the technical philological foundations through which subsequent generations of Christian Hebraists would access the sacred language; and he became the celebrated hero of the Obscuri Viri (Dark Ones) controversy (1510-1514), defending Hebrew studies and Jewish texts against the baptized Jew Pfefferkorn and the Dominican inquisitor Hoogstraten, who sought the destruction of Hebrew books as theologically dangerous and heretically contaminated.

Yet before his technical accomplishments in Hebrew grammar and his polemical victory in defense of Hebraism, Reuchlin's true fame emerged from a single remarkable work: the De Verbo mirifico (On the Miraculous Word), which appeared in 1494 and established him as the equal of Pico de la Mirandole in Christian Kabbalistic speculation. The work takes the form of a philosophical dialogue, modeled on the fashionable dialogue genre of Renaissance humanism, featuring three interlocutors who meet at Pforzheim: Sidonius, a curious Epicurean philosopher traveling to expand his knowledge; Baruchias, a learned Jewish sage; and Capnion, Reuchlin himself, the Christian mediator and synthesizer. The narrative framework locates mystical conversation within the streets and inns of a German town rather than an Italian court, signaling that Kabbalistic learning had become available to northern European humanists, not merely to the privileged circle around Lorenzo de' Medici. The three books of the dialogue unfold systematically: in the first, the Epicurean Sidonius expounds his materialist philosophy and skepticism regarding divine names and mystical language; in the second, Baruchias presents the Jewish mystical understanding of the power of divine language; and in the third, Capnion reveals how all the powers resident in Hebrew divine names are ultimately concentrated and perfected in the single name of Jesus, thus completing the Christian Kabbalistic project of showing how Hebrew mysticism prophetically anticipated Christian trinitarian theology and Christology. The dramatic structure itself embodies the argument: Christian truth emerges not as negation of Jewish wisdom but as its culmination and perfection, the realization toward which all previous mystical tradition pointed.

Baruchias emerges as a fully realized characterization, not a mere symbolic mouthpiece: Reuchlin describes him as diminutive in stature, with a long beard, a somewhat sorrowful countenance, aquiline nose, and garbed in the long mantle with yellow badge mandated by medieval Christian authorities for visible Jewish identification—a sartorial detail that grounds mystical speculation in the lived reality of Jewish persecution and marginalization. Yet Baruchias is simultaneously marked by Saturn, the planet of contemplation, suggesting that his outward humility conceals depths of esoteric knowledge. Remarkably, Baruchias represents an exception among German Jewry, whom Reuchlin characterizes as deeply pious but philosophically defensive, persuaded that Greek philosophy is the source of all heretical error and thus best avoided. Baruchias, by contrast, enthusiastically defends Kabbalah as something fundamentally different from human philosophy: it is divine tradition transmitted from Abraham (though the text expresses uncertainty about Abraham's actual historical identity—a telling recognition of the legendary status of much kabbalistic attributions), through Simon ben Yohai, Abraham Abulafia, and Menahem Recanati, transmitted orally from master to disciple as sacred knowledge rather than rational speculation. His defense of the Hebrew language achieves a lyrical intensity: he characterizes it as supple, pure, holy, concise, and vigorous—the language God himself employed when speaking to humanity, the language angels understand directly, face-to-face, without intermediaries, as friend speaks to friend. This language embodies divine power because it was the vehicle of the divine name itself, and here Reuchlin records the medieval Jewish tradition that Jerome identified ten divine names, Pseudo-Dionysius forty-five, and other sources sixty-two or more. Before humanity's fall into sin, there existed only a single divine name; the fall caused humanity to lose knowledge of it, but the prophet Zacharias promised a future day when God would be One and His name One—an apocalyptic hope woven throughout Jewish mystical thought.

Baruchias then expounds the doctrine of divine names, invoking first the principle articulated by Pico de la Mirandole: "No name in licit magic art possesses as much power as the Hebrew." The supreme divine names emerge in careful progression. The first name, Ehieh (rendered from Exodus 3:14), reveals God's essential being, His separation from all creatures, His self-revelation to humanity—the foundation of all subsequent mystical knowledge. The second name, Hu (from Isaiah 43:11—"I am the Lord, Hu is His name"), completes the trinity of supreme names. The third name, Esh (Fire), receives attestation across all religions, suggesting its status as a fundamental principle of divinity. These three names together constitute a species of trinity expressing the supreme essence of God, comprehensible only through the framework of the ten Sephiroth or divine attributes: Kether (Crown), Hochma (Wisdom), Binah (Intelligence), Netzah (Victory), Hod (Majesty), Tifereth (Beauty), Geburah (Power), Malkhuth (Kingdom), Hesed (Mercy), and Pachad (Fear). Yet scholars have noted significant errors in Reuchlin's enumeration: he omits Yesod (Foundation), he conflates Geburah and Pahad (which actually occupy different positions in standard kabbalistic doctrine), and his organizational scheme suggests he may have lacked access to authoritative kabbalistic texts that would have provided the canonical ordering of the ten sephiroth. Reuchlin explicitly declares that the Pythagorean doctrine and Pico's teachings had instructed him that Malkhuth occupies the tenth place, yet he presents the names in an arrangement that privileges Kether, Hochma, and Binah—the three supernal sephiroth—and treats the remainder with less systematic clarity. His actual concern becomes evident as he demonstrates how nine divine names appear throughout Scripture, linking them to specific biblical passages: wisdom and intelligence flow from Hochma and Binah through passages in Proverbs and the Song of Songs; victory (Netzah) appears in royal passages concerning Israel's triumph; majesty and splendor (Hod) manifest in the psalms and historical narratives; beauty and glory (Tifereth) shines at the heart of the divine edifice; power and strength (Geburah) reveal themselves in divine judgment and salvation narratives; mercy and grace (Hesed) flow throughout the juridical and covenantal passages; and finally fear and awe (Pachad) complete the enumeration.

Reuchlin's interpretation moves beyond mere naming into gematric calculation and symbolic interpretation. The divine name Elohim, when fully manifest, becomes YHWH, the Tetragrammaton—that unpronounceable four-letter name at the center of Jewish mysticism. This name receives special attention because it represents the "admirable" name not in the sense of Psalm 8:10 ("Thy name is marvelous upon the earth") but as rendered in Judges 13:18—"Why askest thou thus after my name, seeing it is wonderful?" The term translated "wonderful" (Peli in Hebrew) carries the sense of transcendence and mystery. The medieval Jewish tradition preserved this name in a secret form known as the Schem ha-Mephorash, the Explained Name consisting of seventy-two letters, which could be ordered according to the Ladder of Jacob. The four-lettered Tetragrammaton sits at the summit of this cosmic ladder, and from it emanate the seventy-two names, each derived from three consecutive letters extracted from verses of the Psalms—a methodology suggesting that every verse of the Psalms contains encoded within it three letters that, when arranged in a specific manner, yields one of the seventy-two divine names. This system remained obscure to subsequent commentators, prompting monks and scholars to appeal to Reuchlin for clarification. Augustine Wolfgang, an abbot writing in 1501, confessed that he had heard Baruchias (through the dialogue) explain how the Tetragrammaton could be comprehended within seventy-two verses, but could not grasp the mechanism, despite possessing the Sefer Tillim (Book of Psalms) in Hebrew. Similarly, on August 29, 1511, Nicolas Ellenbog (1481-1543), a Benedictine monk at Ottobeuren, pressed Reuchlin for explicit instruction, begging him to "designate the seventy-two seals (signacula) drawn from the seventy-two verses of the psalter. I have written these psalm verses in Hebrew characters so that thou mayest show me the symbols, which, were thou present, thou wouldst indicate with thy finger."

Reuchlin's response of September 30, 1511, reveals the practical mystical pedagogy underlying his theological speculation. He lamented that he lacked red ink to highlight the Hebrew letters but marked each character with double commas so that Ellenbog might later add red and gold—the colors traditionally reserved for sacred text. For each three-letter sequence composing each angelic seal, Reuchlin provided marginal notation indicating the order in which the angels should be arranged. Most remarkably, Reuchlin observed mystical significance in the fact that the opening verse of the Bible—"In the beginning God created the heaven and the earth"—appears as the seventieth verse in his enumeration (the antepenultimate sequence), a position that "contains no small mystery, for it approaches the holy Trinity, tending to accomplish the entire psalter and to lead us to fruition, which is the goal of the Psalms." This comment reveals how Reuchlin saw profound theological meaning encoded in numerical position itself: the first account of creation, the opening of all revelation, occupies a sacred numerical position just short of perfection, suggesting the relationship between creation and eschatological fulfillment. Yet Reuchlin also noted, with almost casual profundity, that "all the verses contain the Tetragrammaton"—a statement that, if true, would suggest the entire psalm collection vibrates with the divine name itself, a mystical presence underlying all prayer and praise. Ellenbog would become a figure representative of the next generation of Christian Kabbalists, a student deeply engaged in Hebrew studies despite formidable institutional obstacles. The son of the physician to the Archduke of Austria, Ellenbog had studied in Cracow under Jean of Glogau and had preserved a prognostication from 1502 where he delighted to see announced the Lutheran heresy—a detail suggesting how some humanist scholars sensed impending religious transformation years before 1517. His monastic vocation, undertaken after traveling to France and experiencing a mystical calling during a Dominican retreat, positioned him to transmit Reuchlin's learned methodology and mystical theology to a new generation of reformed clergy.

The Tetragrammaton, when expanded through liturgical development into the Tetractys, mirrors the Pythagorean cosmic principle, suggesting profound connections between Hebraic and Greek mysticism that Renaissance humanists found endlessly fascinating. Baruchias notes that the tetractys encompasses all philosophy and the entire universe: the four elements, the four qualities, the four geometric principles, the four musical notes, the four rivers of Eden, and the four creatures of Ezekiel's chariot throne—a comprehensive symbolic system that subordinates physical, mathematical, musical, and mystical knowledge under a single tetrad structure. Each letter of the Tetragrammaton YOD, HE, VAU, HE carries its own significance: Yod, valued at ten numerologically, functions as the mathematical point, the foundation of unity and the completion of the decad; He, valued at five, signifies the union of God and nature, the principle of manifestation; Vau, the third letter, serves as copula or joining principle and possesses numerical value six—the number of perfection because on the sixth day God beheld all his creation and declared it good. The doubled appearance of Vau in specific biblical contexts (notably in the word "generations"—Tholdot) carries genealogical significance, pointing mysteriously toward the genealogy of Christ. The final He, equivalent to half the decad, represents mediation itself—that principle of balance and intermediation that allows transcendent reality to bridge the gap between itself and material creation. In Ezekiel's vision, this final He corresponds to the face of the eagle; in Daniel's symbolism, it appears as the fourth beast resembling the Son of God—mystical equations that locate Christ himself within the very structure of the divine name.

The entire dialectical structure moves toward its culmination in the third book, where Capnion synthesizes all the powers and mysteries of the divine names into the single name of Jesus, thus completing the Christian Kabbalistic demonstration that Hebrew mysticism, properly understood, anticipates Christian theology. The work's title, De Verbo mirifico (On the Miraculous Word), receives its explanation through Isaiah 9:5—"For unto us a child is born, unto us a son is given; and the government shall be upon his shoulder; and his name shall be called Wonderful" (where Pele, "wonderful," echoes the "admirable" or "miraculous" name of divinity). The Son of God may be discovered in a single stone (Aben, rendered as a unity from the syllables Ab—Father and Ben—Son, two elements that, when conjoined, form the complete term for the mysterious stone). Even more elaborately, the verb Bara (He created) can be decomposed into its mystical components: Aleph (Ab—Father), Beth (Ben—Son), and Resch (Ruach—Spirit), revealing how the act of creation itself encodes the Trinity. When one links the final letter to the first, one achieves Ab (Father); when one doubles Beth and joins it to Resch, one obtains Bebar, "in the Son," and combining all elements yields the mystical formula: "The Father created in the Son." Thus the very syntax and letter combinations of Hebrew revelation already contain trinitarian theology in its most essential form. The name of the Son emerges as the Tetragrammaton to which a Shin has been added—YHSWH, the Pentagrammaton—a development that Capnion supports through elaborate kabbalistic methodology. Drawing on the "Prophecy of Elijah" that assigns six thousand years to human history followed by the Sabbath, Capnion arranges the Hebrew alphabet into seven groups, and the seventh group, when properly arranged and excluding the Qof, yields Resch (Head), suggesting prophetically that Shin would be the letter most suited to forming the name of Ihsuh (Jesus), whom God the Father established as head of the Church, His body, according to the Epistle to the Ephesians. The very structure of the alphabet predicts the incarnation itself.

Reuchlin's enumeration of the Sephiroth within the dialogue, though containing errors evident to later scholars (omitting Yesod from his initial list, conflating Geburah with Pahad), nonetheless attempts to ground these abstract mystical attributes in Scripture and reason. He finds Hochmah and Binah in Proverbs III, 13 ("Happy is the man who finds Wisdom ... more precious than rubies"), Netzah in 1 Kings XV, 29 (a verse about God's permanence), Hod in various psalmic passages celebrating understanding. The systematic correlation of mystical nomenclature with biblical proof-texts exemplifies humanist methodology applied to mysticism: no claim stands without textual warrant. The Tetragrammaton YHWH, developed through elaborate gematric calculation as a seventy-two-lettered name derived through the Shem ha-meforash liturgy (based on Psalm 113:3-5, three verses of 72 letters each), mirrors the Pythagorean Tetractys (the sacred triangle 1+2+3+4=10, encompassing unity, duality, trinity, and quaternary). The four letters encompass all philosophy and cosmology: four elements (fire, water, air, earth), four qualities (hot, cold, wet, dry), four geometric principles (point, line, plane, solid). Each letter of YHWH carries distinct philosophical meaning: Yod (the mathematical point representing unity and the decad encompassing ten), He (the union of God and nature, mediating transcendence and immanence), Vau (the copula or connective, numerically six, linking and balancing opposites), and final He (mediation and moiety—the half that when added to itself completes the decad). Capnion concludes the dialogue by gathering all divine powers into the Name of Jesus—written Kabbalisticalally as YHSWH with the divine fire-letter Shin added—fulfilling Isaiah IX, 5 ("His name shall be called Pele"—Admirable, Wonderful). The dialogue ends with esoteric silence, the proper response to mysteries beyond rational discourse: Sile, cela, occulta, tege, tace, mussa (Be silent, hide, conceal, veil, be mute, murmur)—a silence that honors the unspeakable nature of divine wisdom.
