Giorgio brings his numerological analysis of Christ to its apex by demonstrating how the divine name Jesus (rendered in Hebrew as YSU) emerges as the perfect fulfillment and completion of the Tetragrammaton (YHWH). The substitution of Shin for the two He letters encodes a profound theological message: Shin represents Shabbat, the cosmic repose that follows creation, and Jesus becomes the embodiment of that eternal rest promised to humanity through redemption and eternal life. Following the precedent established by Heredia and Galatin—earlier Christian kabbalists who had pursued similar mystical identifications—Giorgio locates Jesus and Mary's names hidden within biblical verses through the systematic application of gematria. In Psalm 71, Genesis 49 (the prophecy of Shiloh), and Psalm 95, the initial letters marked by three mystical points spell out IESU (Jesus), while the final He of the Tetragrammaton itself marks the union of divinity with humanity achieved in the Messiah. This hermeneutical practice finds corroboration in the *Sepher Temunoth* (Book of What Must Be Believed), a canonical Kabbalistic text, creating an unbroken chain of authority from Jewish mysticism through Christian theological innovation. Secret's documentation reveals that the question of Jesus' name became a century-long intellectual obsession for Christian humanists, each successive scholar attempting to secure the proper spelling and unlock its mystical implications. Jacques Lefèvre d'Étaples (1505-1508) energized the debate by citing the authorities—Nicholas of Cusa, Pico, and Reuchlin—all of whom commented on the insertion of Shin into the Tetragrammaton to produce a name signifying Christ. The dispute escalated through successive generations: Erasmus's 1521 work faced withering criticism from the Spanish Hebraist López Stunica; Andreas Osiander, himself following Reuchlin's methodology, drew sharp rebuke from Angelo Canini, who explicitly rejected what he termed "cabalist nonsense" as fundamentally incompatible with Christian theology. The Christian kabbalists fractured into competing schools of allegiance: Archangelus of Burgonovo committed himself to Giorgio's system; A. Bosius in 1617 claimed to have proved numerically that Jesus and Silo (the cryptic city name in Genesis 49) were mathematically equivalent—a demonstration later plagiarized without attribution by the polymath Mersenne, who inserted it into his own works. Secret frames the entire century as bookended by two towering monuments: Reuchlin's *De Verbo mirifico* (On the Miraculous Word) at the century's opening, and Luis de León's *De los nombres de Cristo* (On the Names of Christ) at its close, where León embarked on a project he called "kabbalization" rather than Pythagorean numerology, treating the Hebrew name Jehosuah (complete form of Jesus) as containing within itself every letter of the ineffable divine name, thus making Christ the totality of all possible names for God. Giorgio's *Problemata*—an enormous compendium comprising three thousand distinct questions on Scripture and philosophy, organized systematically in six major parts—served as the definitive repository of Christian Kabbalistic exegesis, drawing systematically on the foundational texts of Jewish mysticism (the Zohar, the Sefer Yetzirah, and Porta lucis). Each of Giorgio's three thousand problems follows a consistent hermeneutical strategy: beginning with the literal meaning of a biblical text, it then elevates that literal sense toward "most secret theology," revealing the hidden correspondences through which mundane words become transparencies to divine truth. Examples abound: the word Amen is shown to possess the numerical value 91, which precisely equals the sum of YHWH plus Adonai, the two supreme divine names; the well of Beershaba becomes a node for gathering supernal influences from all seven celestial spheres. Secret's strongest evidence for papal engagement comes from a manuscript titled *De reformatione Ecclesiae* (On Church Reform), composed by Lunarius and dedicated to Pope Paul III, which records the pontiff's documented curiosity about kabbalah, his known ties to Flavius Mithridates (who had served as Pico's Kabbalistic mentor), and Pico's doctrine of the "fifty gates of intelligence" that Moses himself allegedly transmitted orally to Joshua. This papal attention, recorded in manuscript form, demonstrates that even at the highest echelons of ecclesiastical authority, Christian Kabbalah was regarded as a potentially orthodox mystical discipline worthy of serious theological consideration. The chapter closes with Secret preparing to transition into the German reception and transformation of Christian Kabbalah, explicitly contesting the historiographical assumption—widely accepted but, Secret argues, demonstrably false—that Reformation literalism and the rise of Protestant biblical scholarship somehow killed or suppressed kabbalistic interests. On the contrary, Secret will demonstrate that Protestant and Catholic humanists alike quietly continued to cultivate and advance Kabbalistic learning, embedding it within legitimate scholarly discourse and theological innovation despite mounting institutional suspicion and eventual condemnation.
