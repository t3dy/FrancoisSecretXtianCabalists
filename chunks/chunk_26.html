Giorgio completes his exegesis of Jesus (YSU) as the fulfillment of the divine Tetragrammaton: the Shin replacing the two He of YHWH signifies Shabbat (repose), representing Jesus as humanity's rest and eternal life. Following the precedent of Heredia and Galatin, Giorgio identifies Jesus and Mary's names hidden by gematria in biblical verses (Psalm 71, Genesis 49 on Shiloh, Psalm 95), where initial letters crowned with three points spell IESU, and where the final He of YHWH marks the union of divinity with humanity in the Messiah. This reading is supported by the *Sepher Temunoth* (Book of What Must Be Believed). Secret documents the century-long fascination with determining the correct spelling and mystical significance of Jesus' name. Jacques Lefèvre d'Étaples (1505-1508) cites Nicholas of Cusa, Pico, and Reuchlin on the Shin inserted into the Tetragrammaton. The dispute escalates: Erasmus (1521) is criticized by López Stunica; Osiander (following Reuchlin) draws rebuke from Angelo Canini, who explicitly rejects "cabalist nonsense" in Christian theology. Christian kabbalists divide in allegiance: Archangelus of Burgonovo follows Giorgio; A. Bosius (1617) proves Jesus and Silo numerically equivalent (work later plagiarized by Mersenne without attribution). Secret frames the century as bookended by Reuchlin's *De Verbo mirifico* and Luis de León's *De los nombres de Cristo*, where León "kabbalized" rather than pythagorized, treating Jehosuah as containing all letters of the ineffable divine name. Giorgio's *Problemata*—a compendium of three thousand questions on Scripture and philosophy organized in six parts—systematically groups kabbalistic themes from the Zohar, Sefer Yetzirah, and Porta lucis. Each problem elevates literal sense to "most secret theology," exemplified by Amen (gematria 91 = conjunction of YHWH and Adonai), or the well of Beershaba as the gathering of supernal influences. Secret's final evidence is the manuscript *De reformatione Ecclesiae* (Lunarius, dedicated to Paul III), recording papal curiosity about kabbalah, Paul III's ties to Flavius Mithridates (Pico's teacher), and Pico's doctrine of the "fifty gates of intelligence." The chapter closes as Secret transitions to the German reception of Christian kabbalah, contesting the assumption that Reformation literalism killed kabbalistic interests.
