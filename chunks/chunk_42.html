## Hepburn and the Hebraic Synthesis Jacques Bonaventure Hepburn (1573–1621), a Scottish Catholic who lived in France and Rome, emerged as a major Hebraist. Paul IV appointed him guardian of Eastern books and manuscripts. Dempster's *Ecclesiastical History of Scotland* (1628) catalogs his output: Hebrew and Chaldaic dictionaries, Arabic grammar, Psalter commentaries, and notably translations of Ibn Gabirol's *Keter Malcuth* (Crown of Royalty), Rabbi Abraham Aben Ezra's *Sefer Yetzirah* (Book of Creation), Joseph Ezobi's *Canticum*, and R. Abraham Levita ben David's kabbalistic treatise (also translated by Génébrard). He translated the *Portes de justice* and the *Book of Enoch*. Reputed to know seventy-two languages, Hepburn published in Rome (1616) the rare *Virga aurea*, employing Heredia's *Mechkar ha sodoth* (Searcher of Secrets). The *Virga aurea*—engraved sheets dedicated to Pius V—contained Heredia's text in Hebrew with Latin translation, the famous passage attributing to Rabbi Simeon ben Iohai knowledge that Moses's rod bore two divine names: the Tetragrammaton and the 72-letter name, whose virtue accomplished wonders. The work combines kabbalistic alphabets, liturgical invocations to the Virgin, and a perpetual natural-magical calendar organizing contemplation and philosophical knowledge according to divine names in one, two, three (El, Yah, Sadai), four (YHWH), and five letters (YHSWH), extending through senary, septenary, octonary, novenary, and denary (completing all number) to the duodenary (perfection and grace). The colophon warns detractors away, noting that criticism is easy while imitation is hard. Mersenne, attacking François Giorgi of Venice, cited Hepburn's *Eloge de la Vierge* displaying seventy-two alphabets on the origin of *Iod*. De Mély, reissuing the work and calling it *Memento* for alchemists and occultists, perhaps overstated its magical intent. Hepburn's imprimatur appears on Carlo di Fabri da Mondolfo's *Scudo di Christo* (1621), combining Galatin and Angelo Pozzi on divine names, deriving *Ysu* (Jesus) from *Iod* (Tetragrammaton), *Schin* (heaven), and *Vau* (earth). ## Kircher and Collective Engagement Father Kircher in *Oedipus Ægyptiacus* repeatedly reproduced Alabaster's exegeses—notably on Abraham's name decomposed into "Ab" (center, expansion), "Ham" (multitude of men), and mystical transpositions showing Abraham as prefiguring the nations' gatherer and the patriarchs as cosmic types. Similar work appears in Ludovicus Ystella's *Commentaries on Genesis and Exodus* (Rome, 1609), citing Reuchlin and Galatin on Trinitarian proofs; Blasius Viegas's *Apocalypse Commentary* (Cologne, 1614), employing Giorgi on the Tetragrammaton's numerical sphericity; and Sebastianus Barradius's *Gospel Concordance Commentary* (Mainz, 1601), citing Pic's *Apologia* and Galatin alongside the closed Mem and Elijah prophecies. Even Ludovicus ab Alcazar (1554–1613), despite smiling at Galatin's interpretations in his *Vestigatio arcani sensus in Apocalypse* (Lyon, 1618), cited Valverdius's finding of angel names (Raphael, Gabriel, Michael, Nuriel) in the word *Argaman* (purple), mirroring Ezekiel's animals and God's royal raiment. The work also references Michel Aitsinger on the seven angels and their revelation to Blessed Amadeus, connecting to a Sicilian chapel discovery and the subsequent devotion culminating in Michelangelo's Santa Maria degli Angeli. Jean des Anges (*Considérations sur le Cantique des Cantiques*, Paris, 1619) exegetes Jesus as four Hebrew letters—*Iod* (beginning), *He* (this one), *Vau* (life), *Chet* (passion)—declaring Jesus the life's beginning, redeemed through passion. Don Juan de Sancta María, a converted Turk entertained at Ghent castle, questioned in *Perfección de la religión christiana* (Brussels, 1622) whether "Rabalistes" or "Talmudistes" predate Pic, finding no ancient Arab records, and concluding Pic's antiquity claims unbelievable. ## Bacon and Rational Skepticism Francis Bacon (1560–1626) in *De dignitate et augmentis scientiarum* (English 1605, Latin 1623) identified two excesses in scriptural interpretation: one presupposes Scripture's perfection such that all philosophy derives from it (as Paracelsus and his school maintained, originating with rabbis and kabbalists). These seekers fail and dishonor Scripture by raiding it for transitory things—a category error like seeking the living among the dead or conversely seeking the dead among the living. In *Nova Atlantis* (1627), Bacon presents the utopian island Ben Salem with Hebrew inscriptions and Solomon's House (College of Six Days), dedicated to contemplating God's works and creatures. The Jewish character Joabin, though circumcised, acknowledges Jesus's superiority as immaculate virgin's son and leader of Seraphim. Joabin reports that Ben Salem's Jewish laws derive from "secret kabbalah" of Abraham and his son Nachor, awaiting the Messiah's Jerusalem enthronement when the king of Ben Salem will stand at His feet. This utopian vision echoes in Joseph Hall's *Mundus alter et idem* and Thomas Bancroft's epigrams celebrating a precious library containing Tite Live, Enoch's Oracles, Solomon's Physics, and post-diluvian treasures. ## Fludd: Systematization and Controversy Robert Fludd (1574–1637), author of *Philosophia Mosaica*, systematized Christian kabbalah from Pic, Archangelus de Burgonovo, Vigenère, and especially Reuchlin. He repeatedly cited Reuchlin's *De arte cabalistica* on the dark and luminous Aleph, which Mersenne noted as crucial. Fludd declared the *Bereshith* method permits deriving creation's order from that word's letters: *R* (void, darkness), *S* (void filled with divine light), *Aleph* (means and principle filling darkness). He extended this into three metaphysical (incréé) and three physical principles mirroring YHWH (*Res, Het, Iod*—light, spirit, fire), and three microcosmic principles (body, soul, spirit). Mersenne's judgment was damning: Fludd's cabala either reproduces commonplace theology that every Catholic knows or proposes lies. His *Stratum Bonum* (1629) defended true (essential) kabbalah divided into *Bereshith* (chronological) and *Merkava* (notaric and temuraic), versus superstitious kabbalah (gematria, notaric, temurah), but Mersenne rejected both indiscriminately, finding no differentiation between legitimate and superstitious practice. ## Conclusion and Legacy The path lay open for Milton, H. More, and countless later authors associating Christian kabbalah with their speculations. English engagement, though tardier than French or Spanish, ultimately witnessed the most comprehensive synthesis in figures like Fludd, whose attempt to reconcile kabbalistic cosmology with Christian metaphysics represented the peak and perhaps the exhaustion of Renaissance Christian kabbalah as a unified intellectual program.
