Jacques Bonaventure Hepburn (1573–1621), a Scottish Catholic scholar who spent much of his life in France and Rome, emerged as a preeminent Hebraist and synthesizer of kabbalistic learning. Pope Paul IV appointed him guardian of Eastern manuscripts and books in the Curia's collections—a position that facilitated his unparalleled access to kabbalistic texts in various languages. David Dempster's *Ecclesiastical History of Scotland* (1628) catalogs Hepburn's formidable output: Hebrew and Chaldaic lexicons, Arabic grammar, psalm commentaries, and crucially, translations of Ibn Gabirol's mystical *Keter Malcuth* (Crown of Royalty), Rabbi Abraham Aben Ezra's *Sefer Yetzirah* (Book of Creation), Joseph Ezobi's *Canticum*, and R. Abraham Levita ben David's kabbalistic treatise (also translated independently by Génébrard). Hepburn additionally translated the *Portes de justice* (Gates of Justice) and the apocryphal *Book of Enoch*. Reputed to command seventy-two languages, Hepburn published at Rome in 1616 the rare *Virga aurea* (Golden Rod), employing Heredia's Hebrew kabbalistic text *Mechkar ha sodoth* (Searcher of Secrets). The *Virga aurea*—produced as engraved sheets and dedicated to Pope Pius V—contained Heredia's original Hebrew alongside Hepburn's Latin translation, featuring the celebrated passage that attributes to Rabbi Simeon ben Yohai the revelation that Moses's staff bore inscribed two divine names: the Tetragrammaton and the 72-letter name whose virtue accomplished all miracles. The work synthesizes kabbalistic alphabets, liturgical invocations to the Virgin Mary, and a perpetual natural-magical calendar organizing contemplation and philosophical knowledge according to divine names: the monad (El), the dyad (Yah), the triad (Sadai), the tetrad (YHWH), and the pentad (YHSWH), extending through senary, septenary, octonary, novenary, and denary (signifying numerical completion) to the duodenary (representing perfection and grace). The colophon defensively warns detractors away, noting that criticism is facile while imitation demands genius. Marin Mersenne, attacking François Giorgi of Venice, cited Hepburn's *Eloge de la Vierge* (Eulogy of the Virgin) with its display of seventy-two alphabets expounding the origin of *Iod* (the yod, the primordial letter). When Edmond de Mély reissued the work subsequently, titling it *Memento* for alchemists and occultists, he perhaps overstated its purely magical intent, though Hepburn's intellectual energy certainly animated occult learning networks. Hepburn's scholarly imprimatur appears on Carlo di Fabri da Mondolfo's *Scudo di Christo* (1621), synthesizing Galatin and Angelo Pozzi on divine names, deriving the Christological name *Ysu* (Jesus) from *Iod* (the Tetragrammaton), *Schin* (heaven), and *Vau* (earth)—a vertical cosmology compressed into alphabetic form. Father Athanasius Kircher, in his monumental *Oedipus Ægyptiacus*, repeatedly incorporated and elaborated on Alabaster's exegetical innovations, particularly his decomposition of Abraham's name into "Ab" (center, principle of expansion), "Ham" (multitude of men), and through mystical transpositions, revealing Abraham as the cosmic prefiguration of the nations' gatherer and the patriarchs as cosmic typological figures. Parallel work appeared in Ludovicus Ystella's *Commentaries on Genesis and Exodus* (Rome, 1609), citing Reuchlin and Galatin on proofs of the Trinity drawn from divine names; Blasius Viegas's *Apocalypse Commentary* (Cologne, 1614), deploying Giorgi's arguments on the Tetragrammaton's numerical sphericity (the perfection of the four, the tetrad); and Sebastianus Barradius's *Gospel Concordance Commentary* (Mainz, 1601), citing Pic's *Apologia* and Galatin alongside interpretations of the closed Mem and Elijah's prophecy. Even Ludovicus ab Alcazar (1554–1613), despite affecting skeptical amusement at Galatin's exegetical audacities in his *Vestigatio arcani sensus in Apocalypse* (Lyon, 1618), could not resist citing Valverdius's discovery of angel names (Raphael, Gabriel, Michael, Nuriel) encoded within the Hebrew word *Argaman* (purple), mirroring Ezekiel's mystical animals and God's royal raiment in Isaiah's throne-vision. The work also references Michel Aitsinger on the seven angels and their revelation to Blessed Amadeus, connecting to a miraculous chapel discovered in Sicily and the subsequent devotion crystallizing in Michelangelo's Santa Maria degli Angeli. Jean des Anges, in *Considérations sur le Cantique des Cantiques* (Paris, 1619), exegetes Jesus as inscribed in four Hebrew letters—*Iod* (the beginning), *He* (this one, the demonstrative), *Vau* (life, the copula), *Chet* (the passion, the suffering)—thereby declaring Jesus the principle of life's beginning, redeemed and perfected through passion. Don Juan de Sancta María, a converted Turk entertained at the Ghent castle court, questioned in *Perfección de la religión christiana* (Brussels, 1622) whether "Rabalistes" or "Talmudistes" appeared in historical records before Pic, finding no mentions in Arab chronologies, and concluding that Pic's antiquity claims were simply unbelievable—a skepticism that cast doubt on the entire edifice of Christian kabbalism's historical legitimacy. Francis Bacon (1560–1626), in *De dignitate et augmentis scientiarum* (English version 1605, Latin 1623), identified two cardinal excesses in scriptural interpretation: first, the presumption that Scripture contains all philosophy such that the reader may derive the entire natural world from biblical texts (a heresy he traces to Paracelsus and his followers, and more distantly to rabbinical and kabbalistic circles). Such seekers, Bacon argues, dishonor Scripture through their ravenous raids upon it seeking transitory and earthly things—committing a logical category error comparable to seeking the living among the dead or conversely seeking the dead among the living. His *Nova Atlantis* (1627) presents the utopian island of Ben Salem, adorned with Hebrew inscriptions and containing Solomon's House (the College of Six Days), dedicated to contemplating God's works and creatures through natural philosophy. The Jewish character Joabin, though circumcised and observant, acknowledges Jesus's superiority as the immaculate virgin's son and leader of the Seraphim. Joabin reveals that Ben Salem's Jewish laws derive from the "secret kabbalah" of Abraham and his son Nachor, laws awaiting fulfillment when the Messiah's Jerusalem shall be established and the king of Ben Salem shall stand at the Messiah's feet in subordination. This utopian vision reverberated through later literature: Joseph Hall's *Mundus alter et idem* and Thomas Bancroft's *Epigrams* celebrate a precious library containing Livy, the Oracles of Enoch, Solomon's Physics, and treasures predating the Flood—imaginative projections of how Christian kabbalah might be integrated into recovered human wisdom. Robert Fludd (1574–1637), author of *Philosophia Mosaica*, undertook the most comprehensive systematization of Christian kabbalah in the early seventeenth century, drawing on Pic, Archangelus de Burgonovo, Vigenère, and especially Reuchlin. Fludd repeatedly invoked Reuchlin's *De arte cabalistica* on the dark and luminous Aleph, a distinction Mersenne singled out as crucial to Fludd's cosmology. Fludd declared that the *Bereshith* method—deriving creation's order from the letters of the word *Bereshith* (In the Beginning)—permitted reading *R* as the void and darkness, *S* as the void subsequently filled with divine light, and *Aleph* as the means and principle filling and illuminating darkness. He extended this into three metaphysical principles (*incréé*, uncreated) and three physical principles mirroring YHWH (*Res, Het, Iod*—light, spirit, fire), and three microcosmic principles (body, soul, spirit), thereby constructing an elaborate hierarchical cosmology. Marin Mersenne's judgment was withering: Fludd's kabbalah either merely reproduces commonplace Catholic theology accessible to any educated Christian or proposes falsehoods and superstitions. When Fludd defended his position in *Stratum Bonum* (1629), distinguishing true (essential) kabbalah divided into *Bereshith* (chronological creation-order) and *Merkava* (notaric and temuraic letter-manipulation) from superstitious kabbalah (gematria, notaricon, temurah), Mersenne rejected the entire taxonomy indiscriminately, finding no meaningful differentiation between legitimate and superstitious practice—a refusal that marked the growing skepticism among Catholic authorities toward all forms of mystical hermeneutics. The intellectual path lay open for Milton, Henry More, and countless later English and European authors to associate Christian kabbalah with their speculations on cosmology, theology, and mysticism. English engagement, though chronologically tardier than French or Spanish, ultimately produced the most ambitious synthetic vision in figures like Fludd, whose sustained attempt to reconcile kabbalistic cosmology with Christian metaphysical doctrine represented simultaneously the peak and perhaps the exhaustion of Renaissance Christian kabbalah as a unified intellectual program—a system that would fragment in subsequent centuries into multiple competing interpretations and ultimately lose cultural authority as newer epistemologies superseded hermetic mysticism.
