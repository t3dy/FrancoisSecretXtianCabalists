Secret surveys a cohort of Catholic sympathizers and enthusiasts who embraced Kabbalah not cautiously but openly and without apologetic hedging, integrating it seamlessly into mainstream exegetical and devotional literature across multiple linguistic and regional contexts. Johannes Baptista Theatinus, publishing his work in 1520 at Ancona in the heartland of Italy, explicitly cited Cardinal Giles of Viterbo approvingly and drew extensively on the *Gale Razeia* (a kabbalistic compilation) and the *Bircat Cohanim* (the priestly blessing traditionally attributed to Ambrogio Traversari's Chaldean translation). Theatinus includes a striking kabbalistic secret in his work: the divine name Adonai was originally written in three dots by the Great Assembly (the authoritative rabbinic body), while Rabbi Lunagra's mystical saying that "this circle is not closed until God is enclosed in a circle with men" prefigures and anticipates the Christian doctrine of incarnation. His argumentative strategy traces this kabbalistic wisdom-tradition backward through classical authorities including Suidas and Jerome, and draws on rabbinic accounts (preserved in Postel's *Protevangelion* and circulating in other sources) of Jesus being presented before the Sanhedrin in his role as high priest—evidence, in Theatinus's interpretation, of early mystical overlap between Jewish and Christian traditions regarding Messianic identity. Ambrogio Fiandino (also known as Ambrogius Parthenopaeus), an Augustinian canon and bishop of Mantua celebrated as a "Christian Plato" for his syncretic philosophical-theological project, mounted vigorous defenses of Giles of Viterbo against the critiques of the naturalist philosopher Pomponacci while closely following the intellectual trajectories of Lazzarelli, Ficino, Pico, Reuchlin, and Rici, sometimes explicitly acknowledging these debts and sometimes appropriating their work without direct attribution. He borrowed entire passages wholesale from Reuchlin's *De arte cabalistica*, particularly the allegorical symbolism of the garden of nuts and golden apples, and similarly adopted Reuchlin's discussion of transmigration doctrine as a framework for understanding spiritual transformation. Aloysius Lippomanus, though criticized by Sixte de Sienne for strategically omitting Pico's *Heptaplus* from his compilations on the grounds that it constituted Platonism rather than authentic exegesis, nonetheless combined the Tetragrammatic explanations developed by both Rupert of Deutz and Francesco Giorgio in his *Catena in Genesim* (1546), offering an innovative reading in which Y represents the divine principle (God the Father), H represents "this" or concrete manifestation (the Son), V represents the copulative verb "is" (the Holy Spirit), with Lippomanus doubling the letter H to encode Christ's dual nature as both divine and human. Seraphinus Cumiranus, a Franciscan friar of Feltre and devoted disciple of Francesco Giorgio, published his *Conciliations of Scripture* as a compilation of 600 passages deliberately arranged to honor the perfect number six, explicitly and unapologetically adopting cabalist dogmas and frameworks despite his acknowledgment that Jewish practitioners in the modern period had degenerated from ancient mystical wisdom. His exegetical method seamlessly merges the kabbalistic concept of Justice and Mercy meeting in *Tiferet* (the sixth sephira corresponding to incarnation) with Psalmist theology, particularly the language of Psalm 85. He liberally invokes En-Soph (the infinite and transcendent Godhead beyond manifestation) and the ten divine "garments" or emanations (the sephiroth), interpreting the angel Metatron—traditionally understood in Jewish mysticism as conducting Moses—as the divine viceroy or representative through which the infinite Godhead communicates with the created realm. Archangelus of Burgonovo (the historical name Angiolo Pozzi, 1557–1569) became so thoroughly identified with Pico della Mirandola's philosophical and theological legacy that later scholars struggled to distinguish between Pico's original contributions and Archangelus's elaborations. Archangelus published detailed commentaries on Pico's famous 71 Conclusions (or Theses) with extensive kabbalistic exegeses demonstrating how each conclusion encoded or anticipated kabbalistic wisdom. His documented bibliography lists numerous unpublished treatises addressing *Sepher Yetzirah* (the Book of Formation), the 50 gates of intelligence, the 32 paths of wisdom, the Shem ha-mephorash (the divine name of 72 letters), the menorah, the sephirothic tree, the four-letter name (Tetragram), and apocalyptic interpretations of Kabbalah. Though some scholars have judged Archangelus's works as essentially plagiaristic compilations from his predecessors, Secret argues they merit serious study as repositories of otherwise lost kabbalistic commentary that would be entirely unavailable without these compilations. Faustino Tasso, a Franciscan friar and intellectual friend of the renowned poet Torquato Tasso, published his *Twenty Familiar Discourses on the Messiah's Coming* (1585) explicitly addressing its arguments to "learned rabbis and stubborn Hebrews," employing Galatin's celebrated interpolated Zohar passages (particularly the account of Simon bar Yohai discovering the Trinity through mystical revelation). Tasso innovates methodologically: he demonstrates that the three grammatical terminations of Jesus's name in Latin (*Iesus*, *Iesu*, *Iesum*) can be rearranged to spell out the Latin verb *Sum* (I am), a name exclusive to God in Christian theology and philosophy, thereby proving through Latin philological analysis what Jewish Kabbalah proves through Hebrew gematria. Marco Marini (1541–1594), an Augustinian canon and accomplished hebraist, deliberately chose symbolic titles for his major scholarly works—titling his Hebrew grammar *Gan Eden* (Garden of Eden) and his Hebrew dictionary *Thevath Noe* (Noah's Ark)—signifying Hebrew as the preeminent language of salvation and divine wisdom. Though the poet Desportes joked mockingly that Marini's Ark contained only worthless beasts, Marini cited Galatin's foundational Trinity text, drawing a dismissive response from Drusius, who exclaimed "enough joking; these are merely reveries of men wasting their leisure." In France, the Ligueur cordelier François Feuardent (1539–1610) demonstrated sophisticated kabbalistic knowledge in his 1576 edition and commentary on Irenaeus of Lyon, citing Giorgio, Agrippa, Reuchlin, and Trithemius while reproducing the ten divine names according to Jerome's enumeration. He explains the mystical mechanics whereby Eloha (God) metathesized becomes Ohel (tabernacle), while the Tetragram similarly reduces through transformation to the four-letter name in the same manner that moon reduces to or reflects sun. Pierre Crespet (1543–1594), a Celestine preacher and ecclesiastical polemicist, was reportedly (according to Labitte's historical account) an author of kabbalistic works, though his published writings focus narrowly on Lilith mythology and Jewish superstition. His 1604 *Discours catholiques* on the essence of the soul, however, treats the Christian Trinity explicitly under the mystical name Adonai, citing Maimonides, Pierre Alphonse, and Reuchlin for authority; he notably refuses to cite Paul Rici's works (which carried censorial disapproval) yet freely quotes Giorgio on numerical mysticism. Maurice Hylaret d'Angoulême (writing 1587) cited Hamerus, Margarita, Reuchlin, Galatin, and *Rabbenu Haccados*, offering Hebrew translations of the Ave Maria prayer with accompanying Latin exegesis assigning A to love or the Holy Spirit, V to truth or the Son, and E to eternity or the Father. Jean Benedicti (known variously as Benedictus Brito or Jean Benoît), a hebraist and traveler with extended time based in Jerusalem, authored the influential *Somme des péchez* (1584), a work frequently cited by the courtier and historian Brantôme. Benedicti frames his theological project as equivalent to the Jewish *Bath Kol* (daughter of voice, the oracular interpretation of divine will), and opens his work with cabalistic cosmology: he describes how visible and invisible realms interpenetrate, how within the intelligible, celestial, and elemental planes, uncreated and created nature interpenetrate and interweave, with the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit (one trinitarian God) serving as the eternal, infinite, incomprehensible source transcending and unifying all realms. Citing Heredia, Galatin, and Pico's Heptaplus, Benedicti compares the humanist Guy Le Fèvre de la Boderie to Pico himself as a kabbalistic initiate, declaring himself swept into the "high seas of secret theology" while simultaneously defending the tradition against Origenic errors concerning angelic creation as taught by both cabalists and Platonists. The final figure mentioned is Joannes Benedictus the Younger (c. 1490–1564), a Cracovian canon and correspondent of Erasmus, who addressed natural and divine visions in 1550, alluding to Kabbalah as a pathway to understanding God's glorious wisdom.
