Secret surveys Catholic sympathizers and enthusiasts who embraced Kabbalah without apology, embedding it in mainstream exegetical and devotional literature. Johannes Baptista Theatinus (published 1520, Ancona) cited Cardinal Giles of Viterbe approvingly and drew on the *Gale Razeia* and *Bircat Cohanim* (priestly blessing attributed to Ambrogio Traversari's Chaldean translation). Theatinus includes a kabbalistic secret: the name Adonai was written in three dots by the Great Assembly, and Rabbi Lunagra's saying "this circle is not closed until God is enclosed in a circle with men" prefigures incarnation. He traces the tradition through Suidas and Jerome, citing a rabbinic account (found in Postel's *Protevangelion* and other sources) of Jesus presented before the Sanhedrin as high priest—early testimony to Christian-Jewish mystical overlap. Ambrogio Fiandino (Ambrogius Parthenopaeus), Augustinian and bishop of Mantua, styled "Christian Plato," defended Giles of Viterbe against Pomponacci and closely followed Lazzarelli, Ficino, Pico, Reuchlin, and Rici, sometimes without attribution. He borrowed entire passages from Reuchlin's *De arte cabalistica* (the garden of nuts, golden apples symbolism) and transmigration doctrine. Aloysius Lippomanus, though criticized by Sixte de Sienne for omitting Pico's *Heptaplus* as Platonism rather than exegesis, combined Rupert's and Giorgio's Tetragrammatic explanation in his *Catena in Genesim* (1546), reading Y=principle (Father), H=this (Son), V=is (copula/Spirit), doubling H for Christ's dual nature. Seraphinus Cumiranus, Franciscan of Feltre and disciple of Francesco Giorgio, published *Conciliations of Scripture* (600 passages honoring the perfect number six), avowedly adopting cabalist dogmas despite Jewish modern degeneration. He seamlessly merges Justice and Mercy meeting in *Tiferet* (the sixth sephira corresponding to incarnation) with Psalmist theology (*Psalm 85*). He invokes En-Soph (the infinite Godhead beyond manifestation) and the ten divine "garments" (sephiroth), interpreting Metatron (the angel conducting Moses) as the divine viceroy. Archangelus of Burgonovo (Angiolo Pozzi, 1557–1569) became inseparable from Pico's legacy, publishing commentaries on the 71 Conclusions with extensive kabbalistic exegeses. His bibliography lists unpublished treatises on *Sepher Yetzirah*, the 50 gates of intelligence, 32 paths of wisdom, the Shem ha-mephorash, the menorah, the sephirothic tree, the four-letter name, and apocalyptic Kabbalah. Though judged plagiaristic by some, Burgonovo's works merit study as repositories of lost kabbalistic commentary. Faustino Tasso, Franciscan and friend of Torquato Tasso, published *Twenty Familiar Discourses on the Messiah's Coming* (1585), addressing "learned rabbis and stubborn Hebrews" with Galatin's interpolated Zohar (Simon bar Yohai discovering the Trinity). Tasso innovates: the three terminations of Jesus's name (*Iesus*, *Iesu*, *Iesum*) form the Latin verb *Sum* (I am), exclusive to God. Marco Marini (1541–1594), Augustinian canon and hebraist, titled his grammar *Gan Eden* (Garden of Eden) and his dictionary *Thevath Noe* (Noah's Ark), signifying Hebrew as the language of salvation. Though the poet Desportes joked the Ark contained worthless beasts, Marini cited Galatin's Trinity text, drawing Drusius's dismissal: "enough joking; these are reveries of men wasting leisure." In France, the Ligueur cordelier François Feuardent (1539–1610) demonstrated kabbalistic knowledge in his 1576 edition of Irenaeus, citing Giorgio, Agrippa, Reuchlin, Trithemius, and reproducing the ten divine names per Jerome. He explains that Eloha (God) metathesized becomes Ohel (tabernacle); the Tetragram likewise reduces to the four-letter name as moon to sun. Pierre Crespet (1543–1594), Celestine preacher and polemicist, was reportedly an author of kabbalistic books (Labitte's claim), though his published works focus narrowly on Lilith and Jewish superstition. His 1604 *Discours catholiques* on the soul's essence treats the Trinity under the name Adonai, citing Maimonides, Pierre Alphonse, and Reuchlin; he notably refuses to cite Rici's works (censured) yet quotes Giorgio on numerical mysticism. Maurice Hylaret d'Angoulême (1587) cited Hamerus, Margarita, Reuchlin, Galatin, and *Rabbenu Haccados*, offering Hebrew translations of Ave Maria with Latin exegesis: A=love/Spirit, V=truth/Son, E=eternity/Father. Jean Benedicti (called various forms—Benedictus Brito, Jean Benoît), hebraist and traveler (Jerusalem-based), authored the *Somme des péchez* (1584), which Brantôme frequently cited. Benedicti frames his work as equivalent to Jewish *Bath Kol* (daughter of voice, oracular interpretation) and opens with cabalistic cosmology: between visible and invisible, within intelligible, celestial, and elemental realms, uncreated and created nature interpenetrate—the Father, Son, Holy Spirit (one trinitarian God) as eternal, infinite, incomprehensible source. Citing Heredia, Galatin, and the Heptaplus, Benedicti compares Guy Le Fèvre de la Boderie to Pico, declaring himself swept into "high seas of secret theology" while defending against Origenic errors on angelic creation taught by cabalists and Platonists. The final reference is to Joannes Benedictus the Younger (c. 1490–1564), Cracovian canon and Erasmus correspondent, who addressed natural and divine visions in 1550, alluding to Kabbalah in relation to God's glorious wisdom.
