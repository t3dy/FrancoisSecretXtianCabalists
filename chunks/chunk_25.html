Chunk 25

Pages 79-81

Giorgio of Venice unfolds a sophisticated vision of cosmological correspondence wherein the terrestrial Tabernacle, divinely shown to Moses, serves as both a textual map and architectural blueprint for understanding the entire created order. At the apex of this cosmic edifice stand three supernal luminaries—transcendent beyond the ten Sephiroth themselves—which Giorgio identifies simultaneously with the Christian Trinity and with the three supreme Patriarchs (Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob), thus collapsing the distinction between Christian theology and Jewish mystical tradition. This supernal triad constitutes "name and substance at the root of roots," a phrase drawn from Giorgio's sustained engagement with the Zohar and Bahya ben Asher's Kabbalistic commentary on Exodus. The Tabernacle, then, becomes a condensed microcosm (*Olam Katon*, the "small world" or "world of separation") that mirrors human nature itself—the seeker standing as a miniature cosmos through which the entire celestial hierarchy can be contemplated and traversed. Giorgio systematizes this unified cosmos through a theological principle rooted in the dual attributes from which all creation flows: divine judgment and divine mercy. These twin divine forces find their earthly counterpart in the two master architect-artisans of the Tabernacle—Bezalel and Aholiab—whose collaborative craftsmanship becomes a model for understanding how divine contradiction (severity and gentleness) resolves into harmonious creation. This principle of universal correspondence, which Giorgio traces to ancient philosophers like Anaxagoras and Hippocrates, reaches its perfection in the hands of Hebrew mystics, who recognized that all elements of the cosmic and natural orders participate in a unified system of mutual reflection. The sacred language of Hebrew itself is not arbitrary but carries intrinsic mystical power embedded within its structure. The twenty-two letters of the Hebrew alphabet, when combined with the ten Sephiroth, generate the thirty-two paths of Wisdom—the very pathways through which God spoke the world into being and through which the adept may ascend toward the divine. Giorgio elaborates a comprehensive taxonomy of Hebrew letters that distributes divine functions across the alphabet. Three mother letters (Aleph representing air, Mem representing water, and Shin representing fire) serve as governors of the physical elements themselves. Seven double letters (the Begadkephrath letters: Beth, Gimel, Daleth, Kaph, Phe, and Resh with their doubled forms) correspond to the seven classical planets and their celestial spheres. The remaining twelve simple letters govern the twelve signs of the zodiac, establishing that the entire astronomical order finds itself encoded within the Hebrew grammar. This systematization transforms the study of grammar into a form of cosmology—every conjugation of a verb, every positioning of a letter becomes a lesson in how creation operates and how the microcosm of human consciousness mirrors the macrocosm of the heavens. The section demonstrates Giorgio's extraordinary virtuosity with gematria, the numerological interpretation of Hebrew words and names. When the letter *He* is added to Abram's name (totaling 243 in numerical value), the result is Abraham (248)—a number that Giorgio proves corresponds both to the total number of human bones and to the count of affirmative commandments in the Torah. This is no mere coincidence but evidence of a profound cosmic integration: the human body contains within itself the entire structure of divine law. In an even more audacious maneuver, Giorgio identifies the angel Metatron, the celestial Prince of Faces and divine intermediary, through numerical calculation (alternately yielding 999 and 290). He then demonstrates that Miriam or Mary—the Virgin Mother—yields the same numerical value (290 in Hebrew), leading to his startling conclusion that the name Metatron represents not a specific angelic person but rather an "office" or function, one that could apply equally to the Virgin as she stands as humanity's chosen intermediary before God's throne. This redefinition dissolves the boundary between Jewish angelology and Christian Mariology, making the Kabbalistic system itself a vehicle for validating Christian mysteries. The chapter culminates in Giorgio's intricate numerological meditation on the divine name Jesus in its Hebrew form (YSU), demonstrating how the Tetragrammaton (YHWH) achieves its fulfillment precisely through the Incarnate Word. The number six—the first perfect number, whose divisors (one, two, three) sum to itself—becomes Giorgio's emblem for creation itself, for the six days of Genesis and the six dimensions of space that constitute the universe. By this logic, all cosmic creation participates in and is sustained by the Incarnate Word; every atom of matter contains within itself a trace of Jesus's redemptive presence. The section thus presents a vision of mystical cosmology where Hebrew letters become instruments of divine creation, numerical values encode the structure of reality, and the Tabernacle—both as architectural fact and as textual description—functions as the supreme teaching tool for understanding how all of existence, from the human body to the celestial spheres, forms a unified whole expressing the will of a God who has revealed himself in flesh.
