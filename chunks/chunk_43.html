Secret opens by juxtaposing three authoritative voices on Christian Kabbalah's historical trajectory, each illuminating a different facet of the tradition's paradoxical status. Eugenio Anagnine articulates the central paradox: the same papal authority that proclaimed Kabbalah holy in the sixteenth century—with Pope Sixtus IV himself offering endorsement—would later condemn it as heretical and fraudulent during the Counter-Reformation, rebranding it as fundamentally Jewish deception. This reversal was not a minor theological quibble but a wholesale institutional reversal that forced Christian Kabbalists to navigate increasingly treacherous doctrinal waters. J.L. Blau introduces nuance into this chronology, observing that the tradition's reception among major Catholic and Protestant intellectuals—figures like Reuchlin, Pico, Erasmus, and Mersenne—did not follow denominational lines as one might expect. Instead, agreement or disagreement correlated with individual intellectual stature and personal predilection rather than religious allegiance. Guy Casaril adds a further paradox: those practitioners labeled as "occultist and heretical" by ecclesiastical authorities nonetheless invested themselves entirely in proving Christian truth through kabbalistic methods, creating an ironic inversion where those deemed most dangerous were animated by orthodox religious conviction.

Secret argues that these scholarly judgments, while factually grounded in historical documentation, require careful contextualization within a far more complex chronology than simple Reformation versus Counter-Reformation binaries suggest. The received narratives oversimplify genuine institutional and intellectual complexity. He proposes a methodological reorientation: away from broad categorical sweeps that flatten distinct voices and contexts, and toward meticulous attention to individual personalities, their specific motivations, local circumstances, and legacy. This approach demands patient monographic treatment of individual figures rather than synthetic generalizations. The chapter consequently shifts focus to examine convertite Judaism—converts from Judaism to Christianity—and their role, which Secret reconceives not as apostasy or betrayal but as the primary vehicle through which Christian Kabbalah was actually disseminated, developed, and institutionalized within Catholic Christianity.

Secret carefully acknowledges that not all Hebrew scholars and converts engaged with Kabbalah as an intellectual or spiritual resource. He cites specific counterexamples: Emmanuel Tremellius and the descendants of Elia Levita, whose scholarly work in Hebrew exegesis and linguistic study occurred independently of kabbalistic traditions. Yet the converts who did engage with Kabbalah—and there were numerous such figures operating across several generations—proved extraordinarily influential in shaping Christian Kabbalah's trajectory and institutional embedding. The bulk of Secret's analysis in this section concentrates on Sixte de Sienne (1520–1569), whose life and work exemplify the complex negotiations required of convertite scholars navigating ecclesiastical suspicion. Originally a Franciscan, Sixte converted to the Dominican order and narrowly escaped burning for heresy only through intervention by the future Pope Pius V, an event that profoundly shaped his cautious approach to kabbalistic theology. Secret portrays Sixte as a strategically sophisticated exegete who managed to salvage the Zohar from the flames of Spanish Inquisitorial destruction while simultaneously permitting the Talmud to be consigned to those same flames—a calculated distinction that worked to Christian Kabbalah's advantage. Sixte's magnum opus, the Bibliotheca sancta, established a canonical framework that would prove enormously influential: the sharp distinction between true Kabbalah, which illuminates Scripture through anagogy (the spiritual or mystical sense), and false Kabbalah, which degenerates into occult superstition, demonology, and necromantic fraud. By maintaining this rigid categorical distinction, Sixte shielded Christian Kabbalah from papal condemnation while simultaneously invoking patristic authorities—Jerome, Irenaeus, and others—to legitimize mystical exegesis as an established and venerable Christian methodological tradition rooted in early Church practice.
