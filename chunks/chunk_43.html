Chunk 43

Pages 135-137

Secret opens by juxtaposing three authoritative voices on Christian Kabbalah's historical fate. Eugenio Anagnine notes the paradox of elevation followed by erasure: proclaimed holy by sixteenth-century papal authority (including Pope Sixtus IV's endorsement), Kabbalah was declared heretical and fraudulent during the Counter-Reformation, rebranded as Jewish deception. J.L. Blau qualifies this trajectory, observing that major Catholic and Protestant intellectuals—Reuchlin, Pico, Erasmus, Mersenne—disagreed on the tradition's value according to their own intellectual stature, not denominational allegiance. Guy Casaril paradoxically notes that Christian Kabbalah's "occultist and heretical" practitioners nonetheless devoted themselves to proving Christian truth. Secret argues these judgments, though factually grounded, require contextualization within a more complex chronology beyond simple Reformation/Counter-Reformation binaries. He proposes a methodology centered on individual personalities and patient monographs rather than broad categorical sweeps. The chapter shifts focus to examine convertite Judaism's role—not as apostasy but as the primary vehicle for Christian Kabbalah's dissemination. Secret acknowledges that not all converts engaged with Kabbalah, citing examples of Hebrew scholars (Emmanuel Tremellius, descendants of Elia Levita) for whom Kabbalah played no role. Yet converts who addressed Kabbalah were numerous and influential. The bulk of the section introduces Sixte de Sienne (1520–1569), a Franciscan-turned-Dominican saved from burning for heresy by the future Pope Pius V. Secret paints Sixte as a strategic exegete who salvages the Zohar from Spanish Inquisitorial fire while permitting the Talmud's destruction. Sixte's *Bibliotheca sancta* establishes a canonical distinction: true Kabbalah illuminates Scripture through anagogy (spiritual sense), while false Kabbalah is occult superstition, demonology, and necromantic fraud. By distinguishing true from false, Sixte shields Christian Kabbalah from papal condemnation while using patristic authority (Jerome, Irenaeus) to legitimize mystical exegesis as an established Christian method.
