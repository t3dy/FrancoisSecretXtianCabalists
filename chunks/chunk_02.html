Chunk 2

Pages 5-9

The chunk finishes the **Avant-propos**, where Secret describes his historical-thematic chapter plan using a filing method inspired by Valéry Larbaud: thirteen chapters that move from foundational ancestors through geographical and biographical regions, then conclude with religious reactions and the 17th-century afterlife of Christian Kabbalah. He begins with Pico's claimed "ancestors" — the patristic and medieval witnesses to an oral law — then traces the beginnings in Spain with the medieval apologetic tradition. The Italian milieu occupies three chapters: the period of the two crucial converts Flavius Mithridates and Paul de Heredia; then the *grande époque* of the Ricci family, the Giustiniani, Galatino, Teseo Ambrogio, Francesco Giorgio Veneto, and Egidio da Viterbo; then Agrippa and the spread of Christian Kabbalah northward. A chapter on Germany and Reuchlin follows. The French section — the richest — covers Postel and his intellectual heirs, especially the Le Fèvre de la Boderie brothers (Jean-Antoine and Nicolas) and the polymath Blaise de Vigenère. Spain reappears, then England, before closing chapters on religious reactions (Catholic and Protestant), on the occult philosophy that absorbed Kabbalah, and on the 17th-century survival and transformation of the movement. The **Table des matières** confirms this thirteen-chapter architecture in full, providing a roadmap that reveals Secret's interpretive priorities: the concentration on the Italian (three chapters) and French (three chapters) nodes of the tradition, the strategic positioning of Spain as both source and secondary development, and the attention to the afterlife and opposition that shaped Christian Kabbalah's legacy. The **Table des planches** lists the iconographic and textual plates that accompany the text: portraits of key figures such as Mithridates (from a Vatican manuscript), Galatino, Postel (the "Restitutor"), Vigenère, and the Le Fèvre de la Boderie brothers; and plates of important primary texts including the Syriac New Testament title page (illustrating the trilingual scholarship that made Kabbalah accessible) and the "séphirotique vision of St. John" (the application of Sefiratic diagrams to the Apocalypse). A comprehensive transcription table then provides the Hebrew alphabet with Renaissance variants, diacritical marks, and **numerical values** (**gematria**), the mystical arithmetic that enables letter-value exegesis. Examples from kabbalistic vocabulary illustrate the system: *Bina* (intelligence, the third Sefira), *Hesed* (mercy, the fourth), *Yesod* (foundation, the ninth), *Keter* (crown, the first and transcendent), *Sefira* (the countable units of divinity), *Shekinah* (the divine presence, the tenth Sefira), each with its numerical equivalent essential to the symbolic permutations that follow through the text. **Chapter I, "Les ancêtres de la kabbale chrétienne,"** opens with Giovanni **Pico della Mirandola** (1463-1494), celebrated as the "Phénix de son âge" (Phoenix of his age) — a prodigious genius with legendary capacities of memory and intellect. Pico had announced his intention to defend publicly his 900 theses *de omni scibili et quibusdam aliis* (on everything knowable and some other things besides), a comprehensive synthesis spanning theology, philosophy, magic, and esoteric knowledge. A mythic aura surrounded Pico from his lifetime onward; Secret notes that Guillaume Postel, writing a century later, saw himself mirrored in Pico as a visionary integrator of all knowledge. Secret emphasizes Pico's explicit claim — articulated in his *Apologia* (1493) defending the theses against their condemnation — that he was the first to mention Kabbalah explicitly in Latin, a claim corroborated by **Reuchlin** in his *De arte cabalistica* (1517), by Egidio da Viterbo, and by Pierre **Galatino** in his *De arcanis catholicae veritatis* (1518). Reuchlin himself established Latin distinctions that would persist: *Cabalici* (those practicing Kabbalah), *Cabalei* (those transmitting it), *Cabalistae* (Kabbalists proper). Secret then reconstructs Pico's core theological argument from the magical conclusions and defenses. **Thesis 9** of the magical conclusions held that nothing proves Christ's divinity more powerfully than magic and Kabbalah — a claim so startling that the 1487 papal condemnation targeted it specifically. Pico defended this radical assertion by recourse to a theory of the **double Law**: alongside the written Pentateuch and the visible commandments, God gave Moses on Mount Sinai a secret oral explanation (*Torah she-be'al-peh*) that clarified and deepened the literal sense, entrusted only to seventy sages and transmitted through generations by "reception" or "handing over" — the very meaning of *kabbalah*. The oral law, Pico argued, contains the true mystical sense of Scripture, accessible only through the kabbalistic arts. To legitimate this claim against theological skepticism, he marshaled a series of Christian patristic and biblical witnesses: the text of **IV Esdras** (the apocryphal fourth book attributed to Ezra), which speaks of 70 hidden books containing "the veins of intelligence and the source of wisdom"; **Origen**'s commentary on Romans, where Origen refers to the "oracles of God" in explicit identification with the *Torah she-be'al-peh*; **Hilary of Poitiers** on Psalm II, interpreting the "seventy elders" of the Sanhedrin as guardians of this secret wisdom; and the Gospel passages on Jesus's promise of hidden truth to his disciples. By marshaling these authorities, Pico established a lineage through Christian antiquity back to the apostolic age for the doctrine of an oral, esoteric tradition accessible through the Hebraic letters and names of God.
