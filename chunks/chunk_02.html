Secret concludes his Avant-propos by outlining his thirteen-chapter architecture, which organizes Christian Kabbalah both geographically and chronologically. He adopts the organizational philosophy of Valéry Larbaud (1881–1957), the French poet-novelist and polyglot, who proposed filing method aimed at presenting "all known facts concerning the subject clearly and according to the order of time and the relationships of cause and effect." The opening chapter treats the theological and patristic "ancestors" (ancêtres) whose authority Pico invoked to legitimize Kabbalah as Christian doctrine. The second chapter turns to Spain, where medieval Christian apologetic literature began incorporating kabbalistic elements before the late 15th-century expulsion of Jews from Iberia. The third chapter situates two crucial Jewish converts—Flavius Mithridates and Paul de Heredia—alongside Pico in the Italian milieu that gave birth to Christian Kabbalah. A fourth chapter is devoted to Jean Reuchlin, "the first and most illustrious disciple of Pico della Mirandola," whose German humanist scholarship codified Christian Kabbalistic method. Five chapters then traverse the "golden age" proper: the Italian Renaissance centers—particularly Florence, Rome, and Venice—with figures like the Ricci brothers (Paul and Augustine), Augustine Giustiniani, Pierre Galatino, Teseo Ambrogio, Francesco Giorgio of Venice, and above all Gilles de Viterbe (Egidio da Viterbo), the cardinal whose manuscripts remained central to understanding Christian Kabbalah. A chapter on German developments tracks Conrad Pellican, Paul Fagius, and Jean Pistorius, author of the first comprehensive bibliography of Christian Kabbalah. The French section, reflecting the richness of French Kabbalist production, spans three chapters covering Guillaume Postel, the Le Fèvre de la Boderie brothers (Jean-Antoine and Nicolas), and Blaise de Vigenère. Spain reappears as a secondary but persistent center; England receives its own chapter as a late but vigorous node of the tradition. Two final thematic chapters address religious reactions—both Catholic defensive reactions and Protestant assimilations—and the absorption of Kabbalah into the broader currents of occult philosophy and symbolism that characterized the late 16th and 17th centuries, before a concluding chapter surveys the transformation of Christian Kabbalah in the first half of the 17th century.

The Table des matières (Table of Contents) confirms this architecture and reveals Secret's hermeneutic priorities. The concentration of three chapters on Italy and three on France reflects the tradition's heartland; the positioning of Spain as both originating source (medieval apologetic) and secondary elaboration reveals the impact of the 1492 expulsion, which dispersed Spanish Jews and their intellectual traditions northward and eastward. The chapters on England and Spain in the latter 16th century show the tradition's geographic reach even as its intensity concentrated in Italy and France. The final thematic chapters on religious reactions and on the occult absorption of Kabbalah signal Secret's interest in reception history and in understanding not merely what Christian Kabbalists wrote, but how their work was received, disputed, and transformed by subsequent currents. The Table des planches (Table of Plates) catalogs the iconographic and textual illustrations that accompany the work, establishing a visual apparatus that reinforces the narrative. Portraits of key figures are reproduced: a portrait of Mithridates drawn from Vatican manuscripts, establishing the visual presence of this shadowy Jewish teacher; Pierre Galatino, one of the central architects of Christian Kabbalistic apologetics; Guillaume Postel, shown in his gravity and intensity; Guy and Nicolas Le Fèvre de la Boderie, brothers whose collaborative kabbalistic scholarship bridged the late 16th century; and Blaise de Vigenère, the polyglot commentator. Pages from important manuscripts and printed texts are reproduced in photographic facsimile: the title page of the Syriac New Testament, illustrating the multilingual biblical scholarship that made Hebrew manuscripts and interpretation available to Christian readers; the famous "sepirotique vision of Saint John" (Vision séphirotique de saint Jean), a visual application of the Sefiratic diagram to the Johannine Apocalypse, showing how Christian Kabbalists adapted Jewish mystical symbolism to New Testament theology. A page from the Candelabrum (a major Kabbalist text) in Hebrew demonstrates the density of the original textual tradition. These plates serve not merely as ornament but as evidence of the intellectual apparatus and visual symbolism through which Christian Kabbalists thought and taught.

The Transcription and Numerical Value of the Hebrew Letters table establishes the philological and mystical foundations necessary for understanding the rest of the work. Secret provides the twenty-two letters of the Hebrew alphabet with their standard French transcriptions and variant transcriptions used by Renaissance Hebraists, whose orthographic inconsistencies reflect the difficulty of representing Eastern languages in Western alphabets. More importantly, he provides the numerical values (valeur numérique)—the gematric equivalences—that underlie mystical exegesis: Aleph (1), Bet (2), Gimel (3), continuing through Tav (400), the final letter. The numerical system reaches its power through the principle of gematria, the mystical arithmetic that allows the substitution of words with equivalent numerical values, revealing hidden identities and theological correspondences. The table provides exemplary kabbalistic vocabulary with transliteration and meaning: Bina (the third Sefira, "intelligence," from the root binah, understanding), Hesed or Chesed (the fourth Sefira, "mercy" or "lovingkindness"), Yesod (the ninth Sefira, "foundation," the collector and transmitter of all higher emanations into the material world), Keter (the first and most transcendent Sefira, "crown," representing pure unknowable divinity beyond all differentiation), Sefira itself (plural Sefirot, the "countable" or "luminous" emanations of divinity), and Shekinah (the tenth and manifest Sefira, "dwelling" or "presence," the indwelling presence of God in creation and the feminine aspect of divinity in kabbalistic symbolism). Each term carries centuries of mystical commentary, and Christian Kabbalists would draw on this entire apparatus to map divine action and cosmic order. The alphabet itself becomes a kind of mystical cosmography: the first letter Aleph represents absolute unity; the subsequent letters unfold the drama of creation, differentiation, and manifestation; the final letter Tav represents the end-point and sign of completion. Christian Kabbalists would obsessively explore how this system could be harmonized with Christian numerology, the mystical arithmetic of the Trinity (the number 3), the four gospels (4), the five wounds of Christ (5), the six days of creation (6), and the sacred sevens and twelves that structure biblical revelation.

Chapter I, titled "Les 'ancêtres' de la kabbale chrétienne," opens with Giovanni Pico della Mirandola (1463–1494), presented as the towering genius of the age—the "Phoenix of his age" (Phénix de son âge), whose almost superhuman memory and intellectual capacities became legendary even in his lifetime. The text reconstructs the myth: when Pico was born, a ball of fire allegedly appeared in his mother's chamber; his memory was such that after hearing a poem read aloud once, he could recite it backwards, beginning from the final verse; Guillaume Postel, a century later, saw himself reflected in Pico's visionary genius and wrote of "the divine, truly angelic, and more-than-human spirit that inhabited the Lord Pico della Mirandola." At fourteen, Pico abandoned canon law for philosophy and languages, and his trajectory became meteoric—studies at Bologna, Ferrara, Padua, Paris, and finally Florence, where he entered the circle of Lorenzo the Magnificent. His insatiable appetite for learning encompassed Greek and Latin, Arabic and Hebrew authorities, and at twenty-three he proposed to defend publicly his 900 theses (Conclusiones nongentae), announced as covering de omni scibili et quibusdam aliis (on everything knowable and some other things besides)—a claim of encyclopedic comprehensiveness that scandalized many contemporaries. Historian Lucien Febvre later dismissed these theses dismissively as "ravings scattered through fat volumes," a judgment Secret gently disputes, noting that Pico's complete works, even when combined with those of his cousin Jean-François Pico, constitute only the smallest of the collected infolios—a textual irony that reveals how Pico's legend has often overshadowed scholarly knowledge of his actual corpus.

Yet it is precisely through this legend of comprehensive genius that Pico's historical significance emerges. He is remembered as having "introduced Kabbalah into humanist domains," a tradition established by Pico's own claim in his Apologia (1493), written to defend the 900 theses against ecclesiastical censure: "I believe myself to be the first to have made explicit mention of Kabbalah." This claim was swiftly corroborated by subsequent authorities. Jean Reuchlin, writing in his De arte cabalistica (1517), reports that one of his dialogue's Jewish interlocutors, Simeon, declares: "Those called in Hebrew Mekablim are designated in Latin by the terms Cabalistes or Cabalichi since Jean Pico della Mirandola, before whom this name was unknown in the Roman language." In 1517, Gilles de Viterbe (Cardinal Egidio da Viterbo), himself no admirer of Pico's erudition (preferring Reuchlin's more systematic approach), nonetheless recognized Pico's priority in Kabbalah, and this tradition was fixed in place by Pierre Galatino's De arcanis catholicae veritatis (1518), written explicitly to defend Reuchlin against detractors and perpetually reprinted alongside Reuchlin's works. The legend crystallized: Pico was the Latinator of Kabbalah, the man who had brought Jewish mystical terminology into the vocabulary of Christian intellectual discourse.

Secret emphasizes, however, that understanding Pico's role requires attention to the context of his claim and to the intellectual milieu in which he developed his thought. The famous declaration of Pico's was not simply pride or novelty-seeking; it was a deliberate rhetorical move in his Apologia, aimed at giving historical ancestors to the radical assertion that triggered ecclesiastical suspicion. Thesis 9 of the magical conclusions (Conclusiones magicae iuxta propriam opinionem), among the thirteen theses examined by the papal commission convened by Pope Innocent VIII, declared boldly: "There is no science which gives us more certainty concerning the divinity of Christ than magic and Kabbalah." This assertion was inflammatory for multiple reasons: it invoked magic—a term fraught with associations of demonic dealing; it placed Kabbalah on a plane of theological authority equivalent to Christian theology; it suggested that Jewish esoteric knowledge could demonstrate Christian doctrines. Against the inevitable objections that magic and Kabbalah were demonic, contemptible, or fraudulent, Pico mobilized a historical and textual defense. Reuchlin reports the ridicule that met the term "cabale": "The mere word appeared so horrible to the ears of the learned that when they heard 'Cabalistes,' they thought it must refer not to men but to hirco-cerfs [chimeras], centaurs, or some other monster." Pico's response was to recontextualize Kabbalah entirely within a framework of legitimate mystical theology, grounded in Scripture and patristic authority. His Apologia accordingly marshals a series of Christian witnesses to establish that God had given Moses not merely the written Law but a secret oral explanation (Torah she-be'al-peh, the Oral Law) transmitted to seventy sages and passed down through generations via "reception" or "handing over"—the very meaning of kabbalah.

The theological argument that Pico constructs is intricate and deliberately designed to prevent any gap between Jewish mystical tradition and Christian doctrine. He asserts that beyond the written Pentateuch, God imparted to Moses on Mount Sinai the true meaning of the Law, containing "the manifestation of all the mysteries that are contained beneath the rind and coarse face of the words." This double law—literal and spiritual—Pico grounds through a series of authorities that he claims are Christian authorities, not Jewish ones. He names five Christian witnesses to this doctrine: Esdras (from the apocryphal IV Esdras, also called II Esdras), Paul the Apostle, Origen of Alexandria, Hilary of Poitiers, and references to the Gospels. From IV Esdras 14:45-47 (the apocryphal fourth book of Ezra), Pico cites the passage where the Most High speaks to Ezra: "Publish openly the twenty-four books that you have written, that the worthy and unworthy may read them; but keep the seventy last books secret, to be given to the wise of your people. For in these books is the spring of understanding, the fountain of wisdom, and the stream of knowledge." Pico interprets these "seventy secret books" as the mystical tradition of Kabbalah, hidden from the masses but entrusted to the wise. From Origen's commentary on Romans 3:2 ("What advantage have the Jews, or what is the value of circumcision?"), Pico paraphrases Origen's interpretation: Origen states that the Hebrews were entrusted with the "oracles of God" (eloquia Dei) beyond mere written law. Pico argues that this must refer to what the Hebrews call Torah she-be'al-peh, the oral law (Torah scebealpe), "the law of the mouth," which, because it is received by inheritance, is called cabale. The written law alone is dead; it requires the spirit of interpretation to be vivified. Hilary of Poitiers, in his commentary on Psalm II, provides testimony to the seventy elders: "There were already, since Moses, seventy doctors before the establishment of the Synagogue. For Moses himself, who had put into writing the words of the Old Testament, entrusted separately to the seventy elders some of the most secret mysteries of the secrets of the Law." Pico reads this as Christian patristic confirmation that Moses had transmitted secret wisdom only to seventy chosen sages, paralleling the structure of the Jewish Sanhedrin's governance of the people.

The intellectual sophistication of Pico's defense lies in his reconstruction of a genealogy of oral tradition through Christian authorities, making Kabbalah not a Jewish eccentricity but a mystical science attested within the Christian patristic tradition itself. He adds the authority of Saint Jerome (348–420), the great Vulgate translator and Hebrew scholar, who repeatedly referenced his "Hebrew masters" (magistri Hebraei) and invoked rabbinic interpretation against purely literal reading. For those who might object that Jerome refers to other Jewish authorities—the philosophers (philosophi) or talmudists (talmudisti)—Pico devises a subtle taxonomy: the Hebrew school divides into three sects: the philosophers (rationalists like Maimonides and Ibn Sina), the Kabbalists (the esotericists), and the Talmudists (the legal interpreters). When Church Fathers speak of wisdom received from the Hebrew tradition, they must necessarily mean the Kabbalists, for the philosophy of the Jews arose late (with Maimonides, "the Egyptian Rabi," contemporaneous with Averroes of Cordoba, dead "some 300 years" ago—a chronological reference that places Maimonides correctly at the late 12th century). The Talmud itself was composed more than 150 years after Christ's death, so Church Fathers like Clement could not have invoked it. Ergo, when Jerome and the other Fathers speak of the Hebrew mysteries, they allude to Kabbalah. In the Heptaplus (Seven Interpretations of Genesis), Pico reinforces this argument by appealing to a strict rule of the ancient Hebrews recorded by Jerome: no one may engage in the Ma'aseh Bereshit (the mystical account of creation) before reaching maturity, a constraint that legitimates Kabbalah as advanced theology for the spiritually mature, not as popular superstition available to all.

The closing movements of this section show how Pico's rhetorical strategy gradually consolidated into a tradition. His successors did not simply repeat his authorities but elaborated them, adding new patristic witnesses and new hermeneutic techniques. Jerome's specific examples of mystical exegesis became touchstones. The addition of the letter He to Abraham's name (from Avram to Avraham) and to Sarah's (from Sarai to Sarah), indicating heightened spiritual status and the entry of divine influx, became a standard exemplum of Hebrew letter-mysticism grounded in a Church Father. The Ath-Bash cipher—the reversal of the Hebrew alphabet, in which Aleph pairs with Tav, Bet with Shin, etc.—whereby the city name Babel (Bavel) becomes Sheshach in Jeremiah 25:26, similarly anchored Jewish mystical technique in Christian antiquity. Reuchlin would later provide full, careful expositions of this cipher. The ten divine names (aseret shem ha-shem, the ten designations of the divine in Jewish theology), each descending in power but increasing in concrete manifestation, culminating in the ineffable Tetragrammaton (Iod-He-Vau-He, the four-letter name of God that cannot be pronounced aloud), became a crucial framework that every Christian Kabbalist elaborated. The mystical meaning of the Hebrew aleph-bet itself—each letter bearing symbolic and theological significance—became another resource that would be endlessly mined. Claude Duret, in his massive Thrésor de l'histoire des langues (1613), would quote Jerome at length explaining the symbolic sense of each Hebrew letter: Aleph (discipline, the first letter, unity), Bet (house, duality of creation), Gimel (fullness of voice, carrying), and so through all twenty-two letters, each yielding a message of cosmic order and divine pedagogy. Irenaeus of Lyon's ancient testimony regarding the name of Jesus—divinely encoded, conveying that He is "Master who contains heaven and earth"—would be glossed by Renaissance Hebraists like Drusius to show how the very name of the Savior was encoded in Hebrew mystical orthography. Even Tertullian's polemical account of the Gnostic heretics Marcus and Colorbasus, who misused letter-mysticism (ludibria litularum), paradoxically testified to the power and attraction of these techniques—danger itself an attestation of power. And the monumental De laudibus Crucis (On the Praises of the Cross) of Raban Maur (776–856), which would be reissued in 1503 at Pforzheim under Reuchlin's patronage, exemplified what Secret calls "the true Christian Kabbalah"—a medieval synthesis in which prose and verse collaborated with twenty-eight mystical figures to expound the mysteries of the faith and the mystical numbers of angels, winds, the seven gifts of the Holy Spirit, and the eight beatitudes. Pico's defense, in short, inaugurated a hermeneutic tradition: the mining of patristic and biblical authorities for evidence of a Christian mystical science that could be harmonized with Jewish Kabbalah.
