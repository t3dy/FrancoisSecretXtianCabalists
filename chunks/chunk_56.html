Renaissance kabbalah spreads widely through Tommaso Garzoni's *Piazza universale* (1585), which includes a chapter on cabalistes citing Reuchlin's bibliography and distinguishing kabbalah from the Lullian art. Garzoni knew Abramo Colorni, a Hebrew engineer of Mantua, and circulated knowledge of works by Camillo, Augustin Rici, and Postel's *Candelabrum*. In France, Ponthus de Tyard (1521-1605) translated León Hebreo's *Dialogues of Love* (1551) and manifested keen interest in kabbalah through his library, which contained works by Galatin, Pico, François Georges, and a Postel manuscript. His *Univers* and later *Deux discours* (1578) repeat correspondences between the seven sephiroth and planets, and cosmology from the *Sefer Yetzirah*. In *De la droite imposition des noms* (1603), he mocks Rabelais, punning on the Hebrew roots of his name. Antoine du Verdier's *Prosopographie* adopts Vigenère's doctrine of three worlds and three sciences, mapping celestial and earthly realms to Kabbalistic *Tiferet* and *Malcuth*. André Thevet, the Franciscan traveler who encountered Postel in the Orient, largely avoids explicit kabbalah discussion in his *Vrais pourtraicts*, though his *Cosmographie du Levant* (1554) affirms kabbalah as "more ancient than other doctrines and truly above all expositions." Jacob Prævost (1570) adds judicial astrology, magic, Lullian art, and Hebrew kabbalah to classical studies. Jean Demons, seigneur d'Hedicourt—whom Nodier mistakenly classified among literary madmen—published elaborate theological-magical treatises (*La démonstration*, 1594; *La Sextessence dialectique*, 1595) enumerating the ten divine names in Hebrew with mystical commentary. His work, drawing on Pic, Burgonovo, Ricius, and Agrippa, proposes a cosmological system where a mystical "green line" (the Kether Elion crown) governs all motion. He explicitly invokes cabaliste doctrine to understand hidden attributes of the divine. The period also witnesses intensified skepticism. Valentin Schindler's widow published his *Lexicon pentaglotton* (1612), which classifies kabbalah into three types: patriarchal tradition (now nearly extinct), oral Torah-exegesis transmitted to Moses, and modern mystical letter-manipulation of dubious authenticity. This classification became influential, adopted by Morin and Buxtorf, establishing scholarly hierarchy against uncritical endorsement of practical cabala.
