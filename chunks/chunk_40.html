## The Jesuit and Portuguese Context del Rio's *Disquisitiones magicæ* (1551–1608) inherited Ciruelo's framework but focused chiefly on magic. Notably, del Rio had already borrowed from Giorgi's *Problemata* in his *Panegyrici in gloriam Mariæ* (Antwerp, 1598), carefully hedging: "What I say is neither mine nor new. The Hebrews thought thus, not in the superstitious and rightfully condemned kabbalah that modern Jews invented and which leads to magic, but in the kabbalah they received from ancestors by tradition, which Philo and many Fathers cultivated symbolically or allegorically." This distinction between "ancient" and "modern" (superstitious) kabbalah became standard defensive rhetoric. F. Luis de Carvajal's position—closer to Zamora than to Ciruelo—reprised itself in his *De restituta theologia* (1545). Though disclaiming "superstitious cabalistic superstitions of the Jews," he eagerly reported Reuchlin on the divine name and the angel Raziel's promise, declaring the tradition admissible because true and consonant with faith. ## Expansion of Kabbalistic Application J. A. de Guevara (c. 1480–1545) used "cabale" loosely in his *Épistres dorées*, associating it with the Talmud. Following Perez de Valencia, he relates how a Jew named Meir (in 314 CE) invented the doctrine of two Torahs—written and oral—earning such credibility that Jews accepted his invention as Mishnah (secret law), later called Talmud. Miguel de Medina's *Parenèsis christiana* (1564) refined the Talmud/kabbalah distinction, invoking Thomas Waldensis (defender against Wicleff at Constance, 1414). Waldensis had cited Matthew XV's warning against Pharisee leaven and Jerome's equation with perverted Marcionist and Valentinian heresies. Medina adds that Ephesians burned fifty thousand denarii of books (Acts XIX, 19)—possibly containing "inane cabalistic insanities"—unless magical works. Yet Medina acknowledges Christian kabbalists' existence and their tendency to find mysteries in minimal words, claiming such facility with Hebrew points makes deriving Trinity or Antichrist equally easy. He questions whether Hebrew was even primordial, dismissing the entire enterprise as cabalistic "foolishness." Jacopo Mazzoni (1508–1590) codified scattered objections into formal theses debated at Rome (1576) on Pic's model. Benedictus Pererius (Pereyra) (1535–1610), a Jesuit from Valencia entering the Society in 1552, penned *Adversus fallaces et superstitiosas artes* (Lyon, 1592; separately 1592), appended to his *Commentary on Daniel* (1587). While attacking magic, divination, and astrology, he noted that Hebrew words carry power—a superstition exorcists and mages exploited, motivating his caution. ## Magicians and Healers: The Dissemination of Magical Kabbalah The magical dimension dominated Spanish attention. Franciscus Vallesius (Valdés), Alcalá's physician whom Philip II summoned to court, attacked "mages, cabalists and Platonists" in his *De sacra philosophia* (Turin and Lyon, 1587) for imagining words possessed miraculous efficacy. He condemned beliefs that Adam's language preserved potency in Hebrew ecclesiastical hymns (Alleluia, Osanna, Sabaoth), echoing Zoroaster's teachings, and that Jews called kabbalists boasted of extracting secret revelations and performing wonders through words and characters. Franciscus de Torreblanca y Villalpando, a jurist proudly identifying as nephew of the famous Villalpando, compiled *Demonologia* (Mainz, 1623) from Ciruelo, del Rio, and Pererius, attacking mages (Agrippa, Pomponazzi, Paracelsus, J. Weyer) alongside kabbalists. He developed arguments against *Ensalmo*—folk incantations for healing, fascinatio, or expulsion—deriving from the verb *ensalmar* (to fascinate, charm, heal). Emmanuel do Valle de Moura, a Dominican physician-theologian and inquisitor, published *Incantationum seu Ensalmis* (Évora, 1620), promising a *De arte cabalistica* in his preface and attacking João Bravus Chamissius, a Coimbra physician who justified *Ensalmi* through kabbalistic doctrine, relying on arguments from Medina, T. Garzoni, and Simon Maiolo. ## Selective Engagement Among Ecclesiastics Not all Spanish ecclesiastics rejected kabbalah. Caspar Barrerios (d. 1610), nephew of João de Barros, attacked "modern Jews" pursuing "delirious interpretations" in his *Commentary on Ophir* (Évora, 1560), yet cited Galatin willingly. Hieronymus Osorius (Osório) (1506–1580), the "Portuguese Cicero," attacked Talmud and kabbalah in *De vera sapientia* (dedicated to Gregory XIII, 1572), concluding with Isaiah's image of basilisk eggs and spider webs—but his argumentation borrowed nothing novel. Ludovicus sancti Francisci (Luiz de São Francisco) (fl. 1586), a Franciscan who took up Hebrew after fifty, published *Globus caneorum et arcanorum* (Rome, 1586), examining language origins, Hebrew grammar, abbreviations, poetry, and divine arcana. He condemned belief in a double law at Sinai and "false kabbalah," yet incorporated Adam as sage and theologian (not cabalist), and reproduced the numerical equivalence of Jesus with Miriam and YHWH. He praised Perez de Valencia, Medina, Steuchus, Génébrard, and even Forster, distinguishing between spurious kabbalah and legitimate *anagogy* (though he rejected the term itself). Dominicus Garzias, a Hebrew professor at Alcalá who became canon and Inquisition censor, avoided the word "kabbalah" in his Latin *Propugnaculum* (1606) against Jews, yet abundantly cited *Gale Razeia* and Galatin and reproduced kabbalistic scholies from Giustiniani's Psalter. Villalpandus, Ystella, Viegas, Barradius, and Alcazar each strategically cited kabbalist sources while distancing themselves from superstition, illustrating the pragmatic compromise sixteenth-century Spanish ecclesiastics reached: condemning magical and superstitious applications while selectively deploying Christian kabbalistic exegesis for devotional and polemical ends.
