Antonio del Rio (sometimes rendered as Martinus Antonius Delrius) inherited Ciruelo's critical framework in his Disquisitiones magicæ (1551–1608), adapting it particularly to attacks on magical practice. Yet del Rio's earlier work, Panegyrici in gloriam Mariæ (Antwerp, 1598), demonstrates how even systematic opponents of kabbalah could selectively draw on kabbalistic sources when defending Christian doctrine. In that work, del Rio employs a crucial rhetorical maneuver that became standard defensive strategy: he acknowledges borrowing from Giorgi's Problemata while hedging fiercely. His formulation deserves citation for its precision: "What I say is neither mine nor new. The Hebrews thought thus, not in the superstitious and rightfully condemned kabbalah that modern Jews invented and which leads to magic, but in the kabbalah they received from ancestors by tradition, which Philo and many Fathers cultivated symbolically or allegorically." This distinction between ancient, revelation-based kabbalah (legitimate) and modern, superstitious kabbalah (condemned) became a standard defensive formula permitting Christian ecclesiastics to cite kabbalistic sources without endorsing contemporary Jewish mysticism. Such rhetorical partition allowed del Rio to position himself as rigorously orthodox while deploying kabbalistic exegetical techniques wherever they served Christian doctrine.

J. A. de Guevara (c. 1480–1545) deployed the term "cabale" loosely in his Épistres dorées, associating it with the Talmud and incorporating a narrative borrowed from Perez de Valencia: a Jewish sage named Meir invented in 314 CE the doctrine of two Torahs (written and oral), achieving such credibility that Jews accepted his fabrication as authentic Mishnah (secret law), subsequently called Talmud. This genealogy—tracing both Talmud and kabbalah to recent Jewish invention rather than ancient revelation—served Spanish ecclesiastics seeking to discredit Jewish exegetical privilege. Miguel de Medina's Parenèsis christiana (1564) systematically refined the Talmud/kabbalah distinction by invoking Thomas Waldensis, the medieval defender against Wycliffe at Constance (1414). Waldensis had cited Christ's warning against Pharisaic leaven (Matthew XV) and Jerome's identification of such leaven with perverted Marcionist and Valentinian heresies. Medina enlarges this critique by noting the book-burning of Ephesus (Acts XIX, 19)—fifty thousand denarii worth—which he speculatively identifies as possibly containing "inane cabalistic insanities" or magical works. Yet even as Medina condemns kabbalistic excess, he acknowledges that Christian kabbalists exist and display a propensity for finding cosmic mysteries in minimal words; he ridicules their facility with Hebrew vowel-points, noting that such manipulation permits deriving either the Trinity or the Antichrist with equal ease. His concluding position—that Hebrew was perhaps not even the primitive language—evacuates the entire foundational presupposition on which Christian kabbalah rested.

The magical dimension of kabbalah dominated Spanish ecclesiastical attention, particularly regarding folk healing practices and demonological speculation. Franciscus Vallesius (Valdés), Alcalá's renowned physician whom Philip II summoned to court, attacked "mages, cabalists and Platonists" in De sacra philosophia (1587) for entertaining the superstition that words possessed miraculous efficacy and that Adam's primordial language retained occult power within Hebrew ecclesiastical hymns—Alleluia, Osanna, and Sabaoth—claims he traces back to Zoroastrian and magical traditions. Franciscus de Torreblanca y Villalpando, a jurist pointedly claiming descent from the famous Villalpandus, compiled Demonologia (Mainz, 1623) from earlier polemics by Ciruelo, del Rio, and Pererius, attacking not only Jewish and Christian kabbalists but also magicians like Agrippa, Pomponazzi, Paracelsus, and Johann Weyer. Torreblanca developed sophisticated arguments against Ensalmo—the Iberian folk practice of healing incantations, the term deriving from the verb ensalmar (to charm, fascinate, heal)—linking such practices to clandestine kabbalistic doctrine. Emmanuel do Valle de Moura, a Dominican physician-theologian and Inquisition functionary, published Incantationum seu Ensalmis (Évora, 1620), promising a future De arte cabalistica and attacking the Coimbra physician João Bravus Chamissius, who had attempted to justify Ensalmi through kabbalistic principles, drawing argumentation from Medina, Tomaso Garzoni, and Simon Maiolo. This cascade of polemics against magical kabbalah reveals how closely ecclesiastical authorities linked Jewish and Christian kabbalistic speculation to popular magical and healing practices that threatened clerical monopoly over sacred efficacy.

Yet neither Spanish nor Portuguese ecclesiastics uniformly rejected kabbalah. Caspar Barrerios (d. 1610), nephew of the celebrated João de Barros, attacked "modern Jews" pursuing "delirious interpretations" in his Commentary on Ophir (Évora, 1560), yet freely cited Galatin's Christian kabbalistic arguments. Hieronymus Osorius (Osório) (1506–1580), celebrated as the "Portuguese Cicero," attacked both Talmud and kabbalah in De vera sapientia (1572, dedicated to Gregory XIII), concluding with Isaiah's vivid imagery of basilisk eggs and spider-webs—a rhetorical flourish that lacked argumentative novelty. Ludovicus sancti Francisci (Luiz de São Francisco) (fl. 1586), a Franciscan who took up Hebrew study only after fifty, published Globus caneorum et arcanorum (Rome, 1586), a voluminous examination of language origins, Hebrew grammar, orthographic abbreviations, poetry, and divine arcana. While condemning belief in a double Torah delivered at Sinai and repudiating "false kabbalah," he incorporated Adam as primordial sage and theologian (though not cabalist) and reproduced the numerical equivalence of Jesus with Miriam and YHWH, citing approvingly Perez de Valencia, Medina, Steuchus, Génébrard, and even Forster, while carefully distinguishing spurious kabbalah from legitimate anagogy—though he rejected the latter term itself. Dominicus Garzias, a Hebrew professor at Alcalá who became cathedral canon and Inquisition censor, strategically avoided the word "kabbalah" in his Latin Propugnaculum (1606) against Jews, yet liberally cited Gale Razeia, Galatin, and reproduced kabbalistic glosses from Giustiniani's Psalter commentary. The collective pattern reveals a pragmatic ecclesiastical compromise: condemnation of magical and superstitious applications while selective deployment of Christian kabbalistic exegesis for devotional meditation and polemical argumentation—a strategy permitting orthodox ecclesiastics to utilize kabbalistic hermeneutics while maintaining nominal distance from Jewish mysticism.
