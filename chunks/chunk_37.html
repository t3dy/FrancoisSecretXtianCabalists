## Critical Perspectives and Incorporation Secret begins by presenting Génébrard's harsh judgment against superficial cabalism: he dismisses notaricon, gematria, and temurah as futile arts invented recently and bearing no relation to authentic kabbalah (understood as oral tradition from ancestors). Ironically, Génébrard then incorporates Zohar passages and kabbalist exegesis into his own works—particularly his *Chronologie* (1585)—using them to argue for Christian doctrines like purgatory and the resurrection of the body. A key figure here is Bartholomaeus Valverdius, a Spanish writer whom Génébrard knew in Padua. Valverdius's *De Purgatorio* (1581) explicitly uses Hebrew authors and kabbalists to prove purgatory's existence, citing passages from Recanati and the Zohar on Gehinnom (the place of purification). Génébrard borrowed extensively from such sources. ## Blaise de Vigenère: The Grand Systematizer Vigenère (1523–1596) emerges as the principal figure in French Christian kabbalah. Though he began writing late (at fifty), he became celebrated as a cryptographer, alchemist, and translator. His three major sciences—theology and kabbalah, astrology and magic, physiology and alchemy—operate in parallel; each has both a vulgar and a secret dimension. Vigenère develops rich imagery around celestial writing (*chetab malachim*, the writing of angels) and divine analogy. The stars form a cosmic language invisible to the untrained; the Kabbalah reveals the structural proportions (*Ghematrie*) underlying both Scripture and the three worlds (intelligible, celestial, elemental). All creation derives from divine causes as two clear streams from an eternal source, permitting reciprocal understanding of God and His works. ## Religious Application and Zohar Exegesis Vigenère's *Traicté des prières* (1595), dedicated to Louis de Gonzague, Duke of Nevers, constitutes a French anthology of Zohar passages on prayer. He drew extensively from Postel's second Zohar translation, though never acknowledged him. The work emphasizes that prayers replace sacrifices after the Temple's destruction—a staple kabbalist theme he attributes to the Zohar. He cites rabbinical sources (e.g., Rabbi Joseph Castilian) and develops the image of prayer as fire igniting the lamp of mercy in heaven. On communal prayer, Vigenère invokes the Zohar's teaching that private prayers rarely reach God's presence unless fervent, whereas communal prayers are purified through collective merit—echoing Abraham's intercession for the righteous in Sodom. His *Traicté du feu et du sel* (1617, posthumous) applies Zohar imagery to alchemical spirituality, depicting the soul's purification through divine fire and salt, drawing parallels to the Cherubim guarding Eden and Adam's original knowledge. Vigenère's cousin Claude Duret condensed these themes in his *Thresor de l'histoire des langues*, stripping out technical digressions on ciphers but preserving the kabbalistic framework. Through Vigenère's systematization, Christian kabbalah becomes not mere numerology but a unified vision of divine emanation, natural law, and spiritual ascent.
