Secret shifts focus to the extraordinary flourishing of Christian kabbalah in Renaissance Italy (1480s–1540s), centered on figures like Galatino, Giustiniani, George of Venice, and the celebrated Hebrew presses of Soncino and Bomberg. Before examining the major figures, Secret sketches two marginal but revealing personalities: Lodovico Lazzarelli (1450–1500), author of the hermetic *Crater Hermetis*, and his mysterious master Giovanni Mercurio. Mercurio performed apocalyptic street theater in Rome (1484), distributing proclamations dressed as a messianic figure; he later appeared in Lyon (1501) claiming possession of all Greek, Hebrew, and Latin wisdom. Lazzarelli's writings blend hermetic philosophy with kabbalistic themes, citing the Beresit Rabba and arguing that the divine names represent pure magical efficacy. Notably, Lazzarelli's *Epistola Enoch* contains an allusion to secret transmission of occult knowledge—possibly referencing Pico della Mirandola—suggesting kabbalah's exclusive revelation to chosen initiates. The section then examines Marsilio Ficino (1433–1499), whose network around Pico introduced him to Hebrew literature and the Bahir. Though Ficino's *De Christiana religione* cites the Bahir without explicitly naming kabbalah, he endorses the magical efficacy of the divine name (Tetragrammaton). His theological speculation was attacked, prompting Jérôme Benivieni to defend both Ficino and Pico against charges of magical practice, insisting that Pico studied kabbalah for scriptural exegesis, not wonder-working. Finally, Secret introduces Léon Hebreu (c. 1470–after 1502), son of Isaac Abarbanel, whose *Dialoghi d'amore* (published 1502, translated to Latin by Joannes Carolus Saracenus and included in Pistorius's collection) presents kabbalah through a Platonic dialogue frame. Léon describes the oral Qabbalah transmitted mouth-to-mouth from Adam through Enoch, Noah, and Abraham, confirmed by Moses and preserved by the learned rabbis. He develops the theme of seven cosmic revolutions lasting forty-nine thousand years, with each revolution renewing the universe—a theory that recurs throughout Renaissance Christian kabbalah.
