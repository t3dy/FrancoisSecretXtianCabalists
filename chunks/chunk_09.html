In late 1486, the thirteen suspect Conclusions triggered papal examination; Pico retracted on March 31, 1487, but faced heresy proceedings that drove him to France and then Florence. Despite this crisis, Pico's curiosity for Kabbalah intensified. He encountered Yohanan ben Isaac Alemano, a Greek and Arabic scholar who wrote commentaries on the Song of Songs and the Pentateuch. In Ferrara after Lorenzo de' Medici's death in 1492, Pico acquired Hebrew manuscripts from a recently expelled Spanish Jew, studying them obsessively. When Innocent VIII died in July 1492, Alexander VI absolved Pico on June 18, 1493, whereupon Savonarola urged him to join the Dominican order. Pico then prepared polemical treatises defending Jerome's translation and the Septuagint against Hebrew critique before dying November 17, 1494. A pivotal disciple was Clément Abraham (originally Isaac), a Spanish rabbi and convert baptized June 23, 1493, at San Marco, Florence, who became master to Sante Pagnini (1470–1536), a dominican and future authority on Hebrew letters and biblical translation. Through Pagnini's work and his associate Symphorien Champier's *Isagoge ad sacras litteras*, Pico's Kabbalistic interests persisted institutionally. Pico's works, published by his nephew in 1498 with many subsequent Renaissance editions, disseminated his syncretic thought—sometimes modified. Benivieni's edition of the *Commento alla canzone d'amore* excised some Kabbalistic and Jewish references but preserved the theme of death by divine kiss (*Binsica*), derived from Talmudic mysticism and elaborated by Maimonides, Léon Hébreu, Reuchlin, François Georges, Agrippa, and Giordano Bruno. The *Heptaplus* references the "very ancient Simeon" (the Zohar's reputed author) and deploys Zoharic etymology, particularly for *Tohu* and *Bohu*. Pico's four-world symbolism and the image of the Menorah in the Heptaplus became touchstones for later Christian Kabbalah, exemplified by Postel's *Candélabre de Moïse*. Thus, despite the scandal, Pico became the legendary father of Renaissance Christian Kabbalah, his library—eventually acquired by Cardinal Grimani and dispersed to Vatican and Venice—seeding generations of humanist mystical synthesis.
