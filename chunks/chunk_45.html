Secret surveys converts in German lands, where Kabbalah played a secondary role compared to Italian and Iberian contexts. Antonius Margarita (converted c. 1522) published *Der gantz Judisch Glaub* (Augsburg 1530), developing the 72 Divine Names as Reuchlin had, while noting Kabbalah's rules (Atbash gematria, Jesus-name exegesis). Later German-speaking converts—Philipp Wolf, Paul Weidner, Joannes Fortius Hortensius, Nathan Aron—published vernacular and Latin treatises on Messianic kabbalah, often recycling Galatin and the 42/72-letter names. A crucial negative example emerges: Gershon ben Meir/Christian Gerson (1569–1627), whose *Der Juden Thalmud* (1613) chapters on Kabbalah relied on Elia Levita's *Tisbi* and Elhanan Paul's (Paul of Prague) exegetical methods. Secret notes Elhanan Paul's notoriety: baptized twice, he played on Hebrew letters to mock Jewish hopes for the Messiah ("Our God is dead"). Yet Paul's kabbalistic writings circulated, influencing later authors. Naphtali Margolioth/Julius Conrad Otto (converted 1603) published the *Gali Razia* (1605, four books), claiming rabbinic testimony proves Jesus was Messiah. He was swiftly exposed for forging and interpolating texts, then renounced Christianity—a watershed moment marking growing textual scrutiny. Richard Simon's letter (1640) records a critical turn: early Zohar manuscripts, examined in Constantinople by Jewish physicians-turned-Christians, allegedly contained Christian-favorable passages deleted by skeptical Jewish censors. Simon himself distinguishes between the authentic printed Zohar (Cremona, Mantua editions) and a false version attributed to Messer Leon, suggesting early modern criticism was developing parallel tracks in Jewish and Christian scholarship. Secret concludes with Samuel Friderich Brenz's 1614 pamphlet against Kabbalah (German converts increasingly resisted it), countered by Solomon Zebi Hirsch's defense that Agrippa, Paracelsus, Pico, and Reuchlin honored Kabbalah's study. Yet even defenders acknowledged golems were manufactured via *Sefer Yetzirah*—a concession that mystical praxis, however marginal, remained conceptually real. The convertite literature, largely manuscript, awaits patient monographic treatment. Secret emphasizes that Vatican and Neophyte House archives hold numerous untranslated and unstudied works critical to understanding Christian Kabbalah's institutional embedding.
