Secret surveys the convertite scholarship emerging from German-speaking lands, a context in which Kabbalah occupied a markedly secondary and less theologically central position compared to the Italian and Iberian spheres where kabbalistic apologetics had achieved their deepest institutional embedding. Antonius Margarita, a convert of approximately 1522, published the influential Der gantz Judisch Glaub (The Complete Jewish Faith, Augsburg 1530), which developed the doctrine of the 72 Divine Names along lines established by Reuchlin while simultaneously outlining Kabbalah's practical methodological rules—including Atbash gematria, the numerical manipulation of letters, and specifically the christological exegesis of the name Jesus through Hebrew letter-values. Margarita's work established a template that subsequent German-speaking converts would refine and recirculate. Later figures—Philipp Wolf, Paul Weidner, Joannes Fortius Hortensius, and Nathan Aron—published vernacular and Latin treatises addressing Messianic interpretations of Kabbalah, frequently recycling Galatin's foundational work and the same numerical mysticism surrounding the 42-letter and 72-letter divine names that had become canonical in Christian Kabbalistic discourse.

Yet German-speaking convertite scholarship also produced counterexamples and cautionary tales. Gershon ben Meir, who adopted the Christian name Christian Gerson (1569–1627), published chapters on Kabbalah within his larger work Der Juden Thalmud (1613) that relied heavily on methodological approaches derived from Elia Levita's Tisbi and from the exegetical strategies of Elhanan Paul (also known as Paul of Prague). Secret flags Elhanan Paul's notoriety with particular emphasis: baptized twice throughout his religious life, Paul had engaged in a kind of textual mockery, manipulating Hebrew letters specifically to deride and undermine Jewish messianic hopes with the bitter pun "Our God is dead." Despite the provocative nature of Paul's approach, his kabbalistic writings nevertheless circulated within Christian scholarly networks, exercising measurable influence on subsequent authors who encountered his work through libraries and manuscript transmission.

A more profound watershed moment arrived with the case of Naphtali Margolioth, who adopted the Christian name Julius Conrad Otto upon his conversion in 1603. Otto published the ambitious four-book work Gali Razia (1605), which claimed to assemble persuasive rabbinic testimony and kabbalistic evidence supposedly demonstrating conclusively that Jesus fulfilled Messianic prophecies embedded within Jewish tradition. However, Otto was relatively swiftly exposed through careful textual examination for having forged and interpolated passages—for having, in other words, constructed false evidence to support his apologetic claims. Discovered in fraud, Otto subsequently renounced his Christian conversion and returned to Judaism, an event that Secret characterizes as a watershed moment in the emerging culture of textual scrutiny. Richard Simon's letter of 1640 documents this critical turn explicitly: early manuscripts of the Zohar, supposedly examined in Constantinople by Jewish physicians who had converted to Christianity, allegedly contained passages favorable to Christian interpretation that had subsequently been deleted by skeptical Jewish censors. Simon himself carefully distinguishes between the authentic printed Zohar editions (the Cremona and Mantua editions, established as canonical) and a false version spuriously attributed to the philosopher and printer Messer Leon. Simon's analysis suggests that parallel tracks of early modern textual criticism were developing simultaneously within Jewish and Christian scholarship, with each tradition subjecting the other's claims to increasingly rigorous scrutiny.

Secret concludes this section by documenting the emergence of explicit resistance to Kabbalah among German converts. Samuel Friderich Brenz published a pamphlet in 1614 arguing forcefully against Kabbalah, representing a broader trend among German-speaking converts toward skepticism regarding kabbalistic claims. This position was countered by Solomon Zebi Hirsch's defense of Kabbalah's study, which marshaled the authority of Agrippa, Paracelsus, Pico, and Reuchlin as cultural authorities who had honored and engaged kabbalistic traditions. Yet even defenders of Kabbalah acknowledged the existence of practical mystical techniques—notably that golems could be manufactured through the proper application of Sefer Yetzirah (the Book of Formation)—a concession that whatever criticism scholars might mount against superstition, mystical praxis, however marginal to theological concerns, remained conceptually real and operative. The broader convertite literature, which survives largely in manuscript form scattered across European archives, awaits patient monographic treatment by future scholars. Secret emphasizes that Vatican archives and the holdings of the Neophyte House contain numerous untranslated and as yet unstudied works that would prove critical to a comprehensive understanding of Christian Kabbalah's institutional embedding within the Catholic Church.
