Secret examines how two figures—Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa and Johannes Trithème—became lightning rods in the conflict between humanist Kabbalah and ecclesiastical authority, their reputations oscillating between genuine erudition and occult fraud. Their correspondence and publications reveal networks of knowledge transmission that circumvented formal institutions. Agrippa (1486–1535), a polymath soldier-scholar who participated in Italian campaigns, became celebrated and condemned for his *De occulta philosophia*, which synthesized Pic de la Mirandole, Reuchlin, Paul Rici, and Giorgio's systems into a triadic magic (natural, celestial, ceremonial). Secret reveals that Agrippa's manuscript was circulated to Trithème for encouragement—both corresponded extensively seeking rare Hebrew texts, particularly Galatin's works and Samuel's Kabbalah. Yet Agrippa's later *De incertitudine scientiarum* (Uncertainty of the Sciences) executed a dramatic reversal, condemning Jewish Kabbalah as "pernicious superstition," arguing that Christians possess superior mystical knowledge through Christ and the Church, rendering Hebrew gematria and letter-permutation (temurah) mere human invention—ultimately no different from Raban Maur's acrostic Latin verses or Valeria Proba's Virgilian cento (texts assembled from fragments). Trithème (1462–1516), Benedictine abbot of Sponheim, achieved legendary status through his *Steganographia* (secret writing), which he claimed was divinely revealed. Yet his mystique attracted both genuine scholars and charlatans. Charles de Bovelles visited in 1503 and recoiled at the barbarous spirit-names and foreign characters; Trithème defended his system as systematic rather than demonic. A mysterious French visitor named Libanius Gallus allegedly transmitted teachings from the hermit Pélage of Majorca and from Pic de la Mirandole himself. Trithème's *Polygraphie* employed Kabbalistic terminology to describe encoded correspondence, framing the true *cabala* as secret transmission between intimates rather than magical incantation. Jean Caramuel Lobkowitz later argued that Trithème employed Kabbalistic methods (Temurah, spirit-invoking via divine procedure)—a rehabilitation attempt shadowed by the *Index*'s suppression of such texts. The section concludes by noting Agrippa's reversals and Trithème's ambiguous reputation left Christian Kabbalah vulnerable to charges of occultism, even as serious scholars continued cultivating mystical exegesis in correspondence networks beyond official scrutiny.
