Secret examines two towering and profoundly contradictory figures—Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa and Johannes Trithème—who became lightning rods in the intensifying conflict between humanist Kabbalah and ecclesiastical authority, their intellectual reputations oscillating wildly between genuine polymathic erudition and dangerous occult fraud. Their far-flung correspondence networks and published works reveal sophisticated channels of knowledge transmission that circumvented formal institutional control, operating instead through patronage, personal letters, and manuscript circulation in a way that both enabled their influence and rendered them vulnerable to attack.

Agrippa (1486–1535) exemplifies the soldier-scholar of the Italian Renaissance—a man who participated in military campaigns alongside his intensive humanist studies. He achieved international celebrity and equally fierce condemnation for his De occulta philosophia (On Occult Philosophy), a monumental three-volume synthesis that drew together the systems of Pico de la Mirandole, Reuchlin, Paul Ricius, and Giorgio himself into an integrated triadic framework encompassing natural magic (the manipulation of natural sympathies and hidden correspondences), celestial magic (the invocation of planetary influences through astrological timing), and ceremonial magic (the use of precise words, symbols, and ritual procedures to effect supernatural transformation). Secret's archival research reveals that Agrippa actually circulated his manuscript to Trithème for critical encouragement—both men sustained extensive correspondence networks in search of rare Hebrew texts, particularly seeking copies of Galatin's Kabbalistic treatises and materials attributed to Samuel on Kabbalistic doctrine. Yet Agrippa subsequently executed a dramatic intellectual reversal in his De incertitudine scientiarum (On the Uncertainty of the Sciences), a work that condemns Jewish Kabbalah as "pernicious superstition" and argues that Christians possess far superior mystical knowledge, mediated through Christ and the institutional Church, rendering all Jewish Kabbalistic techniques—gematria, letter-permutation (temurah), and numerical manipulation—as mere human inventions, epistemologically no different from Raban Maur's acrostic Latin verses or Valeria Proba's Virgilian cento (texts painstakingly assembled from scattered fragments of Virgil to form new meanings). This rhetorical leveling—suggesting that Christian Latin mysticism and Jewish Hebrew mysticism are equivalent human constructs rather than one deriving from divine revelation—became a powerful weapon in the hands of those wishing to discredit Kabbalistic scholarship.

Trithème (1462–1516), the Benedictine abbot of the monastery of Sponheim, achieved almost legendary status in his lifetime through his Steganographia (Secret Writing), a vast treatise on encoded communication which Trithème himself claimed had been divinely revealed to him through visionary experience. Yet this aura of mystical authority attracted to him both genuine scholars seeking authentic erudition and unscrupulous charlatans seeking to exploit his reputation for access to forbidden knowledge. The humanist Charles de Bovelles visited Sponheim in 1503 and recoiled in horror from what he witnessed: the manuscript pages filled with barbarous spirit-names, foreign characters, and apparently meaningless symbols that Trithème defended as systematic and rational rather than demonic or diabolical. A mysterious French visitor identified as Libanius Gallus allegedly brought Trithème teachings supposedly transmitted from Pélage, a renowned hermit of Majorca, and claimed to carry doctrines received directly from Pico de la Mirandole himself. Trithème's subsequent work, the Polygraphie (Polymathic Writing), strategically employed Kabbalistic terminology to describe the mechanics of encoded correspondence, thereby reframing what he termed the true cabala not as magical incantation or demonic conjuring but as the intimate transmission of secret knowledge between trusted initiates—a definition that both dignified the practice and rendered it invisible to ecclesiastical inspection, since only those within the circle of initiates would understand the true significance of the communications.

Jean Caramuel Lobkowitz, in a later rehabilitation attempt, argued that Trithème's Steganographia actually employed authentic Kabbalistic methods—specifically the letter-permutation technique of Temurah and the formal invocation of spirits through precise divine nomenclature—effectively defending Trithème as a legitimate Kabbalistic scholar rather than a charlatan. Yet this rehabilitation was shadowed by the Church's placement of Trithème's works on the Index Librorum Prohibitorum, effectively suppressing their circulation. The section concludes by noting that the combined effect of Agrippa's dramatic recantation and Trithème's ambiguous, contested reputation rendered Christian Kabbalah vulnerable to persistent institutional charges of occultism and demonic practice. Yet paradoxically, Secret shows, serious scholars of Kabbalistic theology continued to cultivate mystical exegesis and numerical erudition within invisible correspondence networks that operated beyond official ecclesiastical scrutiny, creating a shadow intellectual infrastructure that would survive suppression and resurface in subsequent generations.
