The Candelabrum and Tabernacle Symbolism

This section presents detailed samples of Postel's kabbalistic exegesis, focusing on the Candelabrum (Candélabre), a work published in 1547 at Venice that interprets the Temple's seven-branched candlestick as a comprehensive figure of Christ and the universal cosmic order. In Postel's reading, the chandelier represents the total body of Christ in its manifold manifestations, with each of its seven branches corresponding simultaneously to the seven planets of classical astronomy, the seven ages or dispensations of the Church, and the entire system of the ten divine intelligences (Sephiroth) distributed hierarchically across the celestial spheres. The work exemplifies Postel's characteristic hermeneutical method: he seamlessly harmonizes Neoplatonic cosmology (inherited from Ficino and Pico) with Jewish mysticism drawn from the Zohar and Kabbalistic commentaries, integrates scriptural exegesis with astronomical and mathematical theory, and subordinates individual spiritual illumination within a collective historical narrative that moves toward the eschatological restoration of all things. The Candelabrum thus becomes a microcosmic image containing macrocosmic significance—the visible Temple object encodes the invisible architecture of redemption across all times and all beings.

Theological Integration of the Ten Sefirot and Divine Mercy

Postel explicitly elaborates the kabbalistic doctrine of the ten divine names or Sephiroth, distributed on the right hand (pillar of mercy and active creation) and the left hand (pillar of severity and limitation), arranged in patterns of harmony and opposition that mirror the structure of creation itself. Within this framework, he reiterates his central doctrine of Teshuvah (repentance or mystical return)—understood not merely as individual moral reformation but as the cosmic mechanism by which humanity progressively returns to its original relation to Christ across the three ages of Law (natural, written, and gracious). Postel emphasizes that the fourth and eternal age approaches when the law shall cease to be inscribed on stone tablets or written parchments, and instead shall be inscribed directly within human hearts, a vision explicitly tied to the destiny of French monarchy and the anticipated Millennium when Christ's kingship shall manifest visibly on earth. This eschatological vision grounds even the most technical kabbalistic speculation in immediate historical and political expectation.

The Gallic Messianism and Gomerism

A distinctively nationalist feature of Postel's system is his elaborate articulation of Gallic and French identity within a sacred historical schema running parallel to Jewish and Christian prophecy. He argues that the French kingdom derives its historical mission from Gomer, the son of Japheth and grandson of Noah, making Gaul itself—a land name he etymologizes as "Galuyah" (disclosure, elevation to heaven)—the providential instrument of universal revelation in the final age. From this genealogical claim flows his neologism "gomerie" (consummation or completion), derived from the Gomerite mission to bring all history to its appointed consummation. France becomes not merely a political entity among nations but an eschatological agent charged by divine providence with the work of universal restoration. Postel styles himself "sieur de la Gomerie" (lord of consummation), claiming that his prophetic mission is to "gomerize" the entire world—to consummate all fragmentary knowledge, all divided religions, all competing nations—through the revelation of true reason and the Gospel of Christ. This fusion of French national destiny with messianic theology was audacious, even dangerous, in its implications for both church and state.

Mother Jeanne and the Feminine Principle in Divinity

Postel's theology incorporates a distinctive emphasis on the feminine principle through the figure of Mother Jeanne and the mystical significance of the letter He (ה), the final letter of the divine tetragrammaton YHWH. In Postel's exegesis, Scripture deliberately renders this terminal He in a diminished or reduced form wherever it appears throughout the Hebrew Bible (occurring, he claims, over 5,500 times), and this orthographic detail encodes a profound theological truth: the union of masculine and feminine natures within Christ and the necessity of humanity's redemption through maternalistic wisdom—the recuperation of the divine feminine after her suppression in patriarchal religion. Mother Jeanne embodies this divine feminine intelligence restoring the world, and her prophetic utterances become the complement or completion of what the male Hebrew prophets had only partially revealed. This recuperation of the feminine in divinity, while not entirely unprecedented in medieval mysticism, takes in Postel an unprecedented centrality to his entire system.

Elias Redivivus and the Doctrine of Transmigration

Postel was repeatedly accused by both Protestant and Catholic authorities of preaching the heretical doctrine of transmigration of souls (metempsychosis), a charge that would align him with Islamic falsafa and ancient Pythagorean philosophy. In his defense, Postel carefully reframes his doctrine: he does not claim the migration of individual souls from body to body, but rather the circulation of a single divine substance—specifically, the Spirit of Elijah—through successive human vessels across the entire arc of sacred history. Elijah, understood as the greatest and most exalted of the biblical prophets, manifests successively in John the Baptist (as the precursor to Christ), in Christian saints and visionaries like Catherine of Siena and Joan of Arc, and finally—and most consequentially for Postel himself—in Postel as Elias redivivus (Elijah restored or Elijah returned). This doctrine renders Postel not a heretic but a bearer of the highest prophetic office, commissioned to reconcile all fragmentary wisdoms, to reveal the hidden divine names encoded in the Zohar, and to prepare humanity for the final Concord.

Symbolic Polyvalence and Emithology

At the heart of Postel's hermeneutical practice stands his invention of the term "emithologie" (from Emeth, truth in Hebrew), which names his distinctive method of reading texts—biblical, kabbalistic, historical, prophetic, even linguistic—as dense, multivalent symbols whose true sense is accessible only to the spiritually illumined or initiated. The principle undergirding this method is absolute: Everything contains everything; all is in all (Omnia in omnibus, the Neoplatonic maxim he appropriates and transforms). This principle of total interpenetration and infinite regress allows Postel to find in the smallest textual detail—a variant spelling in a manuscript, a numerical equivalence between Hebrew words, a phonetic similarity suggesting hidden connections, a marginal notation—profound cosmic significance resonating across multiple levels of interpretation simultaneously. The method is defended against its many critics as the recovery of a legitimate esoteric wisdom preserved in oral form from Moses through the Jewish Kabbalistic masters and now, through divine grace, revealed to the Christian world via the translation and exposition of the Zohar. Postel positions himself as the restorer of an ancient tradition, not its inventor.

This chunk presents Postel's mature synthesis: a comprehensive metaphysical and historical vision in which Jewish mysticism, French monarchy, Christian eschatology, feminine spirituality, and personal prophetic consciousness fuse into a single unified interpretive system aimed at the universal reconciliation of all knowledge, all peoples, and all creation. The system is audacious to the point of megalomania, yet it possesses an internal coherence that impressed even skeptical contemporary scholars and influenced French intellectual life for generations after Postel's confinement and death.
