Secret concludes the section on French Kabbalah by documenting how mid-century figures normalized Kabbalistic learning within court patronage and humanist circles, while opponents simultaneously mobilized religious arguments to discredit it. The era witnessed both institutional establishment of Hebrew studies and polemical attack on Kabbalistic excesses. Agrippa's famous recantation in *De incertitudine* became a touchstone for critics and apologists alike. He depicted Kabbalistic letter-manipulation as frivolous allegory-spinning—texts that Jews "easily put forward and just as easily despised and rejected"—fundamentally no more authoritative than acrostic games. Yet this critique itself was redeployed by later authors arguing against Gnostic heresy: Agrippa traced the Ophites, Gnostics, and Valentinians to the "Jewish leavening of Kabbalistic superstition," claiming they perverted Christian mysteries through Greek letter-mysticism (*arithmetike theologia*), a strategy of esoteric-knowledge gatekeeping that Saint Paul practiced among "the perfect." Paul Perrot, sieur de la Sale, versified Agrippa's skepticism in the *Gygantomachie* (1593), yet his poetic denunciation of "the forge" of Jewish Kabbalah paradoxically preserved the mystique he attacked. Simultaneously, the term *cabale* became domesticated in French: Jean Bouchet (1526) invoked it as a threat (the Church assailed by "caballiers" using conjuration), while Bonaventure des Périers employed it as a synonym for exorcism and demonic binding. Rabelais, though not using "Kabbalah" when satirizing Agrippa as "Herr Trippa," demonstrates sophisticated engagement with the field—recommending that Gargantua study Hebrew, Arabic, and Chaldaic without despising "Talmudists and Cabalistes," citing Kabbalistic concepts like *Belima* (from Sefer Yetzirah) in the *Quart Livre*. Royal lectureships formalized Hebrew expertise: Paul Paradis and Guidacerio taught at the Collège Trilingue. Paradis's *Dialogue on Reading Hebrew* (1534) presents a Socratic inquiry into mystical letter-interpretation, citing Giorgio, Galatin, and Reuchlin as the trio who explained Hebrew letters—a canonical triad now recognized by educated courtiers. Chéradame's *Alphabet de la langue sainte* (1532), praised by L. Febvre as "petit traité de mystique dionysienne," explicitly constructs a Kabbalistic anthropology from the *De arte cabalistica*, mapping Hebrew letters to angelic orders and celestial spheres. By mid-century, Christian Kabbalah had achieved a precarious legitimacy: taught in royal colleges, cited in humanist dialogues, condemned in ecclesiastical polemics, and embedded in erudite correspondence networks—a durable intellectual current that transcended sectarian boundaries and survived its most celebrated practitioners' apostasies.
