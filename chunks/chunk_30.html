Secret concludes this section on the French phase of Christian Kabbalah by documenting a paradoxical phenomenon: mid-century French figures achieved a remarkable institutional normalization of Kabbalistic learning within court patronage and humanist scholarly circles, even as ecclesiastical opponents simultaneously mobilized powerful religious arguments to delegitimize and discredit the field. The era thus witnessed simultaneous movements in opposing directions—the establishment of Hebrew studies as an official royal institutional practice, and the fierce polemical attack on Kabbalistic "excesses" as dangerous superstition. This dialectical struggle produced neither victory nor defeat but a kind of tense intellectual détente, wherein Kabbalah survived as a learned discipline despite mounting institutional pressure. Agrippa's famous recantation in the *De incertitudine* became an enduring touchstone, continuously invoked by both critics seeking to delegitimize Kabbalah and apologists attempting to preserve its reputation. Agrippa depicted Kabbalistic letter-manipulation as merely frivolous allegory-spinning, a kind of intellectual game wherein the Jews "easily put forward and just as easily despised and rejected" their own mystical claims—fundamentally no more authoritative or binding than acrostic competitions played with Latin verse. Yet this critique was itself strategically redeployed by later Christian theologians arguing against Gnostic heresy: Agrippa's argument was retrofitted to trace the Ophites, Gnostics, and Valentinian heretics to "the Jewish leavening of Kabbalistic superstition," claiming that these early Christian heresies had contaminated authentic Christian mysteries through the adoption of Greek letter-mysticism (*arithmetike theologia*). Agrippa thus repositioned Christian Kabbalah as a potential conduit for Gnostic infection rather than Christian wisdom—a rhetorical move that invoked the example of Saint Paul himself, who famously restricted his most esoteric teachings to "the perfect" (*teleioi*), thereby establishing gatekeeping of esoteric knowledge as a precedent of apostolic authority. Paul Perrot, sieur de la Sale, in his *Gygantomachie* (1593), versified Agrippa's skepticism into verse, launching a poetic denunciation of "the forge" of Jewish Kabbalah and its fraudulent pretensions. Yet the paradox was that this very poetic denunciation, by dramatizing and recounting Kabbalistic doctrines in elevated verse, paradoxically preserved and amplified the mystique he explicitly attacked. Simultaneously, the French language itself was incorporating *cabale* as a domesticated term in ordinary discourse: Jean Bouchet (1526) invoked it as a contemporary threat—the Church assailed by heretical "caballiers" wielding conjuration and demonic binding—while Bonaventure des Périers employed *cabale* as a synonym for exorcism and the binding of demonic spirits, transforming it from an exotic learned practice into part of the linguistic vernacular of religious controversy. Rabelais, the century's greatest humorist and most erudite author, presents a more nuanced engagement. Though he mocks Agrippa obliquely by naming him "Herr Trippa" in his satirical portrait, Rabelais simultaneously recommends that Gargantua pursue study of Hebrew, Arabic, and Chaldaic without intellectual prejudice, explicitly advising that one should not despise "Talmudists and Cabalistes" as inferior practitioners of learning. In the *Quart Livre*, Rabelais cites Kabbalistic concepts with evident familiarity—he invokes *Belima*, a term drawn from the Sefer Yetzirah, indicating his direct engagement with foundational Kabbalistic texts. Royal pedagogical formalization further established Hebrew expertise as an official discipline: Paul Paradis and Guidacerio held teaching positions at the Collège Trilingue, making them official interpreters of biblical languages. Paradis's *Dialogue on Reading Hebrew* (1534) presents itself as a Socratic inquiry into the mysteries of mystical letter-interpretation, and it explicitly establishes a canonical triad of authorities—Giorgio, Galatin, and Reuchlin—as the three master explainers of the Hebrew letters themselves, a triumvirate now recognized and cited by educated courtiers throughout the realm. Chéradame's *Alphabet de la langue sainte* (1532), praised by the great historian Lucien Febvre as a "little treatise of Dionysian mysticism," undertakes an ambitious project: the explicit construction of a Kabbalistic anthropology based entirely on Reuchlin's *De arte cabalistica*, systematically mapping each Hebrew letter onto specific angelic orders and celestial spheres, thereby creating a comprehensive diagram of how human nature participates in the entire cosmic hierarchy of being. By the middle of the sixteenth century, Christian Kabbalah had achieved a precarious but nonetheless real legitimacy within French intellectual culture: it was taught formally in royal colleges, cited approvingly in humanist philosophical dialogues, condemned in ecclesiastical polemics from the pulpit, and embedded within erudite correspondence networks that connected scholars across international boundaries. Christian Kabbalah had become a durable intellectual current, one that transcended sectarian boundaries between Catholic and Protestant alike and survived the apostasies and reversals of its most celebrated individual practitioners, suggesting that the tradition itself possessed an internal resilience and intellectual persuasiveness that no single author's recantation could entirely extinguish.
