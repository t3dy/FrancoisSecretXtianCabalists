**Guy Le Fèvre's Poetic and Mystical Syntheses** This section explores Guy Le Fèvre de la Boderie's major works as the fullest literary expression of Postelian Christian Kabbalah in Renaissance France. **The Encyclopedie (1570)** marks Guy's assertion of mystical independence within Postel's orbit. Claiming to have composed the work before reading Pico's *Heptaple* or Giorgi's *Harmonie*, Guy presents himself as the "new Orpheus" discovering Hebrew secrets independently. His Greek, Latin, and Hebrew prefatory poems invoke Proverbs 25:11 (golden apples in a silver setting—the hidden word spoken in proper circles), a verse developed by both Reuchlin and Fiandino. The *Encyclopedie* articulates Postel's central doctrine of divine unity in trinitarian manifestation: "Le Bon est Un en trois, qui s'entendant s'engendre" (The Good is One in three, self-understanding generates itself). Guy versifies Postelian themes of Christ's ecstatic death, the union of reason and faith, and French gallicanist messianism. **The Tabernacle and Candelabrum Mysticism** Central to both Guy's and Postel's vision is the Tabernacle's architecture as a figure of cosmic and spiritual reality. Guy inherited Postel's *Candelabrum* interpretation and developed it through his translation prefaces. Postel's second Zohar translation (1567-1569, completed on the Feast of the Transfiguration) emphasizes the Tabernacle of Bezaleel as the culminating mystical revelation. Guy echoes this, embedding the Tabernacle into French national eschatology and royal destiny. **La Galliade (1578): Mystical Epic of French Destiny** Guy's major poem, *La Galliade ou de la révolution des arts* (published 1578), represents the fullest poetic elaboration of Postelian thought. The work opens with the epigraph "C'est la gloire de Dieu la parole cacher / C'est la gloire des Rois la parole chercher" (It is God's glory to conceal the word; it is the glory of Kings to seek it)—a direct citation of Proverbes 25:2 and encapsulation of emithology's hermeneutical principle. The poem is accompanied by marginal etymologies of Hebrew and Chaldean roots for persons and place names, particularly demonstrating Guy's knowledge of the Zohar, Tiquney Zohar, and Bahir. Key Postelian themes structure the narrative: - **Zarphat (France)**: France is elevated as the chosen vessel of wisdom and justice, the "theater of honor" and "temple of prayer" where authority and reason dwell together. Through France, the world will be "repurged" and restored. - **Gomerie (Consummation)**: The "celeste et divin" divine principle established perfect mastery and true Gomerie (from Gomer), signifying the restoration of all things. - **Razenuins (Secret Keepers)**: The ancient Etruscan priesthood are celebrated as "propagators of mysteries," keepers of nature's deepest secrets revealed through divine law. - **Mount Sinai and the Law**: The law given on thorny Sinai is understood as the model and intelligible archetype of the world, with Sinai's very name signifying the thorns of Christ's Passion and the Messiah as mediator through whom the law was transmitted via angels to Moses. Guy cites Zohar on Leviticus to support this Christological reading. **Ecclesiastical Hymns and the Silkworm Mystery** In *Hymnes ecclesiastiques* (1578), Guy composed a "Chant royal" on the Virgin Mary's Immaculate Conception grounded in two Zoharic passages. The Zohar uses the metaphor of the silkworm—which creates its own cocoon sanctuary, produces precious silk garments, and dies to generate new life—as a figure of the divine generative principle and the mystery of transcendent manifestation. Guy transforms this into Christian Marian theology: the Virgin's womb becomes the sacred dwelling where the Spirit moves, generating the cosmic renewal that Adam's fall had broken. The poem balances technical poetic achievement (adherence to Puy de Palinode traditions) with profound kabbalistic erudition, demonstrating that sacred wisdom need not sacrifice literary elegance. **Nicolas's Systematic Hermeneutics: Le Cœur** While Guy excelled in synthetic and poetic elaboration, his younger brother Nicolas contributed the most systematic and comprehensive French exposition of kabbalistic hermeneutics. His preface to the *Heptaple* translation, titled *Le cœur (Leb), ou les trente-deux sentiers de la Sapience* (The Heart: The Thirty-two Paths of Wisdom), maps the complete architecture of Christian-kabbalistic biblical interpretation. Nicolas begins by rehearsing the classical four-fold hermeneutic: literal/historical, moral, allegorical, and anagogic. He then introduces the kabbalistic method as the distinctly Hebrew form of anagogical interpretation—relating each thing to its cabala (hidden reception), the esoteric science cultivated by kabbalists who leave literal peshat and halakha to rabbis and philosophical progress to Aristotelians. The method unfolds through a journey metaphor: literal interpretation opens the harbor; moral doctrine is the coastal plain; allegory—subdivided into four branches (historical, mundane/philosophical, figurative, symbolic)—represents increasingly treacherous seas; and the symbolic employs mathematics, Hebrew alphabet, numbers, geometry, music, astronomy, and the permutational arts (anagram, siruf/combination, notaricum, gematria) to arrive at port. This systematic traversal yields exactly thirty-two paths—corresponding to the thirty-two paths of wisdom encoded in the *Sepher Yetzirah*. Nicolas's arithmetic proof is elegant: the Hebrew word Leb (heart) yields lamed (30) + beth (2) = 32, corresponding to David's psalm on steps/ascensions in the heart. The thirty-two paths constitute a mystical ladder of scriptural and cosmic understanding. Nicolas expounds the *Sepher Yetzirah*'s doctrine of the three mother letters (Shin for fire, Mem for water/earth, Aleph for air) and the harmonious distribution of the three worlds (divine, celestial, corruptible), each subdivided into three, creating a unified cosmos reflecting the principle "Omnia in omnibus et singula in singulis"—all in all and each in each—a Neoplatonic maxim now grounded in Hebrew lettrism. **Critical Response: Genebrard's Skepticism** The section concludes by noting that not all contemporaries embraced kabbalistic exegesis. **Gilbert Genebrard** (1537-1597), celebrated royal Hebrew lecturer trained by Abraham de Lunel and honored by François de Sales, authorized the publication of Pico's *Heptaple* and Giorgi's *Harmonie* but grew increasingly critical of Kabbalah itself. In his *Chronographie*, Genebrard dismisses the *Sepher Yetzirah* as spurious and condemns Rabbi Simeon ben Yochai (purported author of the Zohar) for founding a "vain and insane" art obsessed with letter-play, numerical equivalence, transposition, anagrams, inversions, and ambiguous wordplay—practices Genebrard conflates as the three rules indicated by Ginat (geometry), though he applies the term to linguistic manipulation. This dissent underscores the tension within Renaissance Christian erudition between those who embraced mystical hermeneutics (Postel, the Le Fèvres, and their circle) and those who—while learning Hebrew and engaging Jewish sources—ultimately rejected kabbalistic interpretation as sophistry unworthy of Christian doctrine. **Legacy and Synthesis** The chunk demonstrates how Postel's mystical vision, mediated through Guy and Nicolas Le Fèvre's poetic, linguistic, and exegetical gifts, attained its most coherent and eloquent expression in the final decades of the sixteenth century. The integration of Neoplatonic cosmology, kabbalistic letter-mysticism, French eschatological nationalism, and Christian Platonism created a comprehensive intellectual framework that influenced humanist circles, royal pedagogy, and literary culture while provoking resistance from more conservative Hebraists. By 1580, Christian Kabbalah in France had evolved from Postel's prophetic eccentricity into an established—if contested—discourse within Renaissance scholarship and letters.
