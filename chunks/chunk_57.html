Carlo Montecuccoli (1592–1611), celebrated as a "Picus redivivus" (Pico born again), produced extensive manuscripts on kabbalah including the *Suc du bosquet* and *Suc du verger*, which were posthumously published with a substantial seven-chapter scholarly introduction authored by his brother in 1612. This introduction demonstrates systematic intellectual rigor, methodically addressing kabbalah's origins, definitive characteristics, internal divisions distinguishing good from corrupt manifestations, the hierarchical structure of the ten sefirot, the four kabbalistic worlds, prescribed prayer-modes within the system, and mystical interpretations of the Eucharist grounded in Kabbalistic symbolism. Montecuccoli's scholarly apparatus reveals extensive engagement with the foundational figures and texts: demonstrable knowledge of Pico and Galatin, consultation of the Rici brothers' materials, and direct engagement with Paul of Heredia's Hebrew work *Mechkar ha-sodot* (Examination of Mysteries). His methodology exemplifies the emerging professional standards for kabbalistic scholarship within Catholic intellectual circles. In Rome, the Hebrew grammarian Benedictus Blancuccius (1608) produced an extensive Hebrew grammar accompanied by remarkably detailed kabbalistic annotations on abbreviations and letter-combinations, drawing on earlier scholars including Marco Marini, Louis of Saint-François, and David of Pomis for understanding gematria and numerical equivalences. Franciscus Donatus (c. 1598–1635), a Dominican friar whose life ended violently at the hands of Muslim opponents, published a Hebrew grammar (1618) that incorporated translations of Salomon ibn Gebirol's mystical poem *Keter Malkhuth* (Crown of Kingship) alongside systematic treatises addressing Hebrew letter-abbreviations, citing authoritative mystical texts including the *Zohar*, the *Tikkun Zohar*, and the enigmatic *Epistola secretorum*. These grammatical works transformed Hebrew philology into a vehicle for transmitting and legitimizing Kabbalistic erudition, encoding mystical knowledge within ostensibly secular linguistic analysis. Hippolyte Obicius of Ferrara (writing 1605) stands as a vigorous protester against kabbalah's degeneration into superstition and magical charlatanism, citing Pico's mature conclusions as transmitted through Archangelus of Burgonovo. The Dominican Giovanni Paolo Nazari of Cremona (1556–1645) pursued rigorous critique in his *Opuscules* (1631), subjecting kabbalah to severe logical scrutiny and distinguishing five suspect propositions that he identified as the foundation of practical kabbalah's illegitimate claims: the assertion that magical efficacy requires either explicit or implicit kabbalistic operation; the corollary that Hebrew divine names alone possess intrinsic power; the claim that numerical characters equal or exceed alphabetic ones in mystical significance; the proposition that pure, unmediated kabbalah surpasses ordinary magic in efficacy; and the foundational division of the science into theoretical and practical domains. Nazari's methodical deconstruction demonstrates the increasing sophistication of kabbalah criticism and the emergence of scholars capable of engaging Kabbalistic claims on their own intellectual ground. Ignazio Landriani (1579–1642), an Olivetan monk and convert-maker of remarkable determination (he claimed to have converted thirty-two Hebrews to Christianity), exemplifies intellectual honesty combined with genuine erudition in Hebraic and Talmudic studies. Landriani consulted rabbinic commentaries and Talmudic works directly in primary Hebrew manuscripts, engaging authentic Jewish sources with scholarly seriousness. His critical reading proved devastatingly effective: he identified the *Gale Razia* as fabricated "by one of ours" (a Christian forgery), decisively rejected unchecked citations from Finus whose quoted passages proved impossible to locate, and candidly admitted that Pagnini's purported quotations from rabbinic sources remained unfound in all available texts. Yet Landriani maintained genuine intellectual appreciation for authentic Kabbalistic materials, treasuring authentic *Zohar* passages and Bechai's substantive commentaries while acknowledging that Jewish communities had deliberately destroyed certain texts to prevent Christian polemical exploitation and appropriation. The Jesuit Scipion Sgambati (1595–1652), whose unpublished *Three Books on the Archives of the Old Testament* appeared posthumously in 1703, accepted del Rio's comprehensive condemnation of practical kabbalah's efficacy while nonetheless systematically multiplying textual citations from the *Portes de lumière* and the *Zohar* itself, particularly passages concerning the mystical figure Henoch. Sgambati's equivocation reveals the institutional complexity of Jesuit engagement with kabbalah: official condemnation of practical claims coexisted with serious textual engagement and scholarly curiosity about authentic materials. His candid confession that he had never encountered the supposedly influential *Epistola secretorum* despite numerous citations casts serious doubt on that text's actual circulation and authenticity, suggesting that Renaissance scholars frequently cited legendary or imaginary works. German-speaking regions produced the period's most vigorous systematic critiques of Christian kabbalah. Christopher Besold (1577–1638), a Tübingen jurist and classical scholar, engaged extensively with Pistorius's important collection of kabbalistic texts yet characteristically avoided deploying the term "kabbalah" in his own work. Guillielmus Schickardus (1592–1635), a Hebrew professor and Besold's close intellectual colleague, published the deliberately provocative *Bechinath ha-pesuschim* (1618), arguing that kabbalah functions fundamentally as "a game of wit" designed for intellectual entertainment and amusement rather than serving as binding doctrine of faith. Schickardus condemned practical kabbalah's magical pretensions with particular severity, directing sharp criticism at Reuchlin's credence in practical secrets and advocating that kabbalah should be deployed solely as polemical weapon against Jews' own superstitions rather than accepted as genuine mystical truth. Matthias Bernegger (1582–1640) at Strasbourg warned his students explicitly against false sciences including the Lullian art, kabbalah, Rosicrucian claims, and the spirit-familiar practitioners actively marketing their services throughout Italy. Bernegger's denunciation extends to the entire Christian kabbalah tradition originating with Pico and Reuchlin, which he branded as blasphemous fiction, supporting his judgment through references to medieval cautionary examples including Raban Maur and Valeria Proba. Petrus Kirstenus (1577–1640) in an Arabic grammar (Breslau, 1608) dismisses cabalistes with withering contempt as "idle scrutinizers of nonsense," explicitly equating kabbalah with accursed magic and forbidden arts.
