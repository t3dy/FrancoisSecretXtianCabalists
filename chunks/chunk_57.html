Carlo Montecuccoli (1592-1611), a "Picus redivivus," produced manuscripts on kabbalah (the *Suc du bosquet* and *Suc du verger*) published with a seven-chapter introduction by his brother in 1612. The introduction systematically treats kabbalah's origin, definition, internal divisions (good versus corrupt forms), the ten sefirot, four worlds, prayer-modes, and the Eucharist's mystical interpretation. Montecuccoli demonstrably knew Pico, Galatin, and the Rici brothers, citing Paul of Heredia's *Mechkar ha-sodot*. In Rome, Benedictus Blancuccius's Hebrew grammar (1608) supplies extensive kabbalistic annotations on abbreviations, drawing on Marco Marini, Louis of Saint-François, and David of Pomis for gematria. Franciscus Donatus (c. 1598-1635), a Dominican beheaded by Muslims, published a Hebrew grammar (1618) with Salomon ibn Gebirol's *Keter Malkhuth* translation and treatises on letter-abbreviations citing the *Zohar*, *Tikkun Zohar*, and *Epistola secretorum*. Hippolyte Obicius of Ferrara (writing 1605) protests kabbalah's degeneration into superstition, citing Pico's conclusions via Archangelus of Burgonovo. Dominican Giovanni Paolo Nazari of Cremona (1556-1645) in his *Opuscules* (1631) rigorously critiques kabbalah as merely natural magic, distinguishing five suspect propositions: magical efficacy requires explicit or implicit kabbalistic operation; Hebrew names alone possess power; numerical characters equal alphabetic ones; pure, unmediated kabbalah surpasses ordinary magic; and the science divides into theoretical and practical domains. Ignazio Landriani (1579-1642), an Olivetan monk, exemplifies honest scholarly rigor. Having converted thirty-two Hebrews to Christianity, he consults rabbinic commentaries and Talmudic works directly. He identifies the *Gale Razia* as fabricated "by one of ours," rejects unchecked citations from Finus, and admits Pagnini's quotations remain unfound. Yet he treasures authentic *Zohar* passages and Bechai's commentaries, acknowledging that Jews destroyed texts to prevent Christian exploitation. Jesuit Scipion Sgambati (1595-1652), in his unpublished *Three Books on the Archives of the Old Testament* (pub. 1703), accepts Del Rio's condemnation of practical kabbalah but multiplies textual citations from the *Portes de lumière* and *Zohar* passages on Henoch. He confesses never encountering the *Epistola secretorum*, casting doubt on its authenticity. German-speaking regions produce vigorous critiques. Christopher Besold (1577-1638), a Tübingen jurist, cites Pistorius's collection yet avoids the term "kabbalah." Guillielmus Schickardus (1592-1635), a Hebrew professor and Besold's friend, published *Bechinath ha-pesuschim* (1618), arguing kabbalah functions as "a game of wit" for entertainment rather than faith doctrine, and condemning practical kabbalah's magical pretensions. He severely criticized Reuchlin's credence in practical secrets, advocating kabbalah's deployment solely against Jews' own superstitions. Matthias Bernegger (1582-1640) at Strasbourg warned students against false sciences including the Lullian art, kabbalah, Rosicrucian claims, and spirit-familiar practitioners sold in Italy. He denounces the Christian kabbalah tradition originating with Pico and Reuchlin as blasphemous fiction, citing Raban Maur and Valeria Proba as cautionary examples. Petrus Kirstenus (1577-1640) in an Arabic grammar (Breslau, 1608) brands cabalistes as "idle scrutinizers of nonsense," equating kabbalah with accursed magic.
