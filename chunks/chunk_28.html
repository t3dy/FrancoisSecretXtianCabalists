Secret documents how Christian Kabbalah established itself in sixteenth-century France, initially through the transmission of Italian and German scholarship but crystallizing in works dedicated to the royal court and ecclesiastical authorities. The narrative centers on how Kabbalistic interpretation became integrated into mainstream humanist study of Hebrew rather than remaining marginal. François Tissard (ca. 1508) represents the first explicit mention of Kabbalah in French, having studied in Ferrare and learned "the art called that of the cabalistes" from skilled masters. The Royal College of Three Languages brought systematic Hebraism to Paris: after Tissard and Jérôme Aléandre (who lamented France's scarcity of Hebrew books), François Vatable emerged as the crucial teacher of Guillaume Postel and others, combining literal exegesis with sensitivity to Jewish tradition. The medical humanist Symphorien Champier (1471–ca. 1539), based in Lyon, provided the first sustained French exposition of Kabbalah, defining it through Pic de la Mirandole, Reuchlin, and Paulus Ricius as "infused science"—knowledge transmitted mouth-to-mouth rather than through reason, exemplified by Saint Anthony and miraculous conversion. By 1518, Champier had formulated a critical stance: authentic Kabbalah concerns the "most secret sense of divine words," while "recent cabalistes" peddle superstition, claiming power over demons and magical miracles. François Ier's curiosity prompted the Franciscan Jean Thénaud to compose the *Saincte et très chrestienne cabale* (1519, in verse). Though the King found it disappointing—expecting technical Kabbalah but receiving instead celestial hierarchies and triadic theology—Thénaud's prose revision, the *Traicté de cabale*, systematically covers four worlds (angelic, celestial, elemental, human), the immortality of souls, the sacred angelic world, and the mystery of Hebrew Kabbalah in twelve chapters derived from Reuchlin, Ricius, and Pic. Thénaud reproduces Raban Maur's *De laudibus sanctae crucis* and experiences visionary absorption at Mount Sinai, ultimately arguing that Latin letters contain as much mystery as Hebrew—a relativization of Hebraic privilege that would echo through later century polemics. This section establishes the royal court as a crucial site for Kabbalistic circulation, where mystical exegesis served reformist and statecraft objectives simultaneously.
