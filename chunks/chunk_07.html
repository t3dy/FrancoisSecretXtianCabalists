Chunk 7

Pages 23-25

This chunk traces Pico's encounter with Mithridates, a Jewish convert of mysterious origins who served as his tutor in Chaldaic and Kabbalistic texts during the crucial years of Pico's intellectual formation in the 1480s. According to Gaffarel's later catalog—itself a significant historical source preserved through his library acquisitions—Mithridates translated three important manuscripts for Pico: Menahem de Recanati's commentary on the Pentateuch, writings of Eleazar of Worms (the Hasid Ashkenazi and author of the *Sodot ha-Shem*), and a work by Sem Tob ibn Joseph ibn Palquera, a 14th-century Spanish philosopher and logician. These translations became foundational to Pico's understanding of medieval Kabbalah, moving beyond superficial acquaintance with Jewish mysticism into genuine engagement with the technical apparatus of sephirotic thought and divine nomenclature. Mithridates served not merely as a language tutor but as an intellectual guide introducing Pico to the philosophical depths of Hebrew mysticism. However, Mithridates' true personality and relationship with Pico emerges strikingly through the marginal glosses—literally marginal notes—he added to these translations. These glosses attacked Pico repeatedly and with considerable vehemence, accusing him of avarice (greed for knowledge and power), dishonesty (intellectual dishonesty in appropriating Jewish teachings), lust (a recurring moral accusation), and cowardice (spiritual or political cowardice in the face of ecclesiastical scrutiny). The physical evidence of these erasures is striking: ink differences between the original glosses and later overwriting reveal that Pico himself—or someone on his behalf—attempted to remove Mithridates' criticism, seeking to expunge the record of this conflicted relationship. Yet despite Pico's erasure efforts, Gaffarel managed to identify and decipher the underlying accusations, preserving this testimony of Mithridates' judgment. The sketched portrait of Mithridates from these glosses resembles a Cervantic rogue character: a man of mercurial piety whose religious commitments shifted dramatically, a figure who claimed conversion from Judaism to Islam to Christianity to natural religion, moving opportunistically through religious identities as circumstances demanded. This portrait raises profound questions about conversion in Renaissance Italy and the sincerity or opportunism of religious allegiance. More historically significant than the colorful character of Mithridates is Paul of Heredia (Pablo de Heredia), a Spanish Jew of obscure origins who converted late in life and became profoundly influential in shaping the entire intellectual apparatus of Christian Kabbalah across the Renaissance. His *Epistola secretorum* (Letter of Secrets), dedicated to a Spanish ambassador to Rome (with the period 1486–1488 proposed), presents what appears to be a spurious Hebrew text, allegedly attributed to Rabbi Simeon and later Kabbalistic authors, claiming to reveal Christian mysteries—the Trinity, Christ's divinity, redemption, and salvation—already present within and derivable from Jewish mystical sources. This was a bold and theologically audacious claim: Heredia argued that the deepest truths of Christian theology could be found encoded in the very textual tradition that Jewish theologians guarded. Though Heredia claimed only to translate the *Gale Razeia* (Revealer of Secrets), Farissol's account suggests these texts were actually fabricated by converted renegades—perhaps including Heredia himself—who sought Christian apologetic ammunition mined from the Aggadoth (narrative expansions in the Talmud) and Midrashim (homiletical interpretations of Scripture). The *Epistola* thus represents both a genuine translation project and a creative forgery, a hybrid document born of the collision between Christian apologetics and Jewish mystical vocabulary. Heredia's *Epistola* would prove enormously consequential for the entire Christian Kabbalistic tradition. Pierre Galatin, a Christian Hebraist, incorporated Heredia's work wholesale into his monumental *De arcanis catholicæ veritatis* (1518)—The Mysteries of Catholic Truth—a work that defined and systematized Christian Kabbalah for generations of Renaissance humanists. The inclusion of Heredia's spurious texts lent apparent authority to the entire enterprise. During this period, the Zohar, that foundational medieval Jewish mystical text, became a battleground: it was normally burned alongside Talmuds by Church authorities, yet Christian scholars extracted copies, had them printed, and accepted the Zohar as an ancient authority conferring legitimacy on Christian Kabbalistic interpretation. In this context, Pico's humanist ambition becomes crystal clear: he claimed to have found within Kabbalistic texts, properly decoded and interpreted, the same dogmatic truths (Trinity, Incarnation, the Messiah) taught by Paul of Tarsus, Denys the Areopagite, Jerome, and Augustine—positioning Hebrew mysticism as philosophical corroboration of Christian doctrine alongside the classics (Pythagoras and Plato). His vast project of 900 theses promised systematic combat against Jewish objections to Christian theology, turning Jewish textual tradition back against itself, using their own mystical sources as weapons in Christian apologetics.
