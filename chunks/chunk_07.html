This chunk opens with Pico's dramatic personal history alongside his emerging scholarly interests in Hebrew and Kabbalah. Following his failed abduction of Margherita, wife of a cousin of Lorenzo de' Medici, near Arezzo—an episode that cost him ten soldiers and two personal wounds requiring treatment in Perugia—Pico entered into a complex relationship with his Hebrew tutor Mithridates. This Mithridates, whose full identity remains historically obscure but whose character is brilliantly documented through textual evidence, became the crucial conduit through which Pico gained access to medieval Jewish mystical texts. Mithridates extracted an oath of secrecy from Pico, refusing to share his Chaldaic knowledge with anyone else, most notably ejecting Jerónimo Benivieno when the latter visited as Pico's guest during an Aramaic lesson. Yet despite this jealous guardianship, Mithridates' very glosses on the texts he translated reveal a deeply ambivalent and hostile relationship with his patron, providing an invaluable window into the intellectual and personal tensions underlying the formation of Christian Kabbalah.

The textual transmission that Pico established becomes visible through Gaffarel's later research. In his cataloging work commissioned by Richelieu in the early 16th century—work that led him to Venice where he purchased manuscripts once belonging to Pico—Gaffarel identified three key manuscripts: Menahem de Recanati's commentary on the Pentateuch (a foundational exegetical work anchoring Kabbalistic interpretation to biblical text), a collection of writings by Eleazar of Worms (the German Hasid and transmitter of mystical technique), and Sem Tob ibn Joseph ibn Palquera's Liber graduum (The Book of Degrees)—a 13th-century philosophical work. These manuscripts bore unmistakable evidence of Mithridates' handiwork: dense marginal glosses added during translation that functioned both as scholarly apparatus and as a running commentary on Pico's character and reliability. The Vatican library, which came into possession of two of these manuscripts alongside others translated by Mithridates, preserves the physical traces of this relationship: visible erasures where ink differing in tone and shade reveals that someone—Pico himself, according to Gaffarel's inference—attempted to remove Mithridates' most stinging accusations.

What makes Gaffarel's analysis so remarkable is his methodical reconstruction of these erasures, piecing together what Mithridates had written about his employer from the underwriting visible where Pico's editing strokes had passed. Mithridates accused Pico of insatiable avarice for knowledge (aviditas sapientiae), of boundless desire for honor (sitis immoderatus honoris), of broken promises (manque de parole), of duplicity and falsehood (duperie et le mensonge), of cowardice, and notably of his incarceration in the Castel Sant'Angelo—references that align with Pico's documented arrest in Rome on charges related to his theses. Mithridates also attacked Pico's moral character regarding women, his vanity concerning his physical beauty, and unspecified other vices. Yet Gaffarel also preserves Pico's own defense through his nephew Francesco's memoir, which confirms that young Pico had indeed been passionate about learning and susceptible to feminine attractions ("ardent to shine and burning with the fire of vain loves, he proved very sensitive to female charms; many women indeed became inflamed for him"). This convergence of source material—Pico's own life as recorded by family, Mithridates' hostile glosses, Gaffarel's scholarly reconstruction—allows Secret to argue that Mithridates was not merely calumnious but observed genuine character flaws, and that his bitterness likely stemmed from unpaid wages, a detail that illuminates the material dimensions of Renaissance intellectual labor.

Mithridates emerges as a figure of remarkable complexity and striking parallel to the picaresque characters in Cervantes' fiction—indeed, Gaffarel explicitly compares him to a master Alphonse encountered by the doctor Torralba, a rogue who boasted of abandoning the faith of Moses for that of Mohammed, then Christianity, then natural religion or atheism, shifting allegiances like a man without deep conviction. This pattern of promiscuous conversion raises fundamental questions about the nature of religious identity in the Italian Renaissance. Mithridates' extensive glosses reveal not merely linguistic competence but theological erudition and a sharp, critical intelligence; he did not hesitate to attack not only Pico but also fellow Jews (including another Mithridates mentioned in the Count's letters, whom he accused of anger, envy, and homicide), nor did he spare Innocent VIII and the cardinals—indeed, labeling himself an "active adversary of our most holy religion." His need to hide from persecution, whether from Christian authorities or Jewish communities, becomes intelligible as a survival strategy of a man whose sharp tongue and shifting allegiances made him dangerous to every community he encountered.

Paul of Heredia, a Spanish Jew of obscure origins whose actual name Secret indicates remains poorly known, represents a qualitatively different but ultimately more historically consequential figure. Unlike Mithridates, whose influence was mediated through private tutelage and fragmentary textual transmission, Heredia authored published works that would shape the entire Christian Kabbalistic enterprise for the next two centuries. He converted in his old age, having previously engaged in theological disputation with the Dominican bishop of Cefalù, Joannes Gattus (died 1484), who was renowned for his learning in Greek, Hebrew, and astronomy and had been a friend of Bessarion. His unpublished work, The Sword of Paul (Gladius Pauli), was promoted by Antonio Flaminio, a Sicilian humanist scholar learned in Greek, Hebrew, and Arabic who taught at the University of Rome. Flaminio's tragic end, living as a misanthrope without family or friends, taking his meals daily at a nearby inn, only to be discovered dead by the innkeeper surrounded by the books he habitually studied on the floor—his library scattered and dispersed—exemplifies the precarity of the Renaissance Hebrew scholar's existence. The Vatican library acquired some of his manuscripts, including one formerly owned by Cardinal Giles of Viterbo, while others passed to Pierre Galatin, who extracted from them kabbalistic texts concerning the divine names—a methodological point of immense importance for the later systematization of Christian Kabbalah.

The two remaining works by Heredia that survive as rare incunabula of uncertain date represent the incunabula stage of Christian Kabbalistic literature and establish the fundamental vocabulary and interpretive strategy that would dominate the field. The Epistola secretorum (Letter of Secrets), dedicated to Don Inigo López de Mendoza, Spanish ambassador to Rome from 1486 to 1488, presents itself as a translation and expansion of an ancient Hebrew text, the Gale Razeia (Revealer of Secrets), attributed to Rabbenu Haccados (meaning "our master"), a figure supposedly inspired by the Holy Spirit and successor to Rabbi Simeon, the legendary author of the Zohar. Heredia claims that this Haccados revealed all the mysteries of Our Lord Jesus Christ, prophesying not future events but recounting accomplished facts like an evangelist. He further cites Rabbi Nehunias ben Hacana, who allegedly manifested with luminous clarity that the Messiah's coming would occur within fifty years, expressing joy that he would witness this event, and calling his work the Iggheret hassodot (Letter of Secrets). This chain of transmission—from the most ancient Simeon through Haccados to Nehunias—creates an illusion of continuous mystical tradition stretching across centuries and converging on Christian truth.

Yet the actual origins of this textual apparatus remain deeply suspect. Abraham Farissol, the celebrated geographer and defender of Judaism who lived at the court of the Ferrara nobility, records in his Magen Abot (Shield of Fathers) a striking confession: that Christian controversialists, despairing of conquering through literal exegesis, had fabricated midrashim and aggadot specifically designed to prove Trinitarian doctrine by mining the language and forms of authentic Jewish exegetical traditions. Farissol reports encountering Spanish exiles who revealed that a small group of renegades—"twelve with their chief"—had recently created these fabricated midrashim in the style of the Zohar, Bereshit Rabba, and authentic midrashic literature. This evidence strongly suggests that Heredia's Epistola, rather than being a mere translation, represents a creative forgery: texts composed in the style and vocabulary of authentic Jewish mysticism, attributed to ancient rabbis, and deployed as apologetic ammunition against Jewish objections to Christian doctrine. The work thus sits at the ambiguous frontier between translation, commentary, and outright fabrication—a hybrid document born from the collision of Christian apologetic necessity and access to authentic Jewish textual tradition.

The Epistola secretorum would prove extraordinarily consequential because it established the foundational strategy and vocabulary for all subsequent Christian Kabbalah. Its second work, La Couronne du Roi (The Crown of the King), dedicates itself to Innocent VIII and addresses the Immaculate Conception, connecting the technical vocabulary of Kabbalah to Marian theology and papal doctrine. Both works deploy the term kabbale (or cabala), positioning these works at the very moment—late 1486—when Pico's Conclusions scandalized Rome and Innocent VIII issued preliminary condemnations. Yet rather than retreating, Heredia and subsequently Pico intensified their engagement with Kabbalistic texts and terminology. In employing the Epistola for his monumental De arcanis catholicæ veritatis (The Mysteries of Catholic Truth, 1518), Pierre Galatin would ensure that Heredia's spurious texts—texts fabricated or heavily shaped by converted renegades—would achieve the status of ancient authority for two centuries of Christian Kabbalistic speculation. The historical irony is profound: Christian scholars preserved and printed the Zohar precisely when Jewish texts were being burned, using Zoharic authority to validate claims that the Zohar itself does not support and likely contradicts.

This material and textual emergence of Christian Kabbalah from the 1480s forward represents not a genuine dialogue with Jewish mysticism but rather a determined appropriation, a weaponization of Jewish textual tradition for Christian apologetic purposes. Pico's 900 theses—or 10,000 propositions if we credit his nephew's amplified count—and his claim to have discovered in Kabbalistic texts, through proper decoding, the identical truths of Trinitarian Christianity, Incarnation, Messiah, and redemption already attested in Paul, Denys, Jerome, and Augustine represents the systematic articulation of this strategy. By positioning Kabbalah as philosophical corroboration for Christian doctrine, equivalent in authority to Pythagoras and Plato (whose works Augustine so praised), Pico inaugurates an intellectual tradition in which the mystical Judaism of the Middle Ages becomes paradoxically the proof text for Christian orthoxy. The very existence of these texts—Mithridates' glosses, Heredia's epistles, Gaffarel's careful reconstruction—testifies to the complex dynamics of textual transmission, intellectual ambition, personal rivalry, material circumstance, and apologetic urgency that generated one of the Renaissance's most distinctive intellectual movements.
