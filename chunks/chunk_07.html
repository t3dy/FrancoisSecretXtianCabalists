Chunk 7

Pages 23-25

This chunk traces Pico's encounter with Mithridates, a Jewish convert of mysterious origins who served as his tutor in Chaldaic and Kabbalistic texts. According to Gaffarel's later catalog, Mithridates translated three important manuscripts for Pico: Menahem de Recanati's commentary on the Pentateuch, writings of Eleazar of Worms, and a work by Sem Tob ibn Joseph ibn Palquera. However, Mithridates' true personality emerges strikingly through glosses he added to these translations—marginal notes attacking Pico repeatedly for avarice, dishonesty, lust, and cowardice. These erasures (visible by ink differences) suggest Pico later attempted to remove Mithridates' criticism, though Gaffarel identified the underlying accusations. The sketch of Mithridates resembles a Cervantic rogue character: a man of mercurial piety who claimed conversion from Judaism to Islam to Christianity to natural religion. More historically significant is Paul of Heredia (Pablo de Heredia), a Spanish Jew of obscure origins who converted late in life and became profoundly influential in shaping Christian Kabbalah. His *Epistola secretorum* (Letter of Secrets), dedicated to a Spanish ambassador to Rome (1486–1488), presents a spurious Hebrew text attributed to Rabbi Simeon and later authors, claiming to reveal Christian mysteries—the Trinity, Christ's divinity, redemption—already present in Jewish mystical sources. Though Heredia claimed only to translate the *Gale Razeia* (Revealer of Secrets), Farissol's account suggests these texts were fabricated by converted renegades seeking Christian apologetic ammunition from Aggadoth and Midrashim. Heredia's *Epistola* would prove enormously consequential: Pierre Galatin incorporated it wholesale into his *De arcanis catholicæ veritatis* (1518), a work that defined Christian Kabbalah for generations. The Zohar—normally burned alongside Talmuds—was printed by Christians and accepted as ancient authority conferring legitimacy on Christian Kabbalistic interpretation. In this context, Pico's humanist ambition becomes clear: he claimed to have found in Kabbalistic texts the same dogmatic truths (Trinity, Incarnation, Messiah) taught by Paul, Denys, Jerome, and Augustine, positioning Hebrew mysticism as philosophical corroboration of Christian doctrine alongside Pythagoras and Plato. His 900 theses promised systematic combat against Jewish objections using their own textual tradition.
