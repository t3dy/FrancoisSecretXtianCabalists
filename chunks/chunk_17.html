Paul Ricius (Paulus Israelita) enters as the dominant kabbalistic voice of the early sixteenth century, though his origins remain mysterious. Secret notes his probable baptism in Italy (1505), his study under Pietro Pomponazzi (1462–1524), and his meeting with Erasmus at Pavia (1506). His spiritual father was Gometius, a Portuguese Franciscan and later Vicar General of the order, who features prominently in Ricius's *De cælesti agricultura*. Ricius began publishing *Sal Fæderis* (1507), dedicated to Louis XII, announcing his aim to defend Christianity against Jewish calumnies and confirm faith through kabbalistic intelligence. He published the *Isagoge* (1509–1510) with endorsements from Gometius, Vincent Dodi, and the influential Augustinian Joannes Benedictus Moncettus. Moncettus's preface declares kabbalah a source of unmatched spiritual delight and sponsors the first Latin translation of the *Porta lucis* (Gate of Light), the Sa'are Orah by Moses de León, announcing it would not be published until 1515 at Augsbourg to avoid frightening the uninitiated. Between 1514 and 1532, Ricius held prestigious positions: physician to the Holy Roman Emperor Maximilian I (1514–1519), corresponding with humanists including Willibald Pirckheimer and Albrecht Dürer. In 1519 he debated Jean Eck on the world soul; in 1530 he received a title of nobility (Freiherr, Baron of Sparzenstein). His *Statera prudentium* (1532), attempting reconciliation of Catholics and Reformers, provoked denunciation by Jérôme Aléandre, who charged Ricius with presupposing a divided Church and usurping episcopal prerogatives. Though Ricius retracted, his entire *De cælesti agricultura* (1541) was later indexed at Trento despite testimonials from Bologna and Padua theologians. Finally, Secret traces Ricius's defense against charges of false kabbalah by the priest Stephanus, affirming that Pico was not an infallible authority and that kabbalistic wisdom—like Pseudo-Dionysius—was legitimately transmitted in esoteric form.
