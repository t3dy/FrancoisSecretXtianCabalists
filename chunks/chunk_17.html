Paul Ricius (Paulus Israelita) commands the stage as the dominant and most influential kabbalistic voice of the early sixteenth century, though biographical mystery shrouds his origins and earliest formation. Secret reconstructs fragmentary evidence: his probable baptism in Italy (1505) marked him as a convert from Judaism, a transition conferring on him a unique interpretive authority within Christian circles—he possessed firsthand knowledge of rabbinic Judaism and intimate familiarity with Hebrew textual traditions, yet he professed Christian faith, enabling him to translate kabbalistic insights into Christian theological idiom. His intellectual formation proceeded under Pietro Pomponazzi (1462–1524), the celebrated Paduan philosopher whose reputation for radical Aristotelianism and heterodox opinions attracted the most ambitious minds in Renaissance Italy. Ricius's meeting with Erasmus at Pavia (1506) indicates his swift integration into the humanist elite—Erasmus, the supreme intellectual authority of northern Europe, apparently recognized in Ricius a kindred spirit and fellow seeker of prisca sapientia (ancient wisdom). His spiritual father and mentor was Gometius, a Portuguese Franciscan of formidable learning and subsequently appointed Vicar General of his order, whose influence shaped Ricius's theological and spiritual sensibility profoundly. Gometius features prominently throughout Ricius's De cælesti agricultura (On Heavenly Agriculture), suggesting not merely patronage but deep intellectual partnership.

Ricius began publishing his kabbalistic writings with Sal Fæderis (The Salt of the Covenant) in 1507, a work dedicated to King Louis XII of France, strategically announcing his theological aims: to defend Christianity against Jewish calumnies and to confirm the faith through kabbalistic intelligence. The work positions kabbalah not as a novel import but as an ancient Christian inheritance—early Church fathers possessed kabbalistic knowledge until scholastic rationalism eclipsed it, and Ricius promises its recovery. His next major publication, the Isagoge (Introduction to Kabbalah) appeared in 1509–1510 and carried endorsements from formidable authorities: Gometius supplied a preface declaring kabbalah a source of unmatched spiritual delight; Vincent Dodi, a prominent Dominican; and most influentially, the Augustinian Joannes Benedictus Moncettus, whose intellectual stature and ecclesiastical position lent enormous weight to the enterprise. Moncettus's preface declares kabbalah a treasury of spiritual wisdom and sponsors the first Latin translation of the Porta lucis (Gate of Light), the foundational kabbalistic work attributed to Moses de León, simultaneously announcing that the translation would be withheld from publication until 1515 at Augsbourg to avoid frightening the uninitiated and provoking premature ecclesiastical censorship.

Between 1514 and 1532, Ricius ascended to positions of extraordinary prestige and influence. From 1514 to 1519 he served as physician to the Holy Roman Emperor Maximilian I, the most powerful political figure in Christian Europe, granting him intimate access to the highest echelons of power. This position exposed him to the learned culture of the imperial court and allowed him to correspond with the greatest humanists of the age, including Willibald Pirckheimer, the Nuremberg humanist who championed ancient wisdom traditions, and Albrecht Dürer himself, the supreme artist-theorist of the Renaissance, whose interests in mathematics, proportion, and esoteric geometry aligned naturally with kabbalistic concerns. In 1519 Ricius participated in a formal theological debate with Jean Eck, the formidable Ingolstadt theologian, on the nature of the world soul—a disputation before such an authority suggests Ricius's standing as a serious theological voice rather than a marginal mystic. In 1530, recognition of his accomplishments culminated in a title of nobility, becoming Freiherr (Baron) of Sparzenstein, a distinction honoring both his intellectual achievements and his service to the Emperor.

Yet Ricius's prominence invited both admiration and systematic opposition. His Statera prudentium (The Balance of the Prudent), published in 1532, attempted an ambitious reconciliation of Catholic and Reformed positions at the moment when the Protestant schism had become irreversible. The work provoked bitter denunciation by Jérôme Aléandre, the influential ecclesiastical diplomat and inquisitor, who charged Ricius with presupposing an already-divided Church (thereby implicitly accepting the schism as legitimate) and with usurping episcopal prerogatives (claiming to judge theological truth through kabbalistic science rather than submitting to ecclesial authority). The charge wounded Ricius, who retracted portions of his argument; nonetheless, the entire De cælesti agricultura (1541), his magnum opus and most comprehensive kabbalistic theology, fell victim to ecclesiastical suspicion and was indexed on the Church's prohibited books list at the Council of Trento, despite passionate testimonials from theologians at the University of Bologna and the University of Padua attesting its orthodoxy and spiritual profundity. The suppression represented a catastrophic victory for the Scholastic establishment defending its dominion against mystical alternatives.

Finally, Secret traces Ricius's vigorous self-defense against charges of false kabbalah leveled by the priest Stephanus, who questioned Ricius's claims to authentic kabbalistic transmission. Ricius responded by asserting that Pico della Mirandola, though a towering authority, possessed no infallibility—kabbalistic wisdom, like the mystical theology attributed to Pseudo-Dionysius, was legitimately transmitted in esoteric form, concealed beneath literal readings and available to adepts who underwent proper training. The charge and countercharge established a crucial interpretive principle: Christian kabbalah could be defended as genuine engagement with Jewish mystical tradition while remaining selective, innovative, and ultimately subordinate to Christian theological needs. Ricius's fierce defense of his own orthodoxy while maintaining the validity of kabbalistic methods established a template for subsequent Christian kabbalists navigating between Jewish sources and Christian institutional authority.
