Chunk 63

Pages 196-197

This chunk continues the extensive footnote documentation (notes 52-128) from chunk 62, maintaining the apparatus's focus on specific works, manuscripts, and scholarly practices in Christian Kabbalah from the Renaissance through mid-17th century. The notes record an impressive array of cabalistic treatises and their authors: Jean Nodin's *Victoria Hebræorum adversus Ægyptios* (1611), attacking apocryphal cabalistic prophecy; Ludovicus Crocius of Bremen accepting cabalistic interpretation and citing both the *Bahir* and *Moechcarhassodoth*; Rabbi Salomon Azubi's October 1632 letter to Peiresc offering a manuscript on the *Othioth* attributed to Rabbi Akiva, on the 22 letters of the Hebrew alphabet and their secrets; Jean de Croix (d. 1659) publishing specimen observations (*Specimen conjecturarum*, 1632) promising a syntagma on the Messiah's names and a Latin edition of the *Sefer Yetzirah* (Book of Creation) with five commentaries. The apparatus tracks bibliographic details of multiple works: Erycius Puteanus's Brussels edition (1643) of *De annagrammatismo Cabalæ*, drawing heavily on Guy Le Fèvre de la Boderie; H. Bardi's *Medicus politus catholicus* (1644), citing Gaffarel's annotations derived from a Spanish rabbi's *Catena cabalistica*; Thomas Dufour's 1642 *Linguæ hebraicæ opus* with discussion of mystical secrets, though Dufour himself rejects the cabala; Nicolas Abram's 1648 *Pharus V. Testamenti* dedicating a chapter to Kabbalah while following Genebrard; and Jean d'Espierres's 1651 *Auctoritas Scripturæ sanctæ*, which questions the Zohar's antiquity but still includes a cabalistic chapter following Bonfrère. The notes collectively demonstrate the breadth of engagement with Hebrew linguistics, rabbinical texts, and cabalistic theory across religious orders (Dominicans, Jesuits, Benedictines, Franciscans) and national locations, establishing how cabalistic knowledge became embedded in the fabric of Catholic intellectual life by the mid-17th century.
