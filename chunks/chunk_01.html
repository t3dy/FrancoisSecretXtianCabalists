Chunk 1

Pages 1-4

This chunk opens with publisher front matter (the Dunod "Collection SIGMA" series list and the title page of *Les kabbalistes chrétiens de la Renaissance* by F. Secret), then begins the **Avant-propos**. Secret distinguishes scholarly "kabbale" — following Gershom **Scholem**'s *Major Trends in Jewish Mysticism* and Georges **Vajda**'s definition (ancient Jewish esotericism plus the Talmudic, Midrashic, and Judeo-Arabic philosophical corpus) — from the debased popular sense of "cabale" (occultism, secret plots, the *Comte de Gabalis*). He reviews the historiography: the érudite Latin tradition (Colomiès, Wolf, Bartolocci, Basnage, Buddeus, Brucker), Adolphe **Franck**'s *La Kabbale* (1843), the occultist responses of Eliphas Lévi and Papus, and the partial successes of A. E. Waite and Paul **Vulliaud**. J. L. **Blau**'s *The Christian Interpretation of the Cabala in the Renaissance* (1944) is named as the first synthetic study, though faulted for ignoring prior scholarship. Secret frames Lucien Febvre's "grosse question" — what Kabbalah meant to Christians — and limits his own scope to the period bounded by the Polyglot Bibles (Alcalá, Antwerp 1572, Paris 1645). He then surveys the sources newly available to Christians: Bomberg's Venice press, the first complete Babylonian **Talmud** (1520-23) under Leo X, rabbinic commentators (Rashi, Ibn Ezra, David **Kimhi**, Nahmanides/Ramban, Gersonides/Ralbag, Saadia, **Maimonides**, Hasdai Crescas), and Midrashim. He sketches the development of Jewish Kabbalah: the early speculations on *Ma'aseh Bereshit* (creation) and *Ma'aseh Merkabah* (the chariot), the *Hekhalot*, *Sefer Yetzirah*, *Raziel*, the German Hasidism of Eleazar of Worms, the prophetic Kabbalah of Abraham **Abulafia**, and the **Zohar** attributed to Simeon bar Yohai, plus the *Bahir*, *Ginnat Egoz*, and Gikatilla's *Sha'are Ora*. The chunk closes by introducing the principal Christian Kabbalists — **Pico** (linked to Ficin's Platonic Academy), **Reuchlin** ("Pythagoras redivivus"), Agrippa, Francesco Giorgio of Venice — and an extended portrait of **Guillaume Postel** (1510-1581) as the illuminist mirror of his century, apostle of universal concord and a "Restitutio."
