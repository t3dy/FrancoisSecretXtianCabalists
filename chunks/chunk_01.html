Chunk 1

Pages 1-4

This chunk opens with publisher front matter (the Dunod "Collection SIGMA" series list and the title page of *Les kabbalistes chrétiens de la Renaissance* by F. Secret), then begins the **Avant-propos** with a methodological clarification that anchors the entire work. Secret establishes a critical distinction between scholarly "kabbale" — following Gershom **Scholem**'s monumental *Major Trends in Jewish Mysticism* and Georges **Vajda**'s definition encompassing ancient Jewish esotericism plus the Talmudic, Midrashic, and Judeo-Arabic philosophical corpus — and the debased popular sense of "cabale" that had accumulated over centuries. This degraded meaning encompassed everything from occultist speculation and secret plots to the salacious *Comte de Gabalis* and the mystifications of modern spiritualism. By establishing this terminological boundary at the outset, Secret signals his intention to treat Renaissance Christian Kabbalah as a serious intellectual and theological current, not as marginal occultism. He conducts a thorough historiographical review, tracing the genealogy of scholarly and popular treatments that preceded his work. The érudite Latin tradition of the 17th and 18th centuries — scholars like Colomiès, Wolf, Bartolocci, Basnage, Buddeus, and Brucker — had provided foundational bibliography but lacked synthetic interpretation. The 19th century saw Adolphe **Franck**'s *La Kabbale* (1843), a major work that nonetheless could not escape the occultist interpretations of Eliphas Lévi and Papus that dominated popular culture. The early 20th century brought partial successes: A. E. Waite contributed serious scholarship despite his own occult affiliations, and Paul **Vulliaud** brought French Catholic perspective to the question. J. L. **Blau**'s *The Christian Interpretation of the Cabala in the Renaissance* (1944) is named as the first fully synthetic study, though Secret notes it as flawed for its conscious or unconscious disregard of prior scholarship, particularly the older érudite tradition. Secret frames his own project against the famous "grosse question" posed by historian Lucien Febvre — what Kabbalah meant to Renaissance Christians — and deliberately limits his chronological scope to the period bounded by the Polyglot Bibles: the Alcalá Polyglot of Cardinal Ximenes (begun 1514), the Antwerp Polyglot (1572, under Plantin), and the Paris Polyglot (1645), which marked both the flowering and the closing of the greatest age of trilingual Christian scholarship. He then surveys the new textual resources that became available to Christian scholars beginning in the late 15th century. The **Bomberg press** in Venice revolutionized Jewish printing, and under the patronage of Pope **Leo X**, the first complete **Babylonian Talmud** was printed between 1520 and 1523 — a watershed moment that gave Christian scholars direct access to rabbinic commentary and dispute. Secret catalogs the major rabbinic figures whose glosses and commentaries shaped Christian understanding: **Rashi** (Solomon ben Isaac, 1040-1105), the foundational commentator; **Ibn Ezra** (1089-1164), the medieval Spanish grammarian and philosopher; **David Kimhi** (1160-1235), whose biblical and grammatical works became standard references; **Nahmanides/Ramban** (1194-1270), the Kabbalist philosopher of Barcelona; **Gersonides/Ralbag** (1288-1344), the rationalist logician; **Saadia** (892-942), the Geonic philosopher whose thought mediated between Judaism and Neoplatonism; and **Maimonides** (1138-1204), the synthesizer of Jewish law and Aristotelian philosophy; and **Hasdai Crescas** (1340-1410), the last great medieval Jewish philosopher. The availability of the Midrashim — the rabbinic homiletical and exegetical literature — complemented this, opening depths of interpretive tradition to Christian minds. Secret sketches the developmental trajectory of Jewish Kabbalah itself, which he presents as a prerequisite for understanding what Christians encountered. He traces the early speculations on *Ma'aseh Bereshit* (the work of creation, cosmogony) and *Ma'aseh Merkabah* (the work of the chariot, mystical ascent), rooted in the mysterious *Hekhalot* literature of the Geonic period describing the seven heavenly palaces. The *Sefer Yetzirah* (Book of Formation), attributed to Abraham, offers a speculative cosmology based on ten sefirot and twenty-two letters of the Hebrew alphabet. The mystical *Raziel* attributed to the angel Raziel and the ethical-mystical tradition of German Hasidism associated with **Eleazar of Worms** (c. 1176-1238) represented earlier currents. The **prophetic Kabbalah** of **Abraham Abulafia** (1240-1291), the ecstatic Kabbalist of Barcelona and Sicily, contributed letter-combination techniques (*gematria*, *notarikon*, *temurah*) aimed at achieving mystical union. The **Zohar** attributed to **Simeon bar Yohai** but actually composed in 13th-century Spain, represented the apex — a dense Aramaic mystical commentary on Torah that codified Sefiratic symbolism and mystical theology. Complementing these were the *Bahir* (Book of Brightness), the *Ginnat Egoz* (Garden of Nuts), and **Gikatilla**'s *Sha'are Ora* (Gates of Light), all providing frameworks for letter-mysticism and Sefiratic theology that Christian scholars would adapt. The chunk closes by introducing the principal Christian Kabbalists who form the nucleus of Secret's narrative. **Pico della Mirandola** (1463-1494), linked to the Platonic Academy (*Accademia Neoplatonica*) of Marsilio Ficin in Florence, appears as the innovator who first yoked Christian theology to Kabbalah explicitly. **Johannes Reuchlin** (1455-1522), the German humanist and Hebrew scholar who styled himself "Pythagoras redivivus" (Pythagoras reborn), codified Christian Kabbalah as a disciplined method in his *De verbo mirifico* and *De arte cabalistica*. **Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa** (1486-1535), the polymathic synthesizer of Renaissance magic and occult philosophy, would systematize Christian esotericism on a larger canvas. And **Francesco Giorgio of Venice** (Franchinus Georgius, 1460-1540), the Franciscan theologian and Hebraist, would construct elaborate Sefiratic cosmologies integrated with Christian theology. The portrait grows more extended and luminous as Secret introduces **Guillaume Postel** (1510-1581), a figure he presents as the "illuminist mirror of his century" — a restless visionary, apostle of universal concord (*concordia universalis*), and prophet of a *Restitutio* (restoration) of all things, whose syncretic vision wove Kabbalah, Islamic mysticism, linguistics, and mystical Christianity into an apocalyptic theology that would dominate French esoteric thought for generations.
