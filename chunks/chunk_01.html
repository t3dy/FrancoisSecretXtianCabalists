Secret's Les kabbalistes chrétiens de la Renaissance opens with a methodological clarification that serves as the intellectual foundation for the entire work. Published in 1963 in the Dunod Collection SIGMA, this volume represents a synthesis of decades of research into the encounter between Renaissance Christian humanists and Jewish mystical theology. Secret's first critical move is to establish a precise distinction between the scholarly term "kabbale" and the degraded popular term "cabale" that had accumulated centuries of semantic distortion. Drawing on Gershom Scholem's revolutionary work Major Trends in Jewish Mysticism (1946, English edition; French translation 1950) and Georges Vajda's systematic Introduction à la pensée juive du moyen âge, Secret defines authentic Kabbalah as "a product that presupposes, beyond ancient Jewish esotericism, the entire body of Talmudic and Midrashic writings, as well as nearly the totality of the theological-philosophical speculations of the Judeo-Arab period." This definition anchors Kabbalah firmly within the Jewish intellectual tradition from its Geonic foundations through medieval Spanish mysticism. In contrast, the Renaissance saw the French term "cabale" degrade toward meaning everything remotely "Hebrew," eventually encompassing secret machinations (menées secrètes), the frivolous Comte de Gabbalis of Montfaucon de Villars, and the literary parody La Rôtisserie de la Reine Pédauque — all monuments to the collision between serious mysticism and popular occultism.

Secret's historiographical review establishes the genealogy of scholarship that preceded his own systematic treatment. He credits the Latin erudite tradition, particularly from the 17th and 18th centuries, with preserving bibliography and textual foundations despite their lack of interpretive synthesis. Key figures in this tradition include Paul Colomiès (Gallia orientalis, 1665), Johann Christoph Wolf (Bibliotheca Hebraea, 1715–1735, a monumental Hebrew bibliography), Giuseppe Bartolocci (Bibliotheca Magna Rabbinica, 1675–1693, later supplemented by Immanuel Azariah di Rossi's Bibliotheca latino-hebraica), Jacques Basnage de Beauval (Histoire des Juifs, 1706), Johann Franz Budaeus (Introductio ad historiam philosophiae Ebraeorum, 1720), and Johann Jakob Brucker (Historia critica philosophiae, 1742–1744, a work that influenced Goethe). The 19th century was marked by Adolphe Franck's seminal La Kabbale ou la philosophie religieuse des Hébreux (1843; second edition 1889), which attempted both to resurrect scholarly interest in Jewish mysticism and to add a section on Christian Kabbalah—a project he ultimately abandoned. Franck's work stimulated serious scientific inquiry, but it also inadvertently popularized Kabbalah among occultists like Eliphas Lévi (Alphonse Louis Constant, 1810–1875), Papus (Gérard Encausse, 1865–1916), and other self-proclaimed "doctors of Kabbalah" (docteurs en kabbale) who invoked Franck's authority while distorting his scholarship. By the second edition of his work, Franck himself had grown ambiguous, even saluting certain representatives of this occultist current as those "disgusted with positivist, evolutionist, or brutally atheistic doctrines that dominate in our country."

The early 20th century saw more measured approaches: Arthur Edward Waite (1857–1942), an English occultist and scholar, produced works on Kabbalah that maintained at least partial scholarly standards despite his esoteric affiliations; and Paul Vulliaud (1875–1950), a French Catholic scholar, explicitly called for a study of Christian Kabbalah and sketched its broad outlines in his works, including his Traduction intégrale du Siphra di-Tzeniutha (1930) and La correspondance de J. de Pauly (1933). Secret credits Joseph León Blau (The Christian Interpretation of the Cabala in the Renaissance, published in New York, 1944) as the first comprehensive monograph on the subject—a work whose unavailability in French translation Secret deplores as a scholarly loss, though he notes Blau's conscious or unconscious neglect of substantial prior scholarship, particularly the older erudite tradition. Secret frames his own project against the famous "grosse question" posed by historian Lucien Febvre in his Au cœur religieux du XVI siècle (1957): "What is Kabbalah for Christians?" This question had animated scholarship from Adolphe Franck through Blau, extending chronologically from Pico della Mirandola and Reuchlin to François-Mercure Van Helmont (whom Franck studied), through Knorr von Rosenroth's monumental Kabbala denudata (1677–1678, two volumes), and even to the early 20th-century French translation of the Zohar by the mysterious Jean de Pauly (published 1911). Secret deliberately restricts his scope to what he calls the "golden age" (l'âge d'or) of Christian Kabbalah, a period stretching from the late Middle Ages—when it first appeared in European thought—to the end of the first half of the 17th century. He justifies this temporal boundary by reference to the Polyglot Bibles: the Alcalá Polyglot initiated by Cardinal Francisco Jiménez de Cisneros (1436–1517) in the early 16th century, the Antwerp Polyglot of Christophe Plantin (1520–1589), published in 1572, and the Paris Polyglot of Michel de Montfaucon, published in 1645. These monumental scholarly editions embodied the ideal of trilingual biblical study—in the original Hebrew, Greek, and Latin—that made Jewish textual traditions newly accessible to Christian scholars.

The rise of Hebrew philology and the study of original languages transformed European Christian intellectual life in the late 15th and 16th centuries. Secret emphasizes that Christian Kabbalah developed precisely "in the century of Polyglots and trilingual colleges" (au siècle des Polyglottes et des collèges trilingues). The ex libris designed by Albrecht Dürer for the humanist Willibald Pirckheimer (1470–1530) exemplifies this ideal: displaying Psalm 111:10 ("The fear of God is the beginning of wisdom") in Hebrew, Greek, and Latin in the order of the title of the cross (titulus), it represents the Renaissance ideal inherited from Saint Jerome—for whom Hebrew was the holy language of creation and revelation, occupying the pride of place. Not all humanists achieved this ideal with equal enthusiasm: Desiderius Erasmus (1466–1536), while encouraging Hebrew studies, notably never learned Hebrew himself, despite formulating its importance. Jules Michelet (1798–1874), reading Franck's work with enthusiasm, intuited that with Pico della Mirandola, Reuchlin, and Guillaume Postel (translator of the Sefer Yetzirah and Zohar), "the first dawn of day appeared"—a perception validated by subsequent scholarship. Abbé A. Humbert, in Les origines de la théologie moderne (1911), more precisely located Christian Kabbalah alongside Pico and Reuchlin within the "new science" (nouvelle science)—a term borrowed from Leo X to designate the critical study of Old and New Testament texts in their original languages, whose rigorous engagement with Hebrew literature, "in its ultimate intentions entirely religious," led naturally to kabbalistic speculation. The patronage of Leo X (Giovanni de' Medici, 1475–1521), son of Lorenzo the Magnificent, for the first complete Babylonian Talmud at the Bomberg press in Venice (1520–1523) marked a turning point: Christian scholars now possessed, in print, the full rabbinic discussion and commentary that had previously been inaccessible or fragmented.

Secret catalogs the immense textual resources that suddenly became available to Christian scholars through the Bomberg press and other Hebrew printing centers, drawing on Conrad Gesner's (1516–1565) bibliographic catalog of Hebrew books published in Basel in 1547. Beyond the Bible itself, with its Targum, Massoretic apparatus, and variant commentaries, Christian scholars encountered the foundational medieval Jewish commentaries: Rashi (Solomon ben Isaac of Troyes, 1040–1105), whose glosses on Torah and Talmud were the starting point for any serious engagement with sacred text; Abraham ibn Ezra (1089–1167), the medieval Spanish commentator and grammarian whose philosophy synthesized Neoplatonism and Judaism; David Kimhi (1160–1235), famous not only for his biblical exegesis but for his grammatical works, particularly the Sepher Michlol (Book of Fullness) and his Shorashim (Roots), which became standard philological references; Nahmanides or Ramban (1194–1270), the great Catalan Kabbalist-commentator from Gerona whose biblical glosses subtly introduced mystical interpretation; Gersonides or Ralbag (Levi ben Gerson, 1288–1344), the rationalist logician and philosopher comparable in intellectual stature to Saadia; Saadia Gaon (882–942), the early medieval genius whose Emunoth we-Deoth (Beliefs and Opinions) had synthesized Jewish theology against Islamic and Christian philosophy; Maimonides (1138–1204), the supreme synthesizer whose Mishneh Torah and Moreh Nevuchim (Guide for the Perplexed) integrated Aristotelian logic with Jewish law and theology; and Hasdai Crescas (c. 1340–1410), the last great medieval Jewish philosopher whose work closed an era of Jewish philosophical integration. Complementing these textual authorities were liturgical works, ethical treatises, and the vast Midrashic literature—the Mishnah with its numerous exegetical traditions, the Midrashim Rabbah (each named after the biblical book it glosses—Bereshith Rabba on Genesis, Shemoth Rabba on Exodus, etc.)—all opening to Christian scholars depths of rabbinic exegetical and homiletical tradition previously opaque.

Secret then surveys the developmental trajectory of Jewish Kabbalah itself as a prerequisite for understanding what Christian scholars encountered and would adapt. He traces the emergence of Kabbalah as a distinct esoteric discipline from its Geonic antecedents through its medieval Spanish flowering. The earliest speculative traditions concerned themselves with Ma'aseh Bereshit (the work of creation, the cosmic cosmogony based on Genesis 1) and Ma'aseh Merkabah (the work of the chariot, the mystical ascent based on Ezekiel's throne vision), whose textual foundations lay in the mysterious Hekhalot literature (texts describing the celestial palaces through which the mystic ascends). The foundational text Sefer Yetzirah (Book of Formation), attributed to Abraham himself, presented cosmology as the interplay of ten sefirot (countable divine emanations) and twenty-two Hebrew letters—a scheme that would become the skeleton of all Christian Kabbalist theology. Related texts including the Raziel (attributed to the angel Raziel, who revealed divine secrets to Adam after the Fall) and the German Hasidic tradition associated with Eleazar of Worms (c. 1176–1238) introduced additional mystical frameworks. Abraham Abulafia (1240–1291), the ecstatic Kabbalist of Barcelona and Sicily, developed specifically the prophetic Kabbalah (Kabbala nevu'it) with its letter-combination techniques (gematria, notarikon, temurah) aimed at achieving direct mystical union with the divine. The apex of this development came with the Zohar (Book of Splendor), attributed in legend to Simeon bar Yohai, the 2nd-century Jewish martyr, but actually composed in 13th-century Spain (likely Castile) as a mystical Aramaic commentary on Torah that codified Sefiratic theology and provided the blueprint for mystical exegesis that Christian scholars would spend centuries adapting to Christian theology. Complementing these were the Bahir (Book of Brightness), an early 12th-century mystical work; the Ginnat Egoz (Garden of Nuts) based on the Song of Songs; and Joseph Gikatilla's (1248–1305) Sha'are Ora (Gates of Light), all providing frameworks for letter-mysticism and Sefiratic symbolism that Christians would eagerly absorb.

Secret introduces the principal Christian Kabbalists who form the nucleus of his narrative, each representing a distinct intellectual context and modality. Giovanni Pico della Mirandola (1463–1494), linked to the Platonic Academy (Accademia Neoplatonica) founded by Marsilio Ficin (1433–1499) in Florence—the great translator of Plato, Plotinus, and Hermes Trismegistus—appears as the innovator who first yoked Christian theology explicitly to Kabbalah. Johannes Reuchlin (1454–1522), the German humanist Hebrew scholar who styled himself "Pythagoras redivivus" (claiming to do for Pythagoras what Lefèvre had done for Aristotle), codified Christian Kabbalah as a disciplined method in his De verbo mirifico (Three Books on the Miraculous Word, 1494) and especially his De arte cabalistica (Three Books on the Art of Kabbalah, 1517), dedicating the latter to Pope Leo X with the explicit claim that he had revived understanding of Pythagoras's esoteric wisdom through kabbalistic science. Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa (1486–1535), the polymathic polymath and synthesizer of Renaissance magic, occult philosophy, and esoteric knowledge, would systematize Christian esotericism in his massive Three Books of Occult Philosophy (De occulta philosophia, 1531), integrating Kabbalah with Hermetic magic and natural magic in a totalizing system. Francesco Giorgio of Venice (Franchinus Georgius, c. 1460–1540), the Franciscan theologian-Hebraist, would construct elaborate Sefiratic cosmologies integrated with Trinitarian Christian theology in works like his De harmonia mundi (1525). And Guillaume Postel (1510–1581), a towering and obsessive visionary whose intellectual life spanned the entire 16th century, embodied what Secret calls the "illuminist mirror of his century"—a restless theorist of universal concord (concordia universalis) and prophet of a cosmic Restitutio (restoration) of all things in their original paradisiacal state, whose syncretic mysticism wove together Kabbalah, Islamic mysticism, linguistic philosophy, and apocalyptic Christian eschatology into a towering if ultimately fragmentary vision that would dominate French esoteric and mystical thought for generations. These figures, Secret suggests, represent not marginal curiosities but central expressions of Renaissance intellectual ambition: the desire to access through language and symbol the deepest mysteries of creation, to reconcile Jewish wisdom with Christian revelation, and to reconstruct a unified intellectual cosmos shattered by centuries of denominal division and scholastic fragmentation.

Secret's introduction emphasizes the international and polymathic character of the men who pursued Christian Kabbalah. Postel in particular exemplifies this: described as "one of the first royal readers" (un des premiers lecteurs royaux) and favored by Francis I (1494–1547), "father of letters"; one of the early recruits to the nascent Society of Jesus; a collaborator on the first edition of the Syriac New Testament; a co-editor of the Antwerp Polyglot; a correspondent with Philipp Melanchthon (1497–1560), the Protestant reformer, with cardinals of the Council of Trent, with different kings of France, and with Emperor Ferdinand (who appointed him reader at the University of Vienna); a defender of the Spanish physician and radical theologian Michel Servet (1511–1553); and an intimate friend of Sébastien Castellion (1515–1563), the great Christian irenic and pluralist. One of Postel's disciples wrote that he had "the whole round of the world around him"—an apt phrase for a figure who drew together the geographical discoveries that were expanding European horizons, the political and economic development of the continent, the military and spiritual confrontation with the Ottoman Turks, and the Renaissance recovery of ancient Greek, Jewish, and Arab thought. Postel the cosmographer, mathematician, Hellenist, and Hebraist published in Paris, Basel, Venice, in French, Latin, Italian, Hebrew, and even Arabic; he produced the first alphabet of twelve languages, the first European Arabic grammar, a novel anthology of the Quran, translations of the Sefer Yetzirah and Zohar, and vast collections of Greek, Hebrew, and especially Arabic manuscripts treating geography, mathematics, medicine, and botany. His lifelong engagement with Islamic civilization—which he emphasized covered "10/12 of the world"—awakened him to the religious condition of Christendom itself, where Catholics and Reformed were locked in intolerant conflict. Sensitive to Luther's reformation even as he admired the apostolic poverty and global missionary reach of the early Jesuits, assured of reason's power (which atheists turned against revelation but which could also defend tradition), Postel came to believe himself the first-born of a great Restitutio—the restoration of humanity to its pre-lapsarian state. Heir to the medieval unitary dream and to the eschatological perspectives maintained by Joachite movements, this prophet of universal concord believed he was the angelic pope of the last times charged with reforming the Church and gathering "all in one flock under one shepherd"—Turks, Jews, and Christians alike. Secret closes this opening movement by noting that his book will follow a dual historical-thematic order, inspired by the filing method of Valéry Larbaud, organizing facts "clearly and according to the order of time and the relationships of cause and effect," moving from conceptual ancestors through geographical regions to religious reactions and the afterlife of the tradition in the 17th century.
