Secret demonstrates that despite widespread attempts to annex Paracelsus (1493-1541) and his followers to the Christian kabbalah tradition, Paracelsus fundamentally understood "Gabala" through an entirely different conceptual framework than Christian Hebraists. Rather than scriptural letter-mysticism or sephirothic emanation theology, Paracelsus conceived kabbalah as imaginative force—the magically-potent imagination capable of producing astral and material effects in the sublunar world. This Paracelsian reinterpretation proved tremendously influential, reshaping how subsequent natural philosophers and medical alchemists approached mystical knowledge systems. Brucker synthesized three foundational Paracelsian theses that defined his understanding of "Gabalistic art": imagination without object-attraction produces nothing; imagination functioning as a womb impresses form onto reality—this is "Gabalistic art"; imagination enables one to produce supernal effects in the sublunar world through "true Gabalia." Yet Paracelsus himself explicitly disavowed Jewish sources, claiming that Jews received Gaba-nala originally from "Ethnics" and corrupted it through ignorance, while Persians and Magi had preserved its truth uncorrupted. In *Philosophia Sagax*, he classifies kabbalah among six magical species—practical techniques by which human voice carries 100,000 paces, or couriers traverse month-long journeys in days. Paracelsus treats kabbalah as practical astrological magic concerning celestial influx and the creation of talismanic bodies charged with cosmic forces. This reframing separates Paracelsian kabbalah entirely from Hebrew textual mysticism, relocating it within natural magic and astrological practice. Unlike Paracelsus himself, his followers attempted to develop more coherent and systematic theories synthesizing Paracelsian magic with traditional kabbalah. Jacques Gohory (1520-1576), known as a translator of Colonna's *Hypnerotomachia Poliphili*, published a 1568 *Resumé* definitively characterizing kabbalah as universal—encompassing all creation—or particular—concerning individual reason and consciousness. For Hebrews, he argues, it denotes uninterrupted tradition of mysteries transmitted from Moses; yet it equally embraces letter-combination, alphabetic revolution, and specific methods for accessing supralunary powers. Gohory cites Pico's seventy books of Ezra and the prophecies of Elijah before carefully distinguishing three categories: characters (magic), numbers (kabbalah), and letters (intermediary) functioning between spiritual and material realms. His syncretic approach attempts to reconcile Paracelsian imaginative magic with traditional kabbalistic letter and number mysticism. Gérard Dorn, another influential Paracelsian figure, attacked Gohory's synthesis sharply, claiming that melancholy had driven him into seclusion and prevented him from grasping true Paracelsian magic, which resides fundamentally in imagination rather than kabbalah's textual foundation. This critique reveals tensions within Paracelsian circles between those seeking to preserve the primacy of imaginative force and those attempting to reconcile Paracelsian innovation with traditional mystical authorities. Yet despite such critiques, synthesis remained a dominant impulse among medical alchemists seeking comprehensive frameworks. Blaise de Vigenère (*Images et tableaux de Philostrate*, 1597) represents a more successful and elaborate synthesis of Paracelsian and kabbalistic cosmologies, demonstrating how two seemingly incommensurable systems could be intellectually unified. Typhon (in Orphic mythology) corresponds precisely to the kabbalistic Zamael and Paracelse's "archée"—the active natural heat operating on universal matter to generate mineral, vegetable, and animal kingdoms progressively. The three material principles (salt, sulfur, mercury) parallel three formal principles (Aresz, Iliaste, archée), systematically mapped through kabbalah's emanatory structure: the Godhead (Ehie) expressing as *Cether* (Crown) through the Seraphim; the second emanation as *Iod* (Jupiter/divine Son) with *Hochma* (Wisdom) emanating through Cherubim to the stellar and elemental spheres. This architecture allows Paracelsian natural philosophy to be reinterpreted through kabbalistic categories while maintaining the primacy of Paracelsian force and imagination. Heinrich Khunrath (1560-1605) represents the apogee of Paracelsian-kabbalistic fusion, synthesizing medical alchemy, theology, and mysticism into an integrated visionary system. His monumental *Amphitheatrum sapientiæ æternæ* comprises a triple Catholic amphitheater—kabbalistic-divine, magical, physico-chemical—venerating Paracelsus as a "Germanic Aesculapius," Capnion (Reuchlin) for his "divine skill in kabbalah," Agrippa, John Dee, and Jean Olearius. The work encompasses symbolic engravings preceded by a prologue with verses equal to calendar days, alongside learned commentaries on Proverbs in Vulgate, Septuagint, and Hebrew simultaneously. Khunrath's language itself represents synthesis: "Christiano-kabbalistic, divino-magical, and physico-chemical"—stars function as divinely-appointed signs (Hebrew *Aot*, alphabet) through which God speaks directly to nature; viriditas (greenness, life-force) simultaneously represents Ruach Elohim (Spirit of God) for the cabalist, natura for the magus, and quintessence for the alchemist. This multilayered vocabulary allows different practitioners to read the same text through their own interpretative lens while addressing ultimate unity of all knowledge. Opposition to Paracelsian kabbalah mounted from conservative physicians and theologians defensive about institutional learning. J. Guinther d'Andernach, physician to Francis I, warned that new "kabbalistic meditations" constituted demoniacal superstition threatening Christian piety. André de Breil (1580) and Thomas Sonnet de Courval (1610) attacked the "detestable sect of chemists and Paracelsists," lumping Reuchlin and all kabbalists together with swindlers and charlatans condemned by the Sorbonne. Antonio Piccioli (1587) dismissed kabbalah as mere "waking reverie," falsely attributing foundational texts to Solomon, Abraham, Enoch, and angels rather than acknowledging their actual medieval Jewish authorship. Yet even hostile critics like Giovan Battista Segni distinguished "bad kabbalah" (superstitious divination) from "true kabbalah" (Christ's oral revelation to apostles), with both responses affirming kabbalah's Christological essence against Jewish misuse. André Cesalpin (1580) rejected occult philosophy promising miraculous results, dismissing kabbalah as a supposed art for evoking supernal powers—a claim falsified, he argued, by human impotence before the divine.
