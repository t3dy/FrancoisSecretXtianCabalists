Secret demonstrates that despite attempts to annex Paracelsus (1493-1541) to Christian kabbalah, he understood "Gabala" fundamentally differently than Christian Hebraists. Rather than scriptural letter-mysticism or sephirothic emanation, Paracelsus conceived kabbalah as imaginative force—the magically-potent imagination capable of producing astral effects in the material world. **Paracelsus and the Imagination-Based Kabbalah** Brucker synthesized three Paracelsian theses: imagination without object-attraction produces nothing; imagination as womb impresses form (this is "Gabalistic art"); imagination enables one to produce supernal things in the sublunar world through "true Gabalia." Yet Paracelsus explicitly disavowed Jewish sources, claiming that Jews received Gaba-nala from "Ethnics" and corrupted it through ignorance, while Persians and Magi preserved its truth. In *Philosophia Sagax*, he classifies kabbalah among six magical species—techniques by which human voice carries 100,000 paces, or couriers traverse month-long journeys in days. He treats it as practical astrological magic concerning celestial influx and talismanic bodies. **Paracelsian Disciples and Systematic Kabbalah** Unlike Paracelsus, his followers attempted coherent theories. Jacques Gohory (1520-1576), translator of Colonna's *Hypnerotomachia Poliphili*, published a 1568 *Resumé* defining kabbalah as universal (encompassing all creation) or particular (concerning individual reason). For Hebrews, it denotes uninterrupted tradition of mysteries from Moses; yet it also embraces letter-combination, alphabetic revolution, and methods for accessing supralunary powers. Gohory cites Pico's 70 books of Ezra and the prophecies of Elijah before distinguishing characters (magic), numbers (kabbalah), and letters (intermediary). Gérard Dorn criticized Gohory sharply, claiming melancholy had driven him into seclusion and prevented him from grasping true Paracelsian magic, which resides in imagination rather than kabbalah's textual base. Vigenère (*Images et tableaux de Philostrate*, 1597) synthesized Paracelsian and kabbalistic cosmologies. Typhon (in Orphism) corresponds to the kabbalistic Zamael and Paracelse's "archée"—the active natural heat operating on universal matter to generate mineral, vegetable, and animal kingdoms. The three material principles (salt, sulfur, mercury) parallel three formal principles (Aresz, Iliaste, archée), mapped through kabbalah's emanations: the Godhead (Ehie), expressing as *Cether* (Crown) through the Seraphim; the second emanation as *Iod* (Jupiter/divine Son) with *Hochma* (Wisdom) emanating through Cherubim to the stellar and elemental spheres. **Heinrich Khunrath and Integral Synthesis** Heinrich Khunrath (1560-1605) represents the apogee of Paracelsian-kabbalistic fusion. His *Amphitheatrum sapientiæ æternæ* (triple Catholic amphitheater—kabbalistic-divine, magical, physico-chemical) venerates Paracelsus as a "Germanic Aesculapius," Capnion (Reuchlin) for his "divine skill in kabbalah," Agrippa, John Dee, and Jean Olearius. The work comprises symbolic engravings preceded by a prologue with verses equal to calendar days, alongside learned commentaries on Proverbs in Vulgate, Septuagint, and Hebrew. Khunrath's language is "Christiano-kabbalistic, divino-magical, and physico-chemical"—stars as divinely-appointed signs (Hebrew *Aot*, alphabet) through which God speaks; viriditas (greenness, life-force) as Ruach Elohim (Spirit of God) for the cabalist, natura for the magus, quintessence for the alchemist. **Critical Reactions** Opposition mounted from physicians and theologians. J. Guinther d'Andernach, physician to Francis I, warned that new "kabbalistic meditations" constituted demoniacal superstition. André de Breil (1580) and Thomas Sonnet de Courval (1610) attacked the "detestable sect of chemists and Paracelsists," lumping Reuchlin and kabbalists with swindlers and charlatans condemned by the Sorbonne. Antonio Piccioli (1587) deemed kabbalah mere "waking reverie," attributing foundational texts falsely to Solomon, Abraham, Enoch, and angels. Giovan Battista Segni distinguished "bad kabbalah" (superstitious divination) from "true kabbalah" (Christ's oral revelation to apostles), yet both are Catholic responses affirming kabbalah's Christological essence against Jewish misuse. André Cesalpin (1580) rejected occult philosophy promising miraculous results, dismissing kabbalah as a supposed art for evoking supernal powers—a claim falsified by human impotence before the divine.
