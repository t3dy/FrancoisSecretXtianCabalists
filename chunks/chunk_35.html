**The Inner Circle of Disciples** This section traces Postel's immediate followers and their roles in disseminating his mystical vision within sixteenth-century French intellectual circles. **Jean Boulaese** (b. 1530) emerges as the first major disciple. While Postel was imprisoned in Rome, Boulaese began copying his manuscripts. Upon Postel's return, Boulaese became an ordained priest (1565) and professor of Hebrew at the Collège des Lombards before teaching at Montaigu. He functioned as Postel's amanuensis, recopying treatises, absorbing his doctrines, and notably transmitting Postel's concept of the celestial oriental alphabet composed of iodim (Hebrew letter yods). Boulaese authored Hebrew grammars and produced *La facile entrée des secrets cachez*, a popularization of the Candelabrum's mysteries, making Postel's esoteric cosmology accessible to educated readers. Boulaese served as the pseudonymous publisher of the *Miracle of Laon* (1566), a relation of the exorcism of Nicole Obry—an episode Postel interpreted as confirming his prophetic mission—using the name "Petrus Anusius Synesius" (the wise madman, the Angelic Pope). Boulaese's activities on behalf of this miracle extended to multiple language editions (French, Italian, Spanish, German) and presentations at the Spanish court and to Pope Pius V, events he claimed inspired the Antwerp Polyglot. He disappears from record after 1579. **Vincent Cossart** and others: A young Norman, Cossart, was directed to Postel by the student Louis Martel. Around 1575, Cossart translated Postel's *Divinationis* (1571) and the *Liber de causis* (1552), and planned further translations of the *De Etruriæ*, *De orbis terræ concordia*, and a revised *Euclides christianus*. Cossart became particularly influenced by Postel's prophetic interests. **Adrien Le Tartrier**, a Champagne physician, commemorated Postel with an epitaph playing on his name ("Postelli, postquam peragravit…poles parisios"). Le Tartrier published *Les promenades printanières* (1586), offering discourses on etymology and Kabbalah in Postel's style, dedicating a work on emithology to Gilbert Genebrard and maintaining friendships with Plantin and Guy Le Fèvre. **Jean Edouard Du Monin** (1557-1586), ranked by Naudé alongside Pico, Scalichius, and Postel for his precocious command of languages and disciplines, translated Du Bartas's creation epic into Latin verse in under fifty days and composed epitaphs for Postel in both Latin and French. His poetry engages kabbalistic themes, particularly the fear that Christian discovery of kabbalah would provoke jealous rabbinical opposition. **The Le Fèvre Brothers: Guy and Nicolas** The most important disciples were the Norman brothers **Guy Le Fèvre de la Boderie** (1541-1598) and **Nicolas Le Fèvre de la Boderie** (1550-1613), descendants of the famous diplomat Antoine. Their prominence derives from their central role in the Antwerp Polyglot Bible (sponsored by King Philip II of Spain), where they edited the Syriac New Testament with Benito Arias Montano. Postel, having provided a Syrian New Testament manuscript, claimed credit for the work through his disciples. **Guy's Intellectual Achievement**: Postel explicitly designated Guy as his successor. Guy published the *Encyclopedie* (1570) with Plantin, a cyclical work claiming to encompass all knowledge. His *Dictionnaire syro-chaldaïque* demonstrates profound engagement with kabbalistic terminology; entries on Zohar, Ilan (the tree of the Sephiroth), and other mystical concepts cite Postel's translations and interpretations. Guy's marginal annotations on a Genesis Zohar, the Tiquney Zohar, and Bahya ben Asher's Pentateuchal commentary reveal sustained scholarly study. Notes on the letter Aleph reference the four Iodim as symbols of the divine tetragrammaton; on Et (aleph-teth), he identifies mystical meanings paralleling Apocalyptic alpha-omega symbolism. His glosses frequently invoke Postel's theology—particularly the motif of the Church as the "hind of the dawn" (feminine soul of the Messiah), echoing Matthew 24:28 and Luke 17:37 on the gathering of eagles around the body. Guy's *Encyclopedie* (1570) and his translations of Pico's *Heptaple* and the *Harmonie du monde* (1579) with Nicolas made Renaissance Christian Platonism and mysticism accessible to French readers. The preface explicitly states he had already sketched these projects before reading Pico, suggesting independent mystical development parallel to Postelian instruction. **Nicolas's Contribution**: Younger brother Nicolas contributed a small treatise (1588) on the holiness of the Hebrew language and copious preface to the *Heptaple* translation titled *Le cœur (Leb), ou les trente-deux sentiers de la Sagesse* (The Heart: The Thirty-two Paths of Wisdom). This preface systematically expounds the kabbalistic doctrine of the thirty-two paths derived from the *Sepher Yetzirah*, mapping interpretive approaches to Scripture (literal, moral, allegorical, anagogic) onto a comprehensive mystical geography grounded in Hebrew linguistics and number symbolism. Nicolas's work represents the fullest French exposition of kabbalistic hermeneutical method in the sixteenth century. **Broader Influence and Associated Figures** The circle extended beyond the core disciples. **Florimond de Raemond** (c. 1540-1602) defended Postel's memory and deployed kabbalistic elements in his *Antichrist* (1597) and *Histoire de la naissance, progrez et décadence de l'hérésie* (1605), particularly the numerical equivalence of Jesus and Miriam and anagrammatic hermeneutics. **Théodore Zwinger** (1533-1588), nephew of Oporin (Postel's printer), corresponded with Postel and edited his manuscripts after Oporin's death. Zwinger's *Théâtre de la vie humaine*, a vast encyclopedic work, cites Postel on kabbalistic matters. **Pierre Victor Palma Cayet** (1525-1610), a converted Protestant logé at Saint-Martin-des-Champs, multiplied references to the Zohar on themes of mystical restoration, alchemy, and spiritual transmutation. **Gilbert Genebrard** (1537-1597), royal Hebrew lecturer, authorized translations of the *Heptaple* and *Harmonie* but grew skeptical of Kabbalah, critiquing the *Sepher Yetzirah* as spurious and dismissing kabbalistic exegesis as vain sophistry. This network of disciples, scholars, poets, and physicians sustained and transmitted Postel's vision across the final third of the sixteenth century, embedding Christian Kabbalah within the institutional fabric of royal pedagogy, biblical scholarship, and humanist letters.
