The Inner Circle of Disciples

This section systematically traces Postel's immediate followers and their distinctive roles in disseminating, elaborating, and institutionalizing his mystical vision within the broader landscape of sixteenth-century French intellectual circles, demonstrating how an individual prophet's private revelations became embedded within mainstream scholarship and letters.

Jean Boulaese (b. 1530) emerges as the first major disciple and perhaps the most devoted. While Postel suffered imprisonment in Rome during the Inquisitorial trials, Boulaese took upon himself the crucial labor of copying and preserving Postel's unpublished manuscripts—a task of enormous importance given the constant danger of loss through fire, confiscation, or decay. Upon Postel's eventual return from captivity, Boulaese pursued ecclesiastical orders, becoming an ordained priest in 1565, and secured positions of teaching authority at the Collège des Lombards and later at Montaigu, where he taught Hebrew. In these positions, Boulaese functioned simultaneously as Postel's amanuensis (personal scribe and secretary), engaging in the constant labor of recopying and correcting Postel's treatises, and as the transmitter of Postelian doctrine to new generations of students. He proved particularly skilled in elaborating and popularizing the more technically difficult aspects of Postel's system—notably the concept of the celestial oriental alphabet composed of iodim (the Hebrew letter yod or iota repeated), a mystical alphabet said to constitute the celestial language underlying all terrestrial speech. Boulaese authored Hebrew grammars and produced La facile entrée des secrets cachez (The Easy Entry to Hidden Secrets), a condensed and accessible popularization of the Candelabrum's cosmological mysteries, making Postel's esoteric system available to educated but non-specialist readers. He also served as the pseudonymous publisher of the Miracle of Laon (1566), a detailed account of the demonic exorcism of Nicole Obry, an episode that Postel himself had interpreted as a prophetic confirmation of his mystical mission and as a sign of the approaching Concorde. Using the pseudonym "Petrus Anusius Synesius" (the name encoding "the wise madman" and "the Angelic Pope" in its hidden meanings), Boulaese orchestrated the publication of multiple language editions—French, Italian, Spanish, and German—and arranged presentations of the miracle account at the Spanish court and to Pope Pius V, events he subsequently claimed had inspired the institutional decision to sponsor the Antwerp Polyglot Bible. Boulaese's activity in the religious and intellectual world extends documented record only until 1579, when he disappears from the archival record.

Vincent Cossart and a constellation of lesser-known disciples extended Postel's influence into younger generations. Cossart, a young Norman, was directed toward Postel's teachings by the student Louis Martel. Around 1575, Cossart undertook translations of Postel's Divinationis (1571) and the Liber de causis (1552), and planned—though apparently did not complete—further translations of the De Etruriæ, De orbis terræ concordia, and a revised Euclides christianus. Cossart became particularly absorbed in Postel's prophetic interests, suggesting a temperament aligned more with visionary enthusiasm than scholarly precision.

Adrien Le Tartrier, a physician from the Champagne region, commemorated Postel through an ingenious epitaph that plays on the prophet's name: "Postelli, postquam peragravit…poles parisios" (Postel, having traversed all things…the Parisian poles), using the name itself as a semantic bridge to mystical geography. Le Tartrier published Les promenades printanières (The Spring Wanderings) in 1586, a work offering philosophical discourses on etymology and Kabbalah modeled on Postel's own expository style, dedicating treatises on emithology (truth-lore) to Gilbert Genebrard and maintaining friendships with the printer Plantin and Guy Le Fèvre de la Boderie, thus moving within the same circles of humanist erudition.

Jean Edouard Du Monin (1557-1586), ranked by the bibliographer Gabriel Naudé alongside Pico, Scalichius, and Postel himself as a prodigy commanding an extraordinary range of languages and disciplines, translated Du Bartas's Huguenot creation epic La Sepmaine into elegant Latin verse in the astonishingly brief span of under fifty days, demonstrating the virtuosity of his linguistic gifts. Du Monin composed learned epitaphs for Postel in both Latin and French, and his surviving poetry engages with kabbalistic themes, particularly voicing the anxiety that Christian discovery and Christian appropriation of Kabbalah would provoke a jealous rabbinical response and opposition from Jewish scholars who guarded the Kabbalah as their exclusive intellectual property.

The Le Fèvre Brothers: Guy and Nicolas—Postel's Designated Heirs

The most important disciples were the Norman brothers Guy Le Fèvre de la Boderie (1541-1598) and Nicolas Le Fèvre de la Boderie (1550-1613), descendants of the celebrated diplomat Antoine Le Fèvre, giving them standing within the French nobility and diplomatic circles. Their prominence among Postel's followers derives from their central and visible role in the Antwerp Polyglot Bible, the vast multilingual biblical edition sponsored by King Philip II of Spain. In this prestigious project, they served as editors of the Syriac New Testament alongside the celebrated Spanish Hebraist Benito Arias Montano. Postel, having provided a valuable Syrian New Testament manuscript to the Polyglot's editorial team, claimed—through the agency of his devoted disciples—significant credit for the work's completion, thus embedding his own scholarly authority within an institutional monument to Christian learning.

Guy's Intellectual Achievement and Mystical Synthesis: Postel explicitly designated Guy as his successor and heir to the prophetic mission. Guy published the Encyclopedie (1570) with the great printer Plantin, a remarkable work claiming to encompass all human knowledge in a cyclical, integrating structure. His Dictionnaire syro-chaldaïque (Syro-Chaldean Dictionary) demonstrates profound engagement with kabbalistic terminology, with entries on the Zohar, Ilan (the Tree of the Sephiroth), and other mystical concepts systematically citing Postel's own translations and interpretative glosses. Guy's surviving marginal annotations on a Genesis Zohar, the Tiquney Zohar, and Bahya ben Asher's Pentateuchal Commentary reveal a scholar engaged in sustained, disciplined study of the most technically challenging Hebrew sources. His notes on the letter Aleph carefully reference the four Iodim (four yods) as mystical symbols of the divine tetragrammaton; his glosses on Et (the combination aleph-teth, א-ת) identify hidden meanings that parallel the Apocalyptic alpha-omega symbolism of Christian eschatology. Throughout his annotations, Guy frequently invokes Postel's theology—particularly the kabbalistic motif of the Church (Ecclesia) as the "hind of the dawn" (ayeleth ha-shachar, the feminine soul or Shekhinah of the Messiah), a reading that echoes Matthew 24:28 and Luke 17:37's mysterious sayings about the gathering of eagles around the body.

Guy's Encyclopedie (1570) and his collaborative translations with Nicolas of Pico's Heptaple and the Harmonie du monde (1579) made Renaissance Christian Platonism and mysticism accessible to French-reading audiences, creating vernacular pathways into the technical mystical traditions that had previously been confined to Latin and Greek learned circles. Notably, the prefatory material to the Encyclopedie explicitly states that Guy had already begun sketching these projects before reading Pico, suggesting that his mystical development proceeded in parallel with Postelian instruction rather than simply deriving from or imitating it—a distinction marking intellectual independence even within discipleship.

Nicolas's Systematic Contribution—The Thirty-Two Paths: The younger brother Nicolas contributed a treatise (1588) on the holiness of the Hebrew language itself as a sacred medium, and more significantly, authored an extensive preface to the Heptaple translation titled Le cœur (Leb), ou les trente-deux sentiers de la Sapience (The Heart: The Thirty-Two Paths of Wisdom). This preface represents the most systematic and comprehensive French exposition of kabbalistic hermeneutical method produced in the sixteenth century. Nicolas methodically maps the classical four-fold interpretive method (literal, moral, allegorical, anagogic) onto the thirty-two paths of wisdom derived from the Sepher Yetzirah, creating a unified architecture of biblical interpretation grounded in Hebrew linguistics and numerological symbolism. This work establishes the doctrinal and methodological foundation upon which subsequent French Christian Kabbalah would be constructed.

Broader Influence and Associated Figures

The Postelian circle extended well beyond these core disciples. Florimond de Raemond (c. 1540-1602) emerged as a defender of Postel's memory and deployed kabbalistic elements strategically in his anti-heresy works, particularly his Antichrist (1597) and Histoire de la naissance, progrez et décadence de l'hérésie (History of the Birth, Progress, and Decline of Heresy, 1605), where he employed the numerical equivalence of Jesus and Miriam and anagrammatic hermeneutics as tools of doctrinal analysis. Théodore Zwinger (1533-1588), nephew of the printer Oporin and thus positioned at the nexus of printing and scholarship, corresponded with Postel during his lifetime and undertook the editorial work of preserving and organizing Postel's manuscripts after Oporin's death. Zwinger's vast Théâtre de la vie humaine (Theatre of Human Life), an encyclopedic compilation of world knowledge, repeatedly cites Postel as an authority on kabbalistic matters. Pierre Victor Palma Cayet (1525-1610), a converted Protestant scholar lodged at the monastery of Saint-Martin-des-Champs (where Postel spent his final years in confinement), multiplied references to the Zohar in his historical writings, particularly on themes of mystical restoration, alchemy, and spiritual transmutation. Yet Gilbert Genebrard (1537-1597), the celebrated royal Hebrew lecturer trained by Abraham de Lunel and honored by François de Sales, represents the critical counterweight within this network: while he authorized publication of Pico's Heptaple and Giorgi's Harmonie, he grew increasingly skeptical of Kabbalah itself, critiquing the Sepher Yetzirah as spurious and dismissing kabbalistic exegesis as vain sophistry unworthy of Christian doctrine.

This network of disciples, scholars, poets, and physicians—extending across France from Normandy to Paris to Champagne, and reaching into Spain and the Low Countries—collectively sustained and transmitted Postel's prophetic vision across the final three decades of the sixteenth century. Through their manifold activities, they embedded Christian Kabbalah within the institutional fabric of royal pedagogy, biblical scholarship, and humanist letters, transforming what might have remained an eccentric individual's private mysticism into an established—if contested—intellectual discourse within Renaissance Europe.
