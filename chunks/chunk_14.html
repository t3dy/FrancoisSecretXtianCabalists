Chunk 14

Pages 44-46

Secret bridges the formal kabbalistic methodology—particularly the three primary operations (Gematria, Notaricon, and Temurah)—with Reuchlin's broader theological project. Gematria assigns numerical values to Hebrew letters, allowing one word to be substituted for another of equal value; an example shows "Istho" (sa femme) equaling 77, like "Esch" (fire) and "We qash" (straw), connecting mystically to prophecy. Notaricon extracts initial letters from words to form new phrases—"Macchabees" generates "Mikamcha baelim YHWH" (Who is like Thee among the mighty, O Lord). Temurah involves systematic letter permutation and substitution, exemplified through the "Ath Basch" alphabet. The text explains the derivation of the seventy-two divine names from three verses of Exodus (14:19-21), each containing 72 letters. By arranging and reading these letters through different spatial alignments, Reuchlin generates names like "Vehuiah" and "Ieliel"—angelic names that, when pronounced with Yah or El terminations, invoke divine protection. This complex numerology demonstrates that insignificant-sounding words hold magical power when properly constructed from God's name. Secret notes Reuchlin's correspondence with the astrologer Jean Stœfller (who falsely predicted a deluge for 1524) and indicates that Christian kabbalah requires long study. The conclusion emphasizes that esoteric interpretation reveals the Messiah and Trinity throughout Scripture and that ethical living—living well to die well—forms the practical sum of kabbalistic philosophy. Finally, Secret discusses the *Augenspiegel* controversy: though Leo X initially favored Reuchlin, he condemned the work only after Luther's rebellion (1520), leading to the indexing of all Reuchlin's writings. Yet abbé Humbert praises Reuchlin's literal commentary depth and fresh empirical approach over sterile scholasticism.
