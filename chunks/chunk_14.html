Secret presents the technical heart of Reuchlin's operative kabbalah by detailing the three primary hermeneutical operations that transform Hebrew into a living oracle of divine mysteries. Gematria, the assignment of numerical values to Hebrew letters (Aleph=1, Beth=2, etc.), operates on the principle that words of equal numerical sum participate in hidden identity or mystical resonance—one word may be substituted for another of identical value to reveal concealed theological connections. A striking example demonstrates the principle: "Istho" (sa femme, his wife) equals 77, as does "Esch" (fire) and "We qash" (straw), creating a mystical correlation suggesting that a wife, like fire consuming straw, burns with divine purpose in the economy of redemption. This method presumes that the Hebrew language itself encodes cosmological wisdom—that God created the world through Hebrew letters, and therefore the numerical dimensions of language mirror the numerical proportions of creation. Notaricon, the second operation, extracts initial letters from Hebrew words or phrases to form entirely new words or sentences, generating prophetic utterances latent within larger textual bodies. An elaborate example: "Macchabees" generates the acronym "Mikamcha baelim YHWH" (Who is like Thee among the mighty, O Lord), a triumphant affirmation of divine supremacy hidden within the name of the Maccabean resistance fighters. The method suggests that historical narratives in Scripture contain compressed prophecies readable only to those who possess the kabbalah's hermeneutical keys—the surface narrative about militant resistance becomes a metaphysical confession of faith in God's transcendent power. This interpretive strategy locates prophetic and theological content at multiple levels simultaneously, rewarding the mystic who pursues systematic textual excavation. Temurah, the third operation, involves the systematic permutation and substitution of letters according to fixed alphabetical formulas, most notably exemplified through the "Ath Basch" alphabet (also called Atbash), in which the first letter of the Hebrew alphabet (Aleph) exchanges with the last (Tav), the second (Beth) with the penultimate (Shin), and so forth. Through Temurah, a skilled kabbalist can transform one biblical word into another entirely different word—a process that reveals how apparent opposites (light and darkness, blessing and curse, Adam and Satan) encode a unified cosmic principle when properly deciphered. The permutations unlock layers of meaning invisible to the literal reader, demonstrating that Scripture's deepest wisdom lies accessible only to those trained in these sophisticated hermeneutical technologies. The seventy-two divine names constitute the crown jewel of Reuchlin's operative theology. These names derive from three consecutive verses of Exodus (14:19-21), each verse containing exactly 72 letters—the total appearing non-accidental to the mystical eye. By arranging and reading these 216 letters (72 × 3) through different spatial alignments and phonetic combinations, the kabbalist generates seventy-two divine names of extraordinary power: "Vehuiah," "Ieliel," "Sitael," and others still. When pronounced with specific Hebrew terminations like "Yah" or "El," these names invoke direct divine presence and protection. The very structure of the Torah encodes a complete system of angelic invocation—insignificant-sounding words become magically potent when properly constructed from God's name. This practice transforms Scripture from a historical document into an operative grimoire whose letters constitute the raw material for mystical communion with angelic intelligence. Secret notes Reuchlin's historical correspondence with the astrologer Jean Stœfller, a prominent figure in Maximilian I's court whose mathematical astronomy attracted both learned admiration and mystical interest. Stœfller infamously predicted a catastrophic deluge for February 1524, which failed to materialize—yet his involvement with figures like Reuchlin indicates how naturally Renaissance courts integrated mathematical astronomy, astrology, kabbalah, and esoteric theology into a unified worldview. Reuchlin himself insists that Christian kabbalah demands lengthy disciplined study, not casual curiosity; the transformation of consciousness it promises requires sustained contemplative labor and ethical refinement. The conclusion of Reuchlin's work emphasizes that esoteric interpretation penetrates the entire Scripture, revealing the Messiah and the Trinity luminously throughout texts apparently concerned with history, genealogy, or law. The practical summation of all kabbalistic philosophy reduces to ethics: "living well to die well," a formula integrating mystical knowledge with moral transformation. True kabbalistic illumination cannot coexist with spiritual pride, licentiousness, or neglect of virtue—the ascent through the gates depends on character development proceeding in tandem with intellectual mastery of hermeneutical methods. Secret finally addresses the *Augenspiegel* controversy that would shape Reuchlin's final decades. Though Pope Leo X initially favored Reuchlin against his scholastic detractors, the papal stance reversed after Luther's rebellion (1520) shattered ecclesiastical unity; the Pope condemned Reuchlin's work to demonstrate his commitment to traditional orthodoxy against Protestant heresy. Consequently, all Reuchlin's writings were indexed on the Church's prohibited books list, effectively outlawing their circulation within Catholic territories. Yet abbé Humbert, assessing the damage done by Reuchlin's suppression, praises the depth of his literal biblical commentary and his fresh empirical approach to textual exegesis, contrasting Reuchlin favorably with the arid scholasticism that had calcified theological discourse for generations.
