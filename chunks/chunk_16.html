Pages 50-52

Secret examines Augustinus Ricius (called Neapolitan, possibly brother of Paul Ricius), who published *De motu octavæ spheræ* (1513) at Casale Montferrat. A student of Abraham Zacuto at Salamanca, Ricius interweaves astronomy, mathematics, and kabbalistic theology, arguing that Hebrew ancients were first inventors of astronomy. His work merges Platonic dogmas with the Hebraic "Cabale" and surveys diverse opinions on the cosmos and divine essence. Ricius defines kabbalah through three emanational modes: Atziluth (emanation), corresponding to Sephiroth and the prelapsarian knowledge of Adam; Beriah (creation ex nihilo); and Assiah/Jecira (formation/facture, the generative mode). He uses light as analogy for divine essence, drawing on Pythagoras, Plato, Plotinus, and Christian authorities. The three highest Sephiroth—Ab (Father/Wisdom), Hochmah (Wisdom/Son), and Ruach hacados (Holy Spirit)—prefigure the Trinity, while the seven lower Sephiroth (Hesed, Pahad, Tiphereth, Nezah, Hod, Yesod, Malcuth) constitute the material cosmos ruled by divine intelligences as patriarchs rule nations. Ricius develops the cosmological theme: the world endures in cycles of one thousand years for the elemental realm, with the seventh thousand marking return to origin—a doctrine found in Talmudic sources, the Zohar, Bahir, and commentators like Ramban and Menahem Recanati. Secret notes that Christian writers simplified this theory, with some adopting the six-thousand-year scheme (deriving from Elijah's prophecy) and others the fifty-thousand-year cycle. The text then covers Girolamo Maggi of Anghiari, Strozzi Cigogna, and Pedro Valderama, who cite these same kabbalistic theories of world duration and apocalyptic renewal, tracing how the doctrine propagated through Renaissance and early modern Christian learning.
