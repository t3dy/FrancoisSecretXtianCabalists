Secret examines Augustinus Ricius, variously called the Neapolitan and possibly the brother of the more famous Paul Ricius, as a crucial systematizer of Christian kabbalistic theology in the early sixteenth century. Augustinus published *De motu octavæ spheræ* (On the Motion of the Eighth Sphere) at Casale Montferrat in 1513, a work ambitious in scope and profound in its synthesis of disparate intellectual traditions. A student of the celebrated Abraham Zacuto at Salamanca—himself a mathematician and astronomer of the highest order—Augustinus received rigorous training in both mathematics and Hebrew learning. His published work interweaves astronomy, mathematical cosmology, and kabbalistic theology into a unified vision, arguing provocatively that the Hebrew ancients constituted the first inventors of astronomical science, thereby locating Jewish intellectual tradition at the foundation of Western scientific knowledge rather than treating it as marginal or derivative. His work merges explicitly Platonic dogmas concerning eternal forms and cosmic harmony with the Hebraic "Cabale," surveying diverse rabbinic and Greek opinions on the structure of the cosmos and the nature of divine essence. Augustinus Ricius's fundamental contribution consists in defining kabbalah through three carefully distinguished emanational modes that describe how divinity manifests throughout creation. Atziluth, the highest mode, designates divine emanation itself—the overflowing of the infinite into finitude—and corresponds to the Sephiroth in their pristine state and to the prelapsarian knowledge possessed by Adam before his rebellion severed direct communion with heavenly intelligences. Beriah designates creation ex nihilo, the bringing of being from absolute nothingness, constituting the second descent into manifestation. Assiah and Jecira designate formation and facture, the generative modes through which permanent beings emerge and maintain themselves within material existence. This tripartite schema explains how the utterly transcendent divinity nonetheless becomes immanent throughout creation without contradicting divine immutability—the modes describe ontological levels where higher realities manifest through lower without losing their transcendence. To illuminate these emanational modes, Ricius employs light as the supreme analogy: just as the sun's radiance diminishes with distance from the source yet remains fundamentally one with that source, divine essence radiates throughout the cosmos in progressively attenuated forms, each level participating in the source's truth while adapted to its own ontological degree. This optical theology draws on Pythagoras, Plato, Plotinus, and Christian Neoplatonic authorities like Pseudo-Dionysius, creating a synthesis where Jewish mysticism and Greek philosophy converge in recognizing light as the fundamental metaphor for divine self-manifestation. The framework permits Ricius to map the three highest Sephiroth—Ab (Father/Wisdom), Hochmah (Wisdom/Son), and Ruach hacados (Holy Spirit)—directly onto the Trinity, thereby demonstrating that Jewish mystical tradition anticipated Christian Trinitarian revelation. The seven lower Sephiroth (Hesed, Pahad, Tiphereth, Nezah, Hod, Yesod, Malcuth) constitute collectively the material cosmos ruled by divine intelligences whose governance mirrors how patriarchs rule their nations—each intelligence participates in higher principles while executing sovereignty over its proper domain. Augustinus develops a comprehensive cosmological theme establishing how the world endures in cycles of temporal duration, each lasting one thousand years within the elemental realm. The seventh thousand marks the moment when generative force becomes exhausted and the cosmos returns to its origin through a process of reabsorption preceding renewed emanation. This cyclical cosmology possesses deep roots in Talmudic sources and the Zohar, and it appears in diverse commentators like Ramban (Nachmanides) and Menahem Recanati, indicating its centrality within Jewish mystical tradition. Secret notes with precision that Christian writers subsequently simplified this theory, with some adopting the six-thousand-year scheme (deriving from the Talmudic calculation based on Elijah's prophecy that the messianic age arrives after six thousand years of history), while others embraced the grander fifty-thousand-year cycle, adapting rabbinic temporality to Christian eschatological needs. The text then expands to survey how these kabbalistic theories of world duration and apocalyptic renewal propagated through Renaissance and early modern Christian learning, tracing their circulation through additional figures: Girolamo Maggi of Anghiari, Strozzi Cigogna, and Pedro Valderama, all of whom cite and elaborate these same kabbalistic theories in their own published works. The doctrine established itself as a standard element in Christian intellectual discourse, transmitted from Italian Platonists through the broader European scholarly network. The very fact that multiple Christian thinkers of varying disciplines and regions cited these theories suggests how thoroughly kabbalistic eschatology had penetrated Renaissance Christianity—the cyclical cosmos with its periodic returns to origin and renewal achieved credibility status within legitimate Christian intellectual discourse, no longer confined to marginalia or heretical circles but welcomed into mainstream theological contemplation.
