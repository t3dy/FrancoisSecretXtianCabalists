This section expounds the philosophical and theological architecture of Gilles' two major works: the *Libellus de litteris hebraicis* (Treatise on the Hebrew Letters, 1517) and the *Scechinah* (unnamed at composition, dedicated to Clément VII but addressed to Charles V). Gilles grounds his method in Platonic etymology (contra Aristotle) and the Cratylus, arguing that Hebrew letters, unlike human-invented languages, encode divine truth. He invokes Scripture—Jeremiah's alphabetically arranged Lamentations, David's Psalms, Solomon's acrostic on the strong woman—as proof that the Masorah's meticulous letter-counting reveals encoded mysteries. The *Libellus* unfolds as an alphabetical exegesis mapping each Hebrew letter to a Sephirah and divine principle. Aleph, seemingly simple but composed of Vau-Iod-Vau (6+10+10=26), signifies the Trinity and the divine name EHYH. Beth, structured from Daleth (door) and Vau (6, the heart-Tipheret), represents the wisdom-principle and its lunar complement in the tenth Sephirah. This hermeneutic extends through the alphabet, incorporating classical (Homeric, Virgilian) and Neoplatonic philosophy, demonstrating that pagan and Hebrew wisdom converge. The *Scechinah*, an ambitious 384-page manuscript (dated before the 1530 Tiber flooding), develops this theology of numbers into a mystical ecclesiastical history. Drawing on Psalm 39:8 ("In the book's head it was written of me"), Gilles constructs a seven-aeon framework: the first seven Sephiroth (upper triad plus seven "edifice" Sephiroth) correspond to 7×7=49 gates opened to Moses, the fiftieth reserved for the Messiah-Tiphereth. Each psalm and its verse-count encode divine time. The tenth century itself (Leo X to Charles V) represents apocalyptic culmination—age of discovery, Hebrew revival, and a new Cyrus (Charles V) who will unite Christendom and vanquish the Turk. Gilles synthesizes Origene, Augustine, Jerome, and Denys the Areopagite as his Christian precedents, finding Kabbalistic knowledge scattered in patristic tradition. He frames Leo X as the "lion" of Psalm 10:9 and Charles V (Medici) as the "sun of righteousness" (Malachi 4:2), embedding contemporary politics within cosmic theological scheme—a bold, if speculative, marriage of humanistic scholarship and prophetic vision.
