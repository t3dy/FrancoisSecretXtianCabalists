The *Libellus de litteris hebraicis* (Treatise on the Hebrew Letters, 1517) represents one of the Renaissance's most ambitious attempts to construct a complete theological system grounded in Hebrew letter-mysticism. Gilles grounds his entire methodological apparatus in Platonic philosophy—specifically in Plato's *Cratylus*, the dialogue exploring whether names possess natural significance or are merely conventional—rather than in Aristotelian logic, which he viewed as fundamentally limited. Against Aristotle's assertion that language is arbitrary human convention, Gilles argues that Hebrew letters, unlike human-invented languages, encode divine truth inscribed into creation itself. The distinction is not merely academic; it fundamentally reframes how one approaches biblical interpretation. If names are natural (divinely ordained correspondences between sound and reality), then the biblical text is not merely a series of propositions to be logically analyzed but rather a mystical repository of cosmic secrets embedded in the very structure of language. Gilles marshals scriptural evidence with considerable force: Jeremiah's alphabetically-arranged Lamentations (each stanza beginning with successive Hebrew letters), David's Psalms structured with mystical intent, Solomon's acrostic on the "strong woman" (Proverbs 31)—all these demonstrate that the Masorah's meticulous attention to letter-counting reveals encoded mysteries. The *Libellus* unfolds as a systematic alphabetical exegesis, mapping each Hebrew letter to a Sephirah and divine principle, treating the alphabet itself as a cosmological map. The letter Aleph—seemingly simple, a single stroke—is decomposed into its component form: Vau-Iod-Vau (6+10+10=26), yielding a numerical mysticism that Gilles claims signifies the Trinity (through various numerical relationships) and encodes the divine name EHYH (I am that I am), the great affirmation of absolute being. Beth, traditionally the door (Daleth in Hebrew), is structurally analyzed as Daleth (door) united with Vau (6, the heart or Tipheret in kabbalistic terminology), representing the wisdom-principle (Chokmah) and its earthly complement in the lunar sphere and the tenth Sephirah (Malkhuth, Kingdom). This hermeneutic extends through all twenty-two letters, incorporating classical authorities (Homeric epithets, Virgilian cosmology) and Neoplatonic philosophy to demonstrate that pagan and Hebrew wisdom converge in the letter-mysticism that Gilles claims to decode. By this method, Gilles attempts to show that the greatest minds of antiquity—Plato, Homer, the Stoics—had intuited the same cosmic truths that the Hebrew initiates had encoded in their alphabet. The *Scechinah*, an ambitious 384-page manuscript that likely occupied Gilles's attention for decades (dated before the catastrophic 1530 Tiber flooding), represents perhaps the Renaissance's most elaborate synthesis of Christian theology and kabbalistic cosmology. The work's full title translates as "the Indwelling Presence"—the Shekinah (Shekhinah), that feminine divine principle in Jewish mysticism that represents God's immanent presence in creation, God's dwelling in Israel, and the erotic dimension of divine-human communion. Gilles reconfigures this concept as fundamentally Christian, identifying the Shekinah with divine Wisdom, the Virgin Mary, the Church, and the mystical body of Christ. The manuscript develops a metaphysical system grounded in the seven-aeon framework derived from kabbalistic cosmology: the first seven Sephiroth (the upper triad of Keter-Chokmah-Binah plus the seven "edifice" Sephiroth) correspond to 7 times 7 equals 49 gates opened to Moses at Sinai, with the fiftieth gate reserved for the Messiah dwelling in Tipheret (Beauty), the central point of the Sephirotic tree. Each psalm and its verse-count encode divine time; each number vibrates with theological significance. Gilles's scheme reaches toward apocalyptic boldness when he positions his own era within this mystical calendar. The tenth century itself—spanning from Leo X (elected pope 1513) through the current reign of Charles V—represents apocalyptic culmination. This was the age of geographical discovery (the Columbus voyages, the opening of the Americas), the age of Hebrew revival (the printing of the Hebrew Bible, the flourishing of Christian Kabbalah), and the age that Gilles claims has revealed a new Cyrus. The biblical Cyrus, the Persian king who freed the Jews from Babylonian captivity, was read by Christian exegetes as a figure of the Christian prince who would restore the Church to spiritual renewal. In Gilles's vision, Charles V embodies this role—the new Cyrus who would unite Christendom, vanquish the Ottoman Turk, and restore the Church to its original purity. This was not mere flattery; it reflected Gilles's genuine apocalyptic conviction that the convergence of linguistic, geographical, and political events in his lifetime constituted proof that the end-times transformation of Christendom was imminent. The logic was kabbalistic: reading history itself as a text encoded with divine meaning, where numbers, names, and the correspondence between terrestrial and celestial realms revealed the pattern of redemption. Gilles synthesizes patristic authorities—Origen, Augustine, Jerome, and Denys the Areopagite—as his Christian precedents, finding kabbalistic knowledge, he claims, scattered throughout Christian tradition. He frames Leo X as the "lion" of Psalm 10:9, drawing on numerological correspondence (Leo X → the number 10 → kabbalistic associations with Malkhuth). Charles V, a Medici and inheritor of the papal tradition, becomes the "sun of righteousness" of Malachi 4:2—the figure who would illuminate and warm all Christendom through spiritual and military restoration. This embedding of contemporary politics within cosmic theological scheme represents a bold, if speculative, marriage of humanistic scholarship and prophetic vision. Gilles essentially claims that the Hebrew alphabet, properly understood, constitutes a text of universal history, and that if one possessed the mystical key—the understanding of letter-mysticism, number-symbolism, and Sephirotic correspondences—one could read off the future. The *Scechinah* thus functions as both esoteric philosophy and political theology, grounding Gilles's vision of Charles V's redemptive role in the deepest structures of reality itself. The *Libellus* and *Scechinah* represent a moment when Christian theology, humanist scholarship, and Jewish mysticism converged in an intellectual synthesis that seemed, to Gilles and his immediate successors, to be genuinely revelatory. They claimed that the recovery of Hebrew sources and the proper understanding of kabbalistic symbolism would not merely correct Christian theology but would unveil the deepest patterns of divine causality and human redemption. The system was internally coherent, learned, and utterly dependent on the conviction that correspondence, analogy, and mystical number-symbolism revealed truth more profoundly than Aristotelian logic. That conviction would not survive the intellectual transformations of the seventeenth century, but in Gilles's lifetime, it appeared not eccentric but rather the cutting edge of Christian erudition.
