Jean Morin (1591–1659), a converted Protestant and student of the formidable Philippe d'Aquin, brought systematic scholarly discipline to kabbalistic study through his comprehensive Exercitationes biblicae (1633). Morin undertook detailed textual investigation to establish that Galatin's De arcanis, despite its claims to rabbinic authenticity, constituted plagiarism of the Victoria Porcheti rather than genuine engagement with Jewish sources, and that Galatin had inflated his materials through incorporation of the spurious Gale Razeia—a text entirely unknown to Jewish scholarship and unattested in any authentic rabbinic sources. Morin advanced a devastating aesthetic comparison: kabbalah resembles Egyptian temples possessing beautiful external facades that conceal within only bones and weeds, an image capturing his judgment that kabbalah's apparent profundity masks empty superstition. He conceded that the ten sephiroth supplied preliminary theological lessons worthy of consideration, yet otherwise dismissed kabbalah as "largely vain and ridiculous," its apparent depth constituting merely adolescent theology dressed in philosophical language intended to impress ignorant audiences. Yet Morin's critique proceeded from sustained engagement with primary sources; he corresponded with Cappel and Buxtorf about the Zohar's textual features and circulated accounts of an African Jew named León (living three centuries prior to his writing) who had allegedly fabricated the Zohar for commercial profit. Morin's 1660 edition of the Exercitationes biblicae devoted an entire chapter to kabbalah, systematically classifying its vocabulary and examining foundational texts including the Bahir, Sefer Yetzirah, and Zohar with comparative precision, cross-referencing passages in Recanati's commentaries and the Amsterdam editions (1651). He detected multiple anachronisms suggesting the Zohar's relatively modern composition, particularly the presence of vocalized Hebrew punctuation within texts purporting to represent ancient revelation.

Philippe d'Aquin (c. 1575–1650), born Mordecai Cresque of Carpentras and trained in Hebrew within Piedmont until age twenty-three, underwent Christian conversion in Avignon (1611) and subsequently arrived in Paris (1614) where he established himself teaching Hebrew. His political connections and learned reputation led to dramatic institutional engagement: the powerful Marshal d'Ancre summoned d'Aquin seeking mystical cure for the illness afflicting Leonora Galigaï, raising d'Aquin's profile but also his vulnerability. Arrested in 1617 and interrogated concerning accusations of magic, d'Aquin defended himself by explaining that Philothée Elias Montalto had employed him translating Hebrew medical texts while pressuring him to divulge kabbalah's deepest secrets. D'Aquin distinguished theoretical kabbalah from practical kabbalah during his interrogation, demonstrated his knowledge through transcribing Hebrew psalms and engraving healing amulets, and was ultimately acquitted on June 12. Following his exoneration, d'Aquin published a series of works systematically weaponizing kabbalah for Christian theological purposes, transforming it from Jewish mystical tradition into Christian exegetical tool.

In Roots of the Holy Language, dedicated to François d'Abra de Raconis, d'Aquin establishes his hermeneutical method by observing that rabbis uncover profound mysteries through sophisticated textual techniques—assembling first and last letters of words and verses—thereby discovering embedded within Zohar passages drawn from Isaiah's opening and concluding words (Hazon, Basar) prophecy concerning the Messiah; the word Basar (flesh) additionally signals the theological mystery of the Incarnation. D'Aquin argues that Isaiah exhibits Messianic perfection exceeded only by Jerome's assessment of Isaiah as essentially Gospel rather than prophecy, positioning the Hebrew prophet within Christian exegetical tradition. In Literal, Allegorical, and Moral Explanations of the Tabernacle (1624), he observes that the sacred prayers bearing the twelve tribes' names and the three patriarchs' combined seventy-two Hebrew letters correspond traditionally to the divine names, a correspondence defended against skeptics through both rational argument and citation of Jewish antiquity. In Thirteen Modes of Exposition of the Ancient Rabbis, dedicated to the Jesuit Jean Arnoux, d'Aquin marshals Messianic, Trinitarian, and saint-invocation testimonies unknown to earlier Christian kabbalistes including Galatin or Reuchlin, drawing evidence from Zohar passages he personally doubts originated with Rabbi Simeon ben Yochai himself. His Interpretation of the Tree of Kabbalah (1625) constructs systematic kabbalistic theology: since God cannot be directly defined, humans access divine nature through revealed names and attributes; the archetypal world (intelligible and incommunicable) comprises six divine names; the angelic realm two; the visible realm two—thereby establishing a rigorous hierarchical emanation accessible to Christian theological reflection.

Claude Duret's massive posthumous work Thresor de l'histoire des langues de cest univers (Cologny, 1613), published after his death in 1611, devotes approximately one-third of its thousand pages to comprehensive treatment of kabbalah through chapters systematically following Du Bartas's organizational rubrics: demonstrating that the Hebrew alphabet contains all visible and believable knowledge; that Hebrew law encompasses all worldly arts and sciences; and that rabbi-cabalistes employ infinite letter-transposition and elaborate numerical methodology. Duret's scholarship appears learned, yet subsequent investigation reveals systematic plagiarism from Blaise de Vigenère's Traicté des chiffres without any attribution, a massive intellectual theft that Gaffarel, despite his critical engagement with both Vigenère and Duret, notably overlooked. The plagiarism undermines Duret's apparent learning, revealing his work as compilation and synthesis rather than original scholarship.

Jean-Pierre Camus (1584–1632), Bishop of Bellay and subsequently devoted follower of Saint Francis of Sales, attempted ambitious kabbalistic encyclopedia through his Diversités (appearing from 1609), though lacking Duret's erudition and Duret's (unacknowledged) source materials. At age twenty-five, intellectual curiosity led him to encounter a "grand cabaliste" from whom he acquired superficial knowledge of kabbalistic doctrine. He reports reading the Merkava, anticipating marvels but encountering disappointment, then consulting Abraham Abulafia's materials and unnamed rabbinical texts (Lights of Eyes, Book of Counsel, Gates of Justice fragments). He demonstrates skepticism toward ancient authorities, preferring modern scholars despite acknowledging his difficulty with unpunctuated, handwritten Hebrew texts. Yet Camus credits Christian scholastics with deriving their finest Trinitarian and mystical conceptions from cabalistes themselves, explicitly esteems the ten sephiroth as kabbalah's "finest piece," and pragmatically warns that contempt for religion proves fundamentally pernicious—hence the secret-keeping rule's profound wisdom. Acknowledging kabbalah's dangerous potential for misuse by heretical Ophites and Gnostics, he concludes with balanced judgment: "The sword bears no fault for homicide."

Marin Mersenne (1588–1648), the prodigiously productive Minim friar and correspondent across European scientific networks, launched systematic warfare against kabbalah, particularly its Rose-Croix applications and claims. Mersenne's critique extends beyond Robert Lenoble's dismissal of kabbalah as mere "word-puzzle club"; instead Mersenne specifically combats Christian kabbalistes' practical magical claims through direct philosophical engagement. His conflict with Philippe d'Aquin reveals Mersenne's profound ignorance of "rabbinical school air" (genuine rabbinic context). D'Aquin had translated texts treating the Derachim (divine paths), emphasizing charity, humility, and radical self-abjection—spiritual observances carrying no explicit reward within the 613 commandments' normative schema yet elevating practitioners toward Hasid (merciful) status within Jewish mystical anthropology. Mersenne denounced these teachings as impiety, fundamentally misunderstanding that these "works of counsel and supererogation" constitute the mystical ascent mechanism toward divine attributes within authentic Kabbalistic theology. His mechanistic philosophical commitments rendered him impervious to kabbalistic claims about mystical practice's efficacy, establishing the intellectual boundary between mechanistic natural philosophy and theurgic kabbalah as fundamentally opposed enterprises.
