Jean Morin (1591-1659), student of Philippe d'Aquin and Protestant convert to Catholicism, treated kabbalah systematically in *Exercitationes biblicae* (1633). He demonstrated that Galatin's *De arcanis* plagiarized the *Victoria Porcheti* rather than the *Pugio*, inflated by the spurious *Gale Razeia*—unknown to all Jewry. Morin judged kabbalah comparable to Egyptian temples with beautiful facades concealing bones and weeds. Save for the ten sephiroth's preliminary theological lessons, he found kabbalah "largely vain and ridiculous," its apparent profundity merely adolescent theology dressed in philosophic reasoning to impress the ignorant. Yet Morin engaged primary sources, corresponding with Cappel and Buxtorf about the *Zohar*. He recounted that an African Jew named León (three centuries prior) was accused of fabricating the *Zohar* for profit. Morin's 1660 *Exercitationes biblicae* chapter on kabbalah classifies its vocabulary, examines the *Bahir*, *Sefer Yetzirah*, and *Zohar*, comparing *Bahir* texts across Recanati's commentaries and Amsterdam editions (1651). He detected anachronisms suggesting the *Zohar*'s modernness, particularly vocalized Hebrew punctuation. Philippe d'Aquin (c. 1575-1650), born Mordecai Cresque of Carpentras, studied in Piedmont until age twenty-three, converted in Avignon (1611), and arrived in Paris (1614) teaching Hebrew. He was summoned by the Marshal d'Ancre seeking a cure for Leonora Galigaï's illness; arrested in 1617 and accused of magic, d'Aquin explained that Philothée Elias Montalto employed him translating Hebrew medical texts and pressing him to reveal kabbalah's secrets. D'Aquin distinguished theoretical from practical kabbalah, transcribing a Hebrew psalm and engraving a healing amulet. Acquitted June 12, d'Aquin published works systematically weaponizing kabbalah for Christianity. In *Roots of the Holy Language* (dedicated to François d'Abra de Raconis), d'Aquin observes that rabbis uncover mysteries through textual techniques—assembling first and last letters—discovering in *Zohar* passages from Isaiah's beginning and end (*Hazon*, *Basar*) the Messiah's prophecy; *Basar* (flesh) additionally signals the Incarnation. He notes Isaiah exhibits Messianic perfection exceeded only by Jerome's assessment as Gospel rather than prophecy. In *Literal, Allegorical, and Moral Explanations of the Tabernacle* (1624), he remarks that the prayers bearing the twelve tribes' names and three patriarchs' total seventy-two Hebrew letters—a tradition-supported correspondence to the divine names, defended against skeptics through reason and Jewish antiquity. In *Thirteen Modes of Exposition of the Ancient Rabbis* (dedicated to Jean Arnoux, S.J.), d'Aquin cites Messianic, Trinitarian, and saint-invocation testimonies unknown to Galatin or Reuchlin, including a *Zohar* passage he doubts originated with Rabbi Simeon ben Yochai. *Interpretation of the Tree of Kabbalah* (1625) expounds: unable to define God, we access His nature through revealed names and attributes. The archetypal world (intelligible, incommunicable) comprises six divine names; the angelic realm two; the visible realm two—establishing hierarchical emanation. Claude Duret's massive *Thresor de l'histoire des langues de cest univers* (Cologny, 1613)—posthumously published after his 1611 death—devotes a third of its thousand pages to kabbalah through chapters borrowing Du Bartas's rubrics: demonstrating that Hebrew alphabet contains all visible and believable knowledge, that Hebrew law encompasses all worldly arts, and that rabbi-cabalistes employ infinite letter-transposition and numerical methodology. Duret extensively plagiarizes Blaise de Vigenère's *Traicté des chiffres* without attribution; Gaffarel, who critiqued Vigenère and Duret, overlooked the massive theft. Jean-Pierre Camus (1584-1632), Bishop of Bellay (later devoted to Saint Francis of Sales), attempted this kabbalistic encyclopedia with less erudition through his *Diversités* (from 1609). At twenty-five, curiosity led him to a "grand cabaliste," acquiring superficial knowledge. He read the *Merkava*, expecting marvels but finding disappointment, then consulted Abraham Abulafia and unnamed rabbinical texts (*Lights of Eyes*, *Book of Counsel*, *Gates of Justice* fragments). He preferred modern authorities to ancient, citing difficulty with unpunctuated, handwritten Hebrew. Yet he credits the Christian scholastics with deriving their finest Trinitarian and mystical conceptions from cabalistes, esteems the ten sephiroth as kabbalah's "finest piece," and warns that contempt for religion proves pernicious—hence the secret-keeping rule's wisdom. Acknowledging kabbalah as a "double-edged sword" abused by heretical Ophites and Gnostics, he concludes it merits respect: "The sword bears no fault for homicide." Marin Mersenne (1588-1648) launched warfare against kabbalah, particularly Rose-Croix applications. Unlike Robert Lenoble's dismissal of kabbalah as "word-puzzle club," Mersenne specifically combats Christian kabbalistes' practical claims. His conflict with Philippe d'Aquin reveals ignorance of "rabbinical school air." D'Aquin translated a text on the *Derachim* (divine paths) emphasizing charity, humility, and self-abjection—observances carrying no reward in the 613 commandments' schema but elevating practitioners to *Hasid* (merciful) status. Mersenne denounced this as impiety, misunderstanding that these "works of counsel and supererogation" constitute mystical ascent to divine attributes.
