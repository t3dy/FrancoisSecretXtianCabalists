Secret launches a frontal challenge to the received historiographical narrative, particularly as formulated by F. Rosenthal and echoed throughout Reformation scholarship, which claims that Protestant literalism and the rise of biblical humanism fundamentally and irrevocably suppressed Christian Kabbalah. The argument rests, Secret demonstrates, on a profound misreading of the evidence. Richard Simon, the seventeenth-century scholar of biblical criticism, had already observed with keen insight that many of the most celebrated Protestant pioneers—figures like Sebastian Münster and Conrad Pellican—had begun their scholarly careers as Franciscan friars, during which they published extensively on Kabbalistic subjects, only to continue that work, albeit with modified rhetoric, after their Protestant conversions. The great historian Lucien Febvre taught us to approach such transitional figures with nuance and sophistication, refusing the crude confessional labels that force complex individuals into binary categories of "Catholic" or "Protestant." These scholars, Secret shows, maintained a remarkable intellectual continuity across the confessional divide, suggesting that the supposed binary opposition between Reformation literalism and mystical esotericism obscures the actual historical complexity.

Conrad Pellican (1478–1556) emerges as the paradigmatic figure for understanding this hidden continuity. His Chronikon reads as a kind of spiritual autobiography, tracing his self-directed pilgrimage through the labyrinthine landscapes of Hebrew learning and Kabbalistic wisdom. The young Pellican experienced a formative humiliation—present at a theological disputation where a Jewish scholar systematically defeated a Christian theologian in exegetical argument, an event that kindled in him an obsessive determination to master Hebrew himself. Without formal instruction, he taught himself from the medieval authorities: Nicolas de Lyre and Paul de Burgos, medieval Christian Hebraists whose works provided the only available scaffolding for a Christian entering Hebrew study. His pedagogical method was brutal and literal: he copied the book of Isaiah letter by letter, familiarizing himself with every character, every diacritical marking, every cantillation sign until the sacred text became embedded in his muscle memory and his mind. He scraped together one and a half florins (borrowed from an uncle) to purchase a Hebrew Bible—a precious and expensive object in the early sixteenth century. Through determined networking, he arranged to meet Reuchlin, exchanging his own drafted dictionary materials for copies of Moses Kimhi's grammatical treatises. He then embarked on systematic manuscript-hunting expeditions, acquiring David Kimhi's Roots (a Hebrew etymological dictionary), Reuchlin's Coupe d'argent (adorned with a technical treatise on the ten Sephiroth), the Sha'arei Orah (Gate of Light) with Heredia's Epistle of Secrets, Moses Maimonides' More Nebuchim (Guide for the Perplexed), and Postel's pioneering early translation of the Zohar itself. When he published his Commentaria Biblicorum in 1532, it incorporated Kabbalistic gematria drawn from Pico's Heptaplus, the legends of Raziel the angel, and Giorgio's own exegesis of how the letter Shin replaces the two He in the Tetragrammaton. Secret observes that Pellican, along with Egidio da Viterbo, Christoph Widmanstetter, and Andreas Masius, devoted extraordinary intellectual energy to Kabbalistic study, accumulating vast manuscript collections and working notes, yet none of these figures published a systematic, monumental treatise on Kabbalah itself—a curious absence that allowed their contributions to remain dispersed and scattered across commentaries, letters, and unpublished folios.

Sebastian Münster (1489–1552), Pellican's most celebrated student, became the most prolific publisher of the age, churning out biblical commentaries, histories, and geographical works. Yet Münster carefully maintained public distance from what he termed Kabbalistic "deliriums," disavowing the field rhetorically while all the while admitting (in company with the medieval Kabbalist Menahem de Recanati) that the Torah possesses multiple layers of interpretation, that Elijah's prophecy concerning the age of redemption encodes its message through the numerical values of Hebrew letters (yielding a calculation of a six-thousand-year cosmic timeline), and that the Fascicle of Myrrh (a Kabbalistic text) offers valid proofs concerning the gematria of the Tetragrammaton. Wolfgang Capito (1478–1541), despite his public dismissals of Kabbalah as futile mystical speculation, nonetheless employed the Kabbalistic concept of the middoth—the divine attributes or emanations—to construct arguments for the Trinity. Paul Fagius (1504–1550), Capito's student, went further still, explicitly championing what he termed the "true kabbalah" as equivalent to Christian tradition itself, deriving the concept of qibbel (reception) from Genesis 26:5 and 1 Timothy 1:15 as the orthodox faith-reception of divine mysteries. Fagius cited Kabbalistic authorities on the serpent's prophetic divination and the Jadua, a mysterious spirit-creature, and he edited the Sepher Emuna (Book of Faith), employing Kabbalistic gematria to prove the Trinity and to demonstrate the supernatural binding of righteous souls beneath God's throne. These four figures—Pellican, Münster, Capito, and Fagius—demonstrate conclusively that both Protestant and Catholic humanists continued to cultivate Kabbalah not as marginal superstition but as legitimate scholarly discourse, woven seamlessly into theological and exegetical work.
