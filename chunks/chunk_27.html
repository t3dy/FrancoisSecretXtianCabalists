Secret challenges the historiographical assumption (citing F. Rosenthal) that Protestant literalism ended Christian kabbalah. Richard Simon had already observed that many Protestant pioneers (Münster, Pellican) were Franciscan friars who published extensively before converting. Lucien Febvre taught us to read such transitional figures with nuance rather than crude confessional labels. Conrad Pellican (1478–1556) exemplifies the committed humanist-mystic. His *Chronikon* reveals his self-directed pilgrimage through Hebrew knowledge: childhood humiliation at a disputation where a Jew defeated a theologian; self-teaching from Nicolas de Lyre and Paul de Burgos; copying Isaiah letter-by-letter; acquiring a Hebrew Bible for one and a half florins (borrowed from an uncle). He meets Reuchlin, exchanges dictionary drafts for Moses Kimhi grammar, travels systematically gathering manuscripts: David Kimhi's *Roots*, Reuchlin's *Coupe d'argent* (with a treatise on the ten Sephiroth), the *Sha'arei Orah* (Portes de lumière) and Heredia's *Epistle of Secrets*, the *More Nebuchim* (Guide of the Perplexed), and Postel's early translation of the Zohar. His 1532 *Commentaria Biblicorum* incorporate gematria from the *Heptaplus*, the Raziel legend, and Giorgio's exegesis of the two He transformed into Shin. Secret notes that Pellican, like Egidio da Viterbo, Widmanstetter, and Masius, left vast manuscript labors but published no systematic monument. Sebastian Münster (1489–1552), Pellican's student, published prolifically yet maintained public distance from kabbalistic "deliriums"—while admitting (with Menahem de Recanati) that Torah possesses multiple faces, that Elijah's prophecy calculates a six-thousand-year cosmos from alphabetic values, that the Fascicle of Myrrh proves the Tetragrammaton's gematria. Wolfgang Capito (1478–1541) dismissed kabbalah as futile yet employed the *middoth* (divine attributes) to argue the Trinity. His student Paul Fagius (1504–1550) explicitly championed the "true kabbalah" as Christian tradition itself (defining *qibbel* from Genesis 26:5 and 1 Timothy 1:15 as orthodox faith-reception), cited kabbalists on the serpent's divination and the *Jadua* spirit-creature, and edited the *Sepher Emuna* (Book of Faith), using gematria to prove the Trinity and the supernatural binding of righteous souls beneath God's throne. These figures demonstrate that Protestant and Catholic humanists alike cultivated kabbalah within legitimate scholarly discourse.
