Chunk 3

Pages 10-12

The chunk continues **Pico's** argument: the secret doctrine, unpublished and revealed only to Moses and the seventy sages, was finally written down in seventy books under **Esdras** after the Babylonian exile (IV Esdras: the books containing "the veins of intelligence, the source of wisdom"). Pico anticipates these seventy books in his *Oratio* and believed he had recovered them by buying kabbalistic manuscripts. He adds the authority of **Jerome** (who cites his Hebrew masters) and argues the Hebrew school divides into three sects — philosophers, kabbalists, and talmudists — so the Fathers must have meant the kabbalists. In the *Heptaplus* he repeats the rule that no one may treat creation (*Ma'aseh Bereshit*) before maturity. Secret surveys the Hieronymian materials later Christian Kabbalists reused: Jerome on the added *He* in Abraham's and Sarah's names; the **Ath-Bash** cipher (reading the alphabet inward, e.g. Babel→Sheshach in Jeremiah 25:26), which Reuchlin would explain; the ten divine names ending in the ineffable Tetragrammaton (Iod-He-Vau-He); and Jerome's exposition of the meaning of the Hebrew letters. Claude **Duret**'s *Thrésor de l'histoire des langues* (1613) is quoted at length giving the symbolic sense of each letter. **Irenaeus** is cited on the name of Jesus, with Drusius's gloss; **Tertullian** (against Marcus and Colorbasus) and Raban Maur's *De laudibus Crucis* (a "true Christian Kabbalah," reissued 1503 under Reuchlin's patronage) round out the ancestors. **Chapter II, "Les débuts de la kabbale en Espagne,"** begins. Secret notes Pico's aversion to the Talmud and turns to the medieval apologetic literature where Christian use of Kabbalah developed. He traces antecedents (Boethius's *apices*, Virgil of Toulouse, Agobard, Roger Bacon, Henricus de Hassia). The key figure is **Moïse Sefardi / Petrus Alfonsi** (converted 1106), whose dialogue read YHWH as one-and-three (unity of substance, trinity of persons), illustrated by a figure of concentric circles. The Trinitarian reading of the Tetragrammaton passed to **Joachim of Fiore**, possibly inspired **Dante**'s three circles in the *Paradiso*, and was spread above all by the Dominican **Ramon Martí**'s *Pugio Fidei* (c. 1278), which read the repeated *He* as the unity of essence among three *Middot* (properties/persons).
