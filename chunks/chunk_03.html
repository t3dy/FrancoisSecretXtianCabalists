The continuation of Pico's defense in Chapter I develops the argument concerning the seventy secret books and their final written codification. Pico constructs his case that these books, originally transmitted orally from Moses to seventy sages in strict secrecy, were finally committed to writing under Esdras after the Babylonian exile and the restoration of the Temple under Zerubbabel (6th century BCE). When the Jews were freed by Cyrus of Persia from the Babylonian captivity and the Temple was rebuilt, Esdras—the scribe who presided over the Synagogue—undertook not only to restore the texts of the Old Testament but to write down the secret oracles of God to prevent their loss during the vicissitudes of Jewish history. Pico interprets IV Esdras 14:45-47 as reporting Esdras's divine commission: "Publish openly the twenty-four books [the canonical scriptures]; but keep the seventy last books hidden, entrust them to the wise of your people, for in them lies the source of understanding, the wellspring of wisdom, and the river of knowledge." Pico makes the theological leap: these "seventy secret books" are the mysteries of Kabbalah, encoded in mystical and numerical language comprehensible only to the initiated. He had purchased kabbalistic manuscripts (largely through his Jewish teacher Flavius Mithridates), believing he had recovered fragments of these very seventy books—a confidence in textual archaeology that reveals both the ambitions and the credulity of Renaissance Kabbalah collecting. The argument moves seamlessly from patristic testimony to textual recovery: what Jerome and Origen reported as Christian tradition about an oral law could now be verified in the Hebrew manuscripts that Christian presses and collectors were acquiring. The authority of Jerome—who had lived in Bethlehem, had learned from Hebrew rabbis, and whose Latin Vulgate stood as the official Bible of Christendom—became crucial to this fusion of Christian antiquity with contemporary Jewish scholarship. When Jerome speaks of "the opinion of his Hebrew masters" in various exegetical contexts, Pico argues, he is invoking Kabbalists, and thus Jerome becomes a Christian foundation for Kabbalist authority.

Secret then catalogs the specific patristic and medieval Christian materials that would become stock citations in every Christian Kabbalist's arsenal throughout the Renaissance. The mystical exegesis of Hebrew letters grounded in Jerome's own writings became a touchstone. Jerome's commentary on the addition of the letter He (representing the divine emanation and fullness) to Abraham's name—changing Avram (exalted father) to Avraham (father of many nations)—and to Sarah's name, became a canonical example of how the divine name itself inflects human names with higher meaning and spiritual status. Jerome's explanation of the Ath-Bash cipher in his commentary on Jeremiah 25:26 ("King Sheshach shall drink after them"), where Sheshach is shown to be Babylon through letter-reversal, exemplifies how the alphabet itself becomes a tool of hermeneutic revelation. Jerome explains the principle: just as the Greek alphabet can be read forward (Alpha, Beta, Gamma... to Omega) and also inverted (Alpha-Omega, Beta-Psi) as an aid to memory, so too the Hebrew alphabet, running from Aleph to Tau, can be reversed, pairing first with last (Aleph-Tau), second with penultimate (Bet-Shin), until reaching the middle letter Kaph-Lamed. By this inversion, Babel becomes Sheshach—a technique that shows how the mere rearrangement of letters can reveal hidden meanings, theological correspondences, and prophetic identities encoded in the text. Reuchlin would later provide meticulous expositions of this cipher and others (Atbash, Albam, etc.), systematizing what Jerome had demonstrated through example. Jerome's testimony regarding Ezekiel and the beginning of Genesis—the Ma'aseh Merkabah and Ma'aseh Bereshit—as texts that cannot be studied before the age of maturity, further solidified the impression that kabbalistic disciplines were recognized within Christian tradition itself as advanced mysteries. The famous list of the ten divine names, each with its own theological valence and cosmic significance—El, Elohim, Elohim Zebaoth, Elim, Eser, Ehyeh, Adonai, Ia, and the Tetragrammaton itself (Yod-He-Vau-He), the "four-letter name" that Jews believe cannot and should not be pronounced aloud but which contains the very essence of divine being—became a framework that Christian Kabbalists would constantly invoke and elaborate. These names were understood to express different aspects of divine manifestation: the transcendent unity, the creative power, the sustaining force, the personal relationship with creation, the hidden essence. Claude Duret's massive Thrésor de l'histoire des langues (Treasury of the History of Languages), published in 1613, gathered together the entire heritage of a century of Christian Kabbalist learning about the Hebrew alphabet. Duret synthesizes Eusebius and Jerome, noting how the Greeks had no symbolic meaning for their alphabet while Hebrew children could immediately explain that Aleph means "discipline," Bet means "house" (implying the house of discipline), Gimel means "fullness of voice" (conveying and abundance), Daleth means "doors" or "books" (access to knowledge), and so through the complete sequence—each letter building toward a total vision of cosmic pedagogy and divine instruction encoded in language itself. The accumulated meaning running through all twenty-two letters yields a profound theological statement: "Discipline of the house, this plenitude of voice and books therein, he lived the living, the good, the good principle, nevertheless by these together the aid and eternal succor, fountain, base of eye and mouth of justice, vocation, signs of the head or teeth"—a mystical reading that transforms the alphabet into a cosmic narrative of divine teaching, incarnation, redemption, and eternal counsel.

Irenaeus of Lyon, the second-century Church Father, provided another critical patristic authority. In his Adversus Valentiniani et omnes haereses, Irenaeus interprets the name of Jesus according to Hebrew mystical orthography: the name Jesus (Yeshua in Hebrew) comprises the divine name IHWH (the Tetragrammaton) with an additional letter, and Irenaeus states that "according to the proper language of the Hebrews, the name of Jesus is composed of two-and-a-half letters, as their doctors say, and signifies that He is the Master who contains heaven and earth." Renaissance Hebraists like Drusius elaborated this: Yod (10), being only a half-letter (reaching only to the middle height of other letters), signifies IHWH (the Master); Shin stands for Shamayim (heaven); Vau for Vearets (and earth)—yielding the complete meaning that Jesus is "Master of Heaven and Earth," the name of God incarnate. Tertullian's account of the Gnostic heretics Marcus and Colorbasus, who "playing with letters drew countless inept and dangerous dogmas, abusing what Christ said in the Apocalypse: 'I am the Alpha and the Omega,'" paradoxically testified to both the danger and the power of letter-mysticism. The very fact that heretics had abused these techniques showed their potency; the Christian task was to purify and Christianize them, not to abandon them.

Secret emphasizes that the medieval period had already developed Christian versions of mystical alphabet-theology. The work of Raban Maur (776–856), titled De laudibus Crucis (On the Praises of the Cross), exemplified what later Kabbalists would call the vera Cabala christiana, the true Christian Kabbalah. This remarkable text, with its interweaving of prose and poetry, collaborated with twenty-eight mystical figures to expound the mysteries of Christian faith through the symbolic meaning of numbers, geometric shapes, and the sacred syllables. The names of angels, the number of winds, the seven gifts of the Holy Spirit (septem dona Spiritus Sancti), the eight Beatitudes (octo beatitudines)—all were mapped onto mystical patterns revealing a cosmos ordered by divine pedagogical intention. When Johannes Wimpfeling (1450–1528), the German humanist and pedagogue, brought out a new edition of Raban Maur in 1503 at Pforzheim under Reuchlin's patronage, this act of editorial recovery signaled Renaissance validation: ancient Christian mysticism could serve as a model and justification for contemporary Christian Kabbalah. The circulation of such authorities created a hermeneutic feedback loop: Christians studying Kabbalah discovered patristic precedents; those precedents then seemed to authorize further Kabbalist study as simply the continuation of ancient Christian mystical theology, now enriched with newly recovered Hebrew sources.

Chapter II opens with Secret noting that despite Pico's documented aversion to the Talmud (evident throughout his theses and apologies, which dismiss rabbinic legal reasoning as contemporary Jewish polemic against Christianity), Pico shows little engagement with the medieval Christian apologetic literature where Kabbalah had already begun to inflect Christian theology. Secret accordingly turns his attention to the medieval and early Renaissance apologetic tradition—the Christian polemics against Judaism that, paradoxically, increasingly incorporated Jewish mystical arguments and frameworks. He gestures toward earlier, more remote antecedents of Christian engagement with Hebrew mysticism: the letter-symbolism (apices) preserved in Boethius (480–524), the medieval philosopher whose works on mathematics and logic passed down Pythagorean and Neoplatonic symbolic systems; Virgil of Toulouse (6th century), known for his symbolic interpretations; Agobard of Lyon (c. 769–840), the Carolingian theologian; Roger Bacon (1214–1292), the Franciscan experimenter and linguist who invoked Hebrew letter-mysticism in his program for scientific and theological reform; and Henricus de Hassia (†1397), a medieval scholastic whom some have credited with kabbalistic tendencies in his calculation of the Antichrist's arrival as 1666 based on the numerical values of Hebrew and Latin alphabets and the calculated duration of Old and New Testament epochs. These figures belong, Secret notes, to a world nourished by Isidore of Seville's Etymologies, where the belief in the Semitic power of language and the Greek conviction (from Plato's Cratylus) that names bear an inherent relationship to their meanings converge. Yet the true beginning of Christian Kabbalah within the apologetic tradition, Secret argues, lies with a more recent figure: Moïse Sefardi, known in his Christian identity as Petrus Alfonsi (fl. c. 1106), a Spanish Jew of Huesca who converted to Christianity and authored the Dialogus Petri et Mosi, a dialogue in which Petrus (the converted Christian) responds to Moseh (the Jewish interlocutor), systematically exposing the logical contradictions in rabbinic Judaism while advancing Christian doctrine.

The pivotal theological innovation of Petrus Alfonsi was his reading of the Tetragrammaton as expressing within itself the Christian doctrine of the Trinity. Petrus asserts that the divine name YHWH (which is written with four letters but pronounced with three, since Yod-He can be read as one unit, and the final He repeats the second letter) is "one and three"—expressing simultaneously the unity of divine substance and the trinity of divine persons. He explains: "If you join the first and second letters, Iod and He, you obtain a first name; if you join the second and third, you obtain a second name; if you join the third and fourth, Vau and He, you have a third name. And if you reunite them, there is only one name." This structure is illustrated by a figure of concentric circles—one circle containing three others—that would become the canonical diagram for Christian Kabbalists seeking to represent the Trinity within the sacred Name of Israel. The theological brilliance of this maneuver is that it reads the Jewish God's own name as proof of Christian doctrine; the tetragrammatic structure itself encodes Trinitarian mystery. This insight passed into the thought of Joachim of Fiore (c. 1135–1202), the visionary Cistercian abbot whose threefold dispensationalism—the ages of the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit unfolding in history—arguably reflected or was reinforced by Alfonsi's concentric-circle theology of the divine name. It has been speculated that Dante Alighieri (1265–1321), in the final vision of the Paradiso where he beholds three circles of divine light (tre giri / Di tre colori), one reflected in another, may have been inspired by or was drawing on this same Alfonsi-Joachimite tradition of the Trinity encoded in geometric and mystical form. The imagery—"In the profound and clear substance of the high Light appeared three circles of three colors and of one measure, and one by another, like Iris by Iris, seemed reflected, and the third seemed a fire which equally breathed forth from here and there"—suggests a vision fundamentally shaped by the trinitarian mysticism of the Tetragrammaton.

The doctrine reached its most systematic and influential form in the Pugio Fidei (Dagger of Faith), composed c. 1278 by the Dominican Ramon Martí (†1284), a monumental work of Christian-Jewish polemic that became the greatest medieval synthesis of Christian polemical theology. The Pugio Fidei systematically marshals Jewish sources—biblical texts, rabbinic interpretations, kabbalistic doctrines—to argue that Judaism's own traditions prove Christianity. Martí's treatment of the Tetragrammaton synthesizes Alfonsi's insight with more elaborate kabbalistic vocabulary. He explains that within the divine name YHWH, the repeated letter He (Heh), appearing as both the second and fourth letter, expresses the unity of essence underlying apparent multiplicity. The three differing letters (Iod, He, Vau) represent three Middoth, "properties" or "persons" (the exact term is crucial—Middoth in Hebrew mystical language means "measures" or "attributes," but Martí readapts it to mean "persons" in the Trinitarian sense). These three properties differ in pronunciation and form, yet they unite in a single and transcendent name of God. As Martí writes: "We can say that in the divine name there are first three letters which differ from one another both in writing and in pronunciation: Iod, He, Vau. Then there is one, the He, which is repeated at the end of the word, which closes and perfects it. This indicates, when this name is applied to the Messiah, that there are in him three substances differing one from another: body, soul, and the wisdom of God. The relationship of the first two to the third is such that it proves that in the Messiah there are not God and a man, two persons, but one, nor two Messiahs but one—just as the three different letters make one single and most glorious name of God, since finally they are collected in one, which is called He." Martí thus establishes a template that would dominate Christian Kabbalism for centuries: the Jewish mystical tradition, properly read, reveals Christian doctrine. The Tetragrammaton becomes a crypto-Christian text, its structure mirroring the Trinitarian economy. The sacred name of the God of Israel, when subjected to kabbalistic analysis, yields the name and nature of Jesus Christ.

This hermeneutic move—reading Jewish mystical sources as containing hidden Christian doctrine—would structure much of Renaissance Christian Kabbalah. The Pugio Fidei circulated widely and was frequently cited as the authoritative medieval precedent for Christian Kabbalist interpretation. Later Christian Kabbalists, like Pierre Galatino and Giovanni Pico himself, would both acknowledge their debt to Martí's pioneering synthesis and would develop more elaborate versions of his arguments. The effect was to establish a genealogy: the medieval Church, through figures like Petrus Alfonsi and Ramon Martí, had already begun to recognize the theological utility of Jewish mystical sources; the Renaissance, discovering Hebrew texts directly and encountering Jewish teachers, was simply carrying forward an established Christian intellectual tradition, enriching it with newly available textual resources and expert instruction. Yet this genealogy obscured a fundamental rupture: medieval Christian polemicists had invented their readings of Jewish texts, often drawing on sources that Jewish Kabbalists did not recognize; Renaissance Christian Kabbalists, by contrast, were actually engaging with living Jewish intellectual traditions, interpreting them through Christian lenses but at least encountering them in their full complexity. The confusion of medieval invention with Renaissance discovery would characterize much Christian Kabbalist writing, creating an illusion of continuity and authorization that actually masked a more radical encounter between Christian theology and Jewish mysticism than either tradition had known before. The shift from medieval polemics citing fragmentary and often corrupted Jewish sources to Renaissance humanists reading kabbalistic texts in their original Hebrew, with Jewish teachers at their side, marks a fundamental transformation in the nature of Christian engagement with Kabbalah—a transformation that Secret's careful historical method aims to illuminate while avoiding the triumphalist or romanticizing distortions that have often obscured it.
