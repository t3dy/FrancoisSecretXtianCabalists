Chunk 3

Pages 10-12

The chunk continues **Pico's** elaborate defense of the secret doctrine by elaborating the fate of these hidden books. According to Pico's reading of IV Esdras, the secret doctrine — unpublished and revealed only orally to Moses and the seventy sages — was finally written down in seventy books under **Esdras** after the Babylonian exile and destruction of the First Temple. These seventy books, as IV Esdras states, contain "the veins of intelligence and the source of wisdom," yet remained hidden from the masses. Pico anticipated the recovery of these very books, believing that the kabbalistic manuscripts he had purchased through his Jewish informants (particularly his convert-teacher Flavius Mithridates) represented fragments of this ancient revelation. In his *Heptaplus* and other works, he adds the authority of **Jerome** — Saint Jerome the Vulgate translator, who had learned from Hebrew masters and repeatedly invoked rabbinical interpretation against purely literal reading. Pico further systematized his claim by arguing that the Hebrew school divides into three sects: philosophers (the rationalists), kabbalists (the esotericists), and talmudists (the legalists), so when the Church Fathers speak of wisdom received from the Hebrew tradition, they must necessarily have meant the kabbalists. In the *Heptaplus* particularly, Pico repeats the strict rule that no one may treat *Ma'aseh Bereshit* (the work of creation, the mystical cosmogony) before maturity, echoing rabbinic interdictions on early study of mysticism — a constraint that legitimates Kabbalah as advanced theology rather than popular superstition. Secret surveys the specific patristic and medieval materials that later Christian Kabbalists reused, establishing a chain of transmission from Jerome through the Renaissance. Jerome on the added *He* in the names Abraham (Avram→Avraham) and Sarah (Sarai→Sarah) — the additional letter signifying heightened spiritual status — became a standard example of letter-mysticism grounded in a Church Father. The **Ath-Bash** cipher, in which the Hebrew alphabet is read inward (aleph paired with tav, bet with shin, etc.), yielding the substitution that reads Babel (*Bavel*) as Sheshach in Jeremiah 25:26, similarly anchored Jewish mystical exegesis in Christian antiquity. Jerome had explained this cipher; **Reuchlin** would later provide full expositions. The ten divine names, each descending in power but increasing in manifestation, culminate in the ineffable **Tetragrammaton** (Iod-He-Vau-He, the four-letter name that cannot be pronounced aloud), the concentration of all divine force — a framework that every Christian Kabbalist would elaborate. Jerome's exposition of the mystical meaning of the Hebrew letters themselves, preserved through commentarial tradition, became a resource: the letter *Aleph* (ox, first, unity), *Bet* (house, duality, the binary of created reality), *Gimel* (camel, carrying), and onward through all twenty-two letters, each bearing symbolic and theological weight. Claude **Duret**'s massive *Thrésor de l'histoire des langues* (1613) would later quote Jerome at length giving the symbolic sense of each letter. **Irenaeus** of Lyon's treatment of the name of Jesus — the divine name par excellence in Christian theology — with glosses by Drusius the Hebraist, provided patristic authority for Christological letter-mysticism. **Tertullian**'s polemics against Marcus and Colorbasus (Gnostic heretics who misused letter-mysticism) showed the dangers and the attractions of these techniques. And **Raban Maur**'s *De laudibus Crucis* (On the Praises of the Cross), a "true Christian Kabbalah" as Secret calls it, with its intricate visual and textual arrangements of sacred syllables, was reissued in 1503 under Reuchlin's patronage, signaling Renaissance approval of this earlier medieval synthesis. **Chapter II, "Les débuts de la kabbale en Espagne,"** then opens with Secret noting Pico's documented aversion to the Talmud (evident in his theses and their defenses), and turning his attention to the medieval apologetic literature where Kabbalah proper began to inflect Christian theology. He traces remote antecedents: Boethius's *apices* (letter-value symbolism), Virgil of Toulouse, Agobard of Lyon, Roger Bacon (the 13th-century Franciscan experimenter who invoked Hebrew letter-mysticism), and Henricus de Hassia. The pivotal figure is **Moïse Sefardi**, also known as **Petrus Alfonsi** (fl. c. 1106), a Spanish Jew who converted to Christianity and authored the *Dialogus*, a dialogue between himself and a Jewish interlocutor exposing the logical contradictions in rabbinic Judaism while advancing Christian doctrine. Alfonsi's crucial innovation was reading the **Tetragrammaton** itself (YHWH) as one-and-three — expressing in the Hebrew divine name the Christian Trinity, with unity of substance underlying trinity of persons — illustrated by a figure of concentric circles that would become canonical in Christian Kabbalist symbolism. This Trinitarian reading of the sacred name of the God of Israel became a template. It passed to **Joachim of Fiore** (c. 1135-1202), the visionary Cistercian abbot whose three-fold dispensationalism (ages of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit) may have been inspired by or reinforced through Alfonsi's concentric-circle theology, and possibly inspired **Dante**'s three circles of the Beatific Vision in the *Paradiso*. The doctrine was spread above all by the Dominican **Ramon Martí**'s monumental *Pugio Fidei* (Dagger of Faith, c. 1278) — the greatest medieval Christian-Jewish polemical text — which read the repeated *He* in the Tetragrammaton as expressing the unity of essence among three *Middot* (properties or persons), synthesizing Trinitarian dogma with Hebrew mystical vocabulary, and establishing a template that would dominate Christian Kabbalism for centuries.
