Chunk 32

Pages 101-103

This chunk traces the remarkable literary productivity and systematic kabbalistic doctrine developed by Postel during his Venetian exile and enforced retreat, establishing him as the architect of Christian Kabbalah's most comprehensive and audacious vision. His **Latin Zohar translation**, long presumed lost to scholarship, was gradually recovered through dedicated detective work: an unnamed érudit discovered one copy in circulation, and Secret himself—employing the archival methods of the modern scholar—located the original Postel manuscripts (comprising some 350 large folio pages) in the **Göttingen** library; additional Postel materials entered the **Sloane** collection at the British Museum, having migrated through Basel and the hands of the printer **Oporin**, who published many of Postel's works. This translation, revised and completed in 1563, was entrusted to **Le Fèvre de la Boderie**, Postel's most devoted disciple, who became the custodian and executor of his textual legacy. During his Venetian sojourn, Postel functioned as the "véloce scribe" or **tachygraphe** (swift writer), producing a torrent of translations and original compositions that filled manuscript volumes: he translated the **Bahir** (the early Kabbalistic work predating the Zohar), significant portions of **Recanati**'s learned commentary on the Torah, wrote original treatises on Genesis and the Book of Ruth, and authored esoteric works with titles such as the *Absconditorum clavis* (The Key to Hidden Things) and *De nativitate mediatoris ultima* (On the Final Birth of the Mediator). His scholarly prestige was such that he even secured appointment as censor of Hebrew books for the papal nuncio **Della Casa**, a position that granted him institutional legitimacy while working within Catholic surveillance structures. During this same period, Postel acquired an extraordinary collection of Arabic and Syriac manuscripts through the diplomatic channels of **Gabriel d'Aramon**'s embassy in Jerusalem, materials he envisioned as components of a comprehensive **polyglot Bible** that would integrate Christian and Jewish sources into a single unified text. But when he returned from these scholarly missions, he discovered that **Mère Jeanne**, his spiritual guide and the woman who had unlocked the Zohar's mysteries, had died during his absence—a loss that precipitated his most radical transformation. In 1552, Postel experienced his central mystical crisis, which he termed the **"Immutation"** (transformation or transfiguration): he claimed to have received the "survêtement" (garment or investiture) that **Mère Jeanne** had prophesied, and in this transfigured state he identified himself as **Caïn restitué** (Cain restored), now the son of the New Eve, claiming to possess bodily immortality in which nourishment dissipated into pure air rather than material digestion. This visionary moment unleashed a cascade of written works—*La Restitution de toutes choses* (The Restitution of All Things) chief among them—in which Postel proclaimed his mission as **Élie** (Elijah) reincarnate, the prophet commissioned to restore all fragmentary knowledge into unity and to prepare the world for the final Concord. The implications were radical: Postel had moved beyond textual erudition into prophetic self-identification, claiming that his illumination granted him access to mysteries closed to ordinary scholarship. This antinomian stance provoked severe persecution. Forced to flee Venice, he wandered through **Besançon**, **Basel** (where he worked with printers), **Vienna** (where he contributed to the Syriac New Testament edition), and returned to Venice, where the **Inquisition** finally apprehended him. The Inquisitorial tribunal, unable to categorize his claims within doctrinal heresy, declared him *amens* (mad or insane) and imprisoned him in Rome under papal custody. He remained confined until the death of **Pope Paul IV** in 1559 brought a modest reprieve; he was then transferred to the monastery of **Saint-Martin-des-Champs** in Paris, where he remained under ecclesiastical confinement until his death in 1581. Secret's systematic analysis of **Postel's kabbalistic system** reveals a **millenarianism** of extraordinary sophistication. In Postel's vision, both world and humanity were created by the **Mediator** (Christ), who stands between the absolutely immobile God and the mobile creation, effecting their union through the mediation of infinite divine reason. History unfolds through four distinct ages, each governed by a different divine dispensation: the first is the *law of nature* (natural reason inscribed in creation); the second is the *written Law* (Torah and prophecy); the third is the *law of grace* (Gospel and Christian charity); and the fourth and final age is the **Concorde** (the restoration of humanity to its pre-lapsarian condition of perfect reason, when mankind shall recover the wisdom lost at the Fall). Postel grounded this system in two essential kabbalistic doctrines: the **spirit of the Messiah**, which he understood as a single divine essence migrating through successive human vessels across sacred history, and **Gilgul** (recirculation or transmigration of souls), which he reinterpreted not as individual soul-migration but as the cosmic circulation of a unified divine substance through the personages of sacred history—condensed in the paradoxical formula "Omnia in omnibus" (all in all). This Messiah-spirit, in Postel's framework, was one and identical across the patriarchs: **Adam**, **Henoch** (Enoch), **Élie** (Elijah), **Moïse** (Moses), and in its feminine aspect, the **Venetian Virgin**, whose prophetic illumination had unlocked these mysteries for him. Throughout his exegetical works, particularly the *De Etruriæ*, Postel developed a symbolic numerology centered on October (the tenth month), which he understood as encoding Yom Kippur/Assereth (the Day of Atonement and the mystery of the Ten Sefirot), the second Passover (the Christian resurrection), the cosmic deluge, and the figure of Janus/Noah as the hinge between ages. Most radically, Postel reattributed the Zohar itself to **Symeon the Just** (the biblical Simeon of Luke 2 who recognized the infant Jesus in the Temple), reading the *Lek Leka* section (God's command to Abraham to go forth) as a veiled prophecy of Christ's epiphany at **Lud** (which Postel etymologized as *Veled*, meaning birth or emergence), a revelation he believed had been preserved in esoteric form by Symeon ben Yochai but later redacted and obscured under the Christian persecutions of the Roman Emperor Antoninus Pius.
