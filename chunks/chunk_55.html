Alfonsus Ciaconius extends Gesner's bibliographic work with a comprehensive alphabetical catalog through 1583, though it was not published until 1731. A 1581 letter to Cardinal Sirleto reveals his defense against critics who opposed his inclusion of rabbinic texts. Ciaconius distinguishes four types of rabbis: those pre-dating Christ (confirming Christianity), medieval commentators of sincere faith, converted rabbis who refuted Judaism, and modern scholars. He argues the Church does not forbid their reading and claims he merely catalogs them for critical examination. Ciaconius maintained connections with important figures like Joannes Paulus Eustachius Nolanus and located lost works, including Antonio de Nebrija's Trilingual Dictionary. He highlights rare manuscripts like Albertus Patavinus's *De Messiae adventu* (c. 1480) and works on Talmudic revision, along with documents by forger Alfonsus Ciccarellus. Giulio Camillo Delminio (c. 1485-1544) emerges as a pivotal figure whose "Theatre of the World"—never completed—incorporated Kabbalistic structures, particularly seven sephiroth corresponding to astrological systems. Camillo drew knowledge from Georges of Venice and Pico della Mirandola, developing a comprehensive method claiming to enable eloquence in multiple languages within a month through devoted study. His theatrical system became emblematic of Renaissance intellectual ambition. Other key figures diverge on kabbalah's validity. Lilio Gregorio Giraldi (1479-1552), friend of Pico della Mirandola, expresses skepticism about the seventy-two divine names, viewing them as cabaliste inventions rather than reverent theology. Alessandro Citolini da Serravale (c. 1500-1565) positions kabbalah alongside necromancy and demonic literature, though he adopts Camillo's analogical method. Conversely, Alessandro Farra (fl. 1561-1571) embraces Pico's Heptaplus and numerological exegesis, discovering the Hebrew letters of his colleagues' names spell MAGA (magic). Fabio Paolini synthesizes Camillo, Farra, and earlier authorities through seven interpretive modes applied to Virgil's *Aeneid*, arguing Virgil possessed Kabbalistic knowledge. J.J. Scaliger accused him of cabalizing; Paolini countered that all philosophers treating numbers qualify as cabalistes. These debates illustrate the emergence of Christian kabbalah as both intellectual legitimacy and methodological innovation during the late sixteenth century.
